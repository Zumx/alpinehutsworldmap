The Alps span six countries, four official languages, and a dozen regional dialects, and every one of them has produced its own word for a mountain hut — along with its own assumptions about what that word implies regarding staffing, amenities, ownership, and the social compact between host and guest. A visitor moving across national borders on a single long-distance walk might sleep in an Austrian Hütte, a Swiss Cabane, an Italian Rifugio, a French Refuge, and a Slovenian Koča within the same week, and each will feel subtly different even when the elevation, the food, and the weather are similar. Understanding the terminology helps manage expectations — and occasionally prevents the embarrassing discovery that the structure you planned to sleep in is an unstaffed emergency shelter with no warden, no food, and no blankets.

## Hütte — The German-Speaking Tradition

In Germany, Austria, and the German-speaking parts of Switzerland and South Tyrol, the standard word is Hütte, plural Hütten. The term covers everything from a full-service mountain inn with hot showers and a three-course dinner to an unmanned emergency shelter (Notunterkunft or Biwakschachtel) with eight emergency blankets and a logbook. Context — specifically, the managing club — determines which kind you are dealing with.

The DAV and ÖAV divide their Hütten into four categories. A Bewirtschaftete Hütte is fully staffed with a warden (Hüttenwirt) who serves meals, manages bookings, and maintains the building. An Unbewirtschaftete Hütte is a self-service facility, typically open year-round, with sleeping space, a stove, and basic supplies that guests pay for using an honesty box. A Stützpunkt is a mid-size staffed hut, typically on a less-visited route, open for a shorter season than a full Hütte. A Biwakschachtel is the minimal emergency shelter — often a prefabricated metal box holding eight to sixteen people, placed at critical points in the high mountains where full huts are impractical.

The word Berghütte (mountain hut) is used interchangeably with Hütte in common speech but tends to appear in marketing and tourism contexts rather than in the clubs' own technical vocabulary.

## Rifugio and Bivacco — The Italian System

In Italy, the standard term for a staffed mountain hut is Rifugio (plural Rifugi). The CAI classifies its rifugi into three grades based on services offered. A Rifugio di I Categoria provides meals, staffed accommodation, and basic equipment rental; most of the famous Dolomite rifugi fall into this category. A Rifugio di II Categoria offers meals and beds but with more limited services. A Rifugio di III Categoria is a self-service facility.

The bivacco (plural bivacchi) is the Italian equivalent of the German Biwakschachtel: an unstaffed emergency shelter, usually a small prefabricated cabin or a converted stone building, left unlocked for mountaineers in need. The Bivacco Comino at 3,006 metres above the Valsesia and the Bivacco Baroni on the approach to the Adamello glacier are well-known examples. Using a bivacco is free, but it is not a substitute for booking a rifugio — the facilities are minimal and the space is limited.

South Tyrol presents a bilingual situation: the same hut may be listed as both a Hütte in German and a Rifugio in Italian, with the same building and warden but two separate entries in two club databases. The Dreizinnenhütte / Rifugio Antonio Locatelli near the Tre Cime di Lavaredo is a notable example.

## Cabane and Refuge — The French and Swiss French Vocabulary

In Switzerland, the French-speaking cantons (Vaud, Valais, Geneva, Fribourg, and Neuchâtel) use Cabane for the SAC's high-mountain huts, a term with slightly grander connotations than the German Hütte — a Cabane is almost always staffed, typically well-equipped, and carries expectations of a proper dinner service. The Cabane de Valsorey (3,030 m), Cabane du Mountet (2,886 m), and Cabane du Grand Combin (2,886 m) are among the SAC's most celebrated.

In France, the Club Alpin Français uses Refuge as its standard term for a staffed mountain shelter. The linguistic distinction between a Refuge and a Cabane is purely geographical — CAF huts in France are Refuges, SAC huts in French Switzerland are Cabanes — but practically speaking the two systems are closely equivalent in terms of services, standards, and the reciprocity discount that applies across both.

The word Gîte d'étape, which appears on some French long-distance hiking routes, is distinct from a Refuge: it refers to a lower-altitude hiking lodge, typically at valley level and accessible by road, that provides dormitory accommodation for walkers. A Gîte is not an Alpine hut in the technical sense and typically does not appear in CAF or SAC databases.

## Koča and Dom — The Slovenian and Balkan Usage

In Slovenia and the Croatian Highlands, the mountain hut is a Koča (plural Koče) if it is a simple staffed or semi-staffed shelter, and a Planinski Dom (mountain house) if it is a larger and more comfortable establishment, often at a lower elevation and accessible by road. The Slovenian Alpine Association (PZS) manages most of the Slovenian network, which includes around 170 facilities across the Julian Alps, the Kamnik-Savinja Alps, and the Karawanken range.

The Triglavski Dom na Kredarici, at 2,515 metres on the northeast ridge of Triglav — Slovenia's highest mountain at 2,864 m — is the highest staffed Koča in Slovenia and one of the most famous in the former Yugoslavia. It has space for 125 guests and is open from late June to late September. The Dom pri Tamarju in the Tamar valley is an example of the Planinski Dom category: larger, at 1,108 metres, accessible by forest road, and open year-round.

## Setr, Fjellstue, and DNT Hytter — Scandinavia

The Norwegian Trekking Association (DNT) uses the term Hytte (hut) for the entire range of its facilities, from unstaffed self-service huts in the Jotunheimen to large staffed mountain lodges in the Rondane and Jotunheimen massifs. A staffed DNT Hytte is called a Turisthytte or Betjent hytte (served hut), while an unmanned facility stocked with food for purchase on an honesty system is an Ubetjent hytte. A Nødhytte (emergency hut) is the minimal safety shelter, locked between seasons but opened by a key available to all DNT members.

The Scandinavian network differs from the Alpine one in a key respect: many DNT huts are separated by a full day's ski tour or summer hike from the nearest road or other hut, and the network is explicitly designed for self-sufficiency over multiple consecutive days without resupply. This shapes the hut culture in a direction distinct from the Alps, where valley access and cable cars make single-day visitors common.

## Why Terminology Matters for Booking

The practical reason for understanding these terms is that booking a bivacco, nødhytte, or Biwakschachtel through a reservations system is not possible — they are by definition unbooked emergency facilities. Confusing a listed bivacco with a listed rifugio when planning a route means arriving to find no warden, no food, no confirmed bed, and no hot shower. This mistake is more common than it might seem, particularly on routes in the Slovenian Julian Alps and the Italian Dolomites where both staffed rifugi and unstaffed bivacchi appear on the same trail maps with similar symbology.
