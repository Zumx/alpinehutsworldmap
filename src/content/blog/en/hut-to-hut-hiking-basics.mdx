Hut-to-hut hiking strips the alpine experience down to what matters: the
terrain, the weather, and your legs. No tent, no full kitchen kit, no
expedition pack. What fits in a 35-litre bag and what the hut provides
determines the whole equation. Get it right once and you will never want to
camp in the Alps again; get it wrong and you spend the week carrying four
kilograms of things you did not need.

## The 30–40 litre bag

A 35-litre pack is the working sweet spot for two to seven-day hut routes. At
30 litres you start making compromises on safety layers; above 40 litres most
people overpack and pay for it on steep ground. The bag should fit the torso
properly and have a functioning hip belt — on long traverses, every gram of
weight shifted from shoulders to hips matters.

Hip belt pockets are worth having. They hold the items you need without
stopping: sunscreen, a snack, sunglasses, a phone, lip balm.

## What to pack

Base layers: one merino or synthetic hiking shirt to wear, one to change into.
Merino resists odour through multiple days without washing, which matters in a
dormitory. Mid layer: a light fleece or down gilet. Outer layer: a hard-shell
waterproof jacket with taped seams — non-negotiable in any range where weather
changes fast. Waterproof trousers are worth their 200 grams.

Insulation: a synthetic or down puffy jacket packs small and serves two
purposes — summit warmth and cold dormitory nights. At 3500 m, dormitories can
be cold even in July.

Feet: trail runners or light hiking boots both work well on maintained paths.
Boots offer more ankle support and are obligatory if the route crosses glacier
or via ferrata sections. Bring gaiters for late-season snow crossings.

First aid: blister kit (pre-cut moleskin and Compeed), ibuprofen, electrolytes,
a SAM splint, a triangular bandage, and any personal medication. A small
emergency bivouac bag weighs under 100 grams and can save a life in a
deteriorating weather situation.

Sun protection: the UV index at 3000 m on a clear day routinely exceeds 10.
SPF 50 sunscreen, glacier glasses (Category 4 lenses), and a sun hat or
brimmed baseball cap are non-negotiable above the treeline.

## The sleeping liner

Every staffed hut in the Alps requires a sleeping liner of some kind. The
rules vary: Swiss SAC huts require a full sheet bag; many Italian rifugi ask
for at minimum a Hüttenschlafsack or provide one for rent. The hut does not
provide a sleeping bag — only blankets.

A silk liner weighs 80–130 grams and compresses to nothing. It is warm enough
for most hut dormitories in summer, where there are heavy wool blankets. A
synthetic liner (200–350 grams) adds meaningful warmth for higher or colder
huts. A cotton liner is bulky, takes longer to dry if wet, and is the worst
choice for a multi-day trip.

## Hut slippers

This is one of those cultural rules that feels minor until you violate it.
Every staffed Alpine hut has a boot room (Schuhraum or local equivalent) just
inside the main entrance where all outdoor footwear stays. You change into
hut slippers before entering the dining room, dormitories, and common areas.
Crocs, lightweight camp sandals, or thin closed-toe slippers all work. Socks
alone are not adequate on cold stone floors and are considered bad form. Pack
a pair of lightweight camp shoes; they weigh 200–400 grams and save
significant friction.

## Dorms versus private rooms

Most huts operate primarily in Matratzenlager (dormitory) style: open-plan
sleeping areas with mattresses side by side, shared blankets, and communal
hooks. Privacy is limited and noise from other guests is a given. Earplugs are
among the most useful 10 grams in the bag.

Some huts offer small private or semi-private rooms (Zimmer or chambres
privées) for couples or families, usually at significant premium and always
booked out weeks in advance on popular routes. On the Haute Route or the Tour
du Mont Blanc, private rooms during peak season (July–August) are typically
gone by April.

Lockers for valuables are rare. Use a small padlock on your bag's zipper and
keep passports, cash, and phones with you or in a dry bag inside your pack.

## Charging electronics

USB charging is standard in most staffed huts, but capacity is limited. There
may be two or four plugs for forty people. Bring a compact power bank (10,000
mAh) and charge it overnight when a plug is available. Do not occupy a socket
all night if the hut is full; charge for a few hours and free the socket.
Navigation devices and satellite communicators should be fully charged before
leaving the valley.

## Navigation tools

Paper maps do not fail, run out of battery, or lose signal. The 1:25,000 or
1:50,000 Swisstopo sheets cover Switzerland with extraordinary accuracy.
Kompass and Alpenvereinskarten maps cover Austria and the eastern ranges.
IGN 1:25,000 sheets cover France. Carry the relevant sheets in a waterproof
map case.

For digital navigation, Swisstopo's mobile app (free offline layers) is the
gold standard for Swiss routes. Komoot is useful for routing and waypoint
management across the Alps; the route planning interface is better than most
alternatives. Outdooractive carries a large library of marked routes with
offline capability. On any multi-day route, download offline maps before
leaving the valley — summit zones and remote valleys have no signal.

A GPS watch or dedicated device is worth having for routes crossing unmarked
glacier or high snow. Know how to use it before you need it.

## Pacing and turnaround discipline

Most hut-to-hut walkers underestimate stage times on their first multi-day
route. Published stage times on Alpine routes use a formula (300 m of ascent
= 1 hour; 4 km horizontal = 1 hour) that assumes fit, unladen walkers in good
conditions. A 35-litre pack, high altitude, and the cumulative fatigue of day
three all extend this time meaningfully. Add 20–30 percent to published stage
times for the first two days while your body adapts, and plan arrival at the
next hut no later than 15:00 to account for afternoon weather.

The turnaround discipline is the practical safety rule: decide your turnaround
time before leaving the hut, and follow it regardless of progress toward the
objective. On most hut-to-hut routes the turnaround is not returning to the
previous hut — it is pressing on to the next hut by the shortest route and
calling ahead to the guardian to report a late arrival.

## First-timer recommendations

For parties doing their first multi-day hut route: choose a 3-day circuit
rather than a linear traverse for the first experience. The Stubai Runde,
the Tour du Massif du Mont Blanc south circuit, and the shorter Dolomites hut
circuits (Tre Cime circuit, Alta Via 1 from Lago di Braies to Rifugio Biella)
all work well. Linear traverses require either a car shuttle or return transport
logistics that add planning complexity.

Second-day fatigue is real and normal. Plan the second day of a multi-day
trip to be the shorter one, not the longer one. The first-day enthusiasm always
leads to moving too fast; the second-day legs carry the consequence.

Plastic bags inside the pack keep electronics, documents, and sleeping liner
dry in the rain. A waterproof pack cover is useful but not a substitute for
internal drybags for critical items — pack covers blow off in wind.
