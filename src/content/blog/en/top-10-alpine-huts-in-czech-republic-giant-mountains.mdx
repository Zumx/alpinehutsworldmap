The Czech Republic's mountain ranges occupy three distinct geographical zones. The Krkonoše (Giant Mountains) along the Polish border in Bohemia contain the highest point in the country — Sněžka at 1,603 m — and an extensive plateau of subalpine moorland known for its harsh weather, rare arctic-alpine flora, and significant tourism infrastructure dating from the nineteenth century. The Jeseníky in northern Moravia reach 1,491 m at Praděd and form a broad, rounded massif with a more rugged character than the Krkonoše. The Beskydy in southeastern Moravia, along the Slovak and Polish borders, are lower (Lysá hora, 1,323 m) but heavily forested and culturally rich. The Czech mountain shelter tradition — the chata (plural chaty), horský hotel (mountain hotel), and bouda (a Krkonoše-specific term for a mountain building) — has deep roots: the first mountain shelters on Sněžka were built in the early seventeenth century, and the network was greatly expanded by German-speaking settlers and hiking clubs in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. The ten shelters below represent the most important overnight points across all three mountain zones.

## 1. Chata Severka, Jeseníky

Chata Severka is positioned at around 1,200 m on the northern slopes of the Jeseníky, near the main ridge trail connecting Praděd (1,491 m) to the lower passes toward Opava. The chata is a traditional timber mountain building with accommodation for around 40 people and a restaurant serving Moravian cuisine — svíčková, svařené víno, and bramboráky. The Jeseníky ridge in late June is carpeted with wild blueberries, and the hut serves as a rest point for both hikers and cross-country skiers who use the well-maintained trail network year-round.

## 2. Barborka, Krkonoše

Chata Barborka is a mountain shelter in the Krkonoše on the approaches to the Sněžka summit plateau, in the zone of dwarf pine (kleč) that characterises the Krkonoše between 1,300 and 1,500 m. The kleč (Pinus mugo) thickets are impenetrable off-trail and the designated paths weave through them. Barborka has basic accommodation and is primarily used by parties doing the full Krkonoše ridge traverse from Harrachov to Sněžka — a two- to three-day route over about 30 kilometres.

## 3. Jiráskova Chata, Krkonoše

Jiráskova chata is one of the most historically significant mountain shelters in Bohemia, located near the Labská reservoir in the western Krkonoše. Named after the Czech author Alois Jirásek, the chata was established in the early twentieth century and represents the Czech national-revival tradition of naming mountain shelters after cultural figures. The building has a distinctive traditional architecture — steep roof, timber frame, stone lower walls — and sleeps around 60 people in well-maintained rooms. The area around the chata is rich in Krkonoše endemic plant species protected within the national park.

## 4. Chata Mamut, Jeseníky

Chata Mamut is on the southern approaches to Praděd in the Jeseníky, near the ski resort infrastructure that serves the mountain in winter. The name — "Mammoth" — refers to the scale and solidity of the building, which has been a landmark on this section of the ridge since the interwar period. The chata has a well-stocked bar, a large dining room, and dormitory plus private room accommodation. In summer the surrounding ridge offers excellent panoramic walking with views reaching into Slovakia and Poland on clear days.

## 5. Turistická Chata Skalka, Beskydy

Turistická chata Skalka sits at around 900 m in the Beskydy, near the rocky outcrop from which it takes its name. The Beskydy range is known for its traditional Wallachian folk culture — the pastoral highland settlers (Valaši) who populated these mountains from the fifteenth century onward left a legacy of distinctive wooden architecture, cheese-making traditions, and the annual Rožnov open-air museum festival. Chata Skalka serves hikers on the blue-marked trail crossing the main Beskydy ridge and is open year-round.

## 6. Vebrovy Boudy, Krkonoše

Vebrovy boudy is a cluster of traditional Krkonoše mountain buildings — the bouda form — near the Pec pod Sněžkou ski resort. The Krkonoše bouda are architecturally distinctive: long, low timber structures with very steep roofs designed to shed the heavy snowfall (Krkonoše receives among the highest snowfall of any range in Central Europe, with snow cover sometimes lasting from November to May at the highest elevations). Vebrovy boudy has been restored and offers accommodation alongside a restaurant with views toward the Sněžka summit.

## 7. Bernava, Jeseníky

Chata Bernava is a mid-mountain shelter in the eastern Jeseníky above the spa town of Karlova Studánka — one of the oldest mountain spas in the Czech Republic, founded in 1785 on mineral springs in a forested valley. The chata serves walkers from the spa doing day hikes into the higher ridge zone and provides overnight accommodation for parties traversing the full Jeseníky ridge. The forest around Bernava contains silver fir stands that are among the oldest in the Jeseníky.

## 8. Načetín, Krušné Hory Foothills

Chata Načetín is positioned in the Krušné hory (Ore Mountains) near the Czech-German border — a range technically outside the main mountain zones but historically important for Czech mountain shelter culture, as the Krušné hory were densely settled by German-speaking miners and farmers until 1945. The chata at Načetín serves hikers on the cross-border trail network and visitors to the Doupovské hory natural area to the south.

## 9. Chata Jelení Louky, Beskydy

Chata Jelení Louky — "Deer Meadows Hut" — is a forest chata in the central Beskydy near Lysá hora (1,323 m), the highest summit of the Czech section of the Beskydy range. The chata is positioned in a clearing where the forest opens to give views of the surrounding ridge crests. The Beskydy is the last large forested mountain zone in the Czech Republic with resident wolf populations — the pack that recolonised the Beskydy from Slovakia in the 2010s is occasionally detected on camera traps near the ridge trails.

## 10. Horský Hotel Čarták, Beskydy

Horský hotel Čarták is a proper mountain hotel at around 850 m in the Beskydy near Velké Karlovice, a village at the head of a broad valley in the Wallachian heartland. The hotel is larger and more comfortable than most chalets on this list — it has a full spa, a restaurant serving regional specialities, and room service. The Beskydy around Čarták is extensively marked for Nordic skiing in winter and cycling in summer, and the hotel's central position in the valley makes it an ideal base for exploring the full range on day trips.

## Find Czech Mountain Chaty on the Map

The three Czech mountain zones — Krkonoše, Jeseníky, and Beskydy — are separated by considerable distances and require different approaches, and the chata network within each zone is best understood visually. [Open the map](/map) to see all shelters plotted on their terrain, with road access, ski resort proximity, and the main trail networks clearly visible. The map is particularly useful for planning the Krkonoše ridge traverse, where the distance between chaty determines whether two or three overnight stops are needed. Czech mountain shelters are typically open year-round or close for only a few weeks in November — a significant advantage over higher Alpine huts — and the trails are generally well-maintained and marked by the Klub českých turistů (Czech Tourist Club), whose trail-marking system has been operating since 1889 and covers the entire country.
