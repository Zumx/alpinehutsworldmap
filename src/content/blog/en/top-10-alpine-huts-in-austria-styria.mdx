Styria's alpine terrain stretches from the southern wall of the Dachstein massif in the northwest through the dramatic gorge landscape of the Gesäuse National Park to the broad ridges of the Niedere Tauern in the south and west. The province contains more than 600 kilometres of marked mountain routes and a hut network that is less famous internationally than Tyrol's but no less rewarding for the effort of reaching it. The limestone towers of the Gesäuse, rising sharply above the Enns River, draw serious rock climbers, while the Dachstein plateau and Schladminger Tauern offer more accessible multi-day traverses. These ten huts cover the range.

## 1. Adamekhaus, Dachstein

The Adamekhaus at 2,196 metres on the Dachstein glacier plateau is the highest staffed hut in Styria and one of the most dramatically positioned in the eastern Alps. It sits on the edge of the Gosaugletscher, the southern ice field of the Dachstein, with direct views across the plateau toward the summit cross at 2,995 metres. The approach from Hallstatt or from Gosau takes between three and five hours depending on the starting point, and the hut is open from late June through September. From here, routes lead across the glacier to the Gjaidsteinhütte and onward to the Wiesberghaus on the longer Dachstein traverse.

## 2. Hesshütte, Gesäuse

The Hesshütte at 1,699 metres is the central hut of the Gesäuse National Park, positioned directly beneath the north wall of the Hochtor group where some of the hardest limestone routes in Austria have been pioneered. The approach from Johnsbach takes around two hours and passes through the floor of a valley that has changed little since the first climbers explored it in the 1880s. The hut accommodates about 80 guests, operates from May through October, and serves as base for routes on the Hochtor, Planspitze, and Reichenstein, all accessible via a network of secured scrambling paths and open rock faces.

## 3. Rotgschirrhütte, Gesäuse

Standing at around 1,260 metres in the Johnsbach valley at the western entrance to the Gesäuse, the Rotgschirrhütte is a smaller, privately run hut with a particularly atmospheric position in the forest at the base of enormous limestone walls. It is used primarily as a base for technical climbing routes on the nearby Rotgschirr and Hexenturm crags, and its intimate scale contrasts with the larger club huts further into the park. The hut opens in the warmer months and is well regarded for its hospitality.

## 4. Ennstalerhütte, Gesäuse

The Ennstalerhütte sits at around 1,160 metres at the floor of the Enns gorge below the Haindlkar cirque, providing access to a different set of routes than the Hesshütte to the south. From here, the Haindlkarsteig — a demanding, partially secured path — climbs through steep rock terrain to the plateau. The hut is open throughout the hiking season and is particularly valued by day visitors approaching from the Enns road who want a comfortable base for exploring the lower gorge trails.

## 5. Schladmingerhütte, Schladminger Tauern

At 2,060 metres in the Schladminger Tauern above the town of Schladming, this hut serves one of the most beautiful high-route traverses in the Niedere Tauern — the Schladminger Vierseenweg, which links four mountain lakes at altitude. The Schladmingerhütte is operated by the OeAV and opens from late June to late September. The surrounding terrain is granite rather than limestone, giving it a different character from the northern ranges: rounder, smoother ridges, deep blue tarns, and a sense of solitude that the more accessible Dachstein cannot always match.

## 6. Kerschbaumerhütte, Seckauer Tauern

The Kerschbaumerhütte at approximately 1,800 metres in the Seckauer Tauern provides access to the Hochreichart (2,416 metres), the highest peak in this section of the Niedere Tauern. The range here is notably quiet compared to the Schladminger Tauern to the west, with fewer marked trails and a more rugged, trackless quality to the ridge walking. The hut opens in summer and serves the small community of serious ridge walkers who appreciate the Seckau area's combination of solitude and genuine alpine character.

## 7. Haindlkarhütte, Gesäuse

The Haindlkarhütte sits at around 1,100 metres at the entrance to the Haindlkar, the dramatic cirque on the north side of the Hochtor group. Like the Ennstalerhütte, it is used primarily as a staging point for the fierce ascent routes into the Gesäuse interior, but it also serves hikers traversing the lower trail system around the base of the national park's northern wall. The hut is a modest structure reflecting the austere character of the gorge landscape around it.

## 8. Gumpenwirt, Gesäuse fringe

The Gumpenwirt at approximately 640 metres operates in the lower Enns Valley near Admont, sitting at the threshold between the flat valley floor and the rising terrain of the Gesäuse massif. Rather than a high mountain hut, it functions as a traditional inn with mountain access, providing a comfortable overnight base for hikers who prefer lower altitude accommodation before or after tackling the gorge routes. The Stiftsbibliothek Admont — one of the largest monastic libraries in the world — is a few minutes' walk from the valley floor.

## 9. Lochbodenhütte, Niedere Tauern

The Lochbodenhütte stands at around 1,420 metres in the Niedere Tauern south of Liezen, offering a quieter alternative to the more-visited huts on the main Schladminger Vierseenweg circuit. The surrounding terrain includes a mix of meadow slopes, forested ridges, and open summits that make it suitable for families and less experienced mountain hikers. The hut opens in summer and is maintained by a local alpine club section.

## 10. Sonnschienhütte, Eisenerzer Alpen

The Sonnschienhütte sits at around 1,350 metres in the Eisenerzer Alpen above Eisenerz, a mining town famous for the Erzberg iron ore mountain — one of the largest open-cast mines in Europe. The Sonnschienhütte provides access to the Pfaffenstein and Kaiserschild summits and sits in an area that has received more attention in recent years as mountain bikers and trail runners have discovered the varied terrain between the Gesäuse and the Hochschwab massif to the east.

## Finding Your Styrian Base

Styria's huts range from simple shelters on granite ridges to substantial club facilities beneath dramatic limestone walls, and travel times between them vary from under an hour to a full day's walk. The Gesäuse National Park in particular demands careful planning, as several key routes pass through zones with strict nature protection rules.
