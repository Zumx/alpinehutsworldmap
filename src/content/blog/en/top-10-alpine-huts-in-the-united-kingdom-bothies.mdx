A bothy is, at its most basic, a building that is unlocked and free to use
as shelter. In the Scottish Highlands — and increasingly in other parts of the
UK — the term specifically refers to shelters maintained by the Mountain
Bothies Association (MBA), a volunteer organisation that has been maintaining
wild shelters in Scotland, England, and Wales since 1965. Unlike the Alpine
hut, the bothy provides no food, no staff, no booking system, and no reliable
heating; you bring everything, you leave the place clean, and you trust the
next visitor to do the same. The culture is self-reliant, respectful, and
specifically Scottish in its relationship to wild land.

## 1. Ben Alder Cottage, 430 m — Loch Ericht, Highland

One of the most celebrated bothies in Scotland, Ben Alder Cottage sits on
the shore of Loch Ericht at 430 m in remote Perthshire. The approach is long
in any direction — at least 18 km from the nearest road at Dalwhinnie or
Corrour railway station — which gives it a genuine wilderness character that
busier bothies lack. Capacity around 12 in the sleeping rooms and main space.
Ben Alder (1148 m) above is a classic Munro with good walking terrain. The
bothy and its position in one of the most remote glens in the Highlands have
made it a reference point in Scottish outdoor culture.

## 2. Camban Bothy, 310 m — Glen Affric, Highland

Deep in Glen Affric — one of Scotland's finest and most ancient native
pinewoods — the Camban Bothy at 310 m is on the main walking route through
the glen toward Kintail and the Five Sisters. A stone building maintained by
the MBA; sleeping for approximately 8–10 people. The approach from Loch Beinn
a' Mheadhoin car park is 13 km one way. The surrounding Caledonian pinewood
remnants are among the most significant in Scotland.

## 3. Suileag Bothy, 80 m — Assynt, Sutherland

In one of the wildest landscapes in Scotland, Suileag sits in open moorland
below the extraordinary peaks of Assynt — Suilven (731 m), Canisp (846 m),
Quinag — at approximately 80 m elevation. The mountains of Assynt rise
dramatically from the Lewisian gneiss moorland, which is among the oldest
exposed rock on Earth. The approach from the Inchnadamph hotel is approximately
6 km. A simple stone building with basic sleeping accommodation; wood-burning
stove.

## 4. Strathchailleach Bothy, 30 m — Cape Wrath area, Sutherland

Near the Cape Wrath peninsula — the most northwesterly point of mainland
Britain — Strathchailleach is one of the most remote bothies in Scotland. The
approach from the ferry across the Kyle of Durness and then on foot to the
bothy is 12 km on the Cape Wrath Trail. Basic shelter in a dramatic coastal
moorland landscape.

## 5. Corrour Bothy, 590 m — Cairngorms, Aberdeen

In the Lairig Ghru — the great mountain pass cutting through the heart of
the Cairngorms between Braemar and Aviemore — the Corrour Bothy at approximately
590 m is the standard overnight stop for through-walkers on the pass. One
of the most visited bothies in Scotland due to the Lairig Ghru's popularity.
The pass rises to 835 m; the surrounding terrain includes some of the finest
arctic-alpine plateau in Britain.

## 6. Slugain Lodge, 520 m — Cairngorms, Deeside

In the upper Quoich valley on the Deeside approach to the high Cairngorms,
Slugain Lodge (sometimes spelled Slugan) is a former gamekeeper's lodge now
maintained as a bothy. At 520 m in the upper forest zone below the open
plateau terrain, it provides shelter for Cairngorm plateau access from the
Braemar side.

## 7. Faindouran Lodge, 450 m — Cairngorms, Speyside

On the remote northern slopes of the Cairngorms in the Dubh Gleann above
Cock Bridge, Faindouran Lodge provides access to the northern faces of Beinn
a' Bhuird (1197 m) and Beinn Avon (1171 m). This is serious winter walking
terrain; the bothy is an important shelter for parties caught out on the plateau.

## 8. Charles Inglis Clark Memorial Hut (CIC Hut), 680 m — Ben Nevis, Highland

Owned and maintained by the Scottish Mountaineering Club rather than the MBA,
the Charles Inglis Clark Memorial Hut at 680 m below the north face of Ben
Nevis (1345 m — the highest peak in Britain) serves as the primary base camp
for climbing the Ben Nevis north face — one of the great winter climbing
destinations in Europe. The CIC Hut has capacity for approximately 24 people
and is booked through the SMC. Not a true "bothy" but a club hut of similar
character and tradition.

## 9. Blackrock Cottage, 290 m — Rannoch Moor, Highland

At the edge of Rannoch Moor below Buachaille Etive Mòr, Blackrock Cottage
is a mountaineering club property maintained by the Ladies Scottish Climbing
Club. Known to every climber who has visited Glencoe — it sits in one of the
most photographed positions in the Scottish Highlands with the Buachaille
pyramid behind it.

## 10. Lagangarbh Hut, 260 m — Glencoe, Highland

Also in Glencoe below the Buachaille Etive Mòr, the Lagangarbh Hut is
maintained by the Scottish Mountaineering Club and used by climbing parties
approaching the Curved Ridge, Rannoch Wall, and the Glencoe winter routes.
Club-managed; non-members can sometimes book through SMC.

## The Mountain Bothies Association

The Mountain Bothies Association (MBA) was founded in 1965 to maintain
open shelters in the Scottish, Welsh, and English uplands. The MBA currently
maintains approximately 100 bothies, predominantly in Scotland. Membership
(approximately £30 per year) is open to anyone and funds the maintenance
work; the bothies themselves are free for all to use.

The MBA's model is distinctive: the bothies are not owned by the MBA but
are maintained under agreements with landowners (estates, the Forestry
Commission, National Trust for Scotland). The MBA provides the volunteer
labour for maintenance; the landowner provides permission. The relationship
has generally been productive; several landowners provide material support
for maintenance work.

The bothy code — leave the bothy as you found it, take your rubbish out,
don't stay longer than necessary, give way to others — is enforced by the
community rather than rules. The self-policing has worked well for 60 years.
Occasional overcrowding in popular bothies and rare incidents of misuse do
not undermine the broader principle.

## Scottish mountain access rights

The Land Reform (Scotland) Act 2003 established a legal right of access
to most land in Scotland for outdoor recreation, provided the access rights
are exercised responsibly. This is the legal framework behind bothy access:
the right to walk on and camp on open land (for up to two or three consecutive
nights in any one location) is a legal right, not a privilege. The Scottish
Outdoor Access Code (outdooraccess-scotland.scot) defines the responsibilities
that come with the right.

England and Wales have a different tradition: access rights are defined by
the Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000 (open access land), which is more
restricted than the Scottish model. The Lake District and Peak District
national parks have more extensive access areas than most of England, but
the general right to camp does not exist outside Scotland.
