The Annapurna Circuit is a 160–230 km loop around the Annapurna massif in
central Nepal, crossing the Thorong La pass (5416 m) — one of the highest
trekking passes in the world regularly crossed by non-technical walkers.
The route passes through terrain ranging from subtropical river gorges to
high-altitude glacial desert, and the teahouse infrastructure is the most
complete of any Himalayan trekking route after the Everest corridor. The
Annapurna Conservation Area Project (ACAP) fee and the TIMS card are required
for all trekkers.

## 1. Manang, 3500 m — Marsyangdi valley

The main acclimatisation town on the Annapurna Circuit, Manang at 3500 m sits
in a wide glacial valley below the south face of Annapurna III and Gangapurna.
The Annapurna Lodge and other guesthouses in Manang accommodate the standard
2-night acclimatisation stop — essential before attempting the Thorong La.
An acclimatisation walk to the Gangapurna Lake (3540 m) or the Ice Lake (4600 m)
is recommended during the rest day. The Himalayan Rescue Association office
in Manang provides AMS consultations.

## 2. Thorong Phedi, 4450 m — base of Thorong La

The last teahouses before the Thorong La pass, at 4450 m in the narrow valley
below the pass face. Thorong High Camp Hotel and two or three other establishments
provide basic accommodation for parties that have walked up from Manang or
Yak Kharka on the day before the pass crossing. The altitude here produces
AMS symptoms in most guests; sleeping is typically poor. Departures for the
pass begin at 04:00–05:00 to cross before afternoon winds build.

## 3. Thorong High Camp, 4925 m — Thorong La approach

At 4925 m on the face of the Thorong La, Thorong High Camp Hotel offers
basic accommodation (dormitory sleeping, simple meals) at the highest staffed
teahouse on the circuit. Parties who sleep here shorten the summit-day
approach significantly but also spend an additional night at very high altitude.
Most guides recommend Thorong Phedi as the better sleeping option; High Camp
as a breakfast stop on the crossing day.

## 4. Muktinath, 3800 m — Mustang district

The sacred town of Muktinath (3800 m), reached after the Thorong La descent,
is the most significant Hindu and Buddhist pilgrimage site on the Annapurna
Circuit. Teahouses here are plentiful and of reasonable standard — it is a
genuine village, not just a trekking waypoint. The descent from the Thorong La
(a 1600 m drop in a single morning) makes Muktinath feel like civilisation
after the pass crossing.

## 5. Tilicho Base Camp Lodge, 4150 m — Tilicho Lake side route

On the side route to Tilicho Lake (4919 m — one of the highest lakes in the
world at a trekking elevation), a lodge at 4150 m provides the overnight stop
before the lake ascent. The Tilicho Base Camp route branches from the main
circuit near Manang and adds 2 days to the journey. The Tilicho Lake viewpoint
— the lake frozen for most of the year, surrounded by the Tilicho Peak north
wall — is one of the most spectacular in Nepal trekking.

## 6. Chame, 2710 m — lower Marsyangdi valley

On the lower, ascending section of the circuit, Chame (2710 m) is a well-
equipped town with multiple teahouses, a hospital, and good connectivity.
The surrounding terrain transitions from subtropical vegetation to the dramatic
Paungda Danda rock face and the Annapurna II north face. Used as a first
acclimatisation stop for parties coming from Besisahar.

## 7. Upper Pisang, 3300 m — Marsyangdi valley, upper section

On the alternative high route above Lower Pisang, the village of Upper Pisang
(3300 m) provides teahouses and a superlative position below the Pisang Peak
(6091 m) — a trekking peak accessible with basic mountaineering training. The
Annapurna II-IV massif fills the southern skyline.

## 8. Ngawal, 3660 m — Annapurna Circuit high route

On the high route variant between Upper Pisang and Manang, Ngawal (3660 m)
is a traditional Tibetan-style village with a small gompa and teahouse
accommodation. The high route here traverses the north side of the valley
with excellent views of Annapurna III, Gangapurna, and the upper Marsyangdi.

## 9. Annapurna Base Camp (ABC), 4130 m — Annapurna Sanctuary

On the separate Annapurna Sanctuary route (the southern approach via the
Modi Khola valley from Pokhara), the Annapurna Base Camp at 4130 m is the
centrepiece. The Modi Khola Lodge and several other teahouses at ABC sit in
the glacial sanctuary enclosed by the Annapurna massif on three sides — one
of the most dramatic enclosed high-altitude settings in Nepal. The approach
from Jhinu Danda takes 3–4 days.

## 10. Machhapuchhre Base Camp (MBC), 3700 m — Annapurna Sanctuary

Below ABC on the Annapurna Sanctuary route, MBC at 3700 m is the last
acclimatisation stop before the sanctuary cirque. The Fishtail Mountain
(Machhapuchhre, 6993 m) rises directly above in a perfect pyramid. The
Annapurna Lodge at MBC provides the most famous teahouse setting in southern
Nepal.

## The Annapurna Conservation Area

The Annapurna region is protected under the Annapurna Conservation Area
Project (ACAP), established in 1986 and managed by the National Trust for
Nature Conservation (NTNC). ACAP encompasses 7,629 km² and is one of the
largest protected areas in Nepal. The ACAP entry permit (approximately NPR
3,000 — USD 22 in 2025) is required for all trekkers entering the conservation
area and is in addition to the TIMS (Trekkers' Information Management System)
card.

The ACAP model — conservation linked to tourism revenue and community
development — is widely cited as a success. The teahouse income supports
community development programmes, trail maintenance, and conservation
enforcement. The no-deforestation rules within ACAP have preserved the
forest cover on the lower valley slopes; the requirement that teahouses use
kerosene or gas rather than firewood for cooking protects the forests above
the treeline.

## The Thorong La crossing

The Thorong La pass (5416 m) is the key challenge of the Annapurna Circuit.
The standard direction is anticlockwise (Manang side to Muktinath side),
taking advantage of the altitude acclimatisation at Manang and the fact that
the Manang-side ascent (1400 m) is more gradual than the Muktinath descent.
The pass is typically crossed in 4–6 hours from Thorong High Camp; departure
at 04:00–05:00 is standard to reach the pass before the afternoon winds.

In November, November–December, and March–April (outside the main seasons),
the pass may carry snow or ice requiring crampons. In January and February,
the pass is frequently closed by snowfall; trekkers attempting a winter circuit
must be prepared to wait in Manang for conditions to improve or to abandon
the crossing and retrace to Besisahar.

The Jeep road now follows the lower valley sections of both the clockwise
and anticlockwise circuit routes, significantly reducing the traditional
walking sections. Trekkers who want the full walking experience must
deliberately bypass the road sections on upper trails; the traditional
Annapurna Circuit as walked in the 1990s no longer exists in its original
form due to road construction. The high sections above Manang and the
Thorong La crossing remain road-free and are the reason the circuit retains
its significance.
