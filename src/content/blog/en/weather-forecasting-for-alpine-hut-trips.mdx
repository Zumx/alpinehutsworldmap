Alpine weather is not simply mountain weather scaled up. It operates on time scales of hours, on spatial scales of kilometres, and with mechanisms — foehn, orographic lift, valley convergence lines — that flat-country forecasters rarely model with accuracy. Knowing which forecast service to trust, how to interpret the data it provides, and how to cross-reference it against what you see from the hut window is a skill that separates confident mountain travellers from anxious ones.

## The Major Forecast Services

Three national meteorological agencies cover the primary Alpine range and are the gold standard for anyone planning a hut trip.

MeteoSwiss, the Swiss Federal Office of Meteorology and Climatology, produces forecasts at a spatial resolution of 1 km in its ICON-CH1 model, updated every three hours. Its free app provides precipitation probability, wind speed and direction at altitude, cloud layer altitude, and a "mountain weather" summary rated on a 1-5 scale. The summaries are written by trained forecasters who review model output and issue corrections, particularly for orographic effects that automatic models miss. MeteoSwiss is widely regarded as the most accurate day-of-forecast service for the Swiss and Austrian Alps.

ZAMG, the Zentralanstalt für Meteorologie und Geodynamik (Austrian Central Institute for Meteorology and Geodynamics), operates its own ensemble forecasting and provides dedicated mountain-area forecasts for Austrian regions. Its Warnungen (warning) system flags thunderstorm risk, heavy precipitation, and wind events with a regional precision that generic European models cannot match. The Bergwetter section of the ZAMG website, available in German and increasingly in English, produces 3-day alpine forecasts for massif-level areas rather than individual summits.

Meteomont, the Italian national avalanche and mountain-weather service operated by the Carabinieri Forestali, focuses heavily on snowpack and precipitation forecasting for the Italian Alps and Apennines. For winter and shoulder-season trips in South Tyrol, the Dolomites, and the Valle d'Aosta, Meteomont bulletins provide snowfall forecasting alongside avalanche risk ratings.

## Understanding CAPE Values

CAPE stands for Convective Available Potential Energy, measured in joules per kilogram (J/kg). It is the single most useful figure for assessing thunderstorm risk in summer alpine trips and is included, usually buried in the technical data section, in most professional forecast products.

CAPE measures the energy available in the atmosphere to drive upward convection. Values below 500 J/kg indicate weak convective potential — thunderstorms are possible but unlikely to be severe. Values between 500 and 1,500 J/kg mean moderate instability: afternoon thunderstorms are likely if moisture is present. Values above 1,500 J/kg indicate high instability; severe convection with hail, lightning, and rapid-onset heavy rain is likely. Values above 3,000 J/kg, which occasionally occur over the southern Alps in July and August, can produce supercell convection with extreme precipitation rates.

In practical terms: when the morning MeteoSwiss forecast shows CAPE above 800 J/kg for your region and afternoon cloud development is already visible by 09:00, plan to reach your destination hut or a protected location before 13:00. The typical storm cycle in the Central Alps runs from cloud build-up at 11:00 to lightning onset at 13:30 to peak activity at 14:30-16:00 and dissipation by 18:00, but this is a median pattern, not a rule.

## Foehn — The Weather Event Most Often Misread

The foehn is a warm, dry downslope wind that flows from the south across the Alpine ridge into northern valleys. On foehn days, the southern Alps are under cloud and rain while the northern side basks in warm clarity — a peculiar reversal that catches visitors by surprise. Foehn days often produce excellent visibility and warm temperatures but carry distinct risks: dry forests make fire risk extreme, and the pressure gradient creates powerful winds that make ridge-level travel difficult.

More dangerously, foehn periods end. The termination of foehn — when the barrier jet from the south weakens and cold air from the north pushes back over the ridge — produces rapid temperature drops and violent precipitation within one to three hours. Experienced guides cancel high routes whenever a foehn break is forecast for the afternoon; the transition is rapid and on exposed ridges it is genuinely dangerous.

ZAMG produces foehn forecasts separately from standard mountain weather bulletins. MeteoSwiss flags foehn in its northern Swiss valley forecasts. The tell-tale signs visible without a forecast: a stationary lenticular cloud cap on the main ridge, warm southerly gusts, exceptional clarity in northward views, and a smell of warm pine resin in the air.

## Reading the Cloud: What the Hut Keeper Sees

Every competent hut keeper in the Alps is an empirical meteorologist. They observe the same terrain every day for weeks or seasons at a stretch, and they have seen every variant of the local weather cycle. This is invaluable information that no model captures.

Questions worth asking any hut keeper: what time does it typically build today, given the current pressure tendency? Has there been unusual snowfall up high recently? Is the Föhn breaking today? Is the morning blue-sky likely to last? Most keepers answer these questions directly; they know their reputation rides on the advice they give.

The standard hut keeper's evening weather assessment includes: pressure reading (almost every hut has a barometer, and a falling pressure below 1,013 hPa in evening signals deterioration overnight), cloud type (cirrostratus forming a halo around the moon means frontal rain within 24 hours), and wind direction (a shift from southwest to northwest typically marks the passage of a cold front in the Central Alps).

## Combining Sources: The Practical Workflow

The best approach on the morning of a hut traverse is threefold. Check the national agency forecast first — ZAMG or MeteoSwiss — for CAPE and precipitation probability. Then check a general model such as Windy.com, which visualises the ECMWF and GFS models side by side and shows wind-barb overlays at selectable altitude levels (700 hPa is roughly 3,000 m). Finally, observe the sky at 06:00 from the hut: is the horizon clear to the west? Are there any lenticular formations on the main summits? Is the wind calm or already moving?

If all three sources agree, trust the consensus. If they disagree, be conservative — take the more cautious forecast as your planning basis and carry a margin of time that allows early shelter.

## Find Your Huts and Plan Your Weather Window

Weather windows in the Alps are real and plannable. A four-day high-pressure block in late June or early September can provide conditions for a complete traverse of the Silvretta, the Stubai, or the Vanoise without any technical compromise. The combination of good information and careful planning is what makes the difference between a trip that runs to plan and one that does not.
