The King Ludwig Way (Königsweg) is a 120-kilometre walking route through the
Bavarian Prealps from Starnberger See to Füssen, following the landscapes
associated with King Ludwig II of Bavaria — the eccentric monarch who built
Schloss Neuschwanstein, Schloss Linderhof, and the Wieskirche, and whose
personal history is inseparable from the Allgäu-Bavarian landscape through
which the route passes. The walking itself ranges from easy lakeside paths to
T3-grade terrain in the Ammergauer Alps; the huts and guesthouses along the
way combine authentic Bavarian hospitality with proximity to the region's
extraordinary cultural heritage.

## 1. Wieskirche Gasthaus, 880 m — Wies, Steingaden

The village of Wies — which exists primarily as the setting for the Pilgrimage
Church of Wies (Wieskirche), a UNESCO World Heritage rococo masterpiece built
1745–1754 — has a small gasthaus serving the King Ludwig Way. The church is
one of the finest baroque interiors in Europe; the gasthaus provides simple
Bavarian meals and, in some seasons, overnight accommodation for pilgrims and
walkers. The King Ludwig Way passes directly in front of the church.

## 2. Schloss Neuschwanstein area, 950 m — Schwangau, Allgäu

The village of Hohenschwangau below Schloss Neuschwanstein — the 19th-century
fairytale castle that was Ludwig II's most ambitious architectural project and
the inspiration for the Disney castle — has full tourist infrastructure including
hotels, gasthauses, and a youth hostel. Many King Ludwig Way walkers spend two
nights here: one for the castle visit (timed entry, booked in advance) and one
as a base for the surrounding Ammergauer Alps walks.

## 3. Teufelstättkopf Hütte, 1691 m — Ammergauer Alps

The highest sustained terrain of the King Ludwig Way traverses the Ammergauer
Alps above the Graswangtal valley. The Teufelstättkopf Hütte at 1691 m
provides the standard alpine overnight on this section — a DAV hut with
capacity around 60, full half-board service. The Teufelstättkopf summit
(1762 m) is accessible in 30 minutes. The views across the Ammergau and toward
the Zugspitze to the east are distinctive on a clear day.

## 4. Füssener Jochhaus, 1453 m — Füssen area

Near the final stages of the King Ludwig Way above Füssen, the Jochhaus at
1453 m provides an alpine overnight before the descent to the route's end.
Füssen (808 m) itself is the natural conclusion of the walk and has a well-
developed guesthouse and hotel network.

## 5. Peißenberger Alm, 910 m — Hoher Peißenberg area

On the northern section of the route near the Hoher Peißenberg (988 m —
one of Bavaria's most prominent isolated summits with a meteorological
observatory that has recorded weather data since 1781), an alm guesthouse
provides traditional Bavarian refreshment and overnight accommodation.

## 6. Benediktbeuern Kloster Guesthouse, 680 m — Benediktbeuern

The Benedictine monastery of Benediktbeuern (founded 739) on the King Ludwig
Way offers pilgrim and walker overnight accommodation in the monastery
guesthouse. The monastery church is one of the finest Baroque buildings
in Bavaria.

## 7. Schaffhauser Alm, 1050 m — Graswangtal

An alm guesthouse in the Graswangtal near Linderhof (where Ludwig II's other
famous palace, Schloss Linderhof, stands surrounded by French-style formal
gardens) provides overnight accommodation with easy day-walk access to the
palace grounds.

## 8. Ammergauer Berghaus, 1540 m — Notkarspitze area

A private mountain guesthouse at approximately 1540 m in the Ammergauer Alps
above Oberammergau — the village famous for its Passion Play (performed every
ten years since 1634) — provides a higher-elevation overnight in the heart
of the route's mountain section.

## 9. Partnachklamm Approach Shelter, 800 m — Garmisch area

Near the Partnach Gorge above Garmisch-Partenkirchen, a shelter facility at
approximately 800 m serves visitors to the Partnachklamm — one of Bavaria's
most spectacular gorges, carved through 700 m of limestone. The King Ludwig
Way passes near here on its approach from the north.

## 10. Starnberger See Lakeside Guesthouses, 590 m — Starnberg

At the starting point of the King Ludwig Way on the western shore of Starnberger
See — the lake where Ludwig II drowned in unexplained circumstances in 1886 —
the lakeside town of Starnberg provides full accommodation services. The cross
in the lake marks the site where his body was found; the surrounding shore
is a weekend retreat for Munich residents 30 km to the north.

## Ludwig II and the landscape

The King Ludwig Way was conceived as a route that connected the sites of
Ludwig II's passionate attachment to the landscapes of his kingdom. Ludwig
(1845–1886) was not a practical man in the political sense — his lavish castle-
building projects nearly bankrupted Bavaria and led to his forced deposition —
but as an aesthetic response to the Bavarian alpine landscape, his buildings
are extraordinary. Schloss Neuschwanstein, begun in 1869, was modelled on
medieval German castles that Ludwig had never seen; it was designed by a
stage-set designer rather than an architect, and the result is a theatrical
confection that is simultaneously ridiculous and beautiful. It receives 1.5
million visitors per year.

The King Ludwig Way offers the chance to engage with the landscape that
inspired all of this — the lake shores, the forested foothills, the sudden
appearance of the Alpine summits above the treeline — at walking pace rather
than from a tour bus window. The route's 120 km take approximately 8–10 days
at a moderate pace; most walkers take guesthouses and gasthöfe rather than
strictly alpine huts for the valley sections, using the mountain huts only
for the Ammergauer Alps days.

## The Ammergauer Alps detour

For walkers with additional days, the Ammergauer Alps above Oberammergau —
the range that forms the landscape backdrop of the King Ludwig Way's mountain
section — offer excellent walking terrain largely ignored by international
visitors who come for Neuschwanstein and the castle culture. The Laberberg
(1684 m) and Notkarspitze (1889 m) above Oberammergau provide summit views
that extend across the Zugspitze massif to the east, the Karwendel to the
north, and the main Alpine chain to the south. The approach from Oberammergau
takes 3–4 hours; the Ammergauer Berghaus near the Notkarspitze provides the
overnight accommodation.

## Practical notes for the King Ludwig Way

The King Ludwig Way is a waymarked route, signed with a blue fleur-de-lys
symbol throughout. It is divided into seven official stages, each between
12 and 20 kilometres, and is designed to be completed in 8–10 days. The
terrain ranges from T1 lakeside paths to T3 mountain sections in the
Ammergauer Alps; walking poles are useful but not mandatory for most walkers.

The route is entirely within Bavaria; public transport connections are
strong at both the Starnberg start (S-Bahn from Munich, 35 minutes) and
the Füssen end (train to Munich Hauptbahnhof, 2 hours). This makes the
King Ludwig Way one of the most accessible multi-day mountain routes in
Central Europe for walkers based in Munich.

Accommodation bookings are advisable in July and August, particularly near
Neuschwanstein: the villages of Schwangau and Hohenschwangau are heavily
visited and beds fill quickly during the castle high season. The mountain
section nights — Teufelstättkopf Hütte and the Ammergau area huts — are
more easily booked but should still be reserved at least a week in advance
for summer weekends.

The King Ludwig Way is not a route that requires mountaineering equipment
or specialist experience. The mountain sections are graded T3 at the most
demanding, and even these — the ridge crossing above Oberammergau — are
well-maintained paths with clear waymarking. The route is suitable for
fit walkers without prior Alpine experience, provided they are prepared
for changeable weather and carry waterproof clothing on the exposed sections.

## Oberammergau and the Passion Play

Oberammergau, which sits in the middle of the King Ludwig Way's mountain
section, is one of the more extraordinary cultural intersections in the
Bavarian Alps. Every ten years (the next in 2030, then 2040), the village
performs the Passion Play — a theatrical tradition begun in 1634 as a
vow of gratitude for deliverance from the plague. The production runs for
five months, with performances five days a week; it attracts visitors from
across the world and requires advance booking years in advance for the
performance season.

The village in non-Passion-Play years is a quiet Bavarian market town
famous for wood-carving — a craft that has been its primary industry since
the 17th century. The painted house facades (Lüftlmalerei) are characteristic
of the village and the wider Ammergau region. Walkers on the King Ludwig
Way passing through Oberammergau in the years between plays get the village
at its most genuinely local, before the world arrives again.
