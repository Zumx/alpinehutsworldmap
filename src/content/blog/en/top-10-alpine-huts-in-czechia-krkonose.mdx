Czechia's mountains are gentle by Alpine standards — the highest peak,
Sněžka (1603 m) in the Krkonoše, would not register as significant terrain
in Switzerland. But the culture of the horská bouda (mountain hut, from the
German Baude) is deeply embedded in Czech and Silesian mountain tradition,
dating from the 17th and 18th centuries when Sudeten German settlers built
the first permanent shelters in the Riesengebirge (Giant Mountains). The
surviving boudy are a remarkable network of historic mountain buildings,
many of them centuries-old, that operate as full-service mountain guesthouses
through both summer hiking and winter skiing seasons.

## 1. Luční bouda, 1410 m — Krkonoše National Park, Hradec Králové

The highest permanently inhabited building in Czechia — and one of the highest
in Central Europe at 1410 m — Luční bouda is a substantial mountain guesthouse
below the main Krkonoše ridge. The building dates to 1607 (with many subsequent
rebuildings) and provides full hotel-standard accommodation with restaurant,
bar, and terrace views across the Riesengebirge. The nearby Luční hora peak
(1555 m) is accessible in under an hour. Cross-country skiing from the bouda
in winter across the Bílé Labe plateau is one of the finest ski-touring
experiences in Central Europe.

## 2. Labská bouda, 1310 m — Krkonoše, Elbe headwaters

Near the headwaters of the Elbe (Labe in Czech), at 1310 m on the main ridge
plateau, the Labská bouda is one of the historic landmark buildings of the
Krkonoše. The plateau landscape here is subalpine — a mix of dwarf pine
(kleč), peat bogs, and grassland above the forest — and the bouda provides
the base for ridge walking and cross-country skiing on the highest terrain.
Built in 1892; renovated multiple times. Full accommodation and restaurant.

## 3. Špindlerova bouda, 1202 m — Špindlerův Mlýn, Krkonoše

Above the main ski resort of Špindlerův Mlýn, the Špindlerova bouda is one
of the most accessible mountain overnight stops in the Krkonoše — reached in
30 minutes on foot from the resort or by ski lift in winter. The position
on the ridge provides views across both the Czech and Polish slopes of the
Krkonoše and serves as the base for the main ridge traverse toward Sněžka.
Full guesthouse facilities; popular as a one-night escape from Prague.

## 4. Bouda na Pláni (Plansenbaude), 1283 m — Krkonoše ridge

A smaller and more traditional bouda on the main Krkonoše ridge plateau, Bouda
na Pláni represents the older style of mountain accommodation in the range:
simple rooms, basic restaurant, a terrace facing the ridge. The ridge walk
between this bouda and the Luční bouda is one of the most enjoyable in the
Krkonoše, entirely above the treeline and with consistent views across the
Sudeten foothills to the north.

## 5. Vosecká bouda, 1414 m — Krkonoše, near Harrachov

Near the western end of the Krkonoše ridge, Vosecká bouda is one of the older
surviving structures in the range and provides access to the Harrachov ski
area in winter and the ridge trails in summer. The bouda has a working character
— a local rather than tourist clientele — that distinguishes it from the more
polished accommodation in the Špindlerův Mlýn area.

## 6. Wiesnerova bouda, 1310 m — Krkonoše, Pec pod Sněžkou

Below Sněžka (1603 m) on the approach from Pec pod Sněžkou, the Wiesnerova
bouda marks the midpoint of the most popular route to the highest point in
Czechia. The bouda provides refreshment and limited accommodation for summit
parties and for those completing the ridge walk from the Luční bouda
direction. The Sněžka summit cable car from Pec operates year-round.

## 7. Schronisko PTTK nad Śnieżnymi Kotłami, 1397 m — Karkonosze (Polish Krkonoše)

Just across the Polish border in the same mountain range (Karkonosze in Polish),
this PTTK schronisko above the glacial Śnieżne Kotły cirques provides a
Czech-Polish cross-border walking connection. The border area is openly walked;
the cirques are among the finest glacial landforms in the Sudeten ranges.

## 8. Chata na Lysé hoře, 1322 m — Moravian-Silesian Beskydy

In the Beskydy Mountains of Moravia and Silesia, the Chata na Lysé hoře sits
on the summit of Lysá hora (1323 m) — the highest point in the Beskydy.
Year-round operation; reached from Ostravice in approximately 3 hours. The
Beskydy Mountains are lower and more forested than the Krkonoše but have a
warmly local mountain culture.

## 9. Chata Šerák, 1351 m — Jeseníky Mountains, Olomouc region

In the Jeseníky — the second-highest range in Czechia — the Chata Šerák
sits near the Šerák summit (1351 m) at the edge of the Jeseníky plateau.
The Jeseníky range carries a Nordic feel — open plateaux, peat bogs, and
the characteristic healing springs of the Jeseník spa tradition. The traverse
from Šerák to the Keprník (1423 m) and Červenohorské sedlo is the main
multi-day route.

## 10. Chata na Pomezí, 1180 m — Jeseníky, Czech-Polish border

On the Czech-Polish border ridge of the Jeseníky, the Chata na Pomezí
(House on the Border) is one of the original tourist chalets in this range,
providing a base for the border ridge walk and cross-country skiing on the
plateau terrain.

## The bouda tradition

The term bouda (plural boudy) for a mountain hut in Czech is distinctive and
reveals the architectural tradition: a bouda is a rounded, thatched or
shingled structure that blends into the treeline and plateau landscape. The
original Krkonoše boudy were shepherd's shelters used for summer cattle
grazing; the conversion to tourist infrastructure began in the late 19th
century with the growth of German and Czech mountaineering tourism.

The Krkonoše was part of the Sudeten German cultural region until 1945; many
of the original boudy names are German (Labská bouda — Elbfallbaude, Špindlerova
bouda — Spindlermühle). The German-speaking population was expelled in 1945
and the boudy continued under Czech administration. The physical structures
and their mountain positions remained; the cultural identity shifted. Several
of the most historic boudy are protected heritage buildings.

## Krkonoše winter tourism

Krkonoše is the most popular winter sports destination in the Czech Republic.
The ski areas of Špindlerův Mlýn, Harrachov, and Pec pod Sněžkou together
form the largest ski area in the country. The high plateau receives reliable
snow from December through March; the cross-country skiing on the plateau
ridge — connecting the boudy in a winter version of the summer ridge walk — is
particularly well-developed.

The Krkonoše in winter can produce genuinely harsh conditions: the Sněžka
summit is one of the windiest places in Central Europe, with frequent storms
blowing in from the northwest. The boudy on the plateau ridge were designed
for this weather; the Luční bouda in particular has a brutal-weather
capability that serves ski tourers who are caught by deteriorating conditions
on the plateau.

The Krkonoše National Park (KRNAP) was established in 1963 and is the
oldest national park in Bohemia and Moravia. The park covers 36,300 hectares;
the plateau zone is sensitive subalpine meadow vegetation (the primary
biome of the Krkonoše above 1300 m) that suffered serious degradation from
atmospheric pollution in the 1970s–1980s and is still recovering. Walking
off-marked paths is restricted in sensitive zones; the boudy are positioned
at the edges of the protected vegetation areas.
