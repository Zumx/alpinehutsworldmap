A hut route rarely asks one thing of your feet. The same day might begin with a hot, dusty forest approach, climb through alpine meadow and scree, and finish by stepping onto a glacier at dawn with crampons strapped on. The boot that handles the ice is stiff enough to feel like a plank on the forest track, and the boot that floats up the path folds uselessly the moment you front-point on hard snow. Choosing footwear for a hut trip is the art of finding the honest compromise for the terrain you will actually meet, and getting it wrong means blisters at best and a dangerous slip at worst.

## The B and C Rating System

The most useful shorthand in the boot world is the British B and C grading, which links boot stiffness to crampon type and is understood across the trade even where other labels are used. Boots are rated B0 to B3 by sole rigidity. B0 is a flexible walking boot or trail shoe, no good with crampons. B1 is a stiffened mountain walking boot that takes a flexible C1 crampon for easy snow. B2 is a stiffer four-season boot with a heel welt, designed for C2 crampons and general mountaineering. B3 is a fully rigid technical boot with toe and heel welts for C3 step-in crampons and steep ice.

The crampons carry matching C0 to C3 grades, and the rule is simple: the crampon grade can equal or be lower than the boot grade, never higher. A C2 crampon works on a B2 or B3 boot; a C3 crampon needs a B3 boot. Memorise this and half the confusion of the boot wall disappears.

## Matching the Boot to the Hut Route

The honest question is what your route demands. A summer hut-to-hut traverse on marked paths with no glacier — much of the Tour du Mont Blanc, the Alta Via 1 in the Dolomites, the Berliner Höhenweg — wants a comfortable B1 or even a sturdy trekking boot, light and kind to the feet over long days. You will not touch a crampon, and a heavy mountaineering boot would be miserable punishment.

The moment a glacier enters the plan — reaching the Konkordiahütte, crossing to the Britanniahütte, climbing from the Rifugio Gnifetti toward the Margherita hut — you need at least a B2 boot and matching crampons, because you will walk roped on firm dawn snow and want a boot that holds an edge and a crampon that stays on. For technical alpine routes with steep ice and mixed ground, the high glacier huts of the Western Alps among them, a B3 boot becomes the right tool. Most hut-goers who venture onto glaciers settle on a versatile B2 as the one boot that does the most.

## Leather, Synthetic and the Insulation Question

Beyond stiffness comes construction. Classic full-grain leather boots are durable, water-resistant once treated and mould beautifully to the foot over years, but they are heavy and slow to dry. Modern synthetic and fabric boots are lighter and dry faster, at some cost in longevity. For three-season hut routes that touch ice, a leather or hybrid B2 is a sound all-rounder.

Insulation matters more than people expect. A single-layer summer boot is fine for dawn glacier travel in July, but the same boot at 4,000 metres in cold conditions, or on a spring ski-touring hut trip, leaves toes numb. Double boots, with a removable insulated inner, are reserved for high cold peaks and winter, and would cook your feet on a summer approach. Match the warmth to the season and the altitude, not to the most extreme trip you might ever do.

## Fit, Sizing and Breaking Them In

No rating saves a badly fitting boot. Mountaineering boots should fit snug through the midfoot and heel with the toes just clear of the front when standing flat, then slide back off the toes when you flex forward as if descending. A heel that lifts will blister; a toe that jams on the downhill will bruise nails black. Try boots on in the afternoon when feet have swollen, wear the socks you will actually use, and test them on a sloped board or the shop's ramp.

Stiff boots must be broken in. Wear them on progressively longer walks for weeks before a big hut trip — never straight from the box onto a multi-day route. The leather and the foot need to negotiate a truce, and the place to discover a hot spot is on a local hill an hour from home, not on the approach to a glacier hut with two more days to walk.

## Socks, Gaiters and Care at the Hut

The boot is a system. A single well-cushioned merino-blend mountaineering sock beats two thin layers for most people, and a dry spare pair lives in the pack. Gaiters keep scree, snow and grit out of the cuff and are worth their small weight on any glaciated route. Carry a small repair option — spare laces and a strip of tape can save a trip.

At the hut, treat your boots well. Loosen them and pull the insoles out to air overnight in the drying room, never by a hot stove that cracks leather. Most huts insist you swap into the communal hut slippers at the door, both to keep the dormitory clean and to let your boots breathe. Dry, aired boots on day three feel like a different pair, and on a long traverse that comfort is worth real planning.
