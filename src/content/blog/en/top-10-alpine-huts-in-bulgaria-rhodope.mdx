The Rhodope Mountains roll across southern Bulgaria toward the Greek border in a vast, rounded landscape of dark spruce forest, deep limestone gorges, and grassy summit meadows. Unlike the jagged granite of the Rila and Pirin to the west, the Rhodopes are older and gentler, their highest peak — Golyam Perelik — reaching 2,191 metres in a broad, forested crest rather than a sharp alpine ridge. This is the legendary homeland of Orpheus, the mythic singer of Thracian antiquity, and the mountains carry a heavy weight of folklore, ancient sanctuaries, and traditional villages. Shelter here is the hizha, the Bulgarian mountain hut, run historically by the national tourist union and now by a mix of clubs and private operators. These ten huts and bases open up the forests, caves, and ridges of the Orpheus mountains.

## The Orpheus mountains

The Rhodopes are woven through with myth. According to legend, Orpheus, the Thracian musician whose song could charm stones and beasts, was born in these mountains, and the region preserves Thracian rock sanctuaries, sacred springs, and the kind of dense, atmospheric forest that gave rise to such stories. The villages of the western Rhodopes — Shiroka Laka, Gela, Momchilovtsi — keep alive traditions of bagpipe music, distinctive architecture, and folk festivals. Walking here is as much a cultural journey as a mountain one.

## Golyam Perelik and the high crest

Golyam Perelik (2,191 metres) is the highest summit of the Rhodopes, a rounded, forested top in a restricted military zone that nonetheless anchors the high crest of the range. Around it spread the highest meadows and the headwaters of rivers that drain south toward the Aegean. The neighbouring peaks of Perelik, Persenk, and Golyama Syutka form the alpine spine of the western Rhodopes, where the spruce forest thins to grassland and the views open across the rolling sea of ridges that gives the range its character.

## Pamporovo and Snezhanka

Pamporovo, Bulgaria's southernmost ski resort, sits at around 1,650 metres beneath the peak of Snezhanka (1,926 metres), crowned by a television tower with a viewing platform that looks out over the whole western Rhodopes. In summer the slopes become walking country, with marked trails through the spruce and easy access to the surrounding ridges. The resort is a practical base for exploring the central Rhodopes, with the nearby town of Smolyan offering the largest service hub in the mountains.

## Trigrad Gorge and the Devil's Throat

The Trigrad Gorge is one of the most dramatic features of the Rhodopes, a narrow limestone canyon where vertical walls rise hundreds of metres above the river. Within it lies the Devil's Throat Cave (Dyavolsko Garlo), where the river vanishes underground into a vast chamber and a waterfall thunders into darkness; legend ties the cave to Orpheus's descent into the underworld. The village of Trigrad makes a base for walks along the gorge rim and into the surrounding karst, and the area is famous for its wallcreepers and nesting raptors.

## Yagodina and the Buynovo Gorge

Near the village of Yagodina, the Buynovo Gorge cuts the longest canyon in the Rhodopes, and the Yagodina Cave — one of the longest in Bulgaria at several kilometres of explored passages — draws visitors to its galleries of formations. The village sits high in the western Rhodopes near the Greek border and offers simple guesthouse and hut accommodation. The surrounding forests and gorges make this one of the wildest corners of the range, with bears and wolves present in the deeper valleys.

## Shiroka Laka and the folk villages

Shiroka Laka, an architectural reserve of stone-and-timber houses strung along a river valley, is the cultural heart of the western Rhodopes and a celebrated centre of bagpipe music. From the village, trails climb into the surrounding hills and connect to the network of huts on the high crest. Staying in a village guesthouse and walking the ridge trails by day is a quintessential Rhodope experience, blending the mountains with a living folk tradition found nowhere else in Bulgaria.

## Smolyan Lakes and the central huts

Above the town of Smolyan lies a cluster of small mountain lakes set among the spruce, ringed by walking trails and served by simple huts and rest stops. Smolyan itself, the highest town in Bulgaria, is the administrative and service centre of the central Rhodopes and the natural staging point for multi-day walks through the range. From here, marked routes lead to the high crest, the gorges, and the forested plateaus that stretch toward the Batak and Dospat reservoirs.

## Batak, Dospat, and the western plateaus

The western Rhodopes broaden into high forested plateaus dotted with large reservoirs — Batak, Dospat, Shiroka Polyana — that have become summer recreation hubs amid endless pine and spruce. The town of Batak carries a heavy place in Bulgarian history, and the surrounding forests offer gentle, rolling walking quite unlike the gorge country to the south. Huts and rest cabins serve walkers and cyclists exploring this quieter, greener side of the Orpheus mountains.

## The hizha and the Bulgarian walking tradition

The hizha is the backbone of Bulgarian mountain walking and carries a history distinct from the Alpine hut culture. Through the twentieth century the national tourist union built an extensive network of hizhi across the country's mountains, and they became popular gathering places, drawing families, school groups, and hiking clubs on weekends and holidays. After 1989 many fell into an uncertain state, and today standards vary widely: some hizhi in the Rhodopes are well run, with a restaurant and heated rooms, while others stand half-abandoned or open only sporadically. For this reason many Rhodope walkers combine hizhi with village guesthouses, which often offer better comfort and a window onto the living folk culture. Asking locally whether a hizha is genuinely open before setting out is wise, since opening hours are rarely reliably documented. The reward is a mountain landscape that still feels undiscovered compared with the more famous Rila and Pirin.

## Seasons, forest, and practicalities

The Rhodopes are walkable across a long season, from late spring through autumn, with the lower forest trails open even in winter when the high crest carries snow. The climate is milder and the terrain less severe than the high granite ranges to the west, making this an excellent area for forest trekking, cave visits, and cultural touring rather than serious mountaineering. Huts vary widely in standard, from well-run hizhi to basic shelters, and many walkers combine hut nights with village guesthouses. The deep forests hold genuine large wildlife, so the usual care with food and waste applies.
