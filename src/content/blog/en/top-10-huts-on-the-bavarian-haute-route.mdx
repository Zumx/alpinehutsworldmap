The Bavarian Haute Route (Bayerische Höhenweg) is a series of long-distance
hut routes crossing the limestone ranges of the Berchtesgadener and Salzburger
Alps along the German-Austrian border. The route is less defined than the Swiss
Haute Route — it is more of a tradition than a marked trail — but the sequence
of huts from the Berchtesgaden Alps across the Steinernes Meer and the Hochkönig
provides 5–7 days of outstanding walking on some of the most dramatic limestone
plateau terrain in the Alps.

## 1. Watzmannhaus, 1930 m — Berchtesgaden Alps, Bayern

The Watzmannhaus at 1930 m below the summit of the Watzmann (2713 m —
Bavaria's highest massif) is the most famous hut in the Berchtesgadener Alps.
Reached from Berchtesgaden (470 m) via the Watzmann-Ostwand base path in
3–4 hours (T3), or from the Königssee direction. The east face of the Watzmann
— one of the great Alpine rock walls — is visible from the hut terrace. DAV
Berchtesgaden section; capacity around 120. The Watzmann summit is a full-day
scrambling route from here; the east face is an elite technical objective.

## 2. Kärlingerhaus, 1638 m — Funtensee, Berchtesgaden

Above the Funtensee — a lake at 1601 m in the heart of the Berchtesgaden
National Park famous for recording Germany's lowest ever temperature (-45.9°C
in January 2001) — the Kärlingerhaus at 1638 m is the most remote hut in
the national park, accessible only on foot from any direction (minimum 4 hours
from the nearest road). NPS-administered; capacity around 100. The high plateau
above the Kärlingerhaus — the Steinernes Meer (Stone Sea) — is one of the most
extraordinary landscape features in the Bavarian Alps: a high karst plateau at
2000+ m with limestone formations and scattered pools.

## 3. Riemannhaus, 2177 m — Steinernes Meer, Salzburgerland

On the Austrian side of the Steinernes Meer at 2177 m, the Riemannhaus is
the key high-plateau overnight on the traverse. ÖAV Salzburg section; capacity
around 80. The surrounding karst plateau landscape with its solution hollows,
pavement limestone, and scattered lakes at 2000–2300 m is unlike any other
terrain in the eastern Alps. The summit of the Breithorn (2504 m) is accessible
in 2 hours from the hut.

## 4. Schiestlhaus, 1802 m — Hochschwab, Steiermark

In the Hochschwab massif of Styria — the largest elevated plateau in the
eastern Alps — the Schiestlhaus at 1802 m was comprehensively rebuilt in 2010
as a model of sustainable mountain architecture in Austria. ÖAV Vienna section;
capacity around 80. The Hochschwab (2277 m) is reached in 2 hours from the
hut; the plateau character is similar to the Steinernes Meer but with less
dramatic rock formations. The Schiestlhaus features photovoltaic generation
and heat recovery systems that make it one of the most energy-efficient huts
in the ÖAV network.

## 5. Ingolstädter Haus, 2119 m — Hochkönig, Salzburgerland

On the Hochkönig massif (2941 m) — the highest peak in the Berchtesgadener
and Salzburger limestone Alps — the Ingolstädter Haus at 2119 m is the primary
overnight for Hochkönig summit ascents. DAV Ingolstadt section; capacity around
100. The Hochkönig is reached via the Matrashaus on the summit plateau. The
northeast face carries a permanent ice cap — a rare feature in the Calcareous
Alps.

## 6. Matrashaus, 2940 m — Hochkönig summit, Salzburgerland

On the Hochkönig summit plateau at 2940 m, the Matrashaus is one of the
highest accessible huts in the Berchtesgaden Alps. The summit views across
the Salzburger land and the Dachstein massif are outstanding. Basic overnight
accommodation for summit-day staging parties.

## 7. Teufelstättkopf Hütte, 1691 m — Ammergauer Alps

On the northern approach section of the Bavarian Haute Route above Ettal
in the Ammergauer Alps, the Teufelstättkopf Hütte provides access from the
northern foothills through the limestone transition zone.

## 8. Riemann-Alm, 1770 m — Loferer Steinberge, Salzburg

On the Loferer Steinberge — a compact limestone range west of Lofer with
excellent walking terrain — the Riemann-Alm hut provides a shorter traverse
option on the western section.

## 9. Hintersee Alm, 780 m — Berchtesgaden valley

A valley-level alm guesthouse below the Berchtesgadener Alps proper, useful
as an approach or departure overnight before or after the higher huts.

## 10. Reiteralm Hütte, 1750 m — Reiteralpe, Bayern

On the Reiteralpe above Ramsau — the forested range between Berchtesgaden
and the Steinernes Meer — a small private hut provides a quieter alternative
overnight on the more forested section of the traverse.

## The Berchtesgaden National Park character

The German section of the Bavarian Haute Route passes through the Berchtesgaden
National Park (Nationalpark Berchtesgaden) — the only Alpine national park in
Germany, established in 1978 around the Königssee and the Watzmann massif.
The park covers 210 square kilometres and is managed for strict wilderness
preservation; camping outside designated areas is prohibited, and the huts
within it (including the Kärlingerhaus) operate under special permits.

The park is famous for chamois, marmots, golden eagles, and — increasingly —
the return of wolves. The limestone geology creates dramatic cliff faces, deep
gorges, and the characteristic Berchtesgaden rock scenery that forms the
backdrop to the huts.

## The Hochkönig loop

The Hochkönig massif in Salzburg deserves emphasis as a standalone extension
or sub-route for parties without the full 7-day commitment to the complete
traverse. The Hochkönig circuit — a 2–3 day loop from Mühlbach am Hochkönig
via the Ingolstädter Haus and Matrashaus — provides the finest high-altitude
experience in the Salzburger Kalkalpen in a compact package. The summit plateau
of the Hochkönig at 2941 m — carrying a permanent ice cap, with views across
the Salzburg basin to the north and the main Alpine chain to the south —
rewards the additional effort of the steep approach from the valley.

The Matrashaus (2940 m) is typically open only in the high summer season and
closes by late September; timing a Hochkönig visit for July or August ensures
the summit facilities are available.

## Practical notes

The Berchtesgaden National Park operates a stringent trail use system; some
routes require pre-registration or fee payment. The Kärlingerhaus is reachable
by boat on the Königssee (to St. Bartholomä) and then a 3-hour walk — the
boat service runs from Berchtesgaden town and provides a spectacular
approach through the glass-still lake enclosed by limestone walls.

The cross-border character of the route — German national park on the west,
Austrian Bundesland of Salzburg on the east — means different regulations
apply at different points. The Austrian sections have no national-park
camping restrictions equivalent to the Berchtesgaden NP rules; however, the
ÖAV huts on the Steinernes Meer and around the Hochkönig book out for summer
weekends well in advance. Reservations through the ÖAV online system are
essential from June onwards.

The optimal season is mid-June to mid-September. Snow can persist on the
Steinernes Meer plateau well into June; by October, the higher huts begin
closing and the early snowfalls of the Berchtesgaden Alps can make the
exposed plateau crossings serious undertakings. A good map (ÖAV 1:25,000
sheets covering the Berchtesgaden and Hochkönig areas) and downloaded GPS
tracks provide the navigation backbone for the cross-border section where
trail signage changes character.

## The Königssee approach

The classic entry to the Bavarian Haute Route from the west begins with
the Königssee boat — one of Bavaria's great introductions to landscape.
The Königssee (603 m) is an electric-boat lake: only electric vessels
have been permitted since 1909 to preserve the water quality of what is
the cleanest lake in Germany. The 35-minute ride to St. Bartholomä, with
the Watzmann east face rising vertically from the lake's western shore,
provides a sense of scale that no road approach can match.

From St. Bartholomä, the path climbs steeply through forest and limestone
terrain to the Kärlingerhaus — 3 hours and 1000 m of ascent. This is the
proper beginning of the Bavarian Haute Route: arriving at the Kärlingerhaus
in late afternoon with the Steinernes Meer stretching ahead, the boat
crossing and the Watzmann east face already behind you, is one of the
finest day's journeys available in the German Alps.
