North of Tehran the Alborz range rises in a single great wall between the capital and the Caspian Sea, and out of it lifts Mount Damavand, a near-perfect snow cone reaching 5,610 metres — the highest peak in Iran, the highest volcano in Asia, and the highest in the entire Middle East. Damavand is woven through Persian mythology, the prison of the tyrant Zahhak in the Shahnameh, and it draws thousands of climbers each summer up its non-technical but high and demanding slopes. The mountain's shelter system is run largely through the Iranian Mountaineering Federation and the network of panahgah — refuges — that dot the standard routes. Beyond Damavand, the wider Alborz holds bivouacs and refuges on peaks from Sabalan in the northwest to the karst summits closer to Tehran.

## Bargah Sevom (Camp 3)

The defining shelter on Damavand is Bargah Sevom, Camp 3, on the standard southern route at around 4,200 metres. There are two buildings here: an older stone refuge and a modern metal hut that sleeps over a hundred. This is where the vast majority of climbers spend the night before the summit push, leaving in the dark for the long, sulphur-scented final climb past the steaming fumaroles near the top. It is busy, basic, and the pivot of the whole ascent.

## Goosfand Sara (Camp 2)

Lower on the southern route at around 3,000 metres, Goosfand Sara — the name means sheepfold — is the road-head shelter and mosque where climbers gather, organise mules, and start the walk up to Camp 3. It is the practical base camp of the south side, reachable by vehicle and the staging point for the two-day ascent that the altitude demands.

## The northern route shelters

Damavand's northern face, longer and colder, carries the mountain's famous frozen waterfall and a chain of higher, sparser refuges. Climbers on this route sleep progressively higher in basic huts before a summit day that is more committing than the southern walk-up. The north is for those who want the mountain quieter and harder.

## Federation refuges and the Polur base

The Iranian Mountaineering and Sport Climbing Federation maintains the Damavand base at Polur, the village at the foot of the southern route. Registration, guides, and mule transport are arranged here, and the federation hut serves as the administrative and acclimatisation base for the mountain. Climbing Damavand without acclimatising in Polur or higher is a common and dangerous mistake.

## Janpanah Korkas and the central Alborz

Away from Damavand, the central Alborz holds peaks like Korkas and a scatter of janpanah — small emergency shelters — that serve walkers and climbers in the ranges closer to Tehran. The Janpanah Korkas is typical: a basic roofed refuge on the approach to a popular summit, the kind of unstaffed shelter that Iranian mountaineering clubs build and maintain across the range.

## Sabalan and the northwest refuges

Far to the northwest, near Ardabil, Mount Sabalan rises to 4,811 metres, a dormant volcano with a crater lake near its summit and a refuge system of its own. The northeastern shelter on Sabalan — the panahgah on that flank — stages the summit climb, and the mountain is famous for its hot springs and the nomadic Shahsavan herders who graze its lower slopes in summer.

## The Tehran wall: Tochal and the club huts

Right above Tehran, Mount Tochal at 3,964 metres is the city's mountain, served by a long gondola and a string of club-built shelters along its ridge. Walkers use these huts as staging points for the traverse toward the higher Alborz, and the proximity to a city of millions makes Tochal one of the most-walked peaks in the country.

## Caves, bivouacs, and the improvised shelters

Much Iranian high-mountain shelter is improvised: caves, bivouac sites, and herders' stone huts pressed into service by passing climbers. The lower Alborz, with its dramatic karst and gorges, holds many such spots known to local clubs, and a cave bivouac is a normal part of multi-day routes on the less-developed peaks.

## Eco-lodges and the herding settlements

In the valleys and on the lower slopes, traditional black-tent camps of the nomadic herders and a growing number of eco-lodges provide warmer lodging before and after the high climbs. These reflect the deep pastoral tradition of the Alborz, where summer grazing on the high meadows has shaped the landscape and its trails for millennia.

## National park shelters

Protected areas across the Alborz and beyond — including parks far to the south of the range — maintain ranger posts and basic visitor shelters. These mark the growing formalisation of Iranian mountain protection and offer rough lodging for walkers exploring the country's wilder high country away from the famous volcanoes.

## Damavand in myth and culture

No mountain in Iran carries the cultural weight of Damavand. In Ferdowsi's Shahnameh, the great epic of Persian kings, it is where the hero Fereydun chains the demon-king Zahhak, whose shoulders sprout serpents fed on human brains — the mountain as a prison for evil at the edge of the world. The peak appears on the back of the 10,000-rial banknote and stands as a national symbol of resistance and endurance. Climbers feel this layered meaning on the way up: the sulphur reek near the summit, the steaming vents, and the eerie silence of the crater rim all seem to belong to the myth. To climb Damavand is to walk into the oldest stories of Persia, and Iranian hikers treat the summit with a reverence that visitors quickly come to share.

## Hospitality and the practicalities

Iranian mountain hospitality is famous, and the huts of the Alborz reflect it. Even at busy Bargah Sevom, strangers share tea, dates, and advice, and the federation system makes the standard routes well organised for foreign visitors. Independent travel is entirely possible, but most climbers register at Polur, hire a local guide or at least arrange mule transport for gear to Camp 3, and join the steady stream of Tehrani weekenders heading up. Cash, modest dress, and a few words of Persian go a long way; the warmth shown to visiting walkers is one of the lasting memories of a Damavand trip. Friday and holiday weekends see the huts at their fullest, so a midweek ascent rewards those who can manage it with quieter trails and an easier bunk.

## When to go

Damavand's climbing season is short and clear: July and August are the reliable window, when the snow has firmed and the weather is most settled. June and September are possible for the experienced but bring more snow and cold. Acclimatise for at least a night at Polur or Goosfand Sara, sleep at Bargah Sevom, and start the summit day before dawn — the altitude is real, and altitude sickness turns back many unprepared parties each year. Carry sun protection against the fierce high-altitude glare and warm layers against the summit cold.
