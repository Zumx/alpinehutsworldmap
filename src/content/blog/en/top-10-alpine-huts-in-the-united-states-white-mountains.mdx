The Appalachian Mountain Club's White Mountain hut system is the oldest and largest staffed backcountry hut network in the United States. The AMC opened its first facility — the original log structure at Madison Springs — in 1888, and the network now comprises eight full-service staffed huts stretching nearly 90 kilometres across the White Mountains of New Hampshire. Each hut offers bunk beds for 36 to 90 hikers, communal meals cooked by crews of young seasonal staff called hutmasters and croo, and educational programmes focused on the alpine ecology of the Presidential Range. The altitude of the huts ranges from 732 metres at Lonesome Lake to 1,533 metres at Lakes of the Clouds, just below the summit of Mount Washington. The main staffed season runs from mid-June to mid-September; three huts operate on a self-service caretaker basis from September through May. The White Mountains receive extraordinary weather — Mount Washington recorded a world-record surface wind of 372 km/h in April 1934 — and the huts provide critical safety infrastructure for the millions of hikers who visit annually.

## 1. Lakes of the Clouds Hut, Mount Washington

Lakes of the Clouds Hut, at 1,533 metres on the southwest shoulder of Mount Washington, is the highest and largest hut in the AMC system, sleeping up to 90 guests. It sits beside two small glacial tarns — the Lakes of the Clouds — which give the hut its name, and commands panoramic views of the Southern Presidential peaks: Monroe, Eisenhower, Franklin, and Pierce. On summer weekends, the crew manages turnover of up to 180 guests across two meals. The hut also includes the Dungeon, a basement emergency shelter used by hikers caught by the notorious Mount Washington weather. The approach from the base of Mount Washington via Ammonoosuc Ravine Trail takes about three hours.

## 2. Madison Spring Hut, Madison Gulf

Madison Spring Hut at 1,457 metres in the col between Mounts Madison and Adams is the second-highest hut and one of the most demanding to reach. Approaches via the Valley Way, Air Line, or Madison Gulf Trail all involve substantial elevation gain, the shortest being around three hours from Appalachia trailhead on Route 2. The hut sleeps 50 and was reconstructed in the 1980s with modern insulation and energy systems. The Northern Presidentials around the hut — Adams (1,615 m), Madison (1,636 m), Jefferson (1,741 m) — offer some of the most exposed and wild ridge walking in the eastern United States.

## 3. Greenleaf Hut, Franconia Ridge

Greenleaf Hut at 1,073 metres on the shoulder of Mount Lafayette is the gateway to the Franconia Ridge trail, which connects Lafayette and Lincoln along one of the most celebrated above-treeline ridgewalks in New England. The hut sleeps 48 and is approached via the Greenleaf Trail from the Franconia Notch State Park, a two-hour ascent. The ridge above the hut provides a three-kilometre exposed walk with views west over Franconia Notch and east across the Pemigewasset Wilderness. The hut opened in 1930 and is named after William Greenleaf, an early AMC president.

## 4. Galehead Hut, Twin Mountain area

Galehead Hut at 1,116 metres in the Pemigewasset Wilderness is considered one of the more remote huts in the system, requiring at least three to four hours of approach from any trailhead. The hut sleeps 36 — the smallest capacity in the staffed network — and sits at the base of Galehead Mountain with access trails leading to South Twin (1,641 m) and Garfield (1,317 m). The deep-woods approach through northern hardwood forest and spruce-fir zone creates a sense of real backcountry even within the highly managed AMC network. Galehead was rebuilt following a 1929 fire and has been expanded gradually since.

## 5. Zealand Falls Hut, Zealand Notch

Zealand Falls Hut at 737 metres is among the most accessible in the system — the flat approach along the Zealand Trail from Route 302 covers 3.7 kilometres with minimal elevation gain, making it suitable for families and beginners. The hut sleeps 36 and operates year-round on a self-service basis outside the staffed June-September season. It sits beside a small waterfall on the Zealand River and provides access to the Zeacliff overlook and the high traverse toward Carrigain and Hancock. The surrounding forest is regrowth from logging operations that ended in the 1920s; mature birch and spruce now cover what was once a clearcut landscape.

## 6. Carter Notch Hut, Carter Range

Carter Notch Hut at 1,005 metres in the narrow notch between Wildcat Mountain and Carter Dome is the most architecturally distinctive facility in the system — two stone buildings originally constructed in 1914, the oldest surviving hut structures. The hut sleeps 40 and is situated between two small alpine ponds. The approach via Nineteen Mile Brook Trail from Route 16 takes about two hours. Carter Notch operates as a self-service hut from October through May with a wood stove, basic kitchen, and no staff. In summer the crew provides full meals. Wildcat Mountain's ski lift makes this one of the more unusual mountain hut settings in North America.

## 7. Lonesome Lake Hut, Franconia Notch

Lonesome Lake Hut at 732 metres is the lowest in the system and the gentlest approach: the Lonesome Lake Trail from Lafayette Place Campground ascends 300 metres in 1.6 kilometres. The hut sleeps 48 and sits on the shore of Lonesome Lake, a small pond that attracts loons in summer and provides good swimming on warm days. Despite its ease of access, the surrounding terrain offers ambitious day hikes via the ridge to the north toward Cannon Mountain and south onto Franconia Ridge. The hut opened in 1964 and was the last to join the eight-hut system.

## 8. Mizpah Spring Hut, Mount Clinton

Mizpah Spring Hut at 1,122 metres on the shoulder of Mount Clinton (also called Mount Pierce) is the newest hut in the system, opened in 1965. It sleeps 60 and is sited in the subalpine zone where the spruce-fir forest gives way to the Bigelow's sedge meadows characteristic of the Southern Presidentials. The approach via Mizpah Cutoff from the Crawford Path trailhead on Route 302 takes about 90 minutes, making this among the most accessible staffed overnight options in the system. From Mizpah, hikers reach Mount Eisenhower and Monroe easily on day walks, extending toward Lakes of the Clouds on longer traverses.

## 9. Pinkham Notch Visitor Center, Route 16

Pinkham Notch Visitor Center, at 600 metres on Route 16, is the AMC's primary base camp for the Presidential Range. It is not a backcountry hut in the traditional sense but provides hostel-style accommodation for 104 guests, a full restaurant, gear shop, weather forecasting service, and trailhead access for routes to Tuckerman Ravine, Lion Head, and Mount Washington. Operating year-round, it serves winter skiers and ice climbers as readily as summer hikers. The AMC staffs a forecasting team here that produces the Mount Washington Observatory's summit weather reports used by pilots, climbers, and skiers throughout New England.

## 10. Tuckerman Ravine Shelter, Mount Washington

Tuckerman Ravine Shelter is a US Forest Service lean-to and patrol cabin complex at the base of Tuckerman Ravine, at roughly 1,130 metres. The ravine is famous for spring skiing — skiers travel to ski its headwall in April and May when snow conditions are at their most stable — and the shelter provides warming facilities managed by US Forest Service snow rangers. Overnight use is not available in the traditional staffed-hut sense, but the facility serves tens of thousands of day visitors annually and the snow rangers provide avalanche assessment and safety rescue services. The ravine's headwall rises some 300 metres above the shelter at angles exceeding 45 degrees.
