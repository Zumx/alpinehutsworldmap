The Watzmannhaus sits at 1,930 metres on a broad shoulder called the Falzkopf, directly beneath the long summit ridge of the Watzmann, the great triple-peaked massif that towers over the town of Berchtesgaden and the deep blue Konigssee. From its terrace the panorama sweeps from the limestone walls of the Hochkalter across the valley to the Steinernes Meer plateau, and behind the hut the route to the summits climbs into a world of bare rock and exposure. This is one of the busiest and best-loved huts of the German Alpine Club, the throne room of the Berchtesgaden Alps.

## A hut beneath a legend

The Watzmann is the stuff of German alpine legend, its silhouette familiar from countless paintings and postcards. The massif culminates in the Watzmann Mittelspitze at 2,713 metres, flanked by the Hocheck and the Sudspitze, and folklore casts the surrounding lesser summits as the petrified family of a cruel king. The Watzmannhaus, opened in the late nineteenth century by the Munich section of the DAV and enlarged many times since, serves as base camp for almost everyone who wants to set foot on these peaks, whether on the classic ridge traverse or the straightforward walk to the Hocheck.

## The notorious east face

What gives the Watzmann its dark fame is the Watzmann Ostwand, the east face that plunges some 1,800 metres almost straight down to the Konigssee, the largest continuous rock face in the Eastern Alps. It is a serious, committing climb with a long history of accidents, and it is not reached from the Watzmannhaus but from the lakeshore below, often after a boat ride across the Konigssee. The hut, however, looks out toward the world the face belongs to, and many who stay there are content to admire the Watzmann's might from the safe side of the ridge.

## The walk up from Berchtesgaden

The standard approach climbs from the Wimbachbrucke trailhead near Ramsau, or from the Hammerstiel above Schonau, through forest and then open alpine meadow to the Mitterkaseralm before the final pull to the hut, a climb of well over a thousand metres that most fit walkers manage in three to four hours. The path is well marked and popular, and on a fine summer weekend a steady file of hikers makes the ascent. Reaching the terrace, dropping the pack and ordering a Kaiserschmarrn while the afternoon light moves across the Steinernes Meer is one of the small rituals of Bavarian mountain life.

## To the summits and the ridge

From the Watzmannhaus the path continues to the Hocheck at 2,651 metres, where a small emergency shelter stands, and on to the Mittelspitze, the true summit. Beyond it lies the Watzmann ridge traverse to the Sudspitze, an exposed, cabled scramble that ranks among the most coveted ridge routes in the German Alps and demands a head for heights, sure footing and settled weather. Many walkers turn around at the Hocheck, satisfied with the view; those continuing along the ridge commit to a long, serious day that ends with a steep descent toward the Wimbachgries far below.

## Part of the Berchtesgaden hut network

The Watzmannhaus does not stand alone. The wider Berchtesgaden Alps hold a network of refuges that walkers link into multi-day journeys: the Karlinger Haus by the Funtensee, deep in the karst wilderness of the Steinernes Meer, the Kaltenbrunner Hutte and the Brunnenkopfhutte over toward the Ammergau, and the Tegelberghaus and Berggasthaus Herzogstand in the ranges to the west. The Watzmannhaus is the grandest and most visited of them, a fitting anchor for a region that became Germany's only alpine national park in 1978 and shelters golden eagles, ibex and chamois on its high slopes.

## Practicalities and the alpine club

Run by the DAV, the Watzmannhaus is one of the larger huts in the Bavarian Alps, with several hundred beds split between dormitories and rooms, a big self-service hall and a kitchen turning out classic Bavarian fare. It is staffed from late spring into autumn, with the exact dates depending on snow, and like all DAV huts it offers members of the alpine-club reciprocal network a reduced overnight rate. Because it is so popular, especially at weekends and in the high summer, booking ahead is strongly advised, and the warden will brief you honestly on conditions if you plan to head for the ridge.

## Why it endures

The Watzmannhaus endures because it combines easy access, a magnificent setting and a direct line to one of the most storied summits in the German-speaking Alps. Families come for the rewarding day walk, hikers for the night beneath the stars and the dawn light on the east face, and mountaineers for the serious traverse above. The hut manages to be both a friendly gateway and a launch pad for genuine adventure, and the sight of the Watzmann catching the first sun while the Konigssee still lies in shadow is reason enough to climb up and stay.
