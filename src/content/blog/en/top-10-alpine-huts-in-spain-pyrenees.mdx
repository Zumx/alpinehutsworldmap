The Spanish Pyrenees stretch for roughly 450 kilometres from the Atlantic coast of Navarra to Cap de Creus on the Mediterranean. The highest terrain lies in the central section, in the province of Aragon, where the Monte Perdido massif (3,355 metres), the Maladeta group with the Aneto summit (3,404 metres, the highest peak in the Pyrenees), and the Vignemale (3,298 metres) form a chain of glaciated summits approached by some of the finest mountain trails in southern Europe. The rifugio tradition here draws from both the French and Spanish alpine club systems, and many huts are operated by the FEEC (Federació d'Entitats Excursionistes de Catalunya) or the FAM (Federación Aragonesa de Montañismo). The season in the high Pyrenees runs from late June through September, with some staffed huts opening as early as April for ski touring.

## 1. Refugio de Góriz, Monte Perdido

The Refugio de Góriz at 2,200 metres above the Ordesa canyon is the central hut in the Monte Perdido National Park and one of the most visited refugios in the Spanish Pyrenees, sleeping around 80 guests. It sits at the head of the Ordesa valley, a UNESCO World Heritage site since 1997, and provides the standard overnight base for the ascent of Monte Perdido (3,355 metres) and for the challenging Brecha de Rolando crossing into France. The approach from Pradera de Ordesa takes around three hours. Reservations in summer are essential and should be made weeks in advance.

## 2. Refugio de Estós, Benasque Valley

At 1,890 metres in the upper Estós valley above Benasque in the Maladeta area, the Refugio de Estós is a large, well-equipped hut sleeping around 100 guests and serving as the main approach station for routes toward the Aneto glacier and the Maladeta massif. The valley is broad and wooded in its lower sections, opening into a wide glacially carved basin around the hut. The Estós is a particularly comfortable facility by Pyrenean standards, with separate rooms for smaller groups and a full restaurant service.

## 3. Refugio de Respomuso, Tena Valley

The Refugio de Respomuso at 2,200 metres in the Tena valley above Sallent de Gállego in Aragon sits beside one of the most beautiful high lakes in the Pyrenees, a deep blue reservoir enclosed by smooth granite ridges. The approach from the Formigal ski resort area takes about three hours. The hut sleeps around 75 and provides access to peaks on the watershed ridge above the lake including the Pico de Piedrafita and routes toward the Respomuso and Baciver summits.

## 4. Refugio Cap de Llauset, Ribagorça

At 2,420 metres in the high terrain between the Benasque and Ribagorça valleys in eastern Aragon, the Refugio Cap de Llauset provides access to the little-known Llauset area, a series of glacier-carved lakes and smooth granite ridges that are considerably less busy than the Aneto or Ordesa areas. The hut sleeps around 50 and is operated by the FAM. Its relative obscurity is partly a function of access — the approach from the Llauset reservoir requires a long drive on a mountain road followed by a three-hour walk.

## 5. Refugio de la Renclusa, Maladeta

The Refugio de la Renclusa at 2,140 metres at the foot of the Maladeta massif above Benasque is the primary overnight base for ascents of the Aneto (3,404 metres) — the highest summit in the Pyrenees. On busy summer weekends, parties departing the Renclusa before dawn for the Aneto summit can number in the hundreds. The standard route crosses the Maladeta glacier, which has retreated dramatically since the 1990s and now requires crossing crevassed terrain even in summer. The hut sleeps around 80 and is one of the most booked-out in the Spanish Pyrenees.

## 6. Refugio Ángel Orús, Posets massif

At 2,100 metres in the Barranco de Estós above Benasque, the Refugio Ángel Orús serves the Posets massif — the second highest peak in the Pyrenees at 3,375 metres — and the traverse connecting it to the Maladeta group. Named after a pioneer of Aragonese alpinism in the early twentieth century, the hut sleeps around 65 and is a well-managed facility with a good kitchen. The Posets receives far fewer visitors than the Aneto and the summit itself is a genuinely quieter experience.

## 7. Refugio Pombie, French-Spanish border

The Refugio Pombie at 2,032 metres on the Spanish side of the Col de la Fache above the Ossau valley serves the classic circuit of the Pic du Midi d'Ossau (2,884 metres), the iconic pyramidal basalt tower that dominates the western high Pyrenees. The circuit — roughly 18 kilometres with around 1,200 metres of ascent — passes several other huts and can be done in a long day or spread over two. The Pombie sleeps around 80 and is one of the most atmospheric mountain huts in this section of the range.

## 8. Refugio de Meranges, Cerdanya

At approximately 2,050 metres above the village of Meranges in the Cerdanya area of Catalonia, this small refugio provides access to the Serra del Cadí's eastern approaches and the gentle terrain bordering the Val de Núria ski area. The Cerdanya is a broad, flat-floored valley at around 1,200 metres — an anomaly in Pyrenean geography caused by the collapse of an ancient fault basin — and the surrounding hillsides rise gradually to peaks that rarely exceed 2,900 metres, making the terrain here more suitable for multi-day walking than technical alpinism.

## 9. Refugio Amitges, Aigüestortes

The Refugio Amitges at 2,380 metres in the Aigüestortes i Estany de Sant Maurici National Park above Espot in Lleida is one of the most beautifully positioned huts in the Spanish Pyrenees. The hut sits beside the two Amitges lakes, enclosed by the Agulles d'Amitges — a dramatic fin of granite. The park has strict access rules for private vehicles, so the approach involves a 4WD taxi service from Espot followed by a two-hour walk. The hut sleeps around 50 and is operated by the FEEC.

## 10. Refugio Colomers, Aigüestortes

The Refugio Colomers at 2,135 metres is the largest and most central hut in the Aigüestortes National Park, positioned among a cluster of lakes in the Val de Boí sector near Caldes de Boí. It sleeps around 120 guests and serves as the hub for routes across the western sector of the park, including the GR-11 long-distance path that traverses the entire Spanish Pyrenees from coast to coast. The surrounding granite terrain with its dozen small lakes is one of the signature landscapes of the Pyrenees and draws large numbers of visitors in July and August.

## Planning Your Pyrenean Circuit

The Spanish Pyrenees offer one of Europe's finest multi-day walking and mountaineering experiences, with a refugio network that covers the major massifs from the Atlantic to the Mediterranean. Booking in July and August is essential for the most popular huts — the Renclusa, Góriz, and Colomers can fill up weeks in advance.
