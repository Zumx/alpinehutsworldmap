To climb Triglav is, the old saying goes, the duty of every Slovene at least once in a lifetime. The three-headed peak rises to 2,864 metres at the heart of the Julian Alps and of Triglav National Park, the country's only national park, and its silhouette sits at the centre of the national flag. Around and below it spreads one of Europe's densest networks of mountain huts, the koce and domovi run by the Planinska zveza Slovenije, the alpine association founded in 1893. A summer traverse of these huts, from the high refuge on Kredarica to the green valley domovi, is among the most rewarding hut journeys in the eastern Alps.

## Triglav and Its National Park

Triglav National Park covers much of the Julian Alps in Slovenia's north-west, bordering Italy and reaching toward Austria. Within it lie the turquoise Lake Bohinj, the glacial Lake Bled with its island church, the Soca river with its luminous green water, and the Vrata, Vrsic and Pericnik landscapes beloved of Slovene walkers. The rock is pale limestone, sharp and fissured, riddled with caves and karst, and the high ridges hold snow into early summer.

The peak's name means three heads, and folklore made it the home of a three-headed deity. Whatever its mythic origin, Triglav is a genuine focus of national identity. The first recorded ascent came in 1778, when four men from Bohinj reached the summit, an event Slovenes celebrate as a founding moment of their alpinism. Today the standard routes are protected with via ferrata cables and pins, but they remain serious mountain undertakings demanding a head for heights.

## Triglavski dom na Kredarici

The highest manned hut in Slovenia is the Triglavski dom na Kredarici, perched at 2,515 metres just below the summit pyramid. It is a large, weather-beaten refuge that doubles as a meteorological station and a base for the final push to the top. On a summer morning its terrace fills with climbers buckling helmets and clipping into harnesses before the cabled ridge to the summit cross.

Kredarica sleeps well over a hundred and serves as the crossroads of the high routes. From here one can descend to the Pokljuka plateau, traverse to the Dom Planika pod Triglavom on the southern side, or drop into the wild Vrata valley. Booking is essential in July and August, when Slovene, Italian and German parties fill every bunk, and the hut's guardians manage the flow with brisk efficiency.

## The Approaches and Their Huts

Triglav can be reached from several directions, and each has its own chain of huts. From the north, the Vrata valley leads to the Aljazev dom v Vratih beneath the colossal 1,000-metre north face, one of the great walls of the Alps. From here climbers attempt the face directly or take the Tominsek and Prag routes up cabled rock to Kredarica.

From the east, the Pokljuka plateau and the Vodnikov dom funnel walkers toward the summit, while from the south the Dom Planika and the Konjsko sedlo route climb from the Tosc area. To the west, the high Trenta valley above the Soca offers the longest and wildest approach. Each option strings together huts that let a fit walker spend several days entirely above the valleys, moving from refuge to refuge across the Triglav massif.

## The Seven Lakes Valley

One of the most beautiful hut walks in Slovenia does not touch the summit at all. The Triglav Lakes Valley, the Dolina Triglavskih jezer, holds a string of glacial tarns set in pale rock and dwarf pine. Two huts serve it: the Kocˇa pri Triglavskih jezerih and the Zasavska koca na Prehodavcih, higher up toward the Trenta side. Walkers thread between the lakes, past the Black Lake and the Double Lake, in a landscape that feels both austere and serene.

This valley links the Bohinj basin to the high Triglav routes and makes a superb multi-day traverse on its own. The huts here, like all PZS refuges, run on a system of half board, dormitory bunks and a strong tradition of self-sufficiency: walkers carry their own sheet sleeping bag, the rjuha, and respect the quiet of the mountain evenings.

## Huts Beyond Triglav

Slovenia's hut culture reaches far past the Julian Alps. To the east rise the Kamnik-Savinja Alps, where the Kocˇa na Klemenci jami pod Ojstrico and the Dom v Kamniski Bistrici serve the dramatic peaks of Ojstrica, Planjava and Grintovec. The Karavanke range along the Austrian border holds the Koschutahaus, known in Slovene as the Koca pod Kosuto, a hut shared in spirit between Slovene and Carinthian walkers.

Across the lower hills and plateaus, dozens more domovi welcome day walkers and families: the Mariborska koca above Maribor, the Vojkova koca na Nanosu on the windswept Nanos plateau, the Planinski dom na Kumu in the eastern hills, and the Koca na planini Stador near Bohinj. This breadth means Slovenia offers everything from a casual Sunday hut lunch to a committing alpine traverse, all under the same association.

## Practicalities, Season and Spirit

The high Julian huts open from about mid-June to late September, with the best weather usually from July to early September. Lower domovi often stay open longer or year-round at weekends. The PZS membership card, recognised across the wider alpine club network, brings discounts on overnight stays. Trails are well marked with the distinctive red-and-white Knafelc blazes, a target circle painted on rock and tree.

What gives Slovene hut life its flavour is its blend of seriousness and warmth. The food is hearty, jota stew, struklji dumplings, blueberry strudel and the herbal spirit borovnicke, and the guardians keep a firm but friendly order. Above all there is the sense of pilgrimage: nearly everyone on the Kredarica terrace at dawn is bound for the three heads of Triglav, and the mountain still carries the weight of a whole nation's affection.
