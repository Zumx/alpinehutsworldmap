The meadows, scree fields, and cliff faces around alpine huts host some of the most watchable wildlife in Europe. Above 1,800 m, the thinning forest gives way to open terrain where large mammals move in full view and raptors hang on thermals at eye level. A single morning at a hut terrace can yield chamois grazing a hundred metres away, a marmot colony performing its sentinel duties on a boulder field, and a golden eagle drifting along a ridge. Knowing what to look for and when sharpens the experience considerably.

## Chamois — The Defining Ungulate of the Alps

Rupicapra rupicapra, the Alpine chamois, is the animal most hikers associate with the high mountains. Adults weigh 25 to 50 kg and carry short, hooked horns that grow continuously throughout their lives. Both sexes carry them. Chamois are extremely agile on broken terrain — tendons in their legs act as shock absorbers, and their hooves have hard outer rims and soft inner pads that grip rock like rubber soles.

In summer, chamois typically graze between 1,500 m and 3,000 m. They follow the retreating snowline upward in May and June, grazing alpine meadows at their most nutritious. In autumn they descend to the treeline for warmth and shelter. The rut occurs between November and January; bucks become aggressive and territorial, making winter backcountry travel through chamois habitat worth noting.

Chamois populations collapsed badly in the 19th century due to hunting. Legal protection across the Alps from the early 20th century, combined with the creation of national parks — Gran Paradiso in 1922 being the first in Italy — allowed numbers to recover to several hundred thousand today. In the Hohe Tauern of Austria, chamois are frequently visible from huts in the Glockner group.

## Alpine Ibex — The Comeback Story

The Alpine ibex (Capra ibex) was hunted nearly to extinction by 1800, surviving only in the Gran Paradiso massif on what is now the Italian-French border. Careful reintroduction from Gran Paradiso stock starting in the 1870s has restored the species across much of the Alps; the Swiss population alone now exceeds 17,000 animals.

Ibex are larger and stockier than chamois. Adult males carry massive curved ridged horns that can reach 100 cm in length — unmistakeable at any distance. Females have much shorter horns. The species is strongly social: males and females live separately for most of the year, with mixed groups forming only during the December rut.

The best places to observe ibex near huts include the area around the Vittorio Emanuele II Hut at 2,732 m in Gran Paradiso, where ibex may appear on the slopes within metres of the path, and the upper Engadin in Switzerland near the Chamanna Coaz (2,610 m), where the Roseg valley ibex population has stabilised at several hundred animals.

## Marmots — Sound and Structure of the Alpine Meadow

The Alpine marmot (Marmota marmota) is the most audible resident above the treeline. Its piercing alarm whistle — a single sharp note for aerial predators, a series of repeated notes for ground threats — functions as a public warning system that chamois and ibex visibly respond to. Marmots emerged in the Alps approximately 35,000 years ago and hibernate from October to April, surviving on body fat built up over the summer.

Colonies range from a few animals to over 20 individuals, typically organised around a dominant pair. Burrow systems can extend 10 m horizontally and 3 m deep, with a lined hibernation chamber. On warm summer mornings, marmots emerge to sun themselves on boulders before foraging. The area around the Berliner Hut (2,042 m) in the Zillertal Alps is famous among hut visitors for the density of marmot activity visible from the terrace.

Marmots largely ignore hikers who move slowly and without sudden noise, allowing close observation. They are aggressively protective of burrow entrances and will chase smaller animals — and occasionally bold dogs — away from the colony.

## Golden Eagle — The Apex Raptor

Aquila chrysaetos, the golden eagle, ranges across the Alps from the Maritime Alps to the Karawanken. Wingspan reaches 220 cm; adults weigh up to 6 kg. A pair claims a territory of 50 to 200 square kilometres and returns to the same cliff-ledge nest (eyrie) for decades, adding material each year until the structure may weigh several hundred kilograms.

Golden eagles hunt primarily marmots and hares in summer, shifting to birds and carrion in winter. The hunting stoop is one of the most dramatic sights available to a hut visitor: a bird circles at altitude for several minutes, then folds its wings and drops almost vertically onto a marmot that has strayed too far from its burrow. Success rates are lower than the dive suggests — marmots respond to the alarm calls of their colony and reach burrows quickly.

The Adamello-Brenta Nature Park in Trentino, reachable from the Rifugio Tuckett (2,272 m), hosts a stable breeding population. In the Ecrins massif of France, the area around the Refuge du Glacier Blanc (2,542 m) offers frequent sightings during summer.

## Ptarmigan — Masters of Camouflage

The rock ptarmigan (Lagopus muta) lives year-round above 2,000 m in the Alps — higher than any other bird that does not migrate. Its plumage changes three times annually: cryptic brown-grey in summer, white with black tail feathers in winter, and a mottled transitional pattern in spring and autumn. In winter plumage against snow, a ptarmigan can be invisible at 5 m.

Ptarmigan depend on stable snowpack. As the duration of alpine snow cover shortens — the Swiss Alps have lost roughly six weeks of snow cover since 1970 by some estimates — ptarmigan habitat contracts upward. Finding them requires searching rocky ridges above 2,400 m in July and August; the characteristic low, croaking call is usually heard before the bird is seen.

## Lammergeier — The Bone Breaker

The bearded vulture (Gypaetus barbatus), known in German as the Bartgeier and in French as the gypaète barbu, was extinct in the Alps by 1913. A reintroduction programme launched in 1987 from a breeding centre in Haringsee, Austria, has released over 250 birds since then, and the Alpine population now numbers around 300 individuals with confirmed breeding pairs from the Pyrenees to Slovenia.

The lammergeier feeds almost entirely on bone marrow, dropping large bones from heights of up to 80 m onto rocky "ossuaries" to crack them open. In flight, the species is identifiable by its diamond-shaped tail — unique among European raptors — and by the rust-orange colouring of the adult's breast, achieved not by pigment but by bathing in iron-rich mud. Sightings near the Monte Rosa Huts and in the Hohe Tauern have increased markedly since 2010.

## Where to See Wildlife and Plan Your Approach

Wildlife observation above the treeline rewards early starts and slow movement more than any specialist gear. Dawn and dusk are the productive hours; midday, most large mammals seek shade or shelter from insects in rocky terrain. Keep dogs on leads — marmot colonies in particular are vulnerable to disturbance.
