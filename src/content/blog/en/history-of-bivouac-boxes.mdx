Somewhere above 3,500 m in the Alps, on a ledge or anchored to a snow-scoured ridge, sits a small orange or red structure the size of a large camping trailer. It contains six or eight bunk boards, a few wool blankets, a small emergency food supply, and nothing else. No warden, no heating, no kitchen. The bivouac box — Bivaccobox in Italian, Biwakschachtel in German — represents the minimum viable shelter: the structure that exists to keep stranded climbers alive until morning or until the storm passes. Its history is a compressed version of Alpine climbing history itself.

## Origins in the Great War and Interwar Period

The first fixed high-altitude emergency shelters in the Alps were not purpose-built boxes but repurposed military structures. The First World War was fought at altitude in the Dolomites and on the Ortler group; the Austro-Hungarian and Italian armies installed supply depots and observation posts at elevations above 3,000 m. After 1918, several of these structures were adapted for civilian use by the Italian Alpine Club (CAI) and the Austrian Alpine Club (ÖAV).

The Bivacco Gervasutti on the Fréboudze Glacier below the Grandes Jorasses — arguably the most famous bivouac box in the Alps — was not installed until 1947, when Luigi Carrel and a team from the Torino section of CAI bolted a prefabricated aluminum structure to a rock platform at 2,835 m. The original Gervasutti bivouac, a cylindrical drum about 3 m in diameter, could hold six climbers and was the prototype for the modern form. It was replaced in 2011 by a teardrop-shaped fibreglass pod designed by the Politecnico di Torino, which has become the most photographed bivouac structure in the world.

## Design Evolution: From Steel Box to Aerodynamic Shell

The first generation of purpose-built bivouac boxes, produced from the 1930s onward, were simple steel or aluminium rectangles bolted or cabled to rock anchors. Shape was irrelevant; structural integrity against snow loading and wind was the only design criterion. These boxes weighed 400-700 kg and required helicopter installation even before helicopters could reliably operate at altitude — early installations used cable cranes and manual carrying parties.

The critical design problem is wind. At exposed sites above 4,000 m, sustained winds of 100 km/h are routine in winter, with gusts exceeding 150 km/h during foehn events. A flat-sided box presents maximum surface area to wind; the bolts and cable anchors must carry the full load. Early designs sometimes failed catastrophically — the Bivacco Luca Vuerich on the north face of the Jôf di Montasio collapsed in 1987 after anchor failure and was rebuilt with significantly heavier bolts.

From the 1970s onward, fibreglass and composite materials allowed curved aerodynamic shapes that reduce wind loading dramatically. The teardrop and barrel-vault forms popular in Italian and French bivouac design are not aesthetic choices but engineering solutions: a curved surface deflects wind rather than resisting it, reducing structural loads by 30-40% compared to a flat-sided equivalent. Modern bivouac boxes at high exposure sites are designed with finite element analysis and tested in wind tunnels before installation.

## Who Builds and Maintains Them

Bivouac boxes in the Alps are owned and maintained primarily by national and regional alpine clubs: CAI in Italy, the SAC (Swiss Alpine Club) in Switzerland, the ÖAV and the Alpenverein Südtirol in Austria and South Tyrol, and the FFCAM (Fédération Française des Clubs Alpins et de Montagne) in France.

The maintenance schedule is demanding. Annual visits to restock blankets and emergency provisions, check anchor integrity, and repair structural damage require helicopter access — many bivouac boxes are inaccessible on foot for most of the year. The SAC estimates that each high-altitude bivouac box costs CHF 5,000-15,000 per year to maintain, depending on access difficulty and structural condition. The highest bivouac box regularly maintained in the Alps is the Bivacco Eccles on the Freney Pillar of Mont Blanc, at approximately 4,350 m, maintained by the CAI Torino section.

Local mountain guide associations also play a role; in the Dolomites, many bivouac boxes are formally adopted by individual guide companies or climbing clubs who manage them as a service obligation. The Bivacco Fiori Cesare on the Tre Cime di Lavaredo group is maintained by the Cortina guide association and sees heavy use during summer climbing season.

## Use Rules and Ethics

Bivouac boxes are not huts. They carry no warden, no registration system, and no formal booking. The convention is simple: the box is available to any climber in need. Non-emergency use — sheltering in a bivouac box to avoid booking a staffed hut — is frowned upon in alpine culture and causes real harm in emergencies, when a party that has planned to sleep in the box finds it occupied by comfortable day-trippers.

In Italy, CAI has begun requiring that non-emergency users of staffed-route bivouac boxes pay a nominal fee (typically €5-10) deposited in a box installed for this purpose, with proceeds used for maintenance. This system works on trust; there is no enforcement mechanism.

## Notable Bivouac Boxes in the Alps

The Rifugio Gonella bivouac on Mont Blanc at 3,071 m is a historic structure with direct ties to the earliest Alpine Club expeditions of the 1880s. The Bivacco Luca Vuerich on the Jôf di Montasio (2,753 m) in the Julian Alps, rebuilt after its 1987 collapse, was designed by award-winning architect Gianpietro Cimenti and has become a minor landmark in high-altitude architecture. The Chabod Hut bivouac extension on the Gran Paradiso at 2,710 m serves as an overflow structure for climbers on the standard Gran Paradiso route when the hut is full in August.

In Norway, the DNT (Norwegian Trekking Association) maintains a network of unstaffed emergency shelters across the high plateaux, operated on a key-access system that is philosophically similar to alpine bivouac boxes but architecturally distinct — wooden structures built for cross-country skiing conditions rather than vertical rock faces.

## Planning Routes That Include Bivouac Boxes

Not every alpine route needs a bivouac box, but knowing their locations changes what is possible. A bivouac box on a long ridge allows you to split a two-day technical route differently, spending the night at altitude rather than making a long approach and descent.
