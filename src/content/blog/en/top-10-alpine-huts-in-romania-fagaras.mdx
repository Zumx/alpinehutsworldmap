The Fagaras Mountains form the highest continuous ridge in Romania and the entire Carpathian arc. Running roughly 70 kilometres from west to east across Transylvania, the range culminates at Moldoveanu (2,544 m), the country's highest point, and includes more than a dozen other summits above 2,400 m. The crest is wild, treeless, and frequently swept by severe weather even in July; the deep glacial valleys on either side hold clear lakes, fast rivers, and forests of beech and spruce that were largely untouched until the twentieth century. The Romanian mountain shelter — the cabana — has served as the backbone of Fagaras hiking culture since the late nineteenth century, when Transylvanian and Wallachian hiking clubs began building fixed shelters along the ridge. Today, the cabana network covers the full length of the range and allows experienced hikers to traverse the crest over five to seven days without tents.

## 1. Cabana Postăvaru, Eastern Fagaras

Cabana Postăvaru is positioned above the resort town of Predeal and the Poiana Brasov ski area, at around 1,600 m on the Postăvaru massif. While technically at the eastern edge of the broader range, it serves as a common starting point for parties organising multi-day Fagaras trips by bus or train via Brasov. The cabana has warm beds, a full restaurant, and reliable year-round access — qualities not guaranteed higher on the ridge. It is one of the best-maintained mountain buildings in the region.

## 2. Cabana Bârcaciu, Western Fagaras

Cabana Bârcaciu sits at 1,560 m in the Bârcaciu valley on the southern, Wallachian side of the range. It is the standard base for the ascent of Negoiu (2,535 m), Romania's second-highest peak, and for the western approach to the main ridge traverse. The approach from the Olt valley town of Avrig takes three to four hours on foot. The cabana has around 60 beds, a small kitchen, and a drying room — essential given how frequently afternoon storms drench climbers descending from the ridge.

## 3. Cabana Negoiu, Western Fagaras

Standing at 1,546 m at the head of the Negoiu valley, Cabana Negoiu is positioned lower than its name suggests but serves as the overnight stop closest to the route connecting the Negoiu summit to the Bâlea Lake area. The valley around the hut contains some of the finest old-growth spruce forest in the southern Carpathians. The hut operates seasonally from May through October and has capacity for around 70 people in mixed dormitories.

## 4. Cabana Podragu, Central Fagaras

Cabana Podragu at 2,136 m is one of the highest permanently occupied cabane in Romania and sits directly on the main ridge traverse, below the Podragu Lake. It is three to four hours from Bâlea Lake to the east or from Bârcaciu to the west, placing it at roughly the midpoint of the range. The summit of Moldoveanu is accessible from the cabana in around three hours. Accommodation is simple — dormitory bunks and basic meals — but the views north across the alpine lakes are outstanding.

## 5. Cabana Capra, Central Fagaras

Cabana Capra occupies a dramatic position at 2,040 m on the Transfagarasan road — the serpentine mountain highway built in the 1970s that crosses the range at Bâlea Pass. Because the road brings vehicles directly to high altitude, Capra is one of the most accessible high cabane in Romania. It operates primarily in summer, from June to October, when the road is snow-free. The terrace looks west across the Capra Lake and toward the Transfagarasan cascade.

## 6. Cabana Suru, Western Fagaras

Cabana Suru at 1,414 m lies in the Suru valley, one of the quieter approach corridors on the southern side of the range. The hut is used by parties starting the western ridge traverse or aiming for Suru peak (2,283 m). Capacity is around 50 people. The surrounding forests hold brown bear and lynx populations, and chamois sightings on the valley headwall above the cabana are common in the early morning hours of July and August.

## 7. Cabana Lacul Avrig, Eastern Fagaras

Cabana Lacul Avrig is located at 2,040 m beside the Avrig Lake, one of the largest glacial tarns on the southern slopes of the range. The setting — open alpine bowl, turquoise lake, jagged ridge above — is among the most photographed in Romanian mountaineering. The hut has modest capacity of around 30 beds and is primarily visited by parties traversing the eastern Fagaras section between Bâlea and the Avrig valley. The season is July through September due to the late snow-melt at this elevation.

## 8. Cabana Laita, Southern Approach

Cabana Laita sits at approximately 1,520 m in the Laita tributary valley on the Wallachian southern slope, serving hikers approaching from the Curtea de Arges direction. It is a smaller, locally run hut with a friendly atmosphere and is often less crowded than the more famous cabane along the main ridge. The trail from Laita to the main crest takes four to five hours and passes through excellent raspberry and bilberry fields in late summer.

## 9. Cabana Turnuri, Eastern Fagaras

Cabana Turnuri at 1,520 m takes its name from the "towers" — the steep rock pillars on the ridgeline above the valley. The hut is on the Transylvanian northern side of the range and offers access to the eastern ridge section between Podragu and Bâlea. It has capacity for around 60 people and a particularly good reputation for home-cooked Romanian mountain food — ciorbă de fasole, mămăligă with brânză, and plum brandy distilled locally. The season runs from late May to early October.

## 10. Cabana Dejani, Eastern Fagaras

Cabana Dejani sits at 1,310 m near the eastern end of the range in the Dejani valley, one of the less-visited corners of Fagaras. From here the ridge access is longer but the payoff is solitude — the easternmost peaks see far fewer hikers than the central massifs around Moldoveanu and Negoiu. The hut operates through most of the year and is well positioned for autumn visits in September and early October when the beech forests turn amber and the weather is often more stable than midsummer.
