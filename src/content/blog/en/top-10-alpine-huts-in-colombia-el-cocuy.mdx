In the Eastern Andes of Colombia, where the department of Boyaca rises toward the Venezuelan border, a wall of white peaks catches the equatorial sun. The Sierra Nevada del Cocuy holds the largest glacier mass in Colombia, more than twenty summits above 5,000 metres strung along a high spine of rock and ice. Around their feet spreads the paramo, that strange Andean tundra of giant frailejones plants, and scattered through it are the cabanas and family-run refuges that shelter trekkers. These are not the manned alpine clubs of Europe but working farms, posadas and simple lodges where the high country meets the lives of the campesinos who have always lived here.

## A Glacier on the Equator

The Sierra Nevada del Cocuy, also called the Sierra Nevada de Guican, lies within the Parque Nacional Natural El Cocuy. Its highest point, Ritacuba Blanco, reaches about 5,330 metres, and the range is one of the very few places near the equator where permanent ice survives. The glaciers are retreating fast, and the park authorities have closed all glacier travel to protect both the fragile ice and the indigenous U'wa people, for whom these mountains are sacred ground.

What remains open to visitors is a series of magnificent day walks and short treks across the paramo to the glacier edge. The landscape is unlike any in the Alps or the rest of the Andes: forests of frailejones, woolly-leaved plants that grow a single centimetre a year and store water for the whole ecosystem, stretch across the moors beneath the ice, punctuated by black lakes and red mineral streams.

## Gateway Villages: El Cocuy and Guican

Two small whitewashed towns guard the range, El Cocuy and Guican, both colonial villages of red-tiled roofs at around 2,750 metres. They are the logistical base for any visit: here trekkers register, hire the obligatory local guide now required for park entry, and arrange transport on the early-morning milk trucks, the lecheros, that grind up the rough roads to the trailheads.

Accommodation in and just above the towns ranges from modest hotels to rural fincas. Higher up, at the edge of the park, sit the classic mountain lodges that have served Cocuy trekkers for decades. Cabanas Kanwara and Cabanas Sisuma, near the Lagunillas and Ritacuba sectors, are the best known, simple but warm bases at around 3,900 metres with bunks, blankets and hot meals after a cold day on the paramo.

## Cabanas Kanwara and the Ritacuba Sector

Cabanas Kanwara sits high on the northern, Guican side of the range, beneath the great dome of Ritacuba Blanco. From its terrace the glacier seems almost close enough to touch, and the classic day walk climbs from the lodge to the snout of the ice at roughly 4,800 metres, a strenuous outing in thin air that rewards with a face-on view of the highest summit in the range.

The Kanwara lodge, run by a local family, embodies the Cocuy model: a working base where guests sleep in simple rooms, gather round the wood stove against the night chill, and set off before dawn for the ice. Nearby, other family operations such as Cabanas el Nevado and Hacienda La Esperanza offer the same blend of rustic comfort and direct access to the high paramo.

## Cabanas Sisuma and the Lagunillas

On the southern flank, in the Lagunillas sector reached from El Cocuy town, Cabanas Sisuma serves as the base for one of the park's signature walks: the route to the Laguna Grande de la Sierra, a vast glacial lake cradled in an amphitheatre of peaks including Pan de Azucar and the dramatic Pulpito del Diablo, a sheer rock tower that is the symbol of the range.

The walk from Sisuma to the Laguna Grande crosses classic paramo, weaving through frailejon fields and past smaller tarns before the great lake opens beneath the ice. It is demanding, long and high, but technically straightforward now that glacier travel is forbidden, making it accessible to fit hikers who are well acclimatised. The lodge itself, like Kanwara, is family-run, warm and simple.

## The Park Rules and the U'wa

El Cocuy is not a place to wander freely. After years of unregulated tourism damaged the fragile ice and paramo, the park introduced strict rules: entry only with a registered local guide, no setting foot on the glaciers, no camping inside the park, and fixed daily routes that must be completed and exited the same day. Visitors enter early and are back at the lodges by early afternoon.

These rules reflect more than conservation. The U'wa people regard the Sierra Nevada del Cocuy as sacred, the heart of their cosmology, and have long opposed both mining and intrusive tourism on the ice. Respecting the closures and the guiding requirement is not bureaucratic box-ticking but a recognition that this is living indigenous territory as much as a national park. Trekkers who come with that awareness find a far richer welcome.

## Practicalities and Season

The dry season, roughly December to March and again in July and August, brings the most stable weather, though the paramo can deliver rain, hail and brilliant sun in a single day at any time of year. Nights at the lodges are genuinely cold, often below freezing, and proper layers, gloves and a warm sleeping setup are essential even though you sleep indoors.

Acclimatisation matters: the towns sit near 2,750 metres and the walks climb to 4,800 metres, a serious altitude jump. Most visitors spend a night or two in El Cocuy or Guican before heading up. Getting there is part of the adventure, a long bus journey from Bogota of roughly ten to eleven hours, followed by the lechero trucks to the trailheads. It is remote, and that remoteness is its gift.
