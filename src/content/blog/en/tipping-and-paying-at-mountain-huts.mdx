You have walked seven hours, eaten a vast plate of rösti and a slab of cake, slept in a dormitory above a glacier, and now it is morning and the warden is tallying your bill on a scrap of paper by the light of a window. There is no card machine, the nearest cash point is a day's walk away, and the total is more than you expected. How you handle this moment — what you carry, how you pay, whether and how much you tip — is one of the small competencies that separates the seasoned hut-goer from the surprised one. Paying at a mountain hut is its own quiet art.

## Why Paying at Huts Is Different

A mountain hut is not a restaurant in town, and the economics that govern it are unlike anything in the valley. Everything in that building — every bottle of beer, every sack of flour, every spare blanket — arrived by helicopter, cable lift, mule, or the warden's own back, and the prices reflect that. A staffed hut runs on a short season, a tiny staff, and supply costs that would bankrupt a normal business, which is why a beer that costs two euros in the valley costs five or six at altitude, and why the bill at the end of a night can feel steep.

It also means the warden's relationship to money is practical and often informal. Many huts still tally bills by hand, accept payment in only one or two ways, and operate at the edge of the cash economy. Understanding this before you arrive — rather than discovering it bleary-eyed at breakfast — is the difference between a smooth departure and an awkward standoff at 2,500 metres with no way to settle what you owe.

## Cash Is Still King

The single most important rule of hut payment is to carry enough cash, in the local currency, in small notes. Despite the slow spread of card readers, a great many huts — especially the smaller, higher, or more remote ones — still take cash only. A satellite card terminal needs power and a signal that many huts simply do not have, and even where a machine exists it may be down, the battery flat, or the connection lost in bad weather. Cash never fails.

Carry more than you think you need. The bill usually combines the bed, the evening meal, breakfast, and every drink and snack you bought, and it adds up faster than expected, particularly if you take a second beer or buy lunch supplies for the next day. Bring it in modest denominations, because a warden a long way from a bank cannot always make change for a large note. In the Alps that means euros in Italy, Austria, France, and Germany, and Swiss francs in Switzerland, where the franc rules and euros are accepted only grudgingly and at a poor rate.

## Where Cards and Apps Work

The picture is changing, slowly and unevenly. Larger, lower, or road-accessible huts increasingly take cards, and some of the busy serviced inns on popular routes — parts of the Tour du Mont Blanc, the bigger SAC and DAV huts near valley stations — now have terminals or accept mobile payment. In Austria and Germany especially, contactless and apps are spreading, and a few huts publish a payment app or bank transfer option for booking deposits in advance.

But never assume. The safe approach is to check each hut's own page or call ahead, treat any card facility as a bonus rather than a guarantee, and always carry cash as backup even where cards are advertised, because a single failed connection turns a card-only plan into a crisis. The further and higher you go, the more this matters: the high bivouac and self-service huts are almost universally cash-only, often through an honesty box rather than a person.

## The Honesty Box and Self-Service Huts

At unstaffed self-service huts, there is no one to take your money, and payment runs entirely on trust. You will find an honesty box — a locked metal container, sometimes with a price list pinned beside it — into which you put the correct cash for your bed, any firewood you burned, and anything you took from the stocked larder of tins, pasta, and drinks that many such huts maintain. Some use a self-registration card you fill in and post; the Norwegian DNT network is the classic example, where you pay for food taken from the shelves on trust.

This system only works because almost everyone honours it, and the etiquette is strict: pay the full posted price even when no one would ever know, carry exact or near-exact cash because no change is possible, and never treat a self-service hut as free accommodation. The honesty box is the financial backbone of the unstaffed mountain world, and abusing it threatens a model that has kept high shelters open and stocked for generations.

## The Member Discount and How to Earn It

One of the great open secrets of hut travel is the alpine club member discount. The huts of the Swiss Alpine Club, the German DAV, the Austrian ÖAV, the Italian CAI, and the French clubs are run by their members for their members, and a membership card earns a substantial reduction on the overnight fee — often roughly half — at the home club's huts and, crucially, at the huts of partner clubs across the reciprocal rights agreement that links most of the major alpine associations.

For anyone planning more than a few hut nights a year, the maths is compelling: a single annual membership, which also brings insurance and rescue cover in many clubs, can pay for itself in a week of hut-to-hut walking through the discount alone. Carry the physical card or its digital equivalent and present it when you check in, not when you pay, so the warden enters the member rate from the start. It is the closest thing to a free upgrade that mountain travel offers.

## To Tip or Not to Tip

Tipping at huts is governed by quiet local custom rather than fixed rules, and the broad answer is that it is appreciated but never obligatory, and certainly not at the level expected in a city restaurant. In the Alpine countries, service is included in the bill, and the culture is to round up modestly or leave a small amount for genuinely good service rather than to add a fixed percentage. A few coins or rounding the total up is normal and welcome; a large American-style tip is unusual and unnecessary.

Where a tip genuinely lands is in recognising the warden and their team, who cook, clean, manage rescue coordination, and run a demanding business in brutal conditions for a short season. Leaving the rounding, thanking them sincerely, and above all behaving well — keeping quiet hours, tidying your space, following the hut rules — is worth more than money. The best tip you can leave a mountain hut is to be the kind of guest the warden is glad to have seen, and to settle your bill without fuss, in cash, with a smile.
