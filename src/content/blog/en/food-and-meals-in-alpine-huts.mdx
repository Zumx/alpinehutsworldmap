Eating in an alpine hut is one of the more reliable pleasures of a mountain
trip. The food is rarely elaborate, but after six hours on a ridge it is
reliably good, consistently hot, and almost always prepared by someone who has
been feeding tired climbers for years. Understanding how the system works before
you arrive saves money, avoids friction, and means you eat better.

## The half-board model

Most staffed alpine huts in the Alps sell overnight stays on a Halbpension or
demi-pension (half-board, abbreviated HP) basis. This bundles the bunk, an
evening meal, and breakfast into a single price. In Italy, a CAI rifugio might
charge €55–75 per person all-in; a Swiss SAC hut runs CHF 80–110; French FFCAM
refuges typically fall between €55 and €80. The meal is not optional — it is
the economic foundation that keeps a hut financially viable through a short
summer season.

Dinner is served at a fixed time, usually 18:30 or 19:00, announced by the
guardian or by a bell. There is no à la carte menu. You eat what is cooked,
and the portions are sized for people who have walked all day. If you have
dietary restrictions, email the hut before arriving — most guardians
accommodate vegetarians without difficulty, but remote huts with weekly supply
runs may need advance notice for more specific requirements.

## The staples by range

Regional cooking traditions follow the mountains closely. In the Western Alps
and the Wallis, rösti — pan-fried shredded potato — appears as a side or a
main, sometimes topped with a fried egg or cheese. In the Eastern Alps and
South Tyrol, Knödel (bread dumplings, served in broth or with butter) and
Spätzle (soft egg noodles) are constants on hut menus. In the Italian
Dolomites and Apennines, polenta with mushrooms or venison ragù, risotto, and
pasta with hearty mountain sauces are standard. French huts in the Écrins and
Mont Blanc massif lean toward tartiflette, gratins, soupe de montagne, and
cheese plates from nearby valleys.

These are not tourist inventions — they are historically calorie-dense foods
designed to fuel shepherds and hunters at altitude. The ingredients travel well,
keep without refrigeration for days, and cook in a single pot.

## Breakfast

Breakfast in a staffed hut is typically served from 06:00 to 07:00, sometimes
earlier if the guardian knows guests are doing a technical climb or catching a
cable car. Expect bread, butter, jam, cheese, Birchermüesli or cornflakes, and
coffee or tea. Some huts offer a proper hot breakfast — eggs, Speck, or
porridge — for a supplement. Eat more than you think you need; the next meal is
lunch, and on most hut-to-hut routes that is a packed lunch carried in your
bag.

## Packed lunches

Many huts offer a Lunchpaket or paquet-repas for a small fee, usually CHF 8–15
or €7–12. This is typically a roll or two, a piece of cheese or salami, a
piece of fruit, and something sweet. Order it the evening before — the guardian
prepares them before dawn and will not have time to assemble one at 06:30 as
you are putting on your boots.

If you prefer to self-cater lunch, most huts sell chocolate bars, dried fruit,
and sometimes instant soup or cup noodles at a counter or small shop. Prices
are elevated — a bar of chocolate at 3000 m is not the same price as one at
the supermarket — so stocking up in the valley before a multi-day traverse is
always cheaper.

## Water culture

In the Alps, water is almost never a problem. Most huts have a tap running from
a spring or snowmelt cistern, and the guardian will tell you if it is potable.
A tap outside the boot room running cold, clear water is standard practice
from June through September. Higher or more remote huts — particularly those
above 3500 m in the Monte Rosa or Mont Blanc range — may have limited water and
ask guests to be conservative. Always ask before filling bottles from any
source you are not sure about.

Bottled water is sold at huts but it is expensive and generates plastic waste.
A 1-litre soft-sided bottle or hydration bladder, filled at the hut tap each
morning, is the practical approach.

## No-cook self-catering rooms

Some huts maintain a separate Selbstversorgerraum (self-catering room) — a
basic cooking area with a gas ring, a sink, basic pots, and sometimes a small
pantry of emergency dry goods left by previous parties. These exist primarily
for winter use when the guardian has closed for the season, but some larger
huts keep them open year-round for budget climbers who prefer to cook their own
food. When using a self-catering room, bring everything you need: gas
cartridges, a lighter, all food, and cooking utensils. Leave the space clean
and leave behind any gas or food you cannot carry out.

## What to bring versus what is there

A staffed hut in season provides meals, water, basic snacks for sale, and
sometimes a small bar serving beer, wine, schnapps, and soft drinks. You do
not need to bring a stove, cooking equipment, or a multi-day food supply for
hut-to-hut travel in summer.

Bring: your packed lunch food if not ordering a Lunchpaket, a refillable
water bottle, a small supply of your preferred snacks for the approach
(nuts, energy bars, dried fruit), and cash. Many huts, particularly in France
and Italy, do not accept credit cards reliably, and even where card machines
exist they often fail at altitude due to network outages. CHF or euros in
small denominations are the universal currency.

## Wine, beer, and schnapps

A hut bar is not a gimmick. It is a functional part of the mountain hut
economy and a central feature of evening culture. Austrian and Bavarian huts
stock Tyrolean beer — typically from local regional breweries — and serve
a small wine list (house white, house red) and a selection of schnapps.
Obstler (fruit brandy), Enzian (gentian root spirit), and Kräuterlikör (herbal
liqueur) are the classic post-dinner options and are served in small measures
that are genuinely small.

Italian rifugi stock local wine — Dolomite whites from the Alto Adige and
Trentino valleys, sometimes served by the carafe. French refuges lean toward
wine from the closest valley appellation, often available by the demi-carafe.
Swiss huts serve a more modest selection but reliably stock the local Valais
varietals in lower huts.

The guidance on alcohol at altitude is consistent: limit consumption on the
first two nights at a new elevation (alcohol suppresses breathing during sleep
and worsens altitude headaches), and remain aware that the effects are stronger
at 3000 m than at sea level. A glass of local wine at the Rifugio Lagazuoi
at 2752 m hits differently than the same glass in Cortina.

## Regional cheeses and cured meats

The cheese boards at well-provisioned alpine huts are among the underrated
pleasures of multi-day mountain travel. In South Tyrol, Speck (dry-cured,
lightly smoked ham) and Graukäse (grey cheese, sharp and pungent) are standard
on evening cheese plates. Swiss huts carry Raclette cheese from Valais farms
and Appenzeller from the north. French refuges in the Savoy often serve
Beaufort (a grand cru alpine cheese made from milk of Tarentaise cows), Tomme
de Savoie, and Reblochon.

These are not decorative additions to the menu — they are functional high-fat,
high-protein foods suited to fuelling mountain exertion. Eat the cheese. Order
the extra plate if you are doing a big day tomorrow.
