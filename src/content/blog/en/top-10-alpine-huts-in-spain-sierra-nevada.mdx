The Sierra Nevada of Andalusia is the highest mountain range in mainland Spain, a vast snow-capped wall rising directly above the warm plains near Granada and the Mediterranean coast. Its summit, Mulhacén (3479 m), is the highest point of the Iberian Peninsula outside the Atlantic islands, and its sister peak Veleta (3396 m) towers above Europe's southernmost ski resort. This is a strange high world where alpine summits look down on subtropical valleys, where snow lingers into summer above villages of almonds and oranges. A network of refugios, run by the Andalusian federation and the national park, serves walkers and climbers in this southernmost true mountain country of Europe.

## A mountain island above the Mediterranean

The Sierra Nevada is a kind of high-altitude island, isolated from the other Spanish ranges and rising abruptly from the Andalusian lowlands. Its great height, so close to the warm Mediterranean, creates a unique climate and an extraordinary endemic flora, with more than two thousand plant species, many found nowhere else on earth. Declared a national park in 1999 and a UNESCO biosphere reserve, it protects glacial cirques, high lakes (lagunas), and the famous acequias, ancient Moorish irrigation channels that carry snowmelt down to the white villages of the Alpujarras. The contrast between the snowfields above and the terraced, Moorish-flavoured valleys below is the defining character of these mountains.

## Refugio Poqueira

The Refugio Poqueira at 2500 metres is the principal mountain hut of the high Sierra Nevada, set in the great Poqueira valley beneath Mulhacén and Veleta. A large, modern, staffed refugio, it is the essential base for the ascent of Mulhacén, the highest peak in mainland Spain, reached from the white villages of the Alpujarras such as Capileira. From here walkers climb past glacial lakes to the broad summit, with views stretching to the Mediterranean and, on the clearest days, to the mountains of Morocco across the sea.

## Refugio Postero Alto

The Refugio Postero Alto at 1880 metres lies on the northern side of the range above Jérez del Marquesado, the main base for exploring the wilder, less-visited northern Sierra Nevada. A staffed hut run by the Andalusian mountain federation, it serves the northern peaks and the integral traverse of the range. The northern slopes, falling toward the Marquesado plateau, are quieter and more remote than the famous southern valleys, and the Postero Alto is the gateway to this lonely high country.

## Refugio Carihuela

The Refugio de la Carihuela at around 3200 metres sits high on the ridge near the summit of Veleta, one of the highest shelters in Spain. An unstaffed stone refuge, basic and exposed, it offers emergency shelter to climbers traversing the high ridge and to ski-mountaineers in winter and spring. Its position, just below the third-highest summit of the peninsula and near the head of Europe's southernmost ski area, makes it one of the most dramatically situated huts in the country, a true high-mountain bivouac in the rarefied air of the Sierra Nevada crest.

## Refugio Vivac La Caldera

The Refugio Vivac La Caldera at around 3050 metres sits beside the Laguna de la Caldera, a glacial lake in the heart of the high range directly beneath Mulhacén. A small unstaffed bivouac hut, it is a key staging point for climbers attempting Mulhacén from the high route and for those traversing the main summits. The setting, among the highest lakes of the Sierra Nevada beneath the peninsula's roof, is austere and beautiful, and the hut allows an early start for the final climb to the summit at dawn.

## Refugio Vivac La Carihuela and the high bivouacs

The high Sierra Nevada is dotted with small unstaffed bivouac refuges that make multi-day traverses possible. Alongside La Caldera, shelters such as the Vivac de la Cucaracha and the Refugio Elorrieta, a ruined but historic shelter on the western ridge, provide rough cover for those crossing the high country. These simple stone huts, often little more than a roof and four walls, are the lifeline of the Integral de los Tres Mil, the great traverse of all the three-thousand-metre summits of the range, one of the toughest mountain challenges in Spain.

## Refugio Cebollar and the western valleys

The western Sierra Nevada, above the city of Granada and the old ski road, holds refuges such as the Refugio del Cebollar and the shelters near the Hoya de la Mora, the high trailhead reached by road from Granada. These give the easiest access to the high country, allowing walkers to reach Veleta in a day, and serve as winter bases for ski-touring on the southern slopes. The proximity to Granada, with the Alhambra visible from the foothills, makes the western approach unique: few mountains of this height sit so close to a great historic city.

## The Alpujarras and the white villages

The southern flank of the Sierra Nevada falls into the Alpujarras, a region of steep valleys and white villages of Moorish origin, with flat-roofed houses, terraced fields and the ancient acequias carrying snowmelt to the orchards. Villages such as Capileira, Bubión and Pampaneira, clinging to the Poqueira gorge, are the gateways to the high refugios and among the most beautiful in Andalusia. The Alpujarras preserve a distinctive culture, the last refuge of the Moriscos after the fall of Granada, with their own architecture, crafts and cuisine, and they give the southern approaches to the Sierra Nevada a rich human dimension.

## Wildlife, endemic flora and the high lagunas

The Sierra Nevada is one of the great centres of botanical endemism in Europe, its high meadows and screes home to plants found nowhere else, such as the Sierra Nevada violet and a host of rare alpine flowers. The Spanish ibex, here thriving where its Pyrenean cousin vanished, roams the high slopes in large numbers and is often seen near the refugios. Golden eagles and other raptors patrol the cirques, and the high lakes, the lagunas, scattered among the summits, are relics of the glaciers that once covered the range. The national park's strict protection and its biosphere reserve status safeguard this unique high-altitude island ecosystem, so different from the alpine ranges of the north.

## Practical notes for the Sierra Nevada huts

The Sierra Nevada refugios divide between staffed huts such as Poqueira and Postero Alto, which require reservation, and the unstaffed high bivouacs that are always open. The main season runs from June to September for walking, while winter and spring bring ski-mountaineering and snow climbs, when the high huts demand full winter skills. Gateways include Capileira and the Poqueira villages in the south, Jérez del Marquesado in the north, and the Hoya de la Mora above Granada in the west. The ascent of Mulhacén is non-technical in summer but long and high, and altitude must be respected on the peninsula's roof.
