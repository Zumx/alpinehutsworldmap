The debate between phone apps and folded paper maps runs through every gear forum and hut-terrace conversation in the Alps. Both camps overstate their case. Digital navigation tools have genuinely transformed alpine route-finding; they have also produced a generation of hikers who cannot read a contour line under fog. The honest answer is that the two approaches are not competitors but layers, and understanding the failure modes of each is the starting point for competent navigation above the treeline.

## What Digital Navigation Does Well

Modern GPS applications — Komoot, Swisstopo (the Swiss federal mapping app), Austria's Bergfex, and the offline-capable version of OSM-based apps like OsmAnd — do several things paper cannot. First, they show your exact position relative to the map in real time, eliminating the most common navigational error: mistaking one valley for an adjacent one when features look similar. Second, they log your track automatically, so if you turn back in whiteout conditions you have a recorded path to follow. Third, they calculate ascent rates, estimated arrival times, and remaining elevation gain while you walk — information that paper requires arithmetic to produce.

Swisstopo deserves particular mention because the underlying 1:25,000 and 1:50,000 federal survey maps are among the most accurate mountain cartography ever produced. The app delivers those maps offline after download, with GPS overlay, at no cost beyond the annual subscription fee (CHF 38.95 in 2025). For anyone hiking in Switzerland, it is the single most useful navigation tool available. The Austrian equivalent, the BEV maps available through several Austrian apps, are comparably accurate.

Komoot excels at route planning rather than navigating unfamiliar terrain. Its surface-type rendering and turn-by-turn guidance are well-suited to trail hiking below the treeline where path junctions are frequent. Above the treeline, where trails disappear into boulder fields and cairns replace waymarks, Komoot's guidance becomes less reliable because the route geometry changes faster than map data is updated.

## Where Digital Navigation Fails

Cold kills batteries. At -10°C, a lithium-ion battery at 40% charge may report 15% charge and shut down without warning. At -20°C, phones that are not body-temperature-warm may refuse to turn on at all. A phone tucked in a chest pocket against the body retains charge far better than one clipped to a pack strap. Power banks also lose capacity in cold; a 20,000 mAh bank at 0°C may deliver 12,000 to 14,000 mAh effective output.

Screen glare and precipitation present a second category of failure. Rain-soaked gloves make capacitive touchscreens unresponsive. Bright reflected snow renders phone screens difficult to read even at full brightness. Several navigation apps now support physical button control (volume up/down for zoom) and voice announcements, but neither replaces reading a map face while sheltering behind a rock.

A third failure mode is the confidence effect. Hikers with a GPS position visible on screen consistently underestimate how easy it is to be misled by cached map tiles that have not been updated, by GPS drift in narrow valleys with high rock walls, and by "route snapping" — the app's tendency to lock your position onto the nearest known trail even when you have left it. Real-time position feedback creates a false sense of certainty that experienced navigators on paper rarely feel, because paper demands active interpretation at every step.

## What Paper Maps Do Well

A 1:25,000 topographic sheet shows terrain at a scale and completeness that no screen reproduces. The entire upper Ötztal — all approach valleys, all huts, all glaciers, all ridgelines — fits on a single sheet that costs €10, weighs 60 g, requires no power, and is readable in any weather when protected in a map case. In fog, when landmarks disappear, the ability to read contours — to understand from the spacing and shape of lines whether the slope ahead steepens, where the corrie rim sits, whether the depression is a pass or a dead-end cwm — is a skill that no app delivers passively.

Paper also provides navigational discipline. When every position fix requires a conscious act of triangulation — using compass, a visible summit, and a terrain feature — hikers develop situational awareness that GPS erodes. This is not nostalgia; it is measurable in rescue statistics. Rescue services in Switzerland and Austria consistently report that the majority of serious navigational incidents now involve hikers who were following an app rather than reading terrain.

The practical limitation of paper is its physical format. A wet map in wind is unusable. Pre-planning is essential — you must carry the correct sheets. And the map cannot tell you where you currently are without a compass bearing or identifiable landmark.

## Compass and Altimeter — The Missing Third Layer

Neither apps nor paper operate at their best without two analogue instruments: a baseplate compass and a barometric altimeter. A compass provides a bearing that does not require signal, battery, or calibration beyond knowing magnetic declination (in the Western Alps, approximately 1-3° East as of 2026). An altimeter — either standalone or embedded in a watch — narrows your position on a map to a contour line, which combined with a compass bearing from one visible feature gives a fix in seconds.

Several watches (Garmin, Suunto, Coros) integrate GPS, altimeter, and compass with multi-day battery life and are carried by most serious alpinists as their primary navigation device, supplemented by a phone app for planning and a paper map for emergencies. This three-layer approach — watch, phone, paper — is the current professional standard among mountain guides.

## Practical Workflow for a Hut-to-Hut Traverse

Before departure, download offline tiles at 1:25,000 in the relevant app and load your route. Carry the paper map for the same area in a sealed zip-lock bag. At each hut or significant waypoint, cross-check your app position against the paper map to maintain spatial awareness. If cloud closes in, trust compass bearing over GPS track — GPS drift of 15 m in broken terrain is common, while a compass bearing from a known departure point is precise to within 2-3°.

Note the bearing from each hut to the next before starting each day. Write it on a piece of tape stuck to your compass. If you lose all digital navigation, a compass bearing and timed pace count will carry you across a featureless snowfield to within 50 m of a hut.

## Exploring Hut Networks Before You Go

Digital tools are at their strongest in the planning phase, where battery and connectivity are not constraints. [Open the map](/map) to examine the hut network, identify the exposed ridges and glacier crossings between huts, and understand which sections require the most careful navigation. Cross-referencing the map with a paper topo sheet before departure is the simplest and most effective way to combine both tools — you plan digitally and navigate with whichever layer the conditions demand on the day.
