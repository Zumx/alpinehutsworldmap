Cusco, at 3,400 metres above sea level in Peru's Sacred Valley of the Inca, is the gateway to some of the most compelling mountain trekking in South America. The Cordillera Vilcanota and the eastern Andes around Cusco include peaks above 6,000 metres — Ausangate tops out at 6,384 metres and is the fifth-highest mountain in Peru — and offer multi-day routes through high-altitude terrain that passes glaciers, turquoise-coloured lakes, remote Q'ero indigenous communities, and pre-Inca and Inca archaeological sites. The Salkantay route to Machu Picchu is the most popular alternative to the Inca Trail, crossing the Salkantay Pass at 4,630 metres before descending through cloud forest to the Aguas Calientes railhead. The Ausangate circuit is a more demanding four-to-six-day route that circumnavigates the Ausangate massif through remote altiplano terrain. The Lares route approaches Machu Picchu from the north through the traditional weaving communities of the Lares valley. Infrastructure along all three routes varies from purpose-built eco-lodge camps to basic stone shelters.

## 1. Salkantay Cabins Machu Picchu, Soraypampa

Salkantay Cabins at 3,900 metres in Soraypampa — the large plain below the Salkantay glacier — are the flagship facility of the commercial Salkantay lodge-to-lodge operation. The dome-shaped geodesic sleeping pods on elevated platforms, introduced around 2010, have become one of the most photographed mountain accommodation images in South America. Each sleeping dome sleeps two people in heated comfort with direct views of Salkantay's ice face. Hot meals are provided in a central lodge building. The commercial operation has been credited with reducing both litter and wild camping pressure on the Salkantay corridor.

## 2. Andean Huts, Salkantay valley

Andean Huts is a network of three camps along the Salkantay route operated by a Cusco-based trekking company, providing a mid-range option between the luxury lodge-to-lodge experience and traditional camping. Huts at Soraypampa, Wayra (4,300 m), and Lucmabamba (2,850 m) provide dormitory accommodation, hot food, and gear drying facilities. The Wayra camp near the Salkantay Pass is the highest in the network and most exposed to the extreme daily temperature variations — from near freezing at night to 20°C in midday sun — typical of high Andean terrain.

## 3. Llactapata Lodge, Machu Picchu viewpoint

Llactapata Lodge sits at around 2,750 metres on the ridge above the Inca site of Llactapata, with a direct view across the Aobamba valley to the Machu Picchu citadel on its distant ridge. The lodge serves as the final overnight on the Salkantay route before the descent to Aguas Calientes and is one of the most scenically positioned accommodation options on any route to Machu Picchu. The Llactapata site itself — a rediscovered Inca complex visible to the northwest of the main Machu Picchu complex — adds archaeological interest to the mountain setting.

## 4. Yanacancha, Ausangate circuit

Yanacancha is a small puna settlement at around 4,300 metres on the Ausangate circuit, where Q'ero herder families maintain basic facilities for trekking groups during the April-November dry season. Accommodation is in stone dormitories with alpaca-wool blankets provided by the community. The Yanacancha area lies below Nevado Ausangate's western face and the glacial lagoons in the cirques above are coloured electric blue, green, and turquoise by glacial flour. The community-based tourism model here distributes trekking income directly to the indigenous Q'ero, who consider Ausangate an Apu (sacred mountain spirit).

## 5. Refugio Contrahierbas, Cordillera Blanca

Refugio Contrahierbas operates at around 4,400 metres near Nevado Contrahierbas (6,036 m) in the Cordillera Blanca — Peru's highest range, located 300 kilometres north of Cusco near Huaraz. While outside the immediate Cusco region, it is included here as a representative of the Blanca's refugio network. The hut sleeps 20 and serves as a base for ascents of Contrahierbas via the east glacier. The Cordillera Blanca contains more than 200 named summits above 5,000 metres and is the most densely glaciated tropical mountain range on earth.

## 6. Refugio Llaca, Churup approach

Refugio Llaca at around 4,600 metres near Laguna Llaca in the Cordillera Blanca (also near Huaraz) serves acclimatisation walkers and climbers approaching the northwest face of Nevado Ranrapalca (6,162 m). The refugio is operated seasonally, from May to October, by a Huaraz-based climbing club. Its position at the head of the Llaca valley, with views back over the Callejon de Huaylas, illustrates the extreme altitudinal compression of Peruvian mountain terrain — from 3,000-metre valley floor to 6,000-metre glaciated summit in under 15 kilometres.

## 7. Hiancarpata, Ausangate circuit

Hiancarpata at approximately 4,700 metres is a high-camp settlement on the northern arc of the Ausangate circuit, near the Palomani Pass. Accommodation is in basic stone-walled corrals with corrugated iron roofs — the same structures used by alpaca herders — converted to sleeping spaces for trekkers. The surrounding terrain includes the Sibinacocha lake, one of the highest navigable lakes in South America at 4,860 metres. Altitude affects most walkers at Hiancarpata, and the pace of life adjusts accordingly: afternoons are spent resting, and acclimatisation in Cusco for three or more days before the Ausangate circuit is strongly recommended.

## 8. Refugio de Viñak, Vilcabamba range

Refugio de Viñak operates in the Vilcabamba range west of Cusco, one of the less-trekked highland areas of southern Peru. The refuge sits at around 3,600 metres in valley terrain that combines subtropical vegetation on lower slopes with puna grassland above. The Vilcabamba range was the last refuge of the Inca royal court after the Spanish conquest of 1533; the ruins of Vitcos and Vilcabamba La Nueva are accessible from this zone. The refugio caters primarily to archaeology-focused trekking groups rather than high-mountain climbers.

## 9. Sayllapata, Lares route

Sayllapata village at around 3,800 metres on the Lares trekking route is a staging point where community-run accommodation provides overnight facilities for walkers approaching Machu Picchu from the north. The Lares route passes through indigenous Quechua-speaking weaving communities where traditional textile techniques — using natural dyes and hand looms — have been maintained for generations. The overnight in Sayllapata involves a home-stay style accommodation in a family compound, with meals prepared from local quinoa, chuño (freeze-dried potato), and lamb.

## 10. Quesoyuni, Ausangate circuit southern sector

Quesoyuni is a puna camp at around 4,600 metres on the southern section of the Ausangate circuit, below the Jampa Pass. Accommodation is in basic tent frames or stone shelters, with communal cooking. The approach from Tinki village takes six to seven hours of sustained high-altitude walking across open puna. The landscape here is among the most austere in all of Andean Peru: wind-swept grassland dotted with viscacha (a rabbit-like rodent) colonies, ichu grass, and occasional flamingos on the high lakes. No trees exist at this elevation; all fuel is dried alpaca dung.
