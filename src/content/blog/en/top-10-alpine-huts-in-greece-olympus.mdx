Greece is not typically associated with an alpine hut tradition, but the country contains a remarkable spread of mountain ranges — from the Rhodope massif in the north to the Taygetos in the Peloponnese — and a network of refuges maintained by a patchwork of local mountaineering clubs (EOS — Ellinikos Oreivatikos Syndesmos sections) and the national federation. Mount Olympus, rising to 2,917 metres at the Mytikas summit and visible from Thessaloniki on clear winter days, is the centrepiece, but ranges such as the Pindos, Kissavos (Ossa), and the Agrafa massif in central Greece offer equally rewarding terrain for those willing to look beyond the most famous peak. The refuges described below are identified by their Greek names as maintained in the local club records.

## 1. Γιώσος Αποστολίδης (Apostolidis Refuge), Mount Olympus

The Apostolidis refuge at approximately 2,760 metres on the plateau of Mount Olympus is the highest staffed shelter on the mountain and the standard overnight base for ascents of Mytikas (2,917 metres) via the Kakoskala couloir. Named after Giosos Apostolidis, a pioneer of Olympus mountaineering in the early twentieth century, the refuge is operated by the EOS Thessaloniki and sleeps around 90 guests in summer. The approach from the Prionia trailhead in the lower forest zone takes around four to five hours.

## 2. Καταφύγιο ΕΟΣ Αγρινίου (EOS Agrinio Refuge), Agrapha

The EOS Agrinio refuge sits in the Agrafa massif of central Greece, a deeply cut range of ridges and gorges between the Thessaly plain and the Epirus uplands that was famous during the Ottoman period for being one of the last areas of Greece to resist Ottoman control. The Agrafa peaks reach around 2,100 metres, and the terrain is characterised by dense fir forest, deep ravines, and isolated villages accessible only by mountain road. The refuge is a modest facility providing a base for ridge walks and winter ski touring.

## 3. Καταφύγιο Βαγγέλης Τσάκος (Tsakos Refuge), Pindos

The Tsakos refuge in the Pindos range above Ioannina provides an overnight base in the area that was a critical corridor of the Vikos–Aoös National Park and the historic Zagori region. The Pindos is the spine of mainland Greece, running from the Albanian border south to central Greece, and contains several peaks above 2,400 metres. The refuge is named after Vaggelis Tsakos, a local mountaineer, and is operated by an Ioannina-based club section.

## 4. Καταφύγιο Πάνα (Panas Refuge), Thessaly

The Panas refuge serves the mountainous terrain in the Thessaly borderlands, in the transition zone between the high Pindos and the lower ranges descending toward the Thessaly plain. The routes in this area cross traditional pastoral landscape — summer upland pastures used for centuries by Vlach and Sarakatsani transhumant herders — and the trails follow the same corridors these communities have used for generations.

## 5. Καταφύγιο Κισσάβου (Kissavos Refuge), Mount Kissavos

The Kissavos refuge on Mount Kissavos (ancient Ossa, 1,978 metres) east of Larissa occupies the only notable summit on the eastern side of the Thessaly plain. Olympus lies to the north and the Vale of Tempi — the gorge of the Peneios river — separates the two massifs. The Kissavos is administered by the EOS Larissa and serves as a base for the summit of Profitis Ilias at the top of the range. The lower flanks of Kissavos are heavily forested in chestnut and beech.

## 6. Καταφύγιο Γομάρας (Gomaras Refuge), Pieria Mountains

The Gomaras refuge in the Pieria Mountains south of Katerini sits at the foot of the range that forms the western backstop of the Thermaic Gulf between Olympus and the Macedonian plain. The Pieria reach around 2,190 metres at their high point and are covered in dense mixed forest in their lower sections. The refuge provides a base for ridge walks in this quiet range and sees a fraction of the traffic that Olympus receives, despite being equally accessible from Thessaloniki.

## 7. Ορειβατικό Καταφύγιο Αγράφων (Agrafa Mountain Refuge), Agrafa

This refuge in the Agrafa massif of Evrytania provides shelter for walkers on the long-distance E4 European path, which crosses central Greece through this region. The Agrafa interior — the name means "unwritten" in Greek, referring to its absence from Ottoman administrative records — remains one of the wildest and most remote areas in mainland Greece, and the refuge is an important waypoint for parties crossing the range over several days.

## 8. ΕΟΣ Οίτης (EOS Oiti Refuge), Mount Oiti

The EOS Oiti refuge serves Mount Oiti (2,152 metres) in central Greece, the mountain associated in Greek mythology with the death of Heracles. Oiti is now a national park and the refuge provides overnight accommodation for hikers traversing the summit plateau. The upper zones of the mountain support an unusual mix of fir forest and open moorland, and the views from the summit in clear conditions extend to the Gulf of Corinth in the south.

## 9. Καταφύγιο ΣΕΟ Χορτιάτη (SEO Chortiatis Refuge), Chortiatis

The SEO (Syllogos Ellinon Oreivaton — Greek Mountaineers Association) refuge on Mount Chortiatis above Thessaloniki at approximately 1,200 metres is one of the most accessible mountain shelters in Greece, used by residents of the city for weekend hiking. Chortiatis (1,201 metres) is a modest summit by alpine standards, but its position directly above Greece's second city gives it disproportionate cultural significance and the trails are well maintained throughout the year.

## 10. ΕΟΣ Αλμυρού (EOS Almyros Refuge), Othris

The EOS Almyros refuge serves Mount Othris (1,726 metres) in southern Thessaly, a long, massif stretching between the Pagasetic Gulf and the Sperchios river valley. Othris is mythologically associated with the Titans and geologically with some of the oldest ultramafic rocks in Greece — ophiolite sequences that give parts of the massif an unusual rusty-red colour. The EOS Almyros section manages the refuge and organises regular hiking programmes in the area.

## Discovering Greek Mountain Refuges

The Greek mountain refuge network is looser and more variable in quality than the SAC or OeAV systems of central Europe, and facilities range from well-maintained clubs huts with full catering to simple, unstaffed stone shelters. Opening periods outside the summer months are less predictable, and it is always advisable to contact the operating club section before a visit.
