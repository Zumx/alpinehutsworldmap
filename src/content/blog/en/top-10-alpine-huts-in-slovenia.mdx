Slovenia's mountain hut network is managed by the Planinska zveza Slovenije
(PZS — Slovenian Alpine Association) and its affiliated local alpine clubs.
Despite the country's small size, the Julian Alps and Kamnik–Savinja Alps
hold a dense and well-maintained network of planinskie koče (mountain huts)
that serve everything from the national summit, Triglav (2864 m), to
wilderness ridges in the Savinja valley that few non-Slovenian visitors know
exist. The PZS reciprocity agreement honours UIAA member club cards (CAI, DAV,
SAC, ÖAV) for discounted rates. All ten huts are on the [map](/map).

## 1. Triglavski Dom na Kredarici, 2515 m — Triglav, Julian Alps

The main high camp on Triglav — Slovenia's national mountain and one of the
most symbolically important peaks in the eastern Alps — Triglavski Dom na
Kredarici sits at 2515 m on the south ridge, about 2–3 hours below the summit
(2864 m). The hut is managed by PZS and holds around 60 people in dormitory
accommodation. The approach from Pokljuka plateau or from the Vrata valley
is long — 5–7 hours — and the upper section involves via-ferrata-style aided
climbing. Reached from multiple directions; the north face Bamberg route
is for experienced alpinists only. Book well ahead for July and August; Triglav
attracts tens of thousands of visitors annually.

## 2. Aljažev Dom v Vratih, 1015 m — Vrata Valley, Julian Alps

At the floor of the Vrata valley below the dramatic north face of Triglav,
Aljažev Dom is the base camp hut for the north face routes and the start of
the most popular approach to Triglavski Dom. At 1015 m it is accessible by
vehicle from Mojstrana in summer, which makes it a practical provisioning and
acclimatization stop before pushing higher. Capacity around 80. Named for
Jakob Aljaž, the Borovška priest who purchased and preserved the Triglav summit
tower in 1895.

## 3. Planinski Dom na Komni, 1520 m — Komna Plateau, Julian Alps

On the Komna plateau above Bohinj, a high-elevation karst plateau with views
toward the Crna Prst and the main Triglav range. Reached from Bohinj Bistrica
via the Savica waterfall trail in about 3 hours. The plateau terrain is unusual
for the Julian Alps — wide, open, with sparse vegetation and deep karst
depressions. Capacity around 80, PZS-affiliated, full meals in season.
The Komna is a waypoint on cross-plateau routes west toward Most na Soči.

## 4. Vodnikov Dom, 1817 m — Velo Polje, Julian Alps

On the Velo Polje meadow plateau east of the Triglav main massif, Vodnikov Dom
is a key mid-point on routes traversing between the Vrata valley approaches
and the Pokljuka plateau. Capacity around 50. The hut sits at the junction of
several marked routes and is a natural lunch stop or overnight on multi-day
Julian Alps circuits. Named for Valentin Vodnik, one of the first Slovenian
poets and the first person to describe the Triglav ascent in print.

## 5. Kocbekov Dom na Korošici, 1808 m — Kamnik–Savinja Alps

In the Kamnik–Savinja Alps east of the Julian range, the Kocbekov Dom sits on
the Korošica saddle at 1808 m with views across the limestone towers of the
Kamniske Alpe. Reached from Logarska Dolina (Logar Valley) in about 3 hours.
The Kamnik–Savinja Alps are less crowded than the Triglav area and offer
exceptional ridge walking on the Kamniška Bistrica and Savinja watershed
ridges. PZS-affiliated, capacity around 60, with a reputation for good
Slovenian mountain cooking.

## 6. Frischaufov Dom na Okrešlju, 1396 m — Kamnik–Savinja Alps

In the Logarska Dolina — one of the most beautiful glacial valleys in the
eastern Alps — Frischaufov Dom is the first overnight on routes toward
Ojstrica (2350 m) and the Kamnik–Savinja high route. Named for Czech
geographer Franz Ignaz Frischauf who mapped the Kamnik Alps in the 1880s.
Capacity around 50, full meals from July to September, with a partial service
in June and autumn.

## 7. Dom Planincev v Tamarju, 1108 m — Tamar Valley, Julian Alps

At the head of the Tamar valley in the western Julian Alps above Kranjska Gora,
this hut sits below the Jalovec (2645 m) — one of the finest mountains in
Slovenia for alpinists. The valley is accessible by vehicle in summer, and the
hut serves as base camp for Jalovec routes and approach for the Vrata–Tamar
circuit. Capacity around 80. The Planica ski jumping valley is adjacent — an
unusual combination of high alpinism and international ski sport infrastructure.

## 8. Roblekov Dom, 1657 m — Mangartsko Sedlo, Soča region

Near the Mangart saddle (2055 m) in the far western Julian Alps on the
Italian–Slovenian border, Roblekov Dom serves the approach to Mangart (2679 m)
— one of the finest summit panoramas in the range, taking in the Soča valley,
the Dolomites, and the Carnic Alps. The Mangart road from Bovec is the
highest paved road in Slovenia. Hut capacity around 40.

## 9. Tržaška Koča na Razorju, 1670 m — Razorju, Julian Alps

On a saddle between the Razor (2601 m) and the Prisojnik (2547 m) in the
central Julian Alps above Trenta, the Tržaška Koča is managed by the Trieste
section of the CAI (the hut predates the current political borders). The
approach from the Trenta valley passes through the Soča river source area.
Capacity around 60. This is quieter terrain than the main Triglav routes and
one of the more scenic overnight positions in the range.

## 10. Dom Planika pod Triglavom, 2401 m — Triglav High Route

Just below the summit band of Triglav at 2401 m, Dom Planika serves as the
highest comfortable overnight on the mountain — between Triglavski Dom na
Kredarici and the summit itself. Capacity around 40, PZS-managed. The view
from the terrace at dawn — across the entire length of the Julian Alps to the
northeast and southwest — is cited by most people who have seen it as one of
the finest mountain views in central Europe.

## The PZS network

PZS membership provides discounts at affiliated huts across Slovenia, and the
reciprocity network with UIAA members means European alpine club cards are
widely accepted. Slovenian mountain culture takes the mountains seriously —
trail markings are excellent, hut guardians are knowledgeable, and the
tradition of multi-day tours through the Julian Alps is well-established.

## Triglav and Slovenian national identity

Triglav (2864 m) is not just the highest peak in Slovenia — it is the central
symbol of Slovenian national identity, appearing on the national coat of arms
and the flag. The three-headed peak (tri-glav means "three heads") has been
a place of cultural significance since before recorded Slovenian history. The
tradition of summiting Triglav at least once is deeply embedded in Slovenian
culture: every Slovenian who has grown up in the mountains has either done
it or aspires to.

This cultural weight translates into specific trail and hut realities. In
July and August, Triglav can have 500+ ascents per day on the standard routes.
The Triglavski Dom na Kredarici (2515 m) books out weeks in advance. The summit
itself can have queues at the via-ferrata sections on popular weekends. None
of this diminishes the mountain's quality — the summit panorama across the
Julian Alps and into the Dolomites, Karawanken, and Kamnik ranges is
extraordinary — but timing a Triglav visit for a mid-week window in late
June or early September is strongly advised.

## The Soča Valley connection

The western edge of the Julian Alps drops into the Soča valley — one of the
most beautiful river corridors in Europe, with emerald-green water flowing
through limestone gorges. The Soča was the front line of fighting in World
War I (the Isonzo Front), and the surrounding landscape is saturated with
wartime history. Several huts in the western Julian Alps, including the Roblekov
Dom near Mangart, serve routes that cross this historic terrain.

The Soča valley town of Bovec is the practical base for the western Julian
Alps approach to Mangart (2679 m) and the Kanin plateau. The road from Bovec
to the Mangart saddle (2055 m) is one of the highest paved roads in Slovenia
and provides direct access to the high hut terrain in the summer season.
