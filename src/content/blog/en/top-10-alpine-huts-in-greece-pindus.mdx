Most travellers picture Greece as islands and ruins, and miss the fact that it is one of the most mountainous countries in Europe. The Pindus range — the rocky spine that runs north to south through the mainland — culminates in the wild massifs of Epirus, where the Tymfi (Gamila) plateau, the Astraka towers and the immense Vikos gorge form some of the most dramatic high country in the Balkans. Greek refuges are called katafygia, run by the local branches of the Hellenic mountaineering federation (EOS/SEO), and around Gamila they are the gateway to a landscape of limestone, alpine lakes and the stone villages of the Zagori. These ten cover the northern Pindus and reach across the Greek mountains.

## The Tymfi massif and the Zagori

The heart of the region is the Tymfi (also called Gamila) massif, a vast limestone plateau rising above the 46 stone-built Zagorochoria villages — Monodendri, Mikro Papigko, Megalo Papigko, Tsepelovo — linked by old mule paths and arched stone bridges. Above them yawns the Vikos gorge, one of the deepest canyons in the world relative to its width, plunging some 900 metres from the rim. The high plateau holds the Drakolimni, the Dragon Lake, a tarn at around 2,050 metres famous for its alpine newts and its reflection of the Gamila summits. This is karst country: water vanishes underground, the rock is sharp and pale, and the weather on the plateau turns fast.

## 1. Astraka refuge, Tymfi plateau

The single most important hut in the northern Pindus. The Astraka katafygio sits at 1,950 metres on a saddle between the Astraka peak and Lapatos, reached by a steep three-hour climb from Mikro Papigko. Staffed in season, it is the base for the Drakolimni, the ascent of Gamila (2,497 m), and the high traverses of the plateau.

## 2. Drakolimni and the Gamila high camps

From Astraka, a roughly two-hour walk leads to the Drakolimni at the plateau's edge, with the Gamila ridge behind. Walkers often base at Astraka and day-trip here; experienced parties bivouac on the plateau to catch the dawn light on the lake and the great drop toward the Aoos gorge beyond.

## 3. EOS refuge, Mount Smolikas

Smolikas, at 2,637 metres, is the second-highest mountain in Greece and the giant of the northern Pindus. Its own Drakolimni — a second Dragon Lake — sits high on the flank, and a refuge and shepherd country on the approach make it a classic two-day objective from the villages around Konitsa.

## 4. Refuge on Mount Mavrovouni and the Aoos

Between Tymfi and Smolikas runs the Aoos gorge, a deep wild valley with the Stomiou monastery clinging above the river. Shelters and refuges on this route link the Konitsa country to the Tymfi plateau on one of the great multi-day Pindus crossings.

## 5. Katafygio on Mount Mitsikeli

Above Ioannina and its lake, Mitsikeli is the long ridge that walkers cross on the famous Epirus trail toward the Zagori. Its refuges and shelters make a gentler introduction to the region, with the city, the lake and the island monasteries spread out below.

## 6. EOS refuge, Mount Olympus approaches

East of the Pindus proper, Greece's highest mountain Olympus (Mytikas, 2,917 m) has the country's most developed refuge network. The Spilios Agapitos refuge at 2,100 metres is the busiest hut in Greece, the classic overnight on the way to the summit of the home of the gods.

## 7. Giosos Apostolidis refuge, Olympus plateau

Higher on Olympus, the Giosos Apostolidis refuge at around 2,700 metres sits on the Plateau of the Muses beneath the main summits. It is the launch point for the scrambly final ridges to Mytikas and Stefani, deep in the heart of the massif.

## 8. Katafygio on Mount Kissavos (Ossa)

Across the Thessalian plain from Olympus rises Kissavos, the ancient Mount Ossa, dropping toward the Aegean. Its refuge serves a quieter mountain of beech forest and herb-scented slopes, with the sea visible from the summit ridge.

## 9. Katafygio on Mount Velouchi (Tymfristos)

In the central Pindus above Karpenisi, Velouchi (Tymfristos, 2,315 m) is a winter-sports and walking mountain whose refuge serves the forested Evrytania country, one of the greenest and least-visited corners of mainland Greece.

## 10. Katafygio in the Agrafa mountains

The Agrafa — literally the unwritten lands, so remote they escaped Ottoman tax records — are the wildest, least-developed range in the southern Pindus. Their scattered refuges and shepherd shelters serve walkers seeking genuine solitude in deep gorges and trackless ridges.

## The EOS and SEO system

Greek mountain refuges are operated by branches of the EOS (Hellenic Alpine Club) and the parallel SEO, and the picture is patchier than in the Alps. Some huts, like Astraka and Spilios Agapitos, are reliably staffed and serve meals in season; many others are locked, with keys held by the local branch and arranged by phone in advance. Plan ahead, confirm staffing before you set out, and carry food. The Greek mountaineering season runs roughly June to October, with the high plateaus often snow-covered into early summer.

## Water, heat and the Vikos crossing

The classic walk here is the Vikos gorge itself, descending from Monodendri through the canyon floor to Vikos village, a full day among towering walls with the Voidomatis springs at the lower end. On the plateau, the great hazard is water — the karst leaves few springs, so refuges and the rare cisterns are vital, and you must plan refills carefully. Summer brings real heat at lower altitudes and sudden thunderstorms on the tops. Spring and autumn are gentler; autumn paints the beech and oak forests of the Zagori in extraordinary colour.
