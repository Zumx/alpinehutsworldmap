The Altai is the place where four countries lean against one another — Russia, Kazakhstan, Mongolia and China — and where the Siberian taiga finally gives way to glaciers. At its centre stands Belukha, at 4,506 metres the highest peak in Siberia and a mountain held sacred across the region. Unlike the Alps, the Altai has no dense web of staffed Hütten with hot showers and three-course dinners. What it has instead is a thinner, wilder network of base camps, ranger cordons, climbers' shelters and tourist bases strung along the approach valleys. These ten are the ones a serious visitor structures a Belukha trip around.

## The Altai is not the Alps

Set your expectations before you set foot here. The standard Belukha approach from the Russian side begins at the village of Tyungur on the Katun River and runs for two long days up the Akkem valley to a glacial lake beneath the north face. There are no lifts, no road-served refuges at altitude, and the weather swings from Siberian heat to snow within hours. Most overnighting is in a mix of fixed wooden bases, ranger stations known as kordons, and high climbers' bivouacs. A border-zone permit (the pogranzona) is required for the high valleys near the Kazakh frontier, and it must be arranged in advance.

## 1. Akkem base camp, beneath the north face

The hub of the entire region. Sitting at roughly 2,050 metres beside Akkem Lake, the cluster of cabins, a rescue post and a meteorological station forms the natural staging point for the north wall of Belukha. From here the mountain's icy face fills the head of the valley, and side trips run to the Seven Lakes valley and the chapel built in memory of climbers lost on the peak.

## 2. Tyungur, the trailhead bases

Tyungur itself is the gateway. The tourist bases here — riverside camps offering cabins, banya (the Russian sauna), and horse hire — are where expeditions weigh packs, hire pack horses, and cross the Katun on the famous suspension bridge before the climb over the Kuzuyak pass into the Akkem.

## 3. Tekelyu and the Akkem valley shelters

The two-day walk up the Akkem is broken by simple shelters and herders' huts along the river. They are basic — wood stove, plank sleeping platforms, little more — but in driving rain on the long valley stage they are worth knowing about, and they sit among larch forest with the white pyramid of Belukha appearing and vanishing ahead.

## 4. Tomsk shelters, Belukha climbing routes

High on the mountain, the climbers' shelters and bivouac sites used for the Tomsk and other technical routes onto Belukha are spartan stone-and-tarp affairs at the snowline. They are for roped parties with glacier experience, acclimatised over several days at the Akkem camp first.

## 5. Kucherla valley and lake camps

The parallel approach. The Kucherla River drains a chain of turquoise lakes, and the camps along it — used by trekkers crossing the Karaturek pass between the Kucherla and Akkem valleys — make a circular route possible rather than an out-and-back slog. Kucherla Lake itself, ringed by cedar forest, is one of the most beautiful overnight spots in the range.

## 6. Seven Lakes valley camps

A short, steep day from Akkem Lake, the Valley of the Seven Lakes holds a string of tarns at around 2,600 metres beneath jagged ridges. Lightweight camps and a ranger presence here serve walkers acclimatising before any attempt on the high peaks, and the sunrise light on the lakes is the postcard image of the Russian Altai.

## 7. Multa Lakes bases

West of Belukha, the Multa Lakes form their own trekking district with rustic bases and ranger cordons. Lower and gentler than the Akkem, the Multa region suits a first Altai trip — long lake walks, cedar forest, and overnight cabins without the glacier commitment of the main massif.

## 8. Shavla Lakes camps

Further east in the North Chuya range, the Shavla Lakes sit beneath the spires nicknamed Dream, Beauty and Fairy Tale. The seasonal camps and herders' huts here serve one of the classic Altai treks, and the range offers serious alpine routes for parties not heading to Belukha.

## 9. Aktru base camp, North Chuya

The Aktru valley is the Altai's other great mountaineering centre, a working alplager (climbing camp) at around 2,150 metres with cabins, a long Soviet-era pedigree, and direct access to glaciers and training peaks. For climbers wanting structured instruction and graded ascents rather than a single hard objective, Aktru is the place.

## 10. Chibit and the Chuya approaches

The bases around Chibit on the Chuysky Trakt — the legendary highway running toward Mongolia — open the eastern Altai. They serve the Shavla trek and the North Chuya peaks, and they are the last comfortable beds before the high valleys near the Mongolian border.

## Permits, seasons and getting there

The Altai climbing and trekking season is short: roughly mid-July to late August, when the high passes are clear and the rivers, though still cold, are crossable. Access starts with a long road journey from Gorno-Altaysk, the regional capital, down the Chuysky Trakt and then by rough track to Tyungur or Aktru. The border-zone permit for the frontier valleys takes days to process, so arrange it before arrival. Pack horses, hired in Tyungur, transform the Akkem approach. Treat all river water, carry a proper sleeping bag for sub-zero nights even in summer, and respect the local reverence for Belukha, which Altai people regard as a holy mountain.
