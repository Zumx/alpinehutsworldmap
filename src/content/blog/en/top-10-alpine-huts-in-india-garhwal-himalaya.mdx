The Garhwal Himalaya, in the northern Indian state of Uttarakhand, is a landscape where mountaineering and pilgrimage have always been intertwined. This is the country of Nanda Devi, at 7,816 metres the highest mountain entirely within India and a peak so sacred it gives its name to the goddess of the region. Around it cluster the sources of the Ganges, the meadows of the Valley of Flowers, and trails walked for centuries by sadhus and shepherds. The Indian trekking model is built less on staffed alpine refuges than on Forest Rest Houses, GMVN tourist lodges, ashram dharamshalas and seasonal tent camps. These ten cover the classic Garhwal routes and the way the mountain country is sheltered.

## A different shelter tradition

Forget the catered European hut. In Garhwal, overnighting on a trek means a mix of structures: government-run GMVN (Garhwal Mandal Vikas Nigam) tourist rest houses in the larger villages, basic Forest Department rest houses inside the protected areas, dharamshalas and ashrams along the pilgrim routes, and the seasonal tented camps that trekking operators pitch at high meadows. Permits matter enormously here — the Nanda Devi Sanctuary itself is closed to protect its fragile core, while the Valley of Flowers and other parks require entry permits and, for foreigners, sometimes restricted-area documentation. Altitude is the constant: many camps sit above 3,500 metres, and the route to high passes crosses 4,000 and even 5,000 metres.

## 1. Ghangaria, gateway to the Valley of Flowers

The small seasonal settlement of Ghangaria at around 3,050 metres is the hub for two of Garhwal's greatest objectives: the Valley of Flowers National Park, a UNESCO meadow blazing with alpine blooms in monsoon, and the Sikh pilgrimage lake of Hemkund Sahib at 4,300 metres. GMVN lodges, gurdwara accommodation and guesthouses fill it each summer.

## 2. Govindghat and the Bhyundar valley

The road-head of Govindghat on the Alaknanda river is where the trek to Ghangaria begins. Its gurdwara and lodges shelter the thousands of pilgrims and trekkers heading up the Bhyundar (Lakshman Ganga) valley each season toward the flowers and the holy lake.

## 3. Auli and Joshimath

Joshimath, a major town and one of the four cardinal monastic seats founded by Adi Shankaracharya, is the staging post for the entire Nanda Devi region. Above it, the ski-and-meadow resort of Auli at around 2,900 metres offers GMVN lodges and grandstand views of Nanda Devi and the Hathi-Ghoda peaks.

## 4. Lata and the Nanda Devi outer sanctuary

The village of Lata is the trailhead for the trek into the buffer zone of the Nanda Devi Biosphere Reserve, toward the high meadow of Lata Kharak and Dharansi. Forest rest houses and village homestays serve the limited number of trekkers permitted into this strictly protected region.

## 5. Wan and the Roopkund trail

The hamlet of Wan, beyond Lohajung, is the gateway to the Roopkund trek — the route to the mysterious skeleton lake at 5,029 metres, ringed by the meadows of Ali and Bedni Bugyal. Forest rest houses and seasonal camps shelter trekkers on one of the Himalaya's most famous walks.

## 6. Bedni Bugyal high camps

The vast alpine meadow of Bedni Bugyal at around 3,350 metres, with the peaks of Trishul and Nanda Ghunti towering above, is one of the great campsites of the Indian Himalaya. Seasonal tented camps here serve the Roopkund and Ali Bugyal circuits.

## 7. Gangotri and Bhojbasa

Gangotri, at 3,100 metres, is one of the holiest sources of the Ganges and a major Char Dham pilgrimage temple. From here the trail runs up to Bhojbasa, where an ashram and rest house at around 3,800 metres shelter trekkers approaching the Gaumukh glacier snout and the Tapovan meadow beneath Shivling.

## 8. Tapovan, beneath Shivling and Meru

The high meadow of Tapovan at around 4,460 metres, beyond the Gaumukh source, sits directly beneath the soaring spire of Shivling (6,543 m). Sadhu huts and seasonal camps here serve mountaineers and pilgrims at one of the most spectacular base sites in the range.

## 9. Kedarnath and the Vasuki Tal trail

Kedarnath, at 3,580 metres, is among the most revered Himalayan shrines, reached on a long pilgrim climb. Beyond it, trails lead to the high lake of Vasuki Tal and the Chorabari glacier, with rest houses and camps serving the more adventurous pilgrims and trekkers.

## 10. Har Ki Dun valley huts

In the western Garhwal near the Swargarohini peaks, the Har Ki Dun (Valley of the Gods) is a cradle-shaped glacial valley with the ancient stone villages of Osla and Sankri at its mouth. Forest rest houses and GMVN huts shelter trekkers on this gentle, culturally rich route.

## Permits, seasons and altitude

Trekking in Garhwal demands respect for both bureaucracy and altitude. Many areas lie within national parks or the Nanda Devi Biosphere Reserve and require permits arranged through the Forest Department or registered operators; the inner Nanda Devi Sanctuary remains closed. The main seasons are spring (April to June) and post-monsoon autumn (September to November); the monsoon, roughly July and August, brings the Valley of Flowers to peak bloom but also landslides and washed-out roads. Acclimatise carefully — altitude sickness is a real risk above 3,000 metres — and never push to a high pass without proper acclimatisation days.

## Pilgrimage and mountain together

What gives Garhwal its character is the inseparability of the sacred and the alpine. The same trails carry mountaineers toward glaciers and pilgrims toward shrines; the same rest houses shelter both. A trek to Tapovan beneath Shivling or to Hemkund Sahib is as much a cultural journey as a physical one, threading temple towns, riverside gurdwaras and meadows grazed by sheep. Treat the shrines with respect, follow local customs at the dharamshalas, and the mountains reward you with some of the most storied high country on earth.
