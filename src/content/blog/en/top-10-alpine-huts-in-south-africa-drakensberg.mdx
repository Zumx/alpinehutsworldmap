The Drakensberg (Dragon's Mountains) form the eastern escarpment of southern
Africa's high interior plateau, rising abruptly from the KwaZulu-Natal
foothills in a series of cliffs and buttresses that culminate on the Lesotho
plateau above. The highest peak accessible from the South African side is
Mafadi (3450 m) on the South Africa-Lesotho border; the more famous Sentinel
Peak (3165 m) with its iconic chain ladder access provides the most visited
route above the escarpment edge. The "hut" infrastructure here is a combination
of SANParks-managed camp sites, MCSA (Mountain Club of South Africa) huts, and
basic overnight structures in the national park.

## 1. Sentinel Peak Car Park Huts, 2640 m — Northern Drakensberg, Royal Natal

The starting point for the Chain Ladder route to the top of the Drakensberg
escarpment, the Sentinel car park at 2640 m has a basic overnight facility
maintained by the park authorities. The Chain Ladder (a steel ladder bolted
to the cliff) provides the direct access to the escarpment top. From the
top of the ladder, the walk to the Mont-aux-Sources plateau and the Tugela
Falls (second-highest waterfall in the world at 948 m) takes 3 hours return.

## 2. Witsieshoek Mountain Lodge, 2200 m — Phuthaditjhaba, Free State

Below the Sentinel and the northern escarpment, the Witsieshoek Mountain
Lodge at 2200 m is the highest accessible overnight facility in South Africa
reachable by tar road. It serves as a base for the Sentinel and Chain Ladder
routes and provides the most comfortable accommodation for escarpment access.
Rooms, camping, and meals service.

## 3. Mafadi Base Camp, 2900 m — High Berg, KwaZulu-Natal

The base camp area for ascents of Mafadi (3450 m) at approximately 2900 m
on the escarpment edge is a designated camping site within the uKhahlamba-
Drakensberg Park (UNESCO World Heritage). No permanent structure; tent camping
required. The route to Mafadi from Injisuthi camp takes 2 days of challenging
walking.

## 4. Injisuthi Hutted Camp, 1580 m — Central Drakensberg, KwaZulu-Natal

The Injisuthi hutted camp at 1580 m in the Central Drakensberg section of
uKhahlamba-Drakensberg Park provides SANParks-standard self-catering chalets
and a campsite at the trailhead for routes to Mafadi, Giant's Castle, and
the high escarpment. Bookable through SANParks reservations.

## 5. Cathedral Peak Hotel, 1400 m — Cathedral Peak area, KwaZulu-Natal

The Cathedral Peak Hotel has served the Drakensberg tourism and mountaineering
community since 1939. At 1400 m below the Cathedral Peak (3004 m) in the
northern Central Drakensberg, it provides accommodation, a guide service,
and the base for Cathedral Peak summit routes (Chain Ladder approach, 5 hours
return) and the surrounding valley walks.

## 6. Mountain Club of South Africa Natal Midlands Section Huts, 1800–2400 m

The MCSA maintains a small network of huts in the Drakensberg, primarily used
by club members undertaking technical climbs on the Amphitheatre (the great
Drakensberg cliff face above the Royal Natal National Park) and the escarpment
buttresses. Access requires MCSA membership.

## 7. Giant's Castle Rest Camp, 1790 m — Giant's Castle, KwaZulu-Natal

In the Giant's Castle Game Reserve section of the uKhahlamba-Drakensberg Park,
the rest camp at 1790 m provides self-catering chalets with access to the
Giant's Castle peak (3315 m) trails. Giant's Castle is also the primary
location for witnessing bearded vultures (lammergeiers) feeding at hides
operated by SANParks.

## 8. Monk's Cowl Camp, 1520 m — Champagne Valley, KwaZulu-Natal

The Monk's Cowl campsite at 1520 m provides access to the Champagne Valley
section of the Drakensberg, including the popular Monk's Cowl peak (3234 m)
summit trail and the Champagne Castle approach (3377 m — third highest in
South Africa). SANParks facility with camping and basic ablutions.

## 9. Sani Pass Base, 2874 m — Lesotho border, KwaZulu-Natal

At the top of the Sani Pass on the Lesotho border at 2874 m, the Sani Mountain
Lodge (private) provides the highest bar in South Africa (by their own claim)
and overnight accommodation on the plateau edge. Access via the 4WD Sani Pass
road. The landscape here transitions from the steep Drakensberg escarpment
to the vast Lesotho plateau.

## 10. Bushman's Nek Hut, 1770 m — Southern Drakensberg, Underberg area

In the southern Drakensberg near the Bushmans Nek Plateau section, a basic
overnight shelter provides a base for the escarpment approaches from the
southern end of the range. The southern Drakensberg is less visited than the
central and northern sections.

## The Drakensberg and San rock art

The uKhahlamba-Drakensberg Park's UNESCO World Heritage designation covers
not only the landscape but the San rock art paintings that are found throughout
the range — over 35,000 individual paintings in approximately 600 sites, the
largest and most significant concentration in southern Africa. The San people
(Bushmen) inhabited the Drakensberg highlands for thousands of years; the
paintings represent both their daily life and their religious and spiritual
practices.

The most accessible rock art sites in the park are in the Giant's Castle
area — where two sites (Main Cave and Bamboo Hollow) can be visited with
a park guide — and in the Cathedral Peak region. The paintings are fragile
and irreplaceable; the park rangers strictly enforce a no-touch rule and the
no-flash-photography requirement.

## Wildlife in the Drakensberg

The Drakensberg is one of the best locations in southern Africa for bearded
vultures (lammergeiers/Gypaetus barbatus), a species that was nearly
extirpated from southern Africa in the 20th century through persecution and
habitat loss. The Giant's Castle bone-feeding hide — a SANParks facility
where carcass bones are provided to attract vultures — is one of the world's
best places to observe this spectacular bird at close range. Booking through
SANParks is required; the hide operates during specific winter months (May
to August) when vultures are most active.

Cape vultures, black eagles, and a range of mountain raptors are regularly
seen over the escarpment. Mammals include eland (the world's largest antelope),
mountain reedbuck, grey rhebok, and the endemic Drakensberg rock jumper (a
passerine bird).

## Practical access

The KwaZulu-Natal Drakensberg is reached from Johannesburg or Durban via the
N3 highway to Mooi River and then secondary roads to the various park entry
points. The main areas (Royal Natal, Monks Cowl, Giants Castle, Injisuthi)
are accessible by 2WD vehicle in dry conditions; some approaches require 4WD.
Accommodation booking through SANParks (sanparks.org) is recommended for
all park facilities well in advance of the peak season (June–August).

## Climbing in the Drakensberg

The Drakensberg's technical climbing is concentrated on the Amphitheatre
wall — the great buttressed cliff face above Royal Natal National Park —
and on individual peaks like Cathedral Peak (3004 m) and Monks Cowl (3234 m).
The Mountain Club of South Africa (MCSA) has documented and graded the
technical routes on these faces since the 1930s; the MCSA Natal Midlands
section publishes a guidebook covering the range.

The rock is predominantly basalt — the caprock of the Lesotho plateau —
above the lower sandstone faces. Basalt climbing is less common in the
wider climbing world, and the Drakensberg routes have a distinctive character.
The grades are in the old South African system (Grade 1–7) rather than the
UIAA or French systems; MCSA hut access is relevant for serious climbing
parties on the Amphitheatre and Cathedral Peak faces.

Summer thunderstorms are the primary climbing hazard: the Drakensberg
plateau generates afternoon convective storms from November through March.
Mountain guides, weather monitoring (South African Weather Service, saws.ac.za),
and strict turnaround rules are standard practice for Drakensberg summit days.
