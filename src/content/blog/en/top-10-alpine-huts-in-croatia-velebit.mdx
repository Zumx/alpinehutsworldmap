Croatia's inland mountain landscape divides into two distinct zones. The Velebit range runs 145 kilometres parallel to the Dalmatian coast, a massive limestone barrier between the continental hinterland and the Adriatic, reaching 1,757 m at Vaganski vrh in southern Velebit and designated in large parts as a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve. The Gorski Kotar highlands north of the Kvarner Gulf are geologically related but characteristically forested — dense stands of silver fir, beech, and Norway spruce covering a plateau dissected by deep river valleys. Both areas have well-established planinarski dom networks. Croatia's mountaineering club tradition dates to 1874 with the founding of the Hrvatsko planinarsko društvo, making it one of the oldest in the region, and the hut network reflects this depth of history. The ten huts below represent the essential overnight stops across Velebit and Gorski Kotar.

## 1. Dom Bijele Stijene, Gorski Kotar

Dom Bijele Stijene is positioned near the spectacular Bijele and Samarske Stijene — two clusters of enormous limestone towers in the heart of the Gorski Kotar, protected as a strict nature reserve. The rock formations rise like cathedral buttresses from the surrounding forest floor, with individual columns reaching 30 m. The dom is a modest stone shelter with around 30 beds, used by hikers circuiting the rock formations on the marked trail system. Access from the town of Delnice takes two to three hours by forest road and trail.

## 2. Planinarski Dom Klek, Gorski Kotar

Planinarski dom Klek is perched on the slopes of Klek (1,181 m), one of the most recognisable mountains in Croatia — a limestone crest with a steep north face that dominates the Ogulin basin and features prominently in Croatian folklore. The dom has been operating for over a century and sleeps around 50 people. The north face of Klek is a traditional training ground for Croatian rock climbers, with routes established since the early twentieth century. The summit walk from the dom takes about 45 minutes on the standard south ridge path.

## 3. Kuća Javorova Kosa, Northern Velebit

Kuća Javorova Kosa is set within the Northern Velebit National Park, established in 1999 and one of the youngest in Croatia. The national park protects a highly dissected karst landscape with the deepest vertical cave in the world — Lukina jama reaches 1,431 m depth. The hut is positioned on the main trail crossing the northern section of the range and serves hikers on the Premužić trail, a 57-kilometre stone-paved path built in the 1930s that runs the entire length of Velebit.

## 4. Planinarska Kuća Rudi Jurić, Central Velebit

Named after a Croatian mountaineer, planinarska kuća Rudi Jurić is a shelter on the central section of Velebit in the Paklenica area. Paklenica National Park on the southern edge of northern Velebit protects two canyon systems — Velika and Mala Paklenica — cut into the limestone massif. The hut serves as a high camp for parties ascending the routes above the canyon walls and for hikers traversing the Velebit crest. The capacity is around 20 people, making early booking essential in August.

## 5. Planinarska Kuća Pljuskara, Central Velebit

Planinarska kuća Pljuskara sits on the Velebit plateau near several dolines and karst depressions that collect snowmelt and rainwater, forming temporary pools in spring. The hut is on a section of the Premužić trail and offers emergency shelter capability throughout the year, with staffed operation from May to October. The central Velebit plateau is a habitat for Eurasian lynx, reintroduced to the area in 1974 in one of Europe's first successful large carnivore recovery programmes.

## 6. Planinarski Dom Hahlić, Southern Velebit

Planinarski dom Hahlić is located on the southern section of the range above the Dalmatian hinterland near Obrovac. Southern Velebit — also known as the Tulove Grede area — is characterised by bare karst ridges, extreme weather exposure, and stunning views down to the Novigradsko More sea inlet. The dom is a solidly built stone shelter with capacity for around 40 people and is the standard overnight stop for the southern section of the Velebit traverse.

## 7. Planinska Kuća Bukovac, Gorski Kotar

Planinska kuća Bukovac is a forest shelter in the Bukovac valley area of Gorski Kotar, positioned in the fir-beech zone at around 900 m. The surrounding forest is part of the buffer zone of the Risnjak National Park, Croatia's oldest established national park (1953). The park takes its name from the lynx (ris) and protects the full forest ecosystem of the Gorski Kotar plateau. The hut is used by parties hiking the Risnjak circuit and for overnight stays on cross-country ski trails in winter.

## 8. Lovačka Kuća Perun, Gorski Kotar

Lovačka kuća Perun is a hunting lodge converted to mountain shelter use in the Gorski Kotar forest zone. Perun is a small settlement area in the forested highlands between Delnice and Čabar. The kuća has basic facilities and is primarily used by hikers on the ridge trails connecting the valleys of the Kupa and Čabranka rivers. The forest here is among the snowiest in Croatia, with annual snowfall sometimes exceeding four metres at higher elevations.

## 9. Planinarska Kuća Ivanova Hiža, Gorski Kotar

Planinarska kuća Ivanova Hiža — "Ivan's Hut" — is a small mountain shelter in the Gorski Kotar near the Bjelolasica massif (1,533 m), the highest point in Gorski Kotar. Bjelolasica hosts a winter sports training centre built for the 1985 Universiade and used by Croatian alpine skiing athletes. The kuća Ivanova Hiža serves hikers on the summer ridge trails and is a quiet, family-friendly base for exploring the beech forests below the summit plateau.

## 10. Picelj, Northern Velebit

Picelj is a shelter and emergency overnight point on the high ridge of Northern Velebit, accessible from the main Velebit traverse trail near the Hajdučki and Rožanski kukovi rock formations. These formations — clusters of enormous dolomite and limestone towers reaching 30 to 40 m above the surrounding plateau — are among the most extraordinary rock landscapes in Croatia and are strictly protected within the national park. The shelter at Picelj serves hikers exploring this section of the range, and the summit views from the adjacent ridgeline extend from the Kvarner islands to the Dalmatian coast.
