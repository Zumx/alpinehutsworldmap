The Bieszczady are not high — the loftiest summit, Tarnica, reaches only 1,346 metres — but they feel like the edge of something. This far southeast corner of Poland, pressed against the borders of Slovakia and Ukraine, is the emptiest mountain country in the Carpathians, a land of rounded ridges crowned by the poloniny, the open high-altitude meadows that give the range its singular character. Below the treeline grows some of the wildest beech forest in Europe, home to wolves, bears, lynx and bison. Polish mountain shelters are called schroniska, run largely by the PTTK tourist society, and in the Bieszczady they are sparse, hard-won and unforgettable. These ten cover the range and reach into Poland's other great hut districts.

## The poloniny and the emptiness

What sets the Bieszczady apart is the contrast between dense, almost primeval forest and the sudden grassy openness of the high ridges. The Połonina Wetlińska and Połonina Caryńska are the two most famous of these meadows, long whaleback ridges of windblown grass and dwarf flora where the views run unbroken to Ukraine. The whole eastern end of the range belongs to the Bieszczady National Park, where strict protection and a long depopulation after the upheavals of the 1940s have left valleys all but empty of people. Walking here means long days between shelters, real weather exposure on the open poloniny, and the genuine possibility of seeing a wolf track in the morning mud.

## 1. Chatka Puchatka, Połonina Wetlińska

The most iconic shelter in the range, perched at 1,228 metres on the crest of the Połonina Wetlińska. Tiny, weather-battered and beloved, the so-called Pooh Hut is a basic bivouac with limited beds and a famous wind-blasted terrace — the classic place to watch sunset over the Carpathian ridges.

## 2. Schronisko PTTK Pod Małą Rawką

A traditional PTTK shelter below Mała Rawka in the western Bieszczady, set in the beech forest near Wetlina. It is a comfortable, full-service base for the Wetlińska and Caryńska ridges and a good first night for anyone learning the range.

## 3. Bacówka PTTK pod Małą Rawką

The bacówka is a distinctly Polish hut type — modelled on a shepherd's hut, small and simple, designed by the PTTK for the lower Carpathian ranges. This one sits at a saddle on the way to the high poloniny and embodies the relaxed, woodsmoke-scented character of Bieszczady walking.

## 4. Studencka Chata Huczwice

A student-run shelter near Huczwice in the Bieszczady foothills, part of the long Polish tradition of academic mountain clubs running rustic chatki. Cheap, sociable and deliberately spartan, these huts are where generations of Polish hikers learned the mountains.

## 5. Schronisko Górskie Orzeł

A mountain shelter in the broader Carpathian foothills, the kind of modest PTTK roof that links the long-distance Main Beskid Trail, the great red-marked route that runs the length of the Polish Carpathians from the Bieszczady all the way west to the Beskid Śląski.

## 6. Schronisko PTTK Markowe Szczawiny

Far to the west in the Beskid Żywiecki, Markowe Szczawiny is one of the oldest mountain huts in Poland, the base for climbing Babia Góra (1,725 m), the highest summit of the Polish Beskids and a notoriously weather-prone peak known as the Mother of Bad Weather.

## 7. Schronisko PTTK na Hali Szrenickiej

In the Karkonosze (Giant Mountains) on the Czech border, this hut sits high on the open Hala Szrenicka beneath Szrenica (1,362 m). The Karkonosze are bleaker and more arctic in feel than the Bieszczady, swept by ferocious winds, and their huts are robust, year-round institutions.

## 8. Schronisko Szrenica

Perched right on the summit area of Szrenica in the western Karkonosze, this is one of Poland's classic high shelters, accessible by chairlift from Szklarska Poręba but commanding genuine high-mountain weather and a panorama across the Czech-Polish ridge.

## 9. Schronisko PTTK Jaworzyna Krynicka

In the Beskid Sądecki near the spa town of Krynica-Zdrój, this hut sits below Jaworzyna Krynicka (1,114 m), a gentle, forested range of long ridges and quiet valleys that makes an easier counterpoint to the demanding Bieszczady.

## 10. Schronisko PTTK na Hali Łabowskiej

Also in the Beskid Sądecki, the Hala Łabowska shelter sits on a broad forested ridge on the Main Beskid Trail. It is a quiet, traditional hut that captures the unhurried spirit of Polish long-distance walking through the gentle eastern Beskids.

## The PTTK system and the bacówka

Polish mountain shelters owe their existence largely to the PTTK, the Polish Tourist and Sightseeing Society, whose network of schroniska and bacówki dates back over a century. Huts are generally inexpensive, serve hearty food — żurek soup, pierogi, naleśniki — and operate a famous rule that no one is turned away in bad weather, even if it means sleeping on the dining room floor. There is no mandatory membership system as in the Alps; you simply turn up and pay. The student chatki add a parallel, scruffier and very sociable layer to the network, especially in the Bieszczady.

## Seasons, wildlife and timing

The Bieszczady walking season runs from late spring to October, with autumn — when the beech forests turn copper and the poloniny glow gold — the most celebrated time. Winter brings serious snow and short days; the open poloniny become genuinely dangerous in poor visibility. This is bear and wolf country, so store food properly and keep noise sensible in the forest; bison roam the lower valleys of the national park. Carry layers for the exposed ridges, where the weather can change in minutes, and book ahead for the popular Chatka Puchatka, whose handful of beds fill fast in season.
