The Via Alpina is a network of five long-distance hiking routes crossing all eight Alpine countries. The Blue Route — designated Route B — runs approximately 700 kilometres from Trieste in Italy through Slovenia, Austria, and Germany, finishing in Vaduz, Liechtenstein. It was formally inaugurated in 2002 as part of the International Year of Mountains and crosses some of the most geographically diverse terrain in the Alps: the karst limestone plateaus of the Slovenian Kamnik-Savinja Alps, the crystalline metamorphic peaks of the Niedere Tauern in Austria, the glacially sculpted Bavarian Alps, and the Rhine valley borderlands of Liechtenstein. The Blue Route is typically walked west-to-east or east-to-west over four to six weeks; most walkers take it in sections over multiple summers. Overnight infrastructure varies considerably by country: Slovenian mountain huts are often staffed by volunteer club members, Austrian huts run on the DAV/ÖAV system, and Bavarian options blend club huts with private guesthouses. The following ten shelters represent key overnight stops along the route from Slovenia through to the western end.

## 1. Štuhecov dom, Kamnik-Savinja Alps

Štuhecov dom at 1,663 metres on the Golte massif in the Kamnik-Savinja Alps is an early highlight of the Blue Route from the Trieste-Kamnik direction. The hut belongs to the Slovenian Alpine Club (PZS) and was rebuilt in the 1990s after storm damage to its 1930s predecessor. It sleeps around 40 in mixed dormitory and smaller rooms, provides full hot meals, and operates from late June through September. The surrounding terrain — glacially smoothed dolomite and limestone outcrops — marks the transition from the lower Savinja valley to the true alpine zone of the Kamniške Alpe. Access from Mozirje town takes about three hours.

## 2. Koča na Pesku, Velika Planina plateau

Velika Planina is a high plateau at around 1,600 metres above Kamnik, historically used as summer pasture and now one of Slovenia's most visited alpine destinations. Koča na Pesku sits on the edge of this plateau and provides overnight accommodation for Via Alpina walkers and the considerable day-tripper traffic accessing the area by gondola from the valley. The traditional shepherd's architecture — round stone huts with wooden roofs — that characterises Velika Planina gives the plateau a distinctive visual identity. The koča (hut) operates from May to October and sleeps about 30.

## 3. Mariborska koča, Pohorje plateau

The Pohorje plateau is a gentle forested massif south of Maribor, reaching just over 1,500 metres, that differs markedly from the limestone peaks farther east. Mariborska koča at 1,454 metres sits near the plateau's highest point and serves both summer walkers on the E6 and Via Alpina and winter Nordic skiers on the Pohorje trail network. The hut is a large facility by Slovenian standards, sleeping 80 and operating a full restaurant. The dense spruce and beech forest surrounding it gives way to open meadow only at the summit ridge.

## 4. Dom nad Zalo, Logarska dolina

Dom nad Zalo sits above the Logarska dolina (Logartal), one of the most photogenic glacial valleys in Slovenia, at around 1,400 metres. The valley is a protected natural reserve and the hut provides access to routes toward the Ojstrica peak (2,350 m) and the Klemenča špica. The Blue Route traverses the ridge above the valley, and Dom nad Zalo is a natural overnight stop for walkers descending from the Kamniške Alpe before continuing west into Austrian Styria. The hut sleeps 30 and the kitchen specialises in traditional Slovenian mountain dishes including žganci (buckwheat porridge) and clear mushroom soup.

## 5. Gippelhütte, Lower Austria

The Gippelhütte at 1,450 metres in the Türnitzer Alps of Lower Austria marks the route's entry into Austrian territory. The hut belongs to the Austrian Alpine Club (ÖAV) section Türnitz and was built in 1921; it sleeps 40 in dormitory accommodation with private rooms available. The Türnitzer Alps are a transitional zone between the limestone Voralpen of Lower Austria and the higher crystalline massifs of the Niedere Tauern. Trails here pass through forested ridges with occasional open clearings and are characteristic of the moderate but surprisingly remote Austrian interior highlands.

## 6. Bayreuther Hütte, Bavarian Alps

Crossing from Austria into Bavaria, the Bayreuther Hütte at 1,805 metres in the Ammergau Alps is operated by the German Alpine Club (DAV) section Bayreuth, which built the original structure in 1895. It sleeps 60 in dormitory and private room configurations, provides full meals, and operates from late June to early October. The Ammergau Alps are a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve, and the route through them crosses gentle meadow terrain punctuated by the limestone towers that characterise this corner of the Bavarian Alps. King Ludwig II's fantastical Neuschwanstein Castle is visible from certain points on the ridge — a remarkable juxtaposition of alpine wilderness and nineteenth-century romanticism.

## 7. Dammkarhütte, Karwendel

The Dammkarhütte at 1,680 metres in the Karwendel range east of Mittenwald is a private mountain guesthouse rather than a traditional alpine club hut, but serves the same overnight function for Via Alpina walkers traversing the Karwendel. The surrounding terrain — the Karwendel's main ridge rises to 2,749 m at Birkkarspitze — is among the most dramatic limestone scenery in Bavaria. The hut sleeps around 30 and operates summer and winter. Mittenwald below the hut is famous for its violin-making tradition and painted house facades.

## 8. Brunnsteinhütte, Inn valley

The Brunnsteinhütte at 1,560 metres on the northern face of the Inn valley ridge provides a rest point as the Blue Route traverses the section between the Karwendel and the Mangfall ranges. The hut is DAV property and sleeps 30 in a compact setting within spruce forest. The Inn valley below is one of the main Tyrolian transportation corridors, and the contrast between the busy valley infrastructure and the quiet forest hut above is characteristic of the Via Alpina's ability to thread genuine wilderness through densely populated European landscapes.

## 9. Rauhekopfhütte, Bavarian Prealps

The Rauhekopfhütte at around 1,400 metres in the Bavarian Prealps near Rosenheim provides overnight accommodation for the final Bavarian sections of the Blue Route before it enters the Rhine catchment. The hut is privately operated with 25 beds and a traditional Bavarian kitchen — Leberknödelsuppe, Kaiserschmarrn, and local beer are standard fare. The terrain here becomes gentler as the route descends toward the Rhine basin, but the views south toward the main Alpine chain remain impressive on clear days.

## 10. Berggasthaus Gumen, Liechtenstein

Berggasthaus Gumen at 1,516 metres above Vaduz in Liechtenstein is the terminal hut of the Via Alpina Blue Route — or the first, depending on direction of travel. Liechtenstein's Rhine valley position and its small scale mean the country's alpine terrain is concentrated in a narrow strip above the valley, but that strip includes impressive peaks reaching 2,599 metres at Grauspitz. The Berggasthaus Gumen is a traditional alpine guesthouse with a full restaurant and accommodation for 40 guests. The views down to Vaduz and the Rhine, with Switzerland's Walensee and the Rätikon range visible across the border, make for a memorable final evening on the Blue Route.

## Explore the Full Map

The Via Alpina Blue Route crosses hut systems governed by five different national alpine clubs with separate booking procedures, price structures, and seasonal schedules. All documented huts along the Blue Route and connecting trails in Slovenia, Austria, Germany, and Liechtenstein are mapped with GPS coordinates, altitude, operating season, and website links in the global shelter database at [Open the map](/map). Planning a multi-week section of the Blue Route is greatly simplified when you can see the spacing between huts, assess elevation gains between stages, and identify alternatives where primary huts are fully booked.
