Arrive at a serviced alpine hut in the late afternoon, peel off your boots at the door, and within a few hours you will be sitting at a long communal table as a bowl of steaming soup lands in front of you, followed by a hearty main and something sweet to finish. The next morning, before the sun is properly up, there is bread, jam, cheese and coffee. This rhythm — dinner, a bunk, and breakfast, all arranged for you — is the half-board system, the default way of staying in a staffed hut across the Alps, and understanding it makes the whole experience smoother and the bill far less surprising.

## What Half-Board Means

Half-board, known as Halbpension in German-speaking huts, demi-pension in French and mezza pensione in Italian, is the package of an overnight stay plus the evening meal and breakfast. It is the standard offer at serviced huts of the SAC, DAV, ÖAV, CAI and CAF, and many wardens quote it as a single combined price. The logic is simple: a hut is not a restaurant you can pop into, it is a remote operation that must plan and carry up every ingredient, so it works best when guests commit to eating in.

The evening meal is usually a set menu with little or no choice, served at a fixed time announced when you check in — often around six or seven. You eat what the kitchen has cooked for everyone, at the same hour, together. This is not a constraint to resent but the very thing that makes hut life efficient, sociable and affordable.

## A Typical Dinner

Hut dinners are built for hungry mountain bodies, not for delicate appetites. The classic structure is three courses. First a soup, frequently a barley broth, a vegetable soup or the famous Tyrolean Frittatensuppe with sliced pancake. Then a substantial main: pasta, rösti, polenta, a stew, sausages with sauerkraut, Kaiserschmarrn as a sweet main in Austria, or the cheese-and-bread richness of an Alpine Käseschnitte. A simple dessert, a slice of cake or stewed fruit, closes the meal.

The food is dense, warming and calorie-rich because that is what the day demands and what the larder allows. Wardens cook around ingredients that survive storage and the flight up — dried, tinned, vacuum-packed and long-life staples — supplemented with whatever fresh produce the latest helicopter brought. The result is rarely fine dining, but after eight hours on the trail a plate of buttery rösti at altitude is one of the great meals of mountaineering.

## Breakfast and the Alpine Start

Breakfast is the half-board's quieter bookend, and it is usually a buffet or a laid table of bread, butter, jam, honey, sliced cheese and cold meats, with coffee, tea and sometimes muesli. It is deliberately simple and quick, designed to get a roomful of climbers fed and moving before first light. On huts that serve glacier routes, breakfast may be offered at an unsociable hour — four or even three in the morning — so parties can cross the snow while the overnight freeze still holds.

If you are leaving before the standard breakfast, most huts will prepare a Bergsteigerfrühstück, a climber's breakfast, set out early or packed the night before. Tell the warden your plans when you arrive, because the kitchen schedules its dawn around the parties heading out, and a quiet word the evening before secures your coffee at three.

## Drinks, Extras and the Self-Service Option

Half-board covers the two meals and the bed, but not your drinks, and this is where bills grow. Beer, wine, soft drinks, tea and the afternoon slice of cake are all extra, priced to reflect the helicopter that carried every bottle up. Even water can cost money — tap water is often non-potable at altitude, and huts sell bottled or treated water and charge for refilling bottles from a safe source. Carrying your own purification can save a surprising amount over a multi-day trip.

Many huts also offer a self-catering or Selbstversorger option: a cheaper bed without meals, plus access to a basic kitchen where you cook your own food on the hut's stoves. This suits those on a tight budget or a special diet, but it asks you to carry your food up, and it forgoes the social warmth of the shared table. On most trips half-board is the better value once you account for the weight of food you would otherwise carry.

## Booking, Dietary Needs and Cancellations

Half-board now almost always requires booking ahead, especially in summer on popular routes, and the meal count is part of the reservation. When you book, state any dietary requirements — vegetarian options are now standard, and most huts manage vegan or gluten-free with notice, but a remote kitchen cannot improvise for an unannounced allergy. Confirm your numbers, because the warden cooks and flies up food precisely for the bookings on the list.

Take cancellations seriously. A no-show means meals cooked and ingredients flown up for nothing, a real loss to a small operation, and many huts now charge a fee for failing to cancel in time or to call if you are turning back. A phone call from the valley the moment your plans change is simple courtesy and increasingly a contractual expectation.

## Why the System Endures

The half-board model has survived because it solves the hut's central problem: how to feed unpredictable numbers of people in a place where every ingredient is precious and hard to deliver. By committing guests to meals in advance, it lets the warden plan supply, minimise waste and keep prices reasonable, while giving walkers a light pack and a hot meal at the end of a hard day. The shared table, the fixed hour and the set menu are not quaint survivals but a finely tuned answer to the logistics of living high.

For the traveller, the lesson is to lean into it. Book your half-board, declare your needs, eat what is served, pay cash for your beer, and join the table. The communal dinner in a warm Stube as darkness falls over the peaks is not a side effect of the hut experience; for many it is the very heart of it.
