Bosnia and Herzegovina contains some of the most rugged and least-visited mountain terrain in the entire Dinaric Alps. The ranges here — Bjelašnica, Treskavica, Prenj, Čvrsnica, Visočica, and Cvrsnica — form a limestone plateau world of dolinas, karst pavements, deep canyons, and high meadows that extend from the outskirts of Sarajevo to the Neretva valley near Mostar. Bjelašnica (2,067 m), Prenj (2,155 m), and Čvrsnica (2,228 m) are the principal summits. The mountain shelters here — planinarski domovi and planinarskie kuće — range from solidly built stone lodges to simpler wooden structures, many constructed by the Bosnian mountaineering clubs that were active from the 1890s onward. The war of 1992–1995 damaged or destroyed many huts, and the network has been rebuilt gradually over the past three decades. The ten shelters below represent the key overnight points across the Bosnian ranges.

## 1. Planinarski Dom Bijele Vode, Bjelašnica

Planinarski dom Bijele Vode sits at around 1,400 m on the western flanks of Bjelašnica, near the spring of the same name. Bjelašnica is the closest high mountain to Sarajevo — the summit is about 30 km from the city centre — and was the venue for the 1984 Winter Olympics alpine skiing events. The hut offers accommodation for around 50 people and is one of the most active mountain houses on the mountain, with regular weekend parties arriving from Sarajevo. The trail to the Bjelašnica summit plateau takes about two hours.

## 2. Planinski Dom Lipići, Treskavica

Planinski dom Lipići is located on the northern slopes of Treskavica (2,086 m), a large plateau mountain southeast of Sarajevo that is less visited than Bjelašnica but arguably more impressive in its scale. Treskavica is known for extensive highland pastures, numerous sinkholes, and springs that feed rivers in all directions. The dom at Lipići has modest capacity and is often used by hikers traversing the full Treskavica plateau on multi-day routes connecting the mountain to the Zelengora range further south.

## 3. Planinarska Kuća Ruište, Čvrsnica

Planinarska kuća Ruište is the main hut serving Čvrsnica, the highest massif in Herzegovina at 2,228 m. The hut sits at around 1,350 m on the eastern approach to the mountain from the Duvanjsko Polje plateau. Čvrsnica's northern escarpment drops almost 1,500 m in near-vertical cliffs above the Doljanka river gorge — one of the most dramatic landscapes in the western Balkans. The ascent from Ruište to the summit takes four to five hours through progressively wilder karst terrain.

## 4. Буковик, Central Bosnia

Bukovik mountain house is set on the forested slopes of the Bukovik massif in central Bosnia, north of Sarajevo. The hut is a traditional Bosnian mountain shelter used by hiking clubs from Zenica and Sarajevo for weekend excursions. The surrounding beech forest is dense and well preserved, with trails suitable for family groups and less experienced walkers. The hut operates seasonally from April through October.

## 5. PD Prijatelji Prirode, Sarajevo Region

"Friends of Nature" mountain lodge (PD Prijatelji Prirode) represents one of the smaller but locally beloved huts in the Sarajevo mountain hinterland. The lodge is associated with a mountaineering club of the same name that has operated since the interwar period and continued activities through the most difficult years of the 1990s. The hut's programming includes guided hikes for children and educational nature walks, making it an important community asset beyond simple overnight shelter.

## 6. Planinarski Dom Galica, Prenj

Planinarski dom Galica is positioned on the approaches to Prenj (2,155 m), the massif east of the Neretva valley near Konjic. Prenj is often described as the most challenging of the Bosnian ranges — its complex geology produces dramatic cliff scenery, and the standard routes to the high plateau involve long ascents through mixed forest and scrub. The Galica dom serves hikers approaching from the Konjic direction and has accommodation for around 40 people.

## 7. Planinarska Kuća Orlovačko Jezero, Zelengora

Planinarska kuća Orlovačko jezero sits beside Orlovačko Lake at around 1,700 m on the Zelengora massif in southern Bosnia. Zelengora — the "Green Mountain" — is a forested plateau range at the heart of the Sutjeska National Park, which contains the last primeval forest in Bosnia at Perućica. The lake-side hut is part of the Sutjeska circuit and is one of the key overnight stops for hikers doing the full Zelengora traverse. The national park protects brown bear, wolf, and the rare Huchen (Danube salmon) in the Sutjeska river.

## 8. Planinarski Dom Stanari, Northern Bosnia

Planinarski dom Stanari is a mountain shelter in northern Bosnia associated with the Vučjak and Motajica ranges northwest of Doboj. These are modest hills by Dinaric standards but important for the hiking communities of the industrial towns of the Sava valley. The dom at Stanari serves weekend walkers and maintains marked trails through the forests of oak and hornbeam that cover the lower slopes.

## 9. Rif. Budozelje, Una Valley

The shelter at Budozelje lies near the Una river in northwestern Bosnia, in the Una National Park established in 2008. The Una is one of the most beautiful rivers in Bosnia — its waterfalls at Štrbački buk and Martin Brod are UNESCO-candidate natural monuments — and the shelter here serves hikers and kayakers exploring the river corridor. The surrounding Grmeč mountain forms the backdrop, with forested ridges reaching 1,604 m above the valley.

## 10. Planinarski Dom Bijambare, Sarajevo Region

Planinarski dom Bijambare is located at around 900 m in the Bijambare protected landscape north of Sarajevo, famous for a cave system of five interconnected caverns used during the war as shelter by the local population. The cave entrance is close to the dom, and the surrounding forest is a popular weekend destination for Sarajevo families. The hut is one of the most accessible in the Bosnian network and operates year-round.
