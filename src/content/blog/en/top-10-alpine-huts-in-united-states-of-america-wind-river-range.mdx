The Wind River Range stretches for roughly 160 kilometres along the Continental Divide in west-central Wyoming, a wall of pale granite that holds the seven largest glaciers in the American Rockies. This is wilderness on a scale that has no European equivalent: Gannett Peak, the state high point at 4,209 metres, sits more than 40 kilometres from the nearest road, deep inside the Bridger and Fitzpatrick Wilderness areas. Because almost the entire range lies within designated wilderness, there is no Alpine-style network of staffed refuges here. Shelter in the Winds means a tent at a named basin, a historic ranch lodge at the trailhead, or one of the handful of guest lodges and Forest Service cabins that ring the foothills. This guide treats those base camps and lodges as the practical equivalent of the hut network elsewhere in the world.

## Why the Winds have no European-style hut chain

In the Alps, a hut every few hours is the norm; in the Wind River Range, the Wilderness Act of 1964 forbids permanent commercial structures across most of the high country. The result is a backcountry that rewards self-sufficiency. Trekkers carry everything for trips of five to ten days, resupplying only at trailheads such as Big Sandy, Elkhart Park, or Green River Lakes. What functions as a hut is the established backcountry camp at a lake or basin, combined with the front-country lodges where parties spend the night before and after a traverse. Understanding this is the key to planning a trip here.

## Big Sandy Lodge, gateway to the Cirque of the Towers

Big Sandy Lodge, built in 1929 at around 2,750 metres near the Big Sandy trailhead, is the oldest continuously operating guest lodge in the range. Its log cabins and central lodge serve as the classic launch point for the Cirque of the Towers, the amphitheatre of vertical granite spires — Pingora, Wolf's Head, Warbonnet — that draws climbers from around the world. From the lodge it is roughly 13 kilometres and a crossing of Jackass Pass to reach Lonesome Lake at the heart of the cirque, one of the most photographed campsites in North America.

## Titcomb Basin base camps

Titcomb Basin, reached from the Elkhart Park trailhead above Pine, is the most celebrated high camp in the Winds and the standard staging area for ascents of Gannett Peak. The basin sits at around 3,200 metres beneath Fremont Peak (4,185 metres) and a skyline of jagged summits. The approach is about 24 kilometres past Photographer's Point, Seneca Lake, and Island Lake, and most parties take two days to reach the upper basin. There is no building here — only durable, long-used tent sites among the boulders — but no list of Wind River shelters is complete without it.

## Green River Lakes and the northern approach

The Green River Lakes trailhead, with its iconic view of Squaretop Mountain mirrored in the water, opens the quieter northern end of the range. From here trekkers reach Trail Creek Park, the Highline Trail, and the long approach to the Gannett Glacier. The historic Green River Lakes campground and the nearby guest ranches function as the staging point. This northern corridor sees a fraction of the traffic of the Big Sandy or Elkhart Park entries and offers some of the most solitary walking in the lower 48 states.

## Cirque of the Towers and Lonesome Lake

Lonesome Lake at roughly 3,140 metres is the natural base for the Cirque of the Towers. Because of heavy use, regulations require camping at least 60 metres from the shoreline and prohibit campfires throughout the cirque. From this base, climbers and walkers reach the Texas Pass crossing into the Shadow Lake drainage, a key link in the high route that traverses the range. The granite walls catch the first and last light of the day, and the basin holds snow well into July in heavy winter years.

## The Wind River High Route

The Wind River High Route, popularised in recent decades as an off-trail traverse, links the southern Cirque of the Towers with the northern glaciers over roughly 130 kilometres, much of it above 3,300 metres and away from any maintained path. There are no shelters along it whatsoever; the route is defined by its named passes and basins — Texas Pass, Alpine Lakes Basin, Indian Pass, Knapsack Col at around 3,700 metres. It is among the most demanding non-technical traverses in the United States and a serious test of navigation and self-reliance.

## Pinedale, Lander, and the trailhead towns

The Winds are bracketed by two small towns that function as the human base camps of the range. Pinedale, on the western side, gives access to the Elkhart Park and Green River Lakes trailheads and houses the Museum of the Mountain Man. Lander, to the southeast, is the home of the National Outdoor Leadership School (NOLS), founded here in 1965, and the gateway to the Sinks Canyon and the southern approaches. Both towns offer the last reliable lodging, food, and outfitters before days of self-supported travel.

## Planning a trip in the Winds

A journey into the Winds demands more preparation than almost any other range in the lower 48, precisely because there is no staffed infrastructure in the high country. Overnight permits are not required everywhere, but registration at the trailhead is good practice, and the busiest basins carry specific rules about where camping is allowed. Most parties plan for five to ten days of self-sufficiency, carrying all food, all fuel, and full equipment for weather changes, since there is no shop or hut to fall back on. Water is plentiful from lakes and streams but should be treated, and navigation on the high routes requires map, compass, and the ability to read terrain off-trail. Anyone used to the European model of a staffed hut every few hours must reset their thinking entirely for the Winds: here the solitude is the whole point, and safety rests squarely on one's own planning and judgement.

## Glaciers, weather, and the short season

The Winds hold Gannett, Dinwoody, Fremont, and several other glaciers — relics that are retreating but still demand crampons and an ice axe for the high summits. The practical season is short: high passes typically clear of snow by mid-July and afternoon thunderstorms build almost daily through August, making early starts essential. By late September the first storms can close the range. Black bears require proper food storage, and the bear-resistant canister is standard practice throughout the wilderness.
