The Tetons rise without foothills. From the sagebrush flats of Jackson Hole in Wyoming, the range leaps almost 2,100 metres straight to the granite spire of the Grand Teton at 4,199 metres, one of the most abrupt and dramatic mountain fronts in North America. Climbers have been drawn here for more than a century, but anyone expecting the manned hut system of the Alps will find something quite different. American mountain shelter is its own tradition: a high camp on the Lower Saddle, scattered backcountry patrol cabins, historic fire lookouts and a handful of stone refuges, set within a wilderness ethic that prizes the tent and the self-reliant camp above the guarded inn.

## A Range Without European Huts

It is worth saying plainly: Grand Teton National Park has no network of catered alpine huts in the European sense. The American model, shaped by the wilderness movement and the National Park Service, leans heavily toward backcountry camping under permit, with shelter meaning your own tent at a designated site. Where fixed structures exist, they are patrol cabins, ranger stations, historic lookouts and the occasional climbers' refuge, not the wardened, meal-serving huts of the CAI, DAV or SAC.

This makes the Tetons a fascinating case study in how a great mountain culture shelters itself differently. Instead of hut-to-hut traverses with a bunk and dinner waiting, the Teton experience is built around permits, bear canisters, designated camping zones and a deep respect for leaving no trace. The reward is solitude and wildness that the busiest alpine huts can rarely offer.

## The Lower Saddle: A Camp in the Sky

The closest thing the Tetons have to a high mountain hut is the Lower Saddle, the broad col at about 3,500 metres between the Grand Teton and the Middle Teton. This windswept saddle is the staging point for nearly every ascent of the Grand, and in summer it becomes a small high camp: a cluster of tents, a few semi-permanent shelters used by the concession guides, and climbers organising ropes and racks for a pre-dawn start.

The guide services that hold permits in the park, the historic Exum Mountain Guides and Jackson Hole Mountain Guides, maintain fixed camps on the Saddle for their clients, the nearest thing to a staffed refuge in the range. Independent climbers carry their own tents and camp under a permit. From the Saddle, the standard Owen-Spalding route and the classic Upper Exum Ridge lead up the final 700 metres to the summit, a serious alpine climb requiring rope, rock skills and an alpine start.

## Backcountry Camping Zones

The real shelter system of the Tetons is the network of designated backcountry camping zones. Permits, now largely reserved in advance, allocate climbers and backpackers to zones such as the Garnet Canyon zone below the Grand, the South Fork of Cascade Canyon, the North Fork, and the high lakes of the range. Within each zone you pitch your tent at a low-impact site, store food in a bear-resistant canister, and travel under your own power.

This system underpins the Teton Crest Trail, the range's premier multi-day route, which runs roughly 40 miles along the high western side from Phillips Pass or Granite Canyon to Paintbrush Canyon, crossing Death Canyon Shelf, Alaska Basin and the Paintbrush Divide. There are no huts along it; backpackers carry everything and camp in the zones. It is one of the finest high-country traverses in the American West, and a pure expression of the tent-based ethic.

## Fire Lookouts and Patrol Cabins

If America has a distinctive built mountain shelter, it is the fire lookout. Across the Western ranges, the U.S. Forest Service built hundreds of lookout towers and cabins on high summits to spot wildfires, and many decommissioned ones can now be rented for the night, an only-in-America form of mountain hut. While the Tetons themselves have few, the surrounding national forests and the wider Northern Rockies are rich with them.

Within and around the park, the Park Service and Forest Service maintain patrol cabins and ranger stations used by staff and, in winter, by ski patrols and rangers, occasionally available to the public. These rustic log structures, scattered through the backcountry of the Greater Yellowstone region, carry forward a frontier tradition of the trail cabin, simple shelter for those working or travelling far from the road.

## Winter Yurts and Backcountry Lodges

The hut idea does flower in one American form: the winter yurt and the backcountry ski lodge. Around the Tetons and across the Rockies, operators run yurts and small lodges reached on skis, offering bunks, a wood stove and access to powder slopes. Wyoming and neighbouring states hold many, and a vigorous hut-to-hut ski culture has grown up, most famously in Colorado with the celebrated Tenth Mountain Division Hut Association, a true network of backcountry huts founded in the spirit of the wartime ski troops.

These ski huts come closest to the European model: you book a bunk, share a kitchen and stove, and travel between them on skis through winter forest. They are the American mountain world's answer to the alpine refuge, born not of centuries-old clubs but of the postwar boom in backcountry skiing and a love of the high winter wilderness.

## Planning a Teton Trip

The Teton climbing and backpacking season runs from about July to September, once the snow has melted off the high passes and the Lower Saddle. Permits for backcountry camping and for the Grand are essential and competitive; the park releases a share by advance reservation and holds some for walk-ups. Jackson, the lively town in the valley, is the natural base for gear, food and information.

The mountains demand respect. The Grand Teton is a real alpine climb with rockfall, exposure and fast-moving afternoon storms; the Crest Trail crosses high, snow-prone passes; and the whole region is grizzly bear country, requiring proper food storage and awareness. Come prepared for self-sufficiency, carry your shelter, and you will experience an American high country that, while it lacks the cosy guarded huts of the Alps, offers a wildness all its own.
