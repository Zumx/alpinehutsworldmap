## Why Grading Systems Don't Directly Translate

Each Alpine nation's hiking and mountaineering clubs developed their trail grading systems independently, and the result is a set of parallel scales that overlap without being identical. A walker who is comfortable at T3 on the SAC scale will find some Italian CAI EE routes familiar and others harder than expected, because the calibration assumptions differ. Understanding the internal logic of each system — rather than simply memorising a conversion table — leads to better terrain judgements.

The core difference is in what each system is measuring. SAC grades (T1 through T6) are primarily about terrain character — the type of ground underfoot and the degree to which the hands must be used. CAI grades (T, E, EE, EEA) are more focused on route-finding demands and exposure. DAV and ÖAV in practice use SAC grades for their official trail descriptions, having adopted the Swiss system as a shared standard for the German-speaking Alpine region.

## SAC Hiking Grades: T1 to T6

The Schweizer Alpen-Club scale runs from T1 (hiking) to T6 (difficult alpine hiking), with T3 as the most commonly encountered threshold separating trails accessible to the general public from those requiring specific mountain experience.

T1 covers well-marked paths on gentle terrain — forest tracks, agricultural roads, and lower valley trails. No mountain experience required. T2 moves onto mountain paths with some exposure to gradient and basic trail navigation. T3 introduces terrain where the hands may be needed for balance, where exposed passages occur, and where basic sure-footedness is required. This is where the SAC system begins to filter.

T4 requires alpine experience, hands-on involvement with the rock, and good navigation ability on poorly marked or unmarked terrain. T5 adds demanding exposure, often on ridges or face traverses, and requires rope familiarity and the ability to assess terrain independently. T6 is reserved for extreme alpine hiking terrain — near-vertical scrambles, long exposed ridges, routes that sit at the boundary of technical climbing.

## CAI Grades: T, E, EE, EEA

The Club Alpino Italiano uses a four-level scale applied slightly differently from SAC. T (Turistico) is the easiest category — mule tracks, road access, gentle ascents. E (Escursionistico) covers standard marked trails accessible to regular walkers with appropriate footwear. EE (Escursionismo Esperierto) — literally "experienced hiking" — covers terrain with some exposure, unmarked sections, or demanding physical character. EEA (Escursionismo Esperierto Attrezzato) specifically addresses equipped routes, i.e. via ferrata.

The CAI scale is coarser than SAC's T1-T6, which means each CAI category spans a wider range of actual terrain difficulty. A straightforward EE route in the Dolomites on stable limestone with good waymarking might correspond to SAC T3, while a loose, exposed EE route in the Apennines might feel like T4 to T5 by Swiss calibration.

The CAI via ferrata grading (F, PD, D, MD, D/ED) is a separate system applied specifically to iron-aided routes and does not correspond to the hiking scale at all.

## DAV/ÖAV Practice: SAC Adoption

German and Austrian Alpine clubs formally adopted the SAC hiking grades around 2010 as part of a harmonisation effort. Before this, DAV trail descriptions used informal German descriptions (leicht, mittelschwer, schwer) without a standardised numeric scale. The switch to SAC T1-T6 means that current DAV and ÖAV hut guidebooks and trail information use the same scale as Swiss sources, which considerably simplifies cross-border planning.

Some older DAV guidebooks printed before 2010 still use the legacy descriptive system. When consulting an older source, a rough conversion: leicht corresponds to T1-T2, mittelschwer to T3-T4, and schwer to T4-T5.

## What the Scales Miss: Rock Type and Conditions

All three systems assume dry, stable conditions in summer. The practical difficulty of any route shifts significantly with wet rock, snow patches on approach trails in early season, or ice on exposed ridges after an autumn cold snap. A T3 route becomes objectively more serious when crossed with fresh snow — not because the grade is wrong but because grades describe the route's character in ideal conditions.

Rock type is not captured in any of these scales. Dolomite limestone — the dominant rock in Italy's most spectacular ranges — is famously grippy when dry and becomes glassy when wet. The Kaisergebirge limestone in Tyrol is similarly affected. Granite, dominant in the Mont Blanc massif and many Swiss ranges, behaves differently — more reliable in rain, but more abrasive on hands during prolonged scrambling.

## Exposure and Consequences

One dimension where the systems diverge most significantly is in their treatment of exposure — the psychological and physical experience of moving across terrain where a fall would be serious or fatal. SAC grades T4 and above explicitly incorporate exposure into the grade. CAI EE does so implicitly but less consistently.

For walkers calibrating their comfort level: exposure is the most common reason that technically capable walkers struggle on routes rated within their physical fitness envelope. A walker fit enough to handle the altitude gain and path character of a T4 ridge may find the psychological experience of a narrow, exposed traverse more demanding than the physical difficulty implies.

## Mapping One System onto Another

A pragmatic conversion for planning purposes: SAC T1 broadly matches CAI T; SAC T2 broadly matches CAI E; SAC T3 corresponds roughly to the easier part of CAI EE; SAC T4 covers the harder part of CAI EE into EEA; SAC T5-T6 goes beyond the CAI hiking scale and enters what CAI classifies as alpinismo.

These conversions are approximate. The most reliable approach is to read the route description, not just the grade — the specific character of the terrain, the season, the length of exposed sections, and the nature of any technical moves will communicate more than any single letter or number.
