The layering system is one of those concepts that sounds self-evident until
you actually need it to work at 3200 m in a thunderstorm, or trying to sleep
at 3500 m when the dormitory is simultaneously too warm and too cold. Getting
it right requires understanding what each layer actually does — and what to
leave behind.

## Base layer: the moisture-management layer

The base layer sits next to the skin and its job is moisture management. When
you are working hard on a steep ascent, you sweat. The base layer should move
that moisture away from the skin so you do not end up cold and wet when you
stop. Wet skin is the primary cause of chilling at altitude.

The material choice is the main decision: synthetic or merino wool.

Synthetic base layers (typically polyester or nylon blends) dry faster than
merino, which matters on a multi-day trip where you can't wash clothing.
They are lighter for equivalent warmth. They are cheaper. The downside is
odour retention: synthetic fibres trap bacteria, and by day three on the trail,
the smell is noticeable.

Merino wool base layers manage moisture slightly less aggressively than the
best synthetics, but they resist odour through multiple days without washing
in a way that synthetics do not. A merino base layer worn for four consecutive
days on a hut traverse will not smell in the way a synthetic will. For multi-
day hut trips with no access to laundry, this matters enormously — you are
sleeping in a shared dormitory.

The consensus among experienced alpine hut travellers: bring one merino hiking
shirt to wear and one synthetic to change into. The merino handles the social
obligation; the synthetic dries overnight after a wash in the hut sink.

Weight: a standard lightweight merino T-shirt runs 150–200 g; a zip-neck for
cold days runs 180–250 g.

## Mid layer: warmth and packability

The mid layer provides insulation when you stop moving, when the temperature
drops, or when you need warmth at the hut after a cold day. It must compress
small enough to stow in the top of the pack while walking.

Options: light fleece grid or pile (warm-when-wet, dries fast, breathes well,
slightly heavier), down gilet (superb warmth-to-weight, useless if wet, stows
very small), synthetic insulated gilet (slightly heavier than down, works when
damp, reasonable packability).

A light fleece half-zip or grid fleece is the most versatile mid-layer for
active mountain days: it breathes well enough to wear while moving in cold
conditions and provides enough warmth for summit stops. Weight around 180–280 g.

## Shell: the non-negotiable

A waterproof-breathable hard shell is mandatory on any multi-day alpine hut
trip. Not a softshell. Not a "water-resistant" jacket. A fully waterproof shell
with taped seams and a proper hood that adjusts to fit over a helmet.

The Alps produce weather that can go from sunshine to sleet in 30 minutes.
The approach to the Sulzenauhütte in the Stubai Alps, the crossing toward
the Nürnberger Hütte, or the traverse to the Innsbrucker Hütte — any of these
can catch a party in serious rain or snow at altitude. A shell that keeps you
dry in that situation is not luxury equipment; it is core safety kit.

Gore-Tex Pro or equivalent 3-layer constructions are the benchmark. They are
expensive (EUR 400–700) and heavy (350–500 g) compared to lighter two-layer
shells, but they handle sustained precipitation better and last longer with
rough pack treatment. Lighter "active" shells (200–300 g) are appropriate
for lower-risk routes with better weather predictability.

## Sleep layers

Hut dormitories vary significantly in temperature. A dormitory at 1800 m in
June might be 12–15°C; one at 3300 m in August might be 5°C. The hut provides
blankets — thick ones — but no sleeping bag.

A sleeping liner is the base of the sleep system (see the separate article on
basics). A down or synthetic puffy jacket serves double duty as summit warmth
and additional warmth if the hut dormitory is cold. Bringing one thin fleece
specifically for sleeping is unnecessary weight if the jacket serves the
purpose.

One deliberate decision worth making: a separate set of sleeping clothes — even
just a merino T-shirt and lightweight leggings — means you are not sleeping in
salt-sweat hiking clothes, which is better for you, your liner, and the people
sleeping alongside you.

## Hut slippers

Pack them. They weigh 200–400 g, they are required in virtually every staffed
Alpine hut, and arriving at the boot room without them is a social mistake
that marks you as a beginner. Lightweight EVA sandals (Crocs or equivalent)
are the standard choice — they dry instantly, pack flat, and double as shower
shoes.

## Weight versus warmth: the core decision

Every decision in a multi-day hut layering system comes down to the weight
versus warmth trade-off. A heavier sleeping bag is warmer but costs pack
weight; a lighter bag requires compensating with more worn layers. A bulkier
jacket provides more warmth for summit days but takes more pack volume.

The key metric for sleeping gear is the EN 13537 temperature rating, which
provides comfort and limit ratings independently. For a Haute Route-level
trip in July, a bag with a comfort rating of +5°C will handle most hut
dormitory conditions; for a Tour du Mont Blanc in September, a +2°C comfort
rating is more appropriate. The "limit" rating (typically 5–8°C below the
comfort rating) is for experienced sleepers who sleep warm; most people
should plan to sleep at the comfort temperature.

Down fill power is the relevant specification for sleeping bags and jackets.
800-fill down provides the best weight-to-warmth ratio; 650–700-fill is
more budget-accessible and still excellent. Synthetic insulation is heavier
for equivalent warmth but performs when damp — a real consideration in
multi-day tours where the bag may be packed into a wet pack cover.

## Merino versus synthetic base layers

The merino versus synthetic base layer debate is genuine rather than
marketing. Merino wool's advantages are odour resistance (you can wear
it for multiple days without becoming antisocial), temperature regulation
(it works across a wider temperature range than most synthetics), and
comfort against skin. Its disadvantages are slower drying (particularly
heavy merino) and higher cost.

Synthetic base layers dry faster, are more durable, and are less expensive.
For single-day activities or routes where frequent washing is possible,
synthetic is often the better choice. For a multi-day hut-to-hut traverse
where the base layer is worn for 5+ consecutive days, merino's odour
management is a significant quality-of-life advantage.

Most experienced multi-day hut travellers use merino for the long-haul items
(base layer, sleep layer) and synthetic for the mid-layer where durability
and drying speed matter more.

## The pack weight calculation

For a standard 7-day hut-to-hut traverse with sleeping bag, the clothing
kit (excluding the pack itself) typically weighs 3.5–5 kg depending on the
season and level of optimization. Breaking it down: shell jacket 400–800 g,
mid-layer fleece or softshell 350–600 g, down jacket 300–500 g, base layer
set 200–400 g, sleeping liner 150–250 g, hut slippers 200–400 g, gloves and
hat 150 g, spare socks and underwear 300 g. Total 2–3.5 kg, plus the bag
at 800 g–1.3 kg.

Each decision at the gear shop is a small adjustment to this total. The
difference between an 800 g shell and a 400 g shell (buying the lighter
option) saves 400 g. Over a 14-day traverse, those 400 g are on your back
every day; the accumulation matters.
