India's Himalayan trekking areas span five states — Jammu and Kashmir, Himachal
Pradesh, Uttarakhand, Sikkim, and the eastern Himalayan fringe. The hut
infrastructure is less developed than Nepal, and the term "hut" covers a wide
range of structures: Forest Department rest houses, Mountaineering Institute
lodges, GMVN (Garhwal Mandal Vikas Nigam) Tourist Rest Houses, and camping
sites with basic shelters. Permits and registration requirements vary by region
and change regularly; check with the relevant state tourism authority before
departure.

## 1. Sandakphu Lodge, 3636 m — Singalila Ridge, West Bengal

Sandakphu (3636 m) on the Singalila Ridge along the Darjeeling-Nepal border
is the highest point in West Bengal and commands what may be the finest
Himalayan panorama accessible without technical climbing: Kangchenjunga (8586 m),
Makalu (8481 m), Lhotse, and Everest (8849 m) visible simultaneously on a
clear morning. The trekker's hut and lodge at the summit are operated by the
West Bengal Forest Development Corporation and provide basic dormitory
accommodation. The approach from Mane Bhanjang takes 3 days on the Singalila
trail.

## 2. Roopkund Base Camp, 3800 m — Uttarakhand

Below the famous Roopkund glacial lake (5029 m) — known as the "Mystery Lake"
for the medieval skeletons discovered around its shores — the base camp at
Ali Bugyal meadow (3800 m) serves trekkers and guides on the popular Roopkund
trek. The base camp has camping platforms and basic shelters maintained by
the local guides' association. Altitude gain from Lohajung to Roopkund summit
is approximately 2200 m over 5 days; AMS management is critical.

## 3. Kedartal Camp, 4750 m — Uttarakhand

Kedartal — the source lake of the Kedar Ganga — sits at 4750 m in a dramatic
glacial cirque below the Thalay Sagar peak (6904 m) in the Gangotri area.
Basic camping sites with shelters near the lake serve the relatively few
trekkers who undertake this serious route from Gangotri. The path from
Gangotri (3048 m) to the lake is unmarked in sections and requires a guide.

## 4. Beas Kund Camp, 3700 m — Himachal Pradesh

The Beas Kund — the source of the Beas river — is one of the most accessible
high-altitude glacier camps in Himachal Pradesh, reached from the Solang valley
above Manali in 2 days. A camping site with basic shelters at approximately
3700 m provides the overnight for summit approaches to Friendship Peak (5289 m)
and Shitidhar. The Beas Kund area is managed by the Atal Bihari Vajpayee
Institute of Mountaineering and Allied Sports (ABVIMAS).

## 5. Triund Forest Rest House, 2975 m — Himachal Pradesh

Above McLeod Ganj (the Tibetan government-in-exile town near Dharamsala) in the
Kangra valley, Triund at 2975 m is one of the most popular short treks in
northern India. The Forest Department rest house and camping area with basic
hut accommodation sit below the Dhauladhar ridge. On a clear day the Dhauladhar
snow wall above and the Kangra valley plains below create a dramatic contrast.

## 6. Chitkul Guesthouses, 3450 m — Baspa valley, Himachal Pradesh

The last inhabited village on the Indo-Tibetan border in the Baspa valley,
Chitkul (3450 m) sits at the edge of the restricted Kinnaur border zone.
Guesthouses here serve trekkers on the Baspa valley routes and the approach
toward the Baspa Glacier and the Charang La (5241 m). The village has a wooden
temple and traditional Kinnauri architecture.

## 7. Valley of Flowers Rest House, 3600 m — Uttarakhand

In the Valley of Flowers UNESCO World Heritage Site near Badrinath, the
Forest Department rest house at the valley entrance provides accommodation for
those staying multiple nights to explore the extraordinary high-altitude
wildflower meadows in July–August. The valley is closed from November to April.

## 8. Leh-Manali High Camp, 4900 m — Lahaul-Spiti, Himachal Pradesh

On the high passes of the Leh-Manali road (open June–October, blocked by snow
otherwise), HPTDC (Himachal Pradesh Tourism) operated rest houses at various
elevations serve overland travellers. The camps near Baralacha La (4890 m) and
Taglang La (5328 m) provide emergency shelter at passes where overnight stops
are sometimes forced by vehicle breakdowns or storms.

## 9. Har Ki Dun Forest Lodge, 3566 m — Uttarakhand

The Har Ki Dun valley is one of the finest trekking valleys in Uttarakhand —
a glaciated hanging valley above the Tons river tributary with traditional
Himalayan villages and the approach to Ruinsara lake (3900 m). The Forest
Department lodge at Har Ki Dun village (3566 m) provides basic dormitory
accommodation for trekkers on the standard approach from Sankri.

## 10. Dzongri Rest House, 4020 m — Sikkim

On the Goecha La trek toward the Kangchenjunga sanctuary in Sikkim, the
Dzongri rest house at 4020 m is the standard overnight for the approach to
Dzongri La (4580 m) and the viewpoint. The Sikkim Forest Department maintains
this facility within the Khangchendzonga National Park (UNESCO World Heritage).
A trekking permit and a guide are mandatory for all Sikkim high-altitude routes.

## Trekking permits and Inner Line Permits

India's Himalayan regions are subject to a complex permit system that varies
by state and by proximity to the international border. The Inner Line Permit
(ILP) — a colonial-era system that restricts access to border areas — applies
in Arunachal Pradesh, Manipur, Mizoram, and Nagaland. In Sikkim, permits are
required for all high-altitude trekking; these are obtained through licensed
trekking agencies in Gangtok or Darjeeling.

Uttarakhand (Garhwal and Kumaon) is generally accessible without permits for
Indian nationals; foreign nationals need to register and obtain a Protected
Area Permit (PAP) for areas within 5 km of the Chinese border. The Uttarkashi
and Chamoli district areas that contain most of the major high treks
(Gaumukh, Kedartal, Valley of Flowers) require Forest Department registration.

Himachal Pradesh is the most accessible state for Himalayan trekking, with
minimal permit requirements. Spiti Valley — the most dramatic high-altitude
landscape in Himachal, at 3800–4500 m — requires an ILP for foreign nationals
in certain sections near the Tibetan border.

## The monsoon season

Indian Himalayan trekking is strongly seasonal due to the southwest monsoon.
From mid-June to mid-September, the monsoon brings heavy rainfall to most
of the Himalayan foothills and middle elevations. The exception is the
rain-shadow areas north of the main Himalayan crest: Spiti Valley, Ladakh,
and the upper Zanskar are in the rain shadow and remain accessible through
the monsoon period — in fact, their best season is July–August.

The post-monsoon season (mid-September to November) is the most popular
trekking period across all Himalayan states: clear skies, excellent visibility,
and stable temperatures. October is the peak month for the Roopkund, Har Ki
Dun, and other classic Uttarakhand treks.

The Valley of Flowers National Park in Uttarakhand is UNESCO World Heritage
and is accessible only from mid-July to September when the flowers are in
bloom. The GMVN (Garhwal Mandal Vikas Nigam) guesthouses at Ghangaria
(3048 m) serve as the base for both the Valley of Flowers and the Hemkund
Sahib Sikh pilgrimage site (4329 m). Both are exceptionally popular in
August; book GMVN accommodation well in advance.
