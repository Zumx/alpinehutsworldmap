A helicopter rescue in the Swiss Alps costs between CHF 5,000 and CHF 20,000
depending on the complexity of the operation. A full mountain rescue involving
multiple helicopter sorties, a ground team, and hospitalisation can exceed
CHF 50,000. Without insurance, these costs fall entirely on the rescued person.
Understanding what cover you have — and closing the gaps before you leave —
is not a bureaucratic formality. It is a financial safety matter of real
significance.

## How rescue works in Switzerland

Switzerland has two primary helicopter rescue operators for mountain emergencies:
REGA (Rettungsflugwacht/Garde Aérienne de Sauvetage) and Air Glaciers. Both
operate rescue helicopters across the Swiss Alps, with REGA covering the broader
Swiss territory and Air Glaciers specialising in the Valais and western
Bernese Oberland.

Calling REGA (emergency number 1414, or the international +41 333 333 333)
initiates a rescue assessment. REGA dispenses a crew that includes a rescue
specialist and paramedic alongside the pilot. The decision to fly is based on
conditions and medical urgency, not on whether the patient has insurance.

REGA is funded partly by a "sponsors" programme: an annual contribution of
CHF 40 per individual or CHF 70 per family provides coverage for rescue costs
not covered by insurance, for operations in Switzerland and in some
international situations where REGA is asked to repatriate Swiss nationals.
The sponsorship is widely understood as non-optional for anyone regularly
in the Swiss mountains — the annual cost is trivially small compared to the
potential liability.

## Austria and ÖAV insurance

Austrian Alpine Club (ÖAV) membership includes a rescue insurance component
that covers rescue costs in Austria and provides partial coverage in other
Alpine countries for climbing-related emergencies. The ÖAV rescue cover is
one of the most comprehensive available to a recreational mountaineer in
Europe for the cost of club membership (EUR 50–80 per year for adults).

The DAV (German Alpine Club) offers similar coverage through its membership
structure, as does the SAC. In each case, the rescue coverage is bundled with
the membership rather than separately purchased.

For non-residents planning significant alpine activities in Austria — hut
traverses, ski touring, technical climbing — ÖAV guest membership or specific
mountain insurance is strongly advisable. Austrian mountain rescue is performed
by the Bergrettung, a network of volunteer rescue teams that operate throughout
the Austrian Alps and are funded partly through the Alpine club system.

## France: PGHM and CSMV

In France, mountain rescue is provided by two state-funded bodies. The PGHM
(Peloton de Gendarmerie de Haute Montagne) is a specialised gendarmerie unit
with the primary mountain rescue mandate in high-alpine zones including the
Mont Blanc massif, the Pyrenees, and major Alpine ranges. The CSMV (Compagnie
de Sécurité des Massifs des Vosges) covers lower ranges.

An important and frequently misunderstood feature of French mountain rescue:
in France, helicopter rescue by the PGHM is a public service and does not
generate direct costs to the rescued person in most circumstances. The helicopter
is provided by the state and the PGHM crew are civil servants. This does not
mean insurance is unnecessary — hospitalisation, subsequent medical treatment,
and the costs incurred if a private helicopter is needed for a non-emergency
are not covered — but it does mean that the rescue itself will not generate
a CHF 15,000 bill.

## Italy

In Italy, mountain rescue is performed by the Soccorso Alpino e Speleologico,
with regional sections throughout the Alps and Dolomites. The cost structure
is regionally variable; in some regions, rescue is free; in others, costs are
billed. Travel insurance with specific helicopter rescue coverage is advisable
for any serious technical objective in Italy.

## What travel insurance covers (and doesn't)

Standard travel insurance policies typically cover emergency medical evacuation
but have specific conditions around "hazardous activities." Many standard
policies exclude alpine climbing, ski touring, via ferrata, or activities above
a specific elevation. Reading the policy before the trip — specifically the
"hazardous activities" and "altitude" clauses — is essential.

Policies that explicitly include alpine activities are available from specialist
providers including Allianz Assistance (with alpine module), UIAA-recommended
providers, and the club membership insurances described above. The combination
of REGA sponsorship (for Switzerland), ÖAV membership (for Austria), and a
specialist travel policy that covers climbing globally is the standard
preparation for a season of serious alpine travel.

## Practical steps before departure

1. Check your existing travel insurance for alpine exclusions. Address any gaps
before arriving at the trailhead.
2. Buy REGA sponsorship if operating in Switzerland: CHF 40, done in 5 minutes
at rega.ch.
3. If not already a member, consider ÖAV or SAC membership for the rescue
insurance component alongside hut discounts.
4. Store emergency numbers in your phone before leaving the valley: REGA 1414,
Austrian mountain rescue 140, French PGHM 112 (EU emergency) or specific
regional numbers.

The mountains are safer with these arrangements in place. They do not change
the terrain or the weather, but they ensure that if something goes wrong, the
financial and logistical response is not an additional catastrophe on top of
the physical one.

## The global picture: rescue outside Europe

Mountain rescue outside Europe and North America is substantially less
formalised. In Nepal, helicopter rescue is available but expensive; the
helicopter operators serving the Everest region charge approximately USD
3,000–5,000 for a rescue flight from the Khumbu valley. In Pakistan's
Karakoram, rescue helicopters are operated by the Pakistani army and
require diplomatic-level coordination for international mountaineering
casualties. In Patagonia, weather conditions frequently prevent helicopter
rescue for days at a time.

The standard advice for travel in these regions is to carry specific
mountaineering rescue insurance from a provider that covers the local
costs and coordination. Global Rescue, Ripcord, Battleface, and BMC
(British Mountaineering Council) all provide policies tailored for high-
altitude and remote mountain travel. The specific cost of a Himalayan
rescue should be confirmed with the insurer before the trip; the coverage
maximum on a standard policy (often USD 100,000–300,000) may not cover
all costs of a full expedition rescue.

## Self-rescue capability

Insurance and rescue services provide a safety net; they do not substitute
for self-rescue capability. The fundamental skills — crevasse rescue with
prussiks, companion rescue from avalanche burial, emergency bivouac
construction, and emergency first aid — are what keep parties functional
in the time between an incident and the arrival of professional rescue.

Alpine courses from the SAC, UIAA member clubs, and national mountain
guides associations routinely include these skills in their syllabi.
The time spent learning crevasse rescue in a training session on a
glacier near a hut is time that may, in extremis, save a life.

## Communication in the backcountry

The ability to call for rescue depends on having communication. Most Alpine
hut areas are within mobile phone coverage, but this coverage degrades
rapidly on approaches to high and remote huts. A spot satellite messenger
(SPOT, Garmin inReach) or satellite phone provides communication where
mobile networks fail. The Garmin inReach allows two-way text messaging via
the Iridium satellite network from essentially anywhere on Earth; it also
provides a one-button SOS that connects directly to the GEOS rescue
coordination centre.

Before carrying a satellite communicator into a hut network, confirm
whether the device works in the specific region: inReach requires an active
subscription; SPOT requires an activated plan. Both require pre-registration
of emergency contacts and preferred rescue services. Setting up a device
for the first time at the trailhead is too late.
