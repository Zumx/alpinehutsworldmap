For nearly a century, the Abbot Pass Hut stood at 2,925 metres on the narrow saddle of the Continental Divide between Mount Victoria and Mount Lefroy, straddling the border of Alberta and British Columbia. Built of local stone in 1922, it was for a long time the second-highest permanently habitable structure in Canada and one of the most coveted overnight stops in the Canadian Rockies. Then, in the early 2020s, the slope beneath it began to give way as the permafrost holding the pass together thawed, and Parks Canada was forced to close and ultimately dismantle the historic refuge. Its story is now a stark parable of climate change in the high mountains.

## A Hut on the Great Divide

Abbot Pass connects two of the most famous landscapes in the Rockies: the glacier-fed Lake Louise on the Alberta side and the jewel-like Lake O'Hara basin in Yoho National Park on the British Columbia side. The pass is named for Philip Stanley Abbot, an American climber who died on Mount Lefroy in 1896 in what is often cited as the first mountaineering fatality in North America. His death helped prompt the Canadian Pacific Railway to bring Swiss mountain guides to the Rockies, and it was those guides and their employers who saw the value of a high refuge on the pass.

The hut was constructed by Swiss guides working for the railway, who hauled the stone and supplies up the brutal scree and ice of the approach. For decades it served climbers tackling Mount Victoria and Mount Lefroy, two classic glaciated peaks, as well as adventurous trekkers crossing between the two valleys. In 1992 it was designated a National Historic Site of Canada, a recognition of both its craftsmanship and its place in mountaineering history.

## The Death Trap and the Approach

Reaching Abbot Pass was never easy. The classic approach from the Lake Louise side climbs past the Plain of Six Glaciers and then up a long, steep couloir of loose rock and old snow known, with grim affection, as the Death Trap. The name is earned: the gully funnels rockfall and avalanche debris from the surrounding glaciers, and timing and conditions matter enormously. The alternative approach from Lake O'Hara crosses glaciated terrain and also demands mountaineering skill.

This was never a casual walker's hut. It sat squarely in alpine terrain, and reaching it safely required crampons, ice axe, rope work and sound judgement about rockfall and snow conditions. That seriousness was part of its appeal: a night in the stone hut on the very crest of the Divide was a reward earned through genuine effort.

## Built to Last, Undone by a Warming Climate

The irony of the Abbot Pass Hut is that it was built superbly, of solid stone, to endure the fierce weather of its exposed saddle, and it did so for a hundred years. What undid it was not the wind or the snow but the warming of the ground itself. The pass and the slopes around it are held together in part by permafrost, frozen ground that has remained stable for millennia. As average temperatures rose, that permafrost began to thaw, and the slope on the Lake Oesa side of the hut started to erode and slump.

Parks Canada monitored the movement, attempted stabilisation, and ultimately concluded that the ground could no longer safely support the structure. The hut was closed to overnight use and, after careful study, the decision was taken to dismantle it. For many in the Canadian climbing community, the loss was deeply felt: a beloved historic refuge brought down not by neglect but by a changing climate, a tangible casualty in a landscape where glaciers are visibly retreating year on year.

## What Its Loss Tells Us

The closure of the Abbot Pass Hut is more than a sad footnote. It is a visible marker of how climate change is reshaping the high mountains. Across the Rockies, glaciers such as those feeding Lake Louise and the Plain of Six Glaciers are shrinking, rockfall is increasing as faces destabilise, and classic routes are changing or disappearing. The hut's fate has become a reference point in conversations about how mountain infrastructure, and mountaineering itself, must adapt to a warmer world.

It also raises hard questions about preservation. A National Historic Site that cannot be kept safe in place forces a reckoning: what do we owe to historic structures when the ground beneath them is literally moving? The careful, painful decision to dismantle rather than risk a collapse reflects a sober new reality for mountain heritage.

## Canada's Wider Backcountry Hut Network

While the Abbot Pass Hut is gone, the Canadian backcountry retains a rich and varied network of huts and lodges. The Alpine Club of Canada operates a celebrated system of alpine huts across the Rockies and the Coast Mountains, providing bunks and shelter for climbers and ski tourers in remote terrain. In the Sea to Sky corridor of British Columbia, modern huts such as the Kees and Claire Hut on the Spearhead Traverse near Whistler carry the tradition forward with contemporary design.

Beyond the club huts, British Columbia is dotted with backcountry lodges, ski cabins and remote chalets, from glacier lodges in the interior ranges to rustic cabins reached only by floatplane or a long approach. This network means that, even without Abbot Pass, the Canadian high country remains one of the world's great destinations for hut-based mountaineering and ski touring.

## Remembering the Stone Hut

For those who slept at Abbot Pass before its closure, the memory is vivid: the long, anxious climb up the Death Trap, the sudden arrival at the saddle with its sweeping views down two valleys, and the snug solidity of the stone walls as night fell on the Divide. The hut embodied an era when Swiss guides shaped Canadian alpinism and when a high stone refuge was the height of mountain ambition.

Its absence now marks the pass, and the empty saddle stands as a reminder that even the most solidly built monuments are not immune to the slow, grinding force of a warming planet.
