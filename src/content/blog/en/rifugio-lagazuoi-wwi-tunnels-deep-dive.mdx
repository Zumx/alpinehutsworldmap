## A Battlefield in the Sky

The Lagazuoi massif in the Ampezzaner Dolomites sits at the head of the Falzarego Pass at 2105 metres, with its main summit reaching 2835 metres. During the First World War, from 1915 to 1917, it was the site of an extraordinarily violent and now largely forgotten chapter of the conflict — a two-year campaign of underground mine warfare fought inside the limestone mountain itself, in tunnels dug simultaneously by Austrian and Italian military engineers from opposite sides of the same rock face.

The Austro-Italian front of the First World War ran for over 600 kilometres from the Swiss border to the Adriatic, and in the Dolomites it followed the pre-war border between the Austro-Hungarian Empire and the Kingdom of Italy directly through the high alpine terrain. The Lagazuoi peak sat on the Austrian side of the line; the Col dei Bos, directly below, was Italian-held. The mountain became the literal dividing line, and both armies concluded that the best way to dislodge the other from the sheer limestone walls was to blow them up from below.

## The Mining Campaign: 1915 to 1917

Austrian engineers began tunnel work in the Lagazuoi in late 1915, digging a network of galleries through the soft Dolomite limestone toward Italian positions. The Italian Genio Militare responded in kind. The tactics of mine warfare were well established in the Western Front trench warfare context, but in the Dolomites they were adapted for vertical terrain — miners worked upward through the rock at steep angles to place charges under ledges and observation posts that sat hundreds of metres above the valley floor.

The Austrian galleries extended to over 1000 metres of total tunnel length. The deepest Italian mine chamber was placed approximately 30 metres below the crest of a Lagazuoi spur and detonated in the summer of 1917, destroying an Austrian garrison position. The resulting crater — visible today on the eastern flank of the mountain — measures approximately 25 metres across and 10 metres deep.

The explosions were often catastrophic for both sides. The blast from a mine detonation in limestone propagated differently from the chalk and clay of Flanders. Some chambers failed to produce the expected damage. Others killed the mining crews when the explosion direction was misjudged. The landscape that resulted from two years of this work is visible in the fractured rock faces and debris fields still clearly readable on the mountain flanks.

## Why the Tunnels Survived

Most First World War military infrastructure was deliberately demolished or allowed to deteriorate after the armistice. The Lagazuoi tunnel system survived for a combination of reasons. The limestone geology preserved the tunnel structure better than the clay soils of the Western Front; there was no farming or construction pressure to fill or demolish underground features in the high alpine terrain; and the mountain passed into Italian sovereignty under the Treaty of Saint-Germain in 1919, where it became a national monument and tourism destination.

The Museo della Grande Guerra — the regional First World War museum network in the Cadore and Ampezzano — took an active interest in the site from the 1960s and 1970s, working to stabilise the tunnel entrances and document the layout before they could be further damaged by freeze-thaw weathering.

Today, approximately 1000 metres of tunnel are accessible to visitors, with electric lighting in sections and minimal artificial modification to the original structure. The tunnel entrance near the Rifugio Lagazuoi is the primary access point.

## Rifugio Lagazuoi: The Connection Point

The Rifugio Lagazuoi (2752 m) sits on the summit plateau of the Lagazuoi massif, reached by cable car from the Falzarego Pass or by foot from the same pass via a WWI-era military trail known as the Cengia Martini. The rifugio was built in the 1960s on the site of former Austrian military emplacements and has expanded several times since. It provides dormitory accommodation for around 70 guests and is staffed from mid-June to late September.

The cable car from Falzarego — operating since 1967 — makes the summit accessible to day visitors, which has dramatically increased the traffic to both the rifugio and the tunnel system. The cable car carries 80 passengers per hour and runs continuously during staffed season. This accessibility means the tunnels receive several tens of thousands of visitors annually, a number that creates wear on the tunnel floors and some of the more fragile infrastructure.

## The Tunnels: What to Expect on a Visit

The main accessible gallery begins a few minutes' walk from the cable car upper station. Visitors descend into the mountain through a reinforced entrance cut into a limestone face. The tunnels are typically 1.5 to 2 metres high and 0.8 to 1.2 metres wide — narrow enough that groups pass single-file, and short enough that tall visitors crouch for extended sections.

Temperature inside is stable at approximately 5 to 8 degrees Celsius year-round — cold, damp, and consistent regardless of summer heat on the surface. A warm layer and gloves are not excessive even in July. Headlamps are recommended — the electric lighting is intermittent and some sections are dimly illuminated.

The tunnels contain preserved infrastructure including ammunition alcoves, sleeping niches cut into the walls for soldiers who lived underground during extended firing periods, drainage channels, and the remains of ventilation shafts that were drilled upward to the surface. Original graffiti in German and Italian from soldiers is visible on some walls, protected under a transparent coating.

## Visiting Responsibly

The tunnels are not a managed indoor museum exhibit — they are a largely unmodified underground site with loose rock, slippery floors, and occasional falls from frost action. Visitors have been injured by falling stones and by stumbling in poorly lit sections. Groups with young children or visitors with claustrophobia should assess carefully before entering the deeper sections.

Photography with flash is permitted and widely practised, but touching the tunnel walls accelerates deterioration of the limestone surface and the original graffiti. The tunnel floors contain material from the wartime occupation that is archaeologically significant — food waste, equipment fragments, personal items — and removing objects is illegal under Italian heritage law.

The tunnels are accessed directly from the rifugio and there is no separate admission fee for the basic tunnel network; access to the site is considered part of visiting the rifugio area. Guided tours in Italian and German are offered daily during the staffed season from a desk at the rifugio.
