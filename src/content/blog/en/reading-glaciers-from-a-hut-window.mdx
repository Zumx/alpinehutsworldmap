The best mountaineering decisions are often made with a coffee in hand, hours before the first step onto the ice. From the terrace of a glacier hut — the Cabane du Trient looking out over its great white plateau, the Hollandiahütte above the Lötschenlücke, the Rifugio Mantova below the Lyskamm — a whole day's route lies open to study. A glacier is not a blank sheet of snow but a living, moving body with a grain and a logic, and a climber who learns to read it from the window arrives at the difficult ground already knowing where the danger lies and where the line goes.

## The Glacier as a River of Ice

Start by seeing the glacier for what it is: a river of ice flowing downhill under its own weight, fast in the steeps and slow on the flats, cracking apart where it bends or steepens. Where the ground beneath drops away, the ice stretches and tears open into crevasses; where it compresses on a flat, crevasses close. This single idea explains almost everything you see. The smooth, gently sloping basin below the hut is likely sound walking; the convex bulge where the glacier pours over a step is where the crevasses will be widest and the bridges weakest.

Trace the flow lines with your eye from the high snowfields down to the snout. The glacier tells you where it is straining and where it is calm, and your route should favour the calm.

## Crevasse Fields and How to Spot Them

From a hut window crevasses reveal themselves in the low, raking light of early morning and evening, when shadow pools in every slot. At midday, with the sun overhead, the same field can look deceptively flat and white, which is one reason to study it the night before. Look for the tell-tale parallel lines of sagging snow, the faint blue shadows, and the chaotic broken ground of an icefall where the glacier tumbles over a cliff.

Identify the crevasse zones and the calmer corridors between them. A good route threads the gaps, crossing crevasses at right angles where they are narrowest and skirting the worst icefalls entirely. From the comfort of the terrace you can often see the boot-track of earlier parties stitched across the snow, a priceless hint at the safe line, though never one to follow blindly when conditions have changed.

## Seracs: the Danger That Does Not Wait for You

Seracs are the towering blocks and cliffs of ice that form where a glacier breaks over a steep step. They are beautiful and utterly indifferent, and they collapse without warning, on no schedule a climber can predict. Unlike a crevasse, which sits still and waits for a mistake, a serac can fall while you do everything right. The only defence is to spend as little time as possible beneath them.

From the hut, identify every serac band that overhangs your intended line and the debris fan of shattered ice blocks below it, which marks the runout zone. A route that crosses such a zone must be crossed fast and early, before the day's warmth loosens the ice, and ideally not at all. Reading the seracs the evening before lets you plan your timing around them rather than discovering them, too late, at their feet.

## The Morning Freeze and Why Timing Is Everything

Snow strength is not fixed; it swings with temperature through the day. A good overnight freeze welds the snow into firm, supportive bridges over the crevasses and locks loose rocks and seracs in place. As the sun climbs and the day warms, that bond melts, bridges soften and sag, and ground that was safe at four in the morning becomes treacherous by ten. This is why alpine starts exist, and why the warden may wake you before dawn.

From the hut you can judge the freeze before you commit. A clear, cold, still night gives a hard freeze and good early conditions; a warm, cloudy, windy night gives a poor freeze and soft, dangerous snow even at dawn. Step onto the terrace at the small hours, feel the air, look for frost on the railing and a crust on the snow, and listen to whether your boots squeak on firm névé. The window tells you whether to go early, go fast, or wait.

## Light, Weather and the Long View

The hut window is also a weather station. Watch the evening sky and the morning cloud: a lens-shaped cloud capping a peak warns of high wind aloft, a greasy halo around the sun or moon hints at moisture and a front approaching, and clear cold air with sharp shadows promises a settled day. The behaviour of the first clouds at sunrise often foretells the afternoon, when storms most readily build over warmed glaciers.

Take the long view too. Note where the sun will strike your route and at what hour, because a slope baking in afternoon sun is a different, more dangerous place than the same slope frozen in shadow. The patient observer on the terrace gathers all of this for free, turning a relaxed evening into the most valuable planning session of the trip.

## Turning Observation into a Plan

Reading the glacier is only useful if it shapes a decision. By the time you finish your evening study you should have a route in mind, a turnaround time, an idea of which crevasse zones to thread and which serac bands to clear before sunrise, and a backup if the freeze fails. Compare your reading with the map, the guidebook and, best of all, the warden, who watches this glacier every day and knows how this season's conditions differ from the printed description.

The mountains reward those who look before they leap. The skill of reading ice from a hut window is quiet and unglamorous, but it is the difference between a route walked with understanding and one stumbled into blind. The view from the terrace is not just scenery; it is the most detailed briefing you will ever get.
