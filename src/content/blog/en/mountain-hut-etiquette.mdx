Alpine huts run on a set of conventions that have evolved over 150 years of
concentrated use in small spaces by large numbers of tired people. Most of the
rules are practical rather than ceremonial, and the ones that seem fussy — silent
hours, boot room discipline, fixed breakfast slots — exist because violating
them affects every other guest in the building. Knowing them before you arrive
makes the stay better for you and for everyone sharing the space.

## The boot room

The Schuhraum or local equivalent is the airlock between the mountain and the
hut. All outdoor footwear — boots, trail runners, crampons, gaiters — stays
here. You will find numbered pegs, boot racks, and sometimes a drying area
near a radiator or wood stove. Change into your hut slippers immediately.
Walking through the dining room in hiking boots is the surest way to identify
yourself as someone who has never done this before.

In wet weather, the boot room fills fast. Hang wet gear efficiently and do not
spread across more than your fair share of hooks. Wet socks and gloves left
over a communal radiator for 12 hours create a specific odour that nobody
enjoys. The guardian will ask you to move them.

## Silent hours

Silent hours (Nachtruhe in German, silenzio in Italian) run from approximately
21:00 to 06:00 across the Alps. This is not a posted guideline at most huts —
it is a universal expectation. Some huts post the hours explicitly; most
simply expect guests to know.

After 21:00: no talking above a whisper in dormitories, no phone calls, no
bright overhead lights. Use a headlamp on red mode if you need to move around
in the dark. If you are returning to the dorm after the common room closes,
prepare your alarm, clothes, and breakfast items beforehand so you can leave
without waking others.

In common rooms and dining areas, normal conversation continues until they
close, usually around 21:30 or 22:00. After that, they are sometimes available
for quiet use but not for a social evening.

## Bunk allocation by the guardian

You do not choose your bunk. In a Matratzenlager dormitory, the guardian
assigns beds — often filling dorms sequentially and mixing parties as needed.
This is how a hut accommodates maximum guests in a finite space. You may be
placed next to people you did not arrive with. Requests for adjacent bunks
within a party are usually accommodated, but separate dorms are not guaranteed
unless private rooms are booked.

Claim your bunk, place your sleeping bag and liner on it, and leave the rest
of your gear at the base of the bed or on the hook provided. Do not spread
equipment across adjacent empty bunks — another party may arrive hours after
you.

## Breakfast slots

Many larger huts run breakfast in two or three sittings. Your sitting is
assigned when you check in, often based on your planned departure time. If you
need an early start — a 4:30 summit departure, for example — tell the guardian
at dinner the night before. Most huts will put out bread, jam, and a thermos
of coffee for pre-dawn departures, or prepare a Lunchpaket as a substitute.
Do not appear for breakfast in a sitting that was not assigned to you;
the kitchen and dining space are sized for the allocation.

## Leave no trace in the hut

Hut waste management is a serious logistical problem. In remote locations,
everything that cannot be composted or burned must be carried down by the
guardian or by supply helicopter. Do not leave packaging, food waste, used
gear, or forgotten items behind. Take your rubbish with you or deposit it
only in the designated bins where provided. Wet wipes and hygiene products do
not go in composting bins.

Leave the dormitory roughly as you found it. Fold or stack blankets, return
pillow covers to the pillow, and do not repurpose dormitory towels for cleaning
boots.

## Headlamp discipline

At night, bright white headlamp modes are intrusive in a shared dormitory. Red
mode is the standard for moving around at night — it preserves night vision,
is less disruptive to sleeping neighbours, and is perfectly adequate for
navigating to the bathroom. Keep a headlamp accessible from your bunk so you
do not need to unlock a bag in the dark.

## Paying in cash and alpine club discounts

Cash is the default payment method in alpine huts, and at remote huts it may
be the only option. Card readers fail at altitude due to connectivity issues,
and many smaller huts simply do not have them. Carry sufficient euros or Swiss
francs in small denominations — a CHF 500 or €200 note will not be broken
without friction.

Alpine club membership (CAI, SAC/CAS, ÖAV, DAV, FFCAM, PZS) entitles members
to a meaningful discount on half-board rates, typically €5–15 per night, and
priority in reservations at affiliated huts. The UIAA reciprocity agreement
means that most national club cards are recognised at huts across all member
countries. A DAV card works at SAC huts; a CAI card works at ÖAV huts. Carry
the card, as it is checked at payment. The discount compounds significantly
over a seven-day traverse — membership pays for itself in under a week of
hut nights.

## The guardian relationship

The Hüttenwirt or guardian is not a hotel manager. They are running a complex
operation in an extreme environment: cooking for 40 people, managing weather
forecasts, coordinating rescues, maintaining water and power systems, and
enforcing the reservation list, all in a building accessible only on foot or
by helicopter. Treat them with the respect that role deserves. Ask questions
in the morning or early evening, not at 22:00. Follow instructions about
route conditions and safety closures without argument.
