## Why Bedding Policies Matter for Packing

Arriving at a hut at 6 pm with a full sleeping bag inside a pack that was already heavy enough on the climb is one of the more avoidable miseries in alpine touring. Arriving with only a liner and finding that the hut provides nothing except a bare mattress is an uncomfortable alternative. The bedding situation at alpine huts varies more than most new visitors expect, and the variation is not random — it follows national patterns, altitude bands, and the distinction between summer trekking huts and winter touring huts.

Getting this right is a simple matter of understanding the system by country and by hut type, and then making a packing decision that treats weight seriously.

## Austria: Blankets Standard, Sheets Optional

ÖAV and DAV huts in Austria almost universally provide blankets or duvets in their dormitories. The standard sleeping arrangement is a fixed bunk with a thin mattress, a pillow with a pillowcase, and a blanket or duvet. In summer (June to September), the duvets are typically adequate for temperatures in the dormitory, which rarely drops below 10 degrees Celsius in staffed huts.

A sleeping bag liner adds a hygiene layer — the blankets and pillowcases are laundered periodically, but at busy huts in peak season the laundry schedule is under stress. A 100-gram silk or 150-gram merino liner is the standard addition for walkers who stay at multiple huts in a row. A full sleeping bag is unnecessary in summer at staffed Austrian huts.

In the ski touring season (February to May), ÖAV huts at higher altitudes can be cold at night — duvets are provided but dormitories in old stone buildings may see temperatures in single digits. A liner rated to 5 degrees adds meaningful warmth without the bulk of a full sleeping bag.

## Switzerland: SAC Huts and the Blanket Standard

SAC huts maintain a similar standard to their Austrian counterparts — mattresses with blankets or duvets provided. The SAC publishes explicit guidelines for hut keepers requiring that sleeping accommodation includes bedding, and inspections check compliance. In practice, SAC huts tend to be newer and better insulated than the average ÖAV hut, which means dormitories are warmer and the blankets provided are more consistently adequate.

A silk liner is the recommended packing choice for SAC huts in summer. For the Chamonix-Zermatt Haute Route in late June or early July, when huts at 3000 metres can be cold and the heating is not always running at night, a thin synthetic sleeping bag around 300 grams rated to 5 degrees is a sensible upgrade for the night at the Cabane des Vignettes (3157 m) or the Cabane de Bertol (3311 m).

SAC huts do not expect guests to bring their own bedding. Guests who show up with full expedition sleeping bags will find the bag unnecessary but not offensive; the hut keeper's only concern is that you do not sleep directly on the duvet with your trail clothes on.

## French Refuges: More Variation, Some Bare Mattresses

The French CAF refuge system is less standardised than the Austrian or Swiss systems. Larger and better-maintained refuges — the Refuge du Goûter (3835 m), the Refuge de la Charpoua (2841 m), the Refuge du Couvercle (2687 m) — provide full bedding including blankets and pillow. Smaller and more remote refuges may provide a blanket only, without a pillowcase, and some very remote mountain refuges supply nothing beyond the mattress itself.

The Refuges.info booking platform includes notes on bedding provision for most listed huts, which makes pre-trip research straightforward. A sleeping bag liner is more useful in France than in Switzerland or Austria — bring one. For the higher-altitude and more remote French refuges, a thin bag rated to 5 or 10 degrees is the safe default.

## Italy: Rifugio Standards and Private Management

Italian rifugi vary considerably because many are managed by private concessionaires rather than directly by the CAI. Popular Dolomite rifugi accessible by cable car or road — Rifugio Auronzo, Rifugio Lagazuoi (2752 m), Rifugio Citta di Trento (2438 m) — typically provide full hotel-style bedding including sheets, blankets, and a pillow, reflecting their high proportion of day-visitor and casual overnight guests.

More remote rifugi in the alpine zones of the western Alps and the Apennines operate at a different standard. Some provide a blanket and a pillow with no fitted sheet; others provide nothing. Phoning ahead to verify is more often necessary in Italy than in the German-speaking or Swiss systems.

At Dolomite rifugi specifically, a sleeping bag liner is largely optional in summer but is appreciated for the hygiene benefit. July and August nights at 2400 to 2700 metres are not cold enough in the dormitory to require thermal insulation beyond the provided blanket.

## Germany: DAV Huts Follow Austrian/Swiss Standard

DAV huts in Bavaria and the German Alps follow the same standard as ÖAV huts — blankets or duvets provided, pillow provided, sleeping bag not required. The Zugspitzhaus (2805 m), the Knorrhütte (2051 m), and the Meilerhütte (2366 m) all provide standard bedding. A liner is recommended for hygiene but a full bag is superfluous in summer.

## Scandinavian Huts: The Self-Service Variable

Norwegian DNT and Swedish STF huts diverge from the Alpine model in an important way. The majority of Scandinavian mountain huts are self-service (selvbetjening/självbetjäning) — the hut is open but unstaffed, with a wood stove, basic cooking equipment, and fixed bunks. These huts do not provide bedding. A sleeping bag rated to minus 5 or minus 10 degrees is mandatory for DNT self-service huts in Norway; the nights can be genuinely cold even in late July at high elevations. Staffed DNT and STF service huts (betjente hytter) are the exception and do provide blankets.

## The Sleeping Bag Liner Decision

A 100-gram silk liner serves three purposes: hygiene, a few degrees of additional warmth, and a social courtesy that the hut keeper appreciates. A 150-gram merino liner adds more warmth and is more durable than silk. Either option is the correct choice for summer hut touring in the Alps.

A full sleeping bag is warranted for: Scandinavian self-service huts, Pyrenean huts above 2500 metres in the shoulder season, and any route in the Alps planned for early June or late September when dormitory temperatures may fall below what a duvet manages. Above-treeline huts at elevation that have stone walls and single-pane windows lose heat rapidly on clear nights.
