At 4,370 metres on a barren glacial moraine in Argentina's Mendoza Province, a temporary city of several hundred tents materialises every austral summer at the foot of Aconcagua. Plaza de Mulas is the principal basecamp for the Normal Route up the highest mountain on Earth outside Asia, a peak that tops out at 6,961 metres. For six or seven weeks each season the camp hums with porters, mules, guides, doctors and climbers from a dozen countries, all staging for an ascent that demands two to three weeks of patient acclimatisation. It is one of the largest and best-organised mountaineering basecamps anywhere on the planet.

## The Mountain Above

Aconcagua sits in the Andes, roughly 110 kilometres northwest of the city of Mendoza and close to the Chilean border near the Cristo Redentor pass. The Cerro Aconcagua and the surrounding terrain are protected within Parque Provincial Aconcagua, which controls access through a permit system administered from Mendoza. The Normal Route from the northwest is non-technical in mountaineering terms — there is no roped climbing or glacier travel of any difficulty — but the altitude is brutal. The summit is high enough that supplemental oxygen, while rarely used, sits at the edge of necessity, and the death rate on the mountain has long made it one of the more dangerous of the so-called accessible giants.

The approach to Plaza de Mulas begins at the Horcones ranger station near the Puente del Inca, the famous natural rock arch and hot-spring formation on Ruta Nacional 7. From there the trail runs up the Quebrada de los Horcones, past the intermediate camps of Confluencia at 3,400 metres and the long, dusty Playa Ancha, before the final grind up to the basecamp moraine.

## A City of Tents and Domes

Plaza de Mulas is not a single building but an encampment that grows and shrinks with the season. The various commercial expedition operators erect large geodesic dome tents that function as dining halls, kitchens and communications hubs, surrounded by clusters of personal tents pitched on flattened stone platforms. The original Refugio Plaza de Mulas, sometimes called the highest hotel in the world when it operated, sits a short distance from the main camp; its fortunes have varied over the years, but the camp's social and logistical centre has always been the dome cities of the outfitters.

The camp has a medical post staffed by doctors during the climbing season, a feature that distinguishes Aconcagua from most high mountains. Every climber on the Normal Route is required to undergo a medical check, and the doctors monitor for high-altitude pulmonary and cerebral oedema, the two killers that stalk anyone moving too fast above 5,000 metres. Helicopter evacuation from basecamp is possible, which is part of why so many less-experienced high-altitude climbers attempt the mountain.

## The Acclimatisation Rhythm

A typical Aconcagua expedition spends close to twenty days on the mountain, and a large share of that time is anchored at Plaza de Mulas. The pattern is the classic high-altitude sawtooth: climb high, sleep low. From basecamp, teams make carry days up to Camp Canada at around 5,000 metres and Nido de Cóndores at 5,570 metres, ferrying loads and gaining altitude before descending to sleep at the lower camp. The body needs days, not hours, to manufacture the extra red blood cells that make the thin air survivable.

This rhythm means that Plaza de Mulas becomes a kind of home. Climbers settle into a routine of rest days, laundry in cold meltwater, card games in the dome, and anxious study of the weather forecasts that the camp managers post. The notorious Andean wind, the viento blanco, can pin an entire camp down for days, and patience is the single most important trait an Aconcagua climber can possess.

## Mules, Porters and Logistics

The name Plaza de Mulas — the place of the mules — tells you how the camp is supplied. Long trains of mules carry duffel bags, food, fuel and equipment up the Horcones valley from the trailhead, a job they have done for over a century since the first expeditions probed the mountain. The Italian-born guide Matthias Zurbriggen made the first recorded ascent in 1897 as part of Edward FitzGerald's expedition, and the mule logistics that supported that effort are recognisably the ancestors of today's operation.

Modern outfitters run sophisticated supply chains, with fixed menus, satellite internet in some domes, and porters available for hire to carry loads to the high camps. The contrast between the comfort of basecamp and the spartan brutality of the summit push is one of the defining experiences of the climb.

## Other Andean Refuges for Context

Aconcagua's basecamp culture is distinct from the smaller club refuges scattered through the Argentine Andes further south. In the Bariloche region of Patagonia, the Club Andino Bariloche maintains a network of staffed mountain huts that could not be more different in character — the Refugio San Martín (also known as Refugio Jakob), the Refugio Emilio Frey beneath the granite spires of the Catedral massif, the Refugio Italia Manfredo Segre at Laguna Negra, and the Refugio López. Further into the Comarca Andina, walkers link the Refugio Hielo Azul and the Refugio Cajón de Azul on multi-day treks through lenga forest.

These southern refuges offer bunks, hot meals and a convivial alpine-club atmosphere that has more in common with the European hut tradition than with the industrial-scale basecamp at Plaza de Mulas. Mentioning them underlines just how singular Aconcagua's camp is: a high-altitude logistics machine rather than a cosy refuge.

## Practicalities and the Season

The Aconcagua climbing season runs roughly from mid-November to the end of February, with the most stable weather usually falling in December and January. Permits must be purchased in advance and prices vary sharply between the low shoulder weeks and the high season; the park authority sets these annually. Independent climbers can attempt the mountain self-supported, carrying their own loads from basecamp, but the majority join guided expeditions that handle permits, mules, food and the all-important basecamp infrastructure.

Anyone considering Aconcagua should arrive already fit and ideally already acclimatised from time spent at altitude elsewhere. The mountain forgives a slow, patient climber far more readily than a strong, impatient one.
