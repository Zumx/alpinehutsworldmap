## The Plateau at the Heart of Norway

Hardangervidda is a landscape unlike any other in Europe. Stretching roughly 8,000 square kilometres across the counties of Vestland, Telemark, and Viken, it is the continent's largest mountain plateau — a vast, treeless expanse of fell, glacier remnant, and reindeer pasture sitting at an average elevation of around 1,200 metres. The Hardangervidda National Park, established in 1981, protects the core of this wilderness. The Norwegian Trekking Association (Den Norske Turistforening, or DNT) has threaded the plateau with a network of waymarked trails and staffed huts that make multi-day crossings possible even for hikers without technical mountaineering skills. Summer here runs from late June to mid-September, though snow can arrive in any month. The ten huts below represent the backbone of that network — each one positioned to serve a particular crossing route or to shelter walkers caught by the plateau's notoriously fast-changing weather.

## 1. Finse 1222, Finse

At 1,222 metres above sea level, the Finse 1222 lodge sits beside the Finse station on the Bergen Railway — the only road-free station in Norway accessible by train year-round. The lodge is named after its precise altitude and doubles as the highest point on the railway line. It served as a base for Ronald Amundsen's South Pole preparations and for parts of the *Star Wars* Hoth location filming. DNT-affiliated, the lodge offers dormitory and private rooms for around 100 guests, a full kitchen service, and guided glacier walks onto the Hardangerjøkulen ice cap, the sixth-largest glacier in mainland Norway. The season for hiking access runs from late April to late October, though the lodge stays open all year for ski tourers.

## 2. Haukeliseter Fjellstue, Haukelifjell

Haukeliseter stands at the western edge of the plateau, just off the E134 highway — one of the main mountain crossings between eastern and western Norway. At roughly 1,000 metres, the fjellstue (mountain inn) has operated continuously since the nineteenth century, serving travellers on what was once the main overland route between Oslo and Bergen. Today it functions as a full-service staffed DNT hut with around 150 beds, a restaurant open to non-guests, and direct access to marked trails heading east into the open vidda. It is an obvious starting point for a west-to-east traverse and a reliable last-resupply stop before the wilder interior.

## 3. Litlos, Central Hardangervidda

Litlos is the most isolated staffed hut on the entire plateau. It sits in the geographic heart of Hardangervidda, roughly 27 kilometres by trail from the nearest road, and the walk in takes the better part of a day regardless of the direction of approach. The hut accommodates around 80 guests and is staffed from late June to mid-September. Reindeer are a constant presence in the surrounding terrain; the area supports one of Norway's largest wild reindeer populations, numbering in the tens of thousands. Because of its position, Litlos is a natural midpoint on a full north-south or east-west traverse and is reserved well in advance during the peak July and August weeks.

## 4. Sandhaug, Numedal Approach

Sandhaug lies in the eastern portion of the plateau, offering access from the Numedal valley and the road that climbs toward Dagali. The hut is mid-sized by DNT standards — around 60 beds — and is staffed throughout summer. Its position makes it a key waypoint on the trail linking the eastern road access points with the central huts, and it is frequently combined with Rauhelleren in a two-day leg. The surrounding terrain is gentler than the western plateau, with lower snowpack persistence, which means early-season hikers often find the trails here passable a week or two before the higher western routes open.

## 5. Rauhelleren, Numedal Side

Rauhelleren sits near Numedalsåa, one of the rivers draining the plateau eastward toward the Numedal valley. It is a smaller self-service hut for most of the year, with staffing in peak summer, and it accommodates roughly 40 guests. The name refers to the rocky terrain directly around the hut — rauhelleren translates loosely as "rough ledge." It serves as a critical shelter between Sandhaug and the more remote central huts, and a river ford nearby can be challenging during snowmelt in late June. Hikers on the popular plateau crossing from Rjukan to Eidfjord often stop here on night two or three of their trip.

## 6. Mogenlia, Western Plateau

Mogenlia is a staffed summer hut positioned on the western plateau above the Eidfjord municipality. The approach from the west involves a significant elevation gain from the fjord floor, but the views back over Hardangerfjord on a clear day are exceptional. The hut sleeps around 50 and draws hikers completing the western section of the Hardangervidda traverse as well as day-walkers arriving by boat from Ulvik or Eidfjord. June snowmelt can make the surrounding bogs wet underfoot, but by late July the trails dry to firm gravel and heather. Cloudberries ripen in the terrain around Mogenlia in August, which adds a foraging dimension to an otherwise austere landscape.

## 7. Kiellandbu, Southern Vidda

Kiellandbu is a self-service hut on the southern reaches of the plateau, in the area once used by Amundsen and other polar explorers for winter training. The hut is named after a local family that managed the terrain during the early twentieth century. It sleeps around 30 in bunk rooms, with the standard DNT self-service system of pre-stocked provisions available for purchase. Its southern position connects the plateau trail network to the Telemark region, and it forms a natural waypoint on the trail descending toward Vågslid and the upper Telemark valleys. Snowmobile tracks cross the area in winter, and the hut is accessible on ski from Haukeliseter, roughly 20 kilometres to the west.

## 8. Bjåen Fjellstove, Hovden Area

Bjåen Fjellstove sits near the southern plateau edge, a short walk from the road at Bjåen above the Setesdal valley. As an older farmstead converted to mountain lodging, it retains a distinctly traditional character — low wooden buildings, turf remnants on older outbuildings, and a working farm atmosphere. Staffed through summer, it serves around 40 guests and provides a gentle introduction to the plateau for hikers arriving from the south. The terrain here is relatively accessible: the plateau surface is broad and the elevation gain from Bjåen village is under 400 metres. Combined with Hovden Fjellstoge, it anchors a two-day southern-approach route.

## 9. Hovden Fjellstoge, Setesdal Gateway

Hovden is the main resort village on the plateau's southern fringe, and Hovden Fjellstoge serves the DNT network at this busy access point. The hut is larger than most, with capacity for around 70 guests, and it benefits from proximity to Hovden's infrastructure — ski lifts, a small supermarket, and summer bike rental. From the fjellstoge, marked trails climb north within an hour or two onto the open vidda, connecting toward Sandhaug and eventually to the central huts. The hut is popular for families taking their first steps onto the plateau, and it is also a common endpoint for those completing a north-to-south crossing from Finse.

## 10. Rjukan Fjellstue, Eastern Approach via Telemark

Rjukan Fjellstue provides access from the industrial town of Rjukan in Telemark, famous for the wartime heavy water sabotage operations carried out in the surrounding mountains. The hut is not on the open plateau itself but rather in the steep terrain above the Vestfjorddalen valley, at an elevation where the treeline gives way to open fell. It sleeps around 60 guests and is staffed in summer. From here the trail climbs past Møsvatn reservoir and onto the plateau, where connections to Litlos, Sandhaug, and the wider network become available within one to two hiking days. The approach through Telemark's dramatic gorge landscape is one of the more scenically varied ways to enter Hardangervidda.
