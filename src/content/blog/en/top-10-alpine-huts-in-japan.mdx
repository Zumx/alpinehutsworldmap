Japanese mountain huts — yamagoya (山小屋) — are among the finest in the
world by any measure of meals, service, and infrastructure. In the Kita Alps
(Northern Japanese Alps), particularly the Hotaka and Yari massifs, huts
typically offer two-tatami-mat sleeping spaces, futon bedding, a full
hot dinner and breakfast, and boot storage — all at 2500–3000 m. The
culture is distinct from the European Alps: reservations are obligatory and
standard practice, tatami rooms are shared, and the custom is to arrive in
daylight. All ten huts below are accessible via the [map](/map).

## 1. Karasawa Hut (Karasawa Hytte), 2309 m — Hotaka, Kita Alps

The most famous yamagoya destination in Japan, set in the wide glacial cirque
below Oku-Hotaka (3190 m). Reached from Kamikōchi via the Yokoo approach —
typically 4–5 hours on well-maintained trail. The cirque fills with autumn
colour (koyo) in late September and early October, making the September booking
window the most competitive of any mountain area in Japan. Capacity around 150.
The hut is privately operated and has maintained the same family management
tradition for decades. Book through the hut directly, typically by phone in
April for summer dates.

## 2. Yarigatake Sanso, 3060 m — Yarigatake, Kita Alps

At the foot of the Yari (spear) summit tower at 3180 m — the Matterhorn of
Japan — Yarigatake Sanso is among the highest permanently staffed yamagoya.
The summit itself requires a scramble up exposed rock with iron-rung assists.
Reached via the Kamikōchi–Yokoo–Yari trail (about 6–7 hours from Kamikōchi)
or via the longer Shin-Hotaka Ropeway and Nishi-Kama approach. Capacity
around 250. The surrounding ridges — Higashi-Kama (east ridge) and Nishi-Kama
(west ridge) — extend multi-day traverse options toward Hotaka and the
Sugoroku area.

## 3. Tateyama Murodo Sanso, 2450 m — Tateyama, Kita Alps

Located on the Murodo plateau at the top of the Tateyama Kurobe Alpine Route
— one of the highest public road access points in Japan, open from late April
through November. The snow corridor (yukikairo) in early May reaches up to
20 m high and draws large visitor numbers. At 2450 m with road access, this is
the most accessible high yamagoya in Japan and the entry point for the
Tateyama summit approach (2992 m). Capacity over 300; heavily booked in
Golden Week (late April to early May) and autumn colour season.

## 4. Sugoroku Hut, 2860 m — Yari–Hotaka area, Kita Alps

On the ridge between Yarigatake and Washiba-dake, the Sugoroku Hut is a
staging point on the demanding Yari–Hotaka Main Ridge traverse. This long
ridgeline route requires full-day stages between huts, careful weather
monitoring, and comfort on exposed terrain at or above 2800 m. Capacity
around 80. The terrain on this ridge involves sustained scrambling and no
shelter between huts — strong navigation and weather awareness are essential.

## 5. Hyoutan-ike Hut (Hyoutan Pond Hut), 2985 m — Kita Alps

Near the Gorozawa summit area on the Yari south ridge, the Hyoutan-ike Hut
serves the south approach to Yarigatake from the Nishikamakogen side.
The pond at this elevation is one of the highest alpine lakes in the Kita
Alps. Capacity around 40. This is a quieter approach than the Kamikōchi
routes and attracts experienced walkers seeking the full ridge traverse.

## 6. Akadake Kosen (Akadake Mineral Spring Hut), 2220 m — Yatsugatake

In the Yatsugatake range of the Southern Japanese Alps, Akadake Kosen is a
key overnight point on the approach to Akadake (2899 m), the highest peak
in the range. Reached from Minoto Kogen via the Akadake Kosen trail in
about 3 hours. Yatsugatake is popular year-round, with ice climbing on
Joudo Daira in winter and technical routes in summer. The hut can be
approached by novice hikers in summer; the upper Akadake routes are
more technical.

## 7. Mount Fuji 8th Station Huts (Goraikoukan and others), 3400 m — Fuji-san

Fuji's eight major 8th station huts represent a different category of yamagoya —
tourism infrastructure for the annual pilgrimage season (July to early
September). The Goraikoukan hut at 3450 m on the Yoshida Trail is the most
famous, serving the specific purpose of rest before the 03:00–04:00 summit
push for goraiko (sunrise). Reservations are essential for 8th station huts
during the official season. The summit crater rim (3776 m) has no permanent
staffed hut — emergency shelters only. Altitude sickness is common here as
the typical visitor ascends from near sea level in a single day.

## 8. Senjojiki (Senjojiki Cirque), 2612 m — Kiso-Koma, Chuo Alps

Accessible by ropeway from Komagatake Ropeway station, Senjojiki sits in a
dramatic cirque below Kiso-Koma-ga-take (2956 m). The cirque flower meadows
bloom from late July through August. The ropeway makes this one of the most
accessible high cirque environments in Japan — and one of the most crowded
during koyo season. The Chuo Alps (Kiso Range) have a distinct granite
character different from the Kita Alps limestone.

## 9. Kamikōchi Visitor Hub Huts, 1505 m — Azusa River, Kita Alps

Kamikōchi's valley floor, at 1505 m, holds several huts and lodge-style
accommodations that serve as acclimatization bases for the surrounding high
routes. Motor vehicle access is restricted and the valley is a National Monument,
which preserves the character of the forest and river environment. Arriving at
Kamikōchi the day before a high route and sleeping at valley elevation is good
practice before pushing for 3000 m the following day.

## 10. Mizugaki Sanso, 1700 m — Mizugaki, Oku-Chichibu Mountains

At the foot of the Mizugakiyama (2230 m) massif in Yamanashi Prefecture, the
Mizugaki area is Japan's most celebrated outdoor granite bouldering and crack
climbing area. The Mizugaki Sanso serves both hikers on the mountain and rock
climbers at the formations below. The granite boulders of Mizugaki draw
technical climbers from across Japan year-round. The mountain itself offers a
half-day summit hike.

## Booking and booking culture in Japan

Japanese yamagoya reservations operate almost entirely by phone. Most huts
open their booking lines in March or April for summer dates. English is not
always available. The cultural expectation is firm: if you have a reservation,
you arrive; if you cannot, you cancel as early as possible. The guardian sets
dinner time and wake-up call — follow the schedule without negotiation.

## The Kita Alps long-distance traverse

The most ambitious multi-day route in the Japanese Alps is the Kita Alps Main
Ridge Traverse (Kita Alps縦走), connecting the major summits of the Northern
Japanese Alps from Shirouma-dake in the north to Yarigatake and Oku-Hotaka
in the south — roughly 80–100 km of sustained ridge walking at 2500–3000 m
over 7–10 days. The route links Karasawa Hut, Yarigatake Sanso, Sugoroku Hut,
and a chain of other yamagoya in sequence.

This traverse is the equivalent of the European Haute Route in terms of its
status in the Japanese outdoor community — a serious, respected multi-day
commitment that requires good weather windows, navigation competence, and
the fitness for sustained high-ridge walking. The terrain is not glaciated
(no crevasse hazard) but involves sustained steep trail, exposed ridge sections,
and the unpredictability of Japanese mountain weather — typhoon systems can
arrive with less warning than European frontal systems.

## Mountain etiquette and trail culture

Japanese mountain culture has a set of conventions that, like European hut
etiquette, have evolved from practical necessity. Greet other walkers on the
trail with "Konnichiwa" during the day and "Ohayo gozaimasu" in the early
morning — this is not just courtesy but an acknowledged safety convention that
also confirms another party is on the route. Descending parties yield to
ascending parties on steep terrain.

At yamagoya, shoes are removed at the entrance without exception. Hut slippers
are provided and used throughout. The dining schedule is fixed and communal —
sitting at the assigned table and following the meal timing is the expectation.
Evening lights-out is early (21:00 is standard) and strictly observed, given
the 04:00–05:00 wake-up culture for summit days.
