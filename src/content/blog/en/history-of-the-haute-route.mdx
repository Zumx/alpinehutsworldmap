## The First Crossing

The route between Chamonix and Zermatt across the high passes of the Pennine Alps was completed in its recognisable form by members of the Alpine Club of London in 1861. The party — W. Mathews, F. W. Jacomb, and the guides Michel Croz and Johann-Baptiste Croz — traversed from Chamonix to Zermatt in a route that crossed the Col du Tour, the Col d'Argentière, and the Fenêtre de Sion. This was not a single planned traverse so much as the first complete linking of a series of passes that individual parties had each crossed separately in the preceding years.

The term "Haute Route" — high route — entered formal usage gradually over the subsequent decades, as the Alpine Club and its continental equivalents documented the traverse in guidebooks. The route was understood from the beginning as a serious undertaking requiring guides, good weather windows, and a tolerance for bivouac conditions. The hut infrastructure that makes it accessible to independent walkers today did not yet exist.

## Victorian and Edwardian Development

The Alpine Club's Peaks, Passes and Glaciers series, published from the 1850s onward, included early descriptions of individual Haute Route sections. The Journal of the Alpine Club carried accounts of new variations throughout the 1860s and 1870s. By the 1880s the route was sufficiently well known that it attracted walkers beyond the original guide-dependent style of mountaineering — though a crossing without a professional mountain guide remained exceptional.

The first SAC huts in the Valais were built during the 1870s and 1880s, transforming the logistical character of the traverse. The Cabane de Chanrion (2462 m) in the Bagnes valley, the Cabane de Bertol (3311 m) above Arolla, and the Cabane du Mountet (2886 m) above Zinal progressively replaced the combination of bivouac and high-altitude hotel stays that characterised the Victorian traverse.

## The Ski Touring Adoption

The winter and spring traverse of the Haute Route on skis was pioneered in January 1903 by a party from the Academic Alpine Club of Geneva — the AACG — who crossed from Verbier to Zermatt over the Pigne d'Arolla and the Col d'Hérens. The equipment of the time — long wooden Telemark-style skis with leather bindings, no climbing skins of the modern type, no synthetic insulation — made the crossing a formidable undertaking in conditions that modern tourers would consider extreme.

The ski traverse gained popularity in the 1920s and 1930s as equipment improved. Cable bindings in the 1930s and synthetic skins in the postwar decades made the ascent sections both faster and more reliable. By the 1960s the ski Haute Route — typically completed in seven days with April as the preferred month — had established itself as the benchmark of ski mountaineering in the western Alps.

## The Standardised Modern Traverse

The Haute Route in its most commonly walked form today starts in Chamonix (France) and finishes in Zermatt (Switzerland), crossing approximately 180 kilometres of terrain and gaining and losing around 12,000 metres of elevation in total across 10 to 12 days. The standard stages use CAF and SAC huts that were largely established between 1880 and 1950, with some having been rebuilt or expanded in the postwar period.

Key huts on the classic route include the Refuge du Col de la Forclaz (1526 m) near Martigny — the most common first night — the Refuge de Prafleuri (2624 m) above the Grand Dixence dam, the Cabane des Dix (2928 m) below the Mont Blanc de Cheilon, the Cabane des Vignettes (3157 m) above Arolla, and the Cabane de Schönbiel (2694 m) on the Zmutt glacier approach to Zermatt.

## The Ski Route Variants and Evolution

The ski traverse differs from the summer route in several ways beyond the obvious change of mode. It crosses terrain at a higher altitude on average — the ski Haute Route traverses the main Pennine chain more directly, using glacier snow as a highway that is unavailable in summer when it has melted or become dangerously crevassed. The most famous section of the ski route, the descent from the Col de Bertol to the Cabane des Vignettes, involves glacier travel at approximately 3300 metres that summer walkers avoid entirely by using a lower path.

Since the 1980s, guides and skiers have developed a proliferation of Haute Route variants. The Chamonix-Courmayeur-Zermatt variant via the Italian side of the Mont Blanc massif adds two to three days but includes the spectacular terrain of the Vallée Blanche. The Innsbruck-Nice traverse, sometimes called the Grand Ski Traverse, extends the concept across a much longer arc and requires three to four weeks.

## The Summer Walking Route in the 21st Century

The Walker's Haute Route — the summer pedestrian traverse — diverged from the ski route during the 1970s as walking guidebooks codified a lower-altitude alternative that avoids glacier crossings. The Walker's Haute Route by Kev Reynolds (published by Cicerone, first edition 1982) became the definitive English-language guide and standardised the modern summer traverse as a non-glaciated route. The route crosses into Switzerland via the Col de Balme (2201 m) and uses passes below 3000 metres throughout.

The Walker's Haute Route remains the most popular multi-day traverse in the Alps for general walkers without alpine technical skills. Booking pressure on the SAC huts in August has increased dramatically since the late 1990s; the Chamonix to Zermatt section now requires advance reservations of four to six weeks during peak season.
