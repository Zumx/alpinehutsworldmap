Ecuador's avenue of volcanoes — the double row of peaks aligned along the inter-Andean valley that the naturalist Alexander von Humboldt named the "Avenue of the Volcanoes" during his 1802 expedition — contains an extraordinary density of high summits. Chimborazo (6,263 m) is the highest, and its position on the equatorial bulge makes its summit the farthest point from the Earth's centre of any spot on the planet. Cotopaxi (5,897 m), directly south of Quito, is one of the highest active volcanoes in the world. Cayambe (5,790 m), straddling the equator, is the third-highest and carries the largest glaciated area. The altitude and accessibility of these volcanoes — most are reachable by road to within 400 vertical metres of the permanent snow line — has made Ecuador one of the world's premier destinations for high-altitude acclimatisation and summit-bagging. The refugio system, established primarily by the Ministerio del Ambiente and by private mountain guide agencies from the 1970s onward, provides the logistical backbone for summit attempts. Each major volcano has at least one high refugio serving as the departure point for the standard pre-dawn summit push.

## 1. Refugio Carrel, Chimborazo

Refugio Carrel at 4,800 m on the western flanks of Chimborazo is the lower of the two main shelters on the mountain, named after the Italian guides Jean-Antoine Carrel and Louis Carrel who accompanied Edward Whymper on the first ascent in 1880. The refugio was rebuilt after being damaged by lahars in the 1990s and now sleeps around 60 people in bunk dormitories. It serves as an acclimatisation sleeping stop and the base for equipment checks before parties move up to the higher Refugio Whymper.

## 2. Refugio Whymper, Chimborazo

Refugio Whymper at 5,000 m is the high camp for Chimborazo summit attempts, positioned below the Thielman Glacier approach and the technical sections of the standard northwest face route. Named after Edward Whymper — who first climbed Chimborazo in 1880 and also made the first ascent of the Matterhorn in 1865 — the refugio sleeps around 40 people. Summit departures from Whymper typically begin at midnight to allow parties to reach the summit by early morning before the afternoon convective clouds build and the crevasse bridges soften in the heat.

## 3. Refugio del Volcán Pichincha, Pichincha

The refugio on Pichincha (Rucu Pichincha, 4,696 m) overlooking Quito is the most accessible high refugio in Ecuador, reachable by the TelefériQo cable car from the capital to 4,050 m and then a one- to two-hour hike. Rucu Pichincha is an active stratovolcano whose 1999 eruption deposited ash over Quito. The refugio serves as a base for the summit scramble and for acclimatisation hikes that Quito residents and newly arrived visitors use before attempting higher peaks. The views over Quito from the summit ridge are extraordinary.

## 4. Tungurahua Refugio, Tungurahua

Tungurahua (5,023 m) near Baños is one of Ecuador's most active volcanoes and has been in eruption intermittently since 1999. The refugio on the lower slopes serves hikers and researchers monitoring the volcano rather than summit climbers — the summit has been closed to technical climbing for extended periods due to volcanic activity. The mountain overlooks the town of Baños de Agua Santa at 1,800 m, creating a dramatic juxtaposition of tropical vegetation and snow-capped summit visible from the market streets below.

## 5. Refugio de Montaña Cotopaxi Cara Sur, Cotopaxi

The refugio on Cotopaxi's southern face (Cara Sur) is an alternative shelter to the main José Rivas refuge, positioned on the southern approach to the mountain. Cotopaxi's standard route is via the northern face, but the southern approach offers a different and less crowded perspective on the glacier. The southern face refugio is smaller and more basic than the main shelter, with capacity for around 20 people. Cotopaxi National Park, established in 1975, surrounds the mountain and protects the páramo grassland ecosystem typical of the inter-Andean valley at altitudes between 3,500 and 4,800 m.

## 6. Refugio Nuevos Horizontes, Ilinizas

Refugio Nuevos Horizontes serves the Ilinizas — a pair of volcanic summits south of Quito comprising Iliniza Norte (5,126 m) and Iliniza Sur (5,248 m). Norte is reachable without technical equipment; Sur requires crampons and ice axe. The refugio at 4,740 m is positioned in the saddle between the two peaks and was established by a local mountaineering community organisation. It sleeps around 15 people and is one of the smaller and more atmospheric refugios in Ecuador.

## 7. Refugio José Rivas, Cotopaxi

Refugio José Rivas at 4,800 m on the northern face of Cotopaxi is the main high camp for the standard summit route. Named after a former Ecuadorian park ranger, the refugio was rebuilt in 2015 following damage in the 2015 Cotopaxi eruption and now offers modern dormitory accommodation, a proper kitchen, and a full-time caretaker. The summit of Cotopaxi at 5,897 m is four to six hours from the refugio via the glacier, which requires full crampons and ice axes due to heavily crevassed terrain.

## 8. Refugio Ruales-Oleas-Berge, Cayambe

Refugio Ruales-Oleas-Berge at 4,600 m on the southern face of Cayambe is the main shelter for Ecuador's third-highest volcano. Named after three Ecuadorian mountaineers killed on an Andean peak in the 1970s, the refugio sleeps around 30 people and is the departure point for the standard south face glacier route. Cayambe has the distinction of being the only point on Earth where the equator crosses a glaciated peak, and the summit stands precisely on the equinoctial line. The approach road from Cayambe town takes around two hours.

## 9. Refugio Cara Sur Antisana, Antisana

Antisana (5,753 m) southeast of Quito is the fourth-highest volcano in Ecuador and arguably the least-visited major summit due to the restricted access within the Antisana Ecological Reserve, which requires permits. The refugio on the Cara Sur (south face) serves the few permitted parties that climb the mountain each year. Antisana's ecological reserve is one of the most important Andean condor breeding areas in Ecuador, and the páramo around the approach road frequently has condors soaring overhead.

## 10. Refugio La Virgen, Cotacachi

Refugio La Virgen serves Cotacachi (4,944 m) in the Cotacachi-Cayapas Ecological Reserve north of Quito, near the town of Cotacachi and the famous Laguna Cuicocha crater lake. The volcano's summit is partly collapsed and the climb involves a mix of scrambling on the rim and glacier travel on the upper northern face. The refugio is a modest shelter at mid-elevation and is used by parties that begin the climb from the road above Cotacachi town.

## Plan Your Ecuador Volcano Climbs on the Map

Ecuador's volcanic peaks are distributed along a 400-kilometre corridor, and the refugio locations along each approach road vary significantly in altitude and quality. [Open the map](/map) to see all refugios positioned on their respective volcanoes, with road access routes and elevation contours clearly showing the altitude gain from the trailhead. The map makes it easy to plan an acclimatisation sequence — typically starting with Pichincha or Rucu Pichincha, progressing through the Ilinizas or Cotopaxi, and culminating on Chimborazo or Cayambe over a two-week period. The best summit conditions on most Ecuadorian volcanoes are during the drier months of June through September and December through January, though conditions vary year to year with El Niño cycles strongly affecting snowpack and glacier stability.
