Germany's slice of the Alps is a narrow but spectacular fringe running along the Bavarian border with Austria, from the Allgäu in the west to the Berchtesgadener Land in the east. Though the country has only a single peak above 2900 metres, the Zugspitze (2962 m), this band of mountains is studded with some of the oldest and best-loved huts in the entire Alps, almost all run by the Deutscher Alpenverein, the world's largest mountaineering club. From limestone walls of the Wetterstein to the steep flanks of the Watzmann above the Königssee, the Bavarian huts offer welcoming Bavarian hospitality, hearty Kaiserschmarrn and a dense, well-marked network of trails.

## The DAV and the German hut tradition

The Deutscher Alpenverein, founded in 1869, owns and runs hundreds of huts across the Eastern Alps, and the Bavarian fringe holds many of its flagship houses. DAV huts are graded into categories, with category I huts in remote high terrain kept simple and category III houses near roads offering more comfort. Membership brings reduced overnight fees and reciprocal rights in Austrian, Italian and Swiss club huts, and the club maintains the vast trail network and via ferratas. The culture is one of self-reliance and shared tables, of Hüttenschlafsäcke and morning starts, and it shapes the whole experience of walking in the Bavarian mountains.

## Münchner Haus on the Zugspitze

The Münchner Haus stands at 2959 metres on the very summit of the Zugspitze, Germany's highest mountain, perched on the rocky pinnacle between the glaciers and the cliffs above Garmisch-Partenkirchen. Built in 1897 and run by the DAV, it shares the crowded summit with a weather observatory and the upper stations of the cable cars and cog railway. Few huts in the Alps sit so high on their nation's roof, and despite the throngs that ride the lifts, the Münchner Haus remains a working mountain shelter on a genuinely alpine summit.

## Höllentalangerhütte

The Höllentalangerhütte at 1387 metres lies in the wild Höllental, the Valley of Hell, on the classic and demanding route up the Zugspitze through the gorge and across the Höllentalferner glacier. Rebuilt in striking modern form, it is the base for one of the finest mountaineering ascents in Germany, a route that combines a dramatic gorge, a via ferrata and a small glacier. The hut sits beneath the towering north walls of the Wetterstein, a serious place that gives a true sense of high-mountain country despite the modest altitude.

## Watzmannhaus

The Watzmannhaus at 1930 metres in the Berchtesgaden Alps is the base for the ascent of the Watzmann (2713 m), one of the most iconic and dangerous mountains in Germany, whose famous east face plunges nearly 1800 metres to the Königssee. One of the largest and busiest huts in the Bavarian Alps, it sits on a broad ridge with stunning views over the Berchtesgaden basin. From here climbers tackle the Watzmann Hocheck and the exposed traverse of the summits, while walkers enjoy one of the great panoramic huts of the range.

## Kärlinger Haus

The Kärlinger Haus at 1631 metres sits beside the dark waters of the Funtensee, deep in the remote Steinernes Meer, the Sea of Stone, in the heart of Berchtesgaden National Park. The Funtensee is famous as one of the coldest places in Germany, where cold air pools in the karst basin and record low temperatures are recorded. Reached by long walks from the Königssee or across the limestone plateau, the hut is a base for exploring the strange, waterless karst desert of the Steinernes Meer, a landscape unlike any other in the German Alps.

## Karwendelhaus

The Karwendelhaus at 1771 metres lies just over the popular trekking ground between Bavaria and the Tyrol, in the great Karwendel range whose northern walls rise above Mittenwald. A classic large alpine club hut, it sits beneath the Birkkarspitze, the highest peak of the Karwendel, on the route of the Adlerweg and a favourite of mountain bikers riding up from the Isar valley. The Karwendel, with its immense bare limestone faces and deep valleys, is one of the wildest ranges accessible from Bavaria, and the hut is its central hub.

## Brunnenkopfhütte

The Brunnenkopfhütte at 1602 metres in the Ammergau Alps above Linderhof enjoys a celebrated panorama across to the Zugspitze and the Wetterstein, set on a grassy ridge in the foothills near the famous castles of King Ludwig. A smaller, characterful hut, it is a popular day and overnight destination, reached on pleasant walks through forest and pasture, and is renowned for its sunset views and its convivial atmosphere. It typifies the gentler, lower huts of the Bavarian fringe that welcome families and casual walkers.

## Tegelberghaus and the royal mountains

The Tegelberghaus at 1707 metres sits above Schwangau on the Tegelberg, the home mountain of the Allgäu fringe above the fairytale castle of Neuschwanstein. Reached by cable car or on foot, it is a busy, welcoming hut with grand views over the lakes of the Forggensee and the Bavarian plain, and a launch point for paragliders and ridge walkers. The mountains here, around Füssen and the royal castles of Neuschwanstein and Hohenschwangau, blend tourism and genuine alpine walking in a way unique to this corner of Bavaria.

## Berggaststätte Herzogstand and the Walchensee

The Berggasthaus Herzogstand at around 1575 metres sits on the Herzogstand above the turquoise Walchensee, the favourite mountain of the Bavarian kings, who built a royal pavilion here. Reached by cable car and a short ridge walk, it offers one of the most famous viewpoints in the Bavarian Prealps, looking down on the deep blue lakes of the Walchensee and Kochelsee. The hut and its surroundings are a beloved escape for Munich walkers, a gentle but spectacular introduction to the Bavarian mountains.

## Hut culture, food and the seasons

The Bavarian huts are famous for their warmth and their food. A typical hut table groans with Kaiserschmarrn, Germknödel, Kasspatzn and hearty soups, washed down with Radler or a Weissbier, and the Stube with its tiled stove is the social heart of every house. The DAV huts run mainly from late May to October, with the high huts dependent on snow conditions, while some lower houses open in winter for ski-tourers and snowshoers. The network is dense and well signposted, and long-distance routes such as the Maximiliansweg and the cross-border treks into the Tyrol link the huts into multi-day journeys. Reservation is now strongly advised everywhere, as the popularity of the Bavarian mountains has grown enormously.

## Practical notes for the Bavarian huts

The German alpine huts cluster in distinct ranges: the Allgäu in the west, the Wetterstein and Karwendel around Garmisch, the Ammergau near the royal castles, and the Berchtesgaden Alps in the east. Gateways include Oberstdorf, Garmisch-Partenkirchen, Mittenwald and Berchtesgaden, all reachable by train from Munich. Many huts are accessible to fit day walkers, but the high routes on the Zugspitze and the Watzmann demand mountaineering skill, a head for heights and proper equipment. DAV membership brings real savings and reciprocal rights across the Alps, and online reservation is the norm.
