The Canadian Rockies stretch along the British Columbia-Alberta border from
the US border to just north of Jasper, enclosing some of the finest wilderness
mountain terrain on the continent. The Alpine Club of Canada (ACC) operates
a network of backcountry huts that provide overnight infrastructure for ski
touring, mountaineering, and glacier travel — accessed not by cable car or
maintained path but by serious approaches through wilderness terrain.

## 1. Bow Hut, 2350 m — Wapta Icefields, British Columbia

The Bow Hut on the Wapta Icefield is part of the famous Wapta Traverse —
a 4-day ski touring route across the Columbia Icefield system that is one
of the great ski traverses in North America. Reached from the Highway 1A
Bow Lake trailhead in approximately 4 hours (ski or snowshoe in winter), the
Bow Hut accommodates 30 people and provides the base for the traverse toward
the Peyto Hut and the Balfour High Col. Open in spring ski season (March–May)
and summer; ACC membership required.

## 2. Abbot Pass Hut, 2925 m — Lake O'Hara area, Yoho National Park

The Abbot Pass Hut, sitting at 2925 m on the border of Alberta and British
Columbia between Mount Lefroy and Mount Victoria, is a National Historic Site
of Canada and one of the oldest continuously operated mountain huts in North
America. The stone building, completed in 1922, provides simple accommodation
for approximately 24 people. It was closed indefinitely to the public in 2021
due to structural and rockfall safety concerns — but its history and position
above the Victoria Glacier make it one of the most historically significant
huts in Canada.

## 3. Stanley Mitchell Hut, 1830 m — Little Yoho Valley, Yoho National Park

The Stanley Mitchell Hut in the Little Yoho Valley above the Vice President
cirque is the ACC's most celebrated summer hut in the Rockies. At 1830 m in
a subalpine forest setting, it is reached by a 9 km trail from the Takakkaw
Falls parking area. Capacity around 24; the surrounding terrain offers glacier
access to the Whaleback and the President Range peaks. One of the most
scenically placed overnight huts in the national park system.

## 4. Peyto Hut, 2265 m — Wapta Icefields, Alberta

The second major hut on the Wapta Traverse, Peyto Hut provides accommodation
for the middle section of the ski route between Bow Hut and Scott Duncan Hut.
At 2265 m, it requires a long skin approach from the valley or a continuation
from Bow Hut. Capacity around 20; ACC-operated spring ski touring season.

## 5. Scott Duncan Hut, 2740 m — Wapta Icefields, Alberta

The highest and most remote of the Wapta Traverse huts, Scott Duncan at
2740 m serves the final section of the traverse. The approach involves glaciated
terrain and avalanche exposure; the hut is appropriate only for experienced
ski tourers with glacier travel competence. Capacity around 20; ACC members only.

## 6. Wapta Hut, 2310 m — Wapta Icefields, Alberta

An alternative staging hut on the Wapta system, providing access to the Olive
Hut connector and the southern Wapta terrain. The hut is reached from the
Trans-Canada Highway (Hwy 1) at the Waputik Mountains access point.

## 7. Caribou Pass Hut, 1980 m — Jasper area, Alberta

In the northern Rockies near Jasper, the Caribou Pass Hut provides a remote
overnight option for ski tourers and summer hikers in terrain further from the
Banff-Lake Louise core. The Jasper area has fewer ACC huts than the Columbia
Icefields zone but offers superb wilderness character.

## 8. Neil Colgan Hut, 2900 m — Valley of the Ten Peaks, Banff

A small, high hut at 2900 m in the Valley of the Ten Peaks above Lake Louise,
the Neil Colgan Hut serves technical mountaineers on the Wenchemna Peaks.
The approach involves scrambling terrain above the treeline; this is a hut
for climbers rather than casual trekkers.

## 9. McMurdo Lodge, 2040 m — Banff backcountry

A larger ACC lodge in the backcountry of Banff National Park, McMurdo Lodge
provides guided group accommodation for winter ski-touring courses. ACC
Banff section manages it.

## 10. Sunshine Lodge, 2240 m — Sunshine Village, Banff

Just outside the Sunshine ski resort boundary at 2240 m, Sunshine Lodge is
the most accessible ski-in overnight in the Banff area. Reached by gondola
from the Trans-Canada Highway; provides a base for backcountry touring above
the resort's boundaries.

## The Wapta Traverse in detail

The Wapta Traverse is one of North America's great ski mountaineering routes.
Over 4 days, it crosses the Wapta Icefield from Bow Lake to the Sherbrooke
Lake area (or the reverse), linking the Bow Hut, Peyto Hut, Balfour Hut,
and Scott Duncan Hut in sequence. The crossing days involve glacier travel
at 2500–2800 m with crevasse hazard on the Wapta and Diableret icefields;
weather can deteriorate rapidly, and navigation in cloud on the icefield
requires GPS and compass competence.

The Wapta in spring (March–May) offers deep powder on the glacier descents
and spring corn on the lower slopes — two very different snow textures in a
single day. The ACC huts are equipped with stoves and basic cooking equipment;
parties carry their own food and sleeping gear.

All ACC hut reservations must be made through the Alpine Club of Canada
(alpineclubofcanada.ca). ACC membership is required and is available to
non-Canadians; the annual fee is approximately CAD 85 for adults. Member
rates at the huts are significantly lower than non-member rates.

## The Columbia Icefield context

The Columbia Icefield — the largest in the Rocky Mountains — lies to the north
of the Wapta system and feeds glaciers that drain into three different oceans:
the Pacific (via the Columbia River), the Arctic (via the Athabasca and
Mackenzie Rivers), and the Atlantic/Hudson Bay (via the North Saskatchewan
River). The Athabasca Glacier, accessible from the Icefields Parkway, is
the most visited glacier in North America and provides easy tourist access
to a retreating glacier as a direct climate-change indicator.

The Columbia Icefield area has its own backcountry hut infrastructure, managed
separately from the Wapta system, for ski-touring and climbing parties bound
for peaks like Mount Columbia (3747 m — the second highest peak in the
Canadian Rockies).

Bears and food storage in the Rockies are an important practical consideration:
black bears and grizzly bears are present throughout the national park
backcountry. All food, cooking equipment, and scented items must be stored
in the bear boxes provided at trail camps or in approved bear canisters when
no box is provided. The ACC huts manage food storage within the hut; day
parties between huts should not leave food in packs stored on the terrace
overnight.
