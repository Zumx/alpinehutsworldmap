Tasmania's Overland Track is a 65-kilometre walk through the Cradle Mountain-
Lake St Clair National Park, connecting Cradle Mountain in the north with Lake
St Clair in the south over 6 days. It is widely regarded as Australia's finest
multi-day alpine walk: the terrain is genuinely subalpine above 1200 m, the
endemic wildlife (wombats, echidnas, Tasmanian Devils, endemic wallabies) is
remarkable, and the vegetation — pencil pine, scoparia, buttongrass moorland —
is found nowhere else in the world. The hut system is managed by Parks and
Wildlife Service Tasmania; all hikers must pay a per-night hut fee in addition
to the track booking.

## 1. Waterfall Valley Hut, 1230 m — Day 1, Cradle Mountain area

The first overnight hut on the southbound Overland Track, at 1230 m in the
Waterfall Valley Hut (Cradle Mountain Huts) complex. The hut accommodates
approximately 40 people in the Parks and Wildlife Service facility; a separate
luxury lodge (Cradle Mountain Huts, operated by Tasmanian Walking Company)
provides premium accommodation at the same location. The approach from Cradle
Mountain visitor centre takes 4–6 hours through button grass moorland and the
base of the Cradle Mountain plateau.

## 2. Windermere Hut, 1050 m — Day 2, Lake Windermere

Beside Lake Windermere (1050 m) in the heart of the Overland's mid-section,
the Windermere Hut provides the overnight between Waterfall Valley and New
Pelion. The lake is a glacial tarn with the buttongrass moorland of the
plateau surrounding it. Walking time from Waterfall Valley is 3–5 hours;
the terrain is open subalpine heath with excellent views.

## 3. New Pelion Hut, 1090 m — Day 3, Du Cane Range

At 1090 m in the Du Cane Range, New Pelion Hut (Pelion Hut, Cradle Mountain
Huts) is the base for the optional ascent of Mount Ossa (1617 m) — the highest
peak in Tasmania. The Ossa ascent adds 5–6 hours to the day; the views from
the summit across the Central Plateau and the surrounding ranges are extraordinary.
Capacity around 40.

## 4. Kia Ora Hut, 920 m — Day 4, Kia Ora Hut area

Below the Du Cane Range at 920 m in a valley of endemic pencil pines and
rivulets, the Kia Ora Hut (Cradle Mountain Huts) is the most sheltered of
the main Overland huts. The surrounding vegetation — the ancient pencil pines
(Athrotaxis cupressoides) are the oldest trees in Australia, with some specimens
over 1000 years old — makes this location one of the most ecologically
significant on the walk.

## 5. Windy Ridge Hut, 1100 m — Day 5, Lake St Clair approach

At 1100 m on the exposed ridge above Lake St Clair, the Windy Ridge Private
Hut (Cradle Mountain Huts) earns its name: the southwest winds that funnel
up Lake St Clair make this the most exposed overnight on the Overland. The
views down Lake St Clair — at 169 metres depth, the deepest lake in Australia —
and across to the Western Arthur Range are some of the finest on the walk.

## 6. Bert Nichols Hut, 920 m — Day 5 alternative, Narcissus area

An alternative to Windy Ridge for the final stages, the Bert Nichols Hut near
the Narcissus River provides a lower and more sheltered option. The Narcissus
area has some of the finest pencil pine stands on the walk.

## 7. Echo Point Hut, 730 m — Lake St Clair area, near Derwent Bridge

Near the southern end of the walk above Lake St Clair, Echo Point Hut at
730 m is the last of the series. Accommodation before the final walk to the
Cynthia Bay visitor centre at Lake St Clair village.

## 8. Old Pelion Hut, 1000 m — Historic hut, Day 3 area

An historic hut from the original Overland Track era, now preserved as a
heritage structure rather than a primary overnight facility. The Old Pelion
area has botanical significance — the pencil pine and cushion plant communities
here are rare.

## 9. Cradle Mountain Lodge Area, 920 m — Cradle Valley

At the northern end of the track, the accommodation complex at Cradle Mountain
village (Cradle Mountain Lodge and associated budget accommodation) serves
as the pre-track staging point. Full restaurant, equipment hire, and the
Cradle Mountain visitor centre are located here.

## 10. Lake St Clair Visitor Centre Huts, 730 m — Cynthia Bay

At the southern end of the walk, dormitory accommodation at the Lake St Clair
visitor centre complex provides the final night before road departure toward
Hobart (3.5 hours).

## Booking the Overland Track

The Overland Track requires advance booking (October–May peak season; self-
registration in winter). Bookings open online through the Parks Tasmania portal.
The track fee covers hut accommodation at all main huts (own sleeping bag and
liner required). Numbers are capped at 60 southbound starters per day in
peak season. Bookings for January and February typically sell out within days
of opening.

The track must be walked south-bound (Cradle Mountain to Lake St Clair) during
the peak season. This is a Parks Tasmania direction to manage the flow of
walkers and reduce congestion at the huts. In the off-season (June–September),
the track can be walked in either direction; the huts are unserviced in winter
and the weather is substantially more demanding.

## Weather and conditions

The Overland Track is subject to the weather of western Tasmania — one of the
wettest places in Australia, driven by the westerly winds that circle the Southern
Ocean unimpeded. The plateau sections receive over 2000 mm of annual rainfall;
snow can fall in any month at higher elevations. The standard weather description
for Tasmanian walking is "expect all four seasons in a day."

Waterproof clothing is non-negotiable. Even in the summer months (December–
February), a day walking in shorts and a sun hat can become a survival situation
by mid-afternoon if a frontal system arrives from the southwest. The huts are
well-maintained and serve as genuine emergency shelters; parties who are caught
by bad weather routinely spend an extra night in a hut rather than pushing through
to the next stage.

The Parks Tasmania website publishes weather forecasts specific to the Overland
Track zones during the walking season. The Bureau of Meteorology (bom.gov.au)
provides the underlying forecast; the Parks Tasmania version adds trail condition
notes from the ranger team.

## Tasmanian endemic wildlife

One of the distinctive pleasures of the Overland Track is the wildlife
encountered at and around the huts. Wombats — large, slow, bulldozer-like
marsupials weighing up to 30 kg — regularly visit the hut areas at dusk and
are remarkably unthreatened by humans. Bennett's wallabies, the Tasmanian pademelon,
and (less commonly) Tasmanian Devils are seen regularly. The platypus inhabits
the rivers below the huts and is sometimes seen in the early morning.

Birds include the endemic Swift Parrot (critically endangered), the Green Rosella
(endemic to Tasmania), and the Wedge-tailed Eagle. The pencil pine groves around
Kia Ora Hut and the northern forests around Cradle Mountain are the primary
habitat for the endemic Tasmanian Thornbill. Wildlife photography from the hut
platforms at dusk is a consistent highlight of the walk.
