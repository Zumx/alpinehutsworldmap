The Pyrenees form the natural border between France and Spain along 430
kilometres of high mountain terrain, rising to the Pico d'Aneto (3404 m) in
the Aragonese Maladeta massif. The hut network here is thinner than the Alps —
the ranges are less developed for mass mountain tourism — which gives Pyrenean
hut travel a more exploratory quality. French CAF refuges on the north side,
Spanish federación huts on the south, and a handful of privately operated
stone refugios between: these are the huts that make multi-day traverse on
the GR10, GR11, and Haute Randonnée Pyrénéenne (HRP) possible.

## 1. Refuge Wallon - Marcadau, 1865 m — Vallée du Marcadau, Hautes-Pyrénées

The Refuge Wallon - Marcadau is the most popular and best-known refuge on the
French side of the western Pyrenees. Located at 1865 m in the broad Marcadau
valley above Cauterets, it is accessible to a wide range of fitness levels —
the 3-hour walk from Cauterets is on a well-graded path through forest and
high meadows. Capacity around 100; full half-board service run by the CAF
in summer season. The surrounding terrain offers day walks to the Lac du
Marcadau, the Col du Cambalès, and the Pic de Cambalès (2965 m). The Refuge
Wallon is the classic first-night stop for parties beginning the walk toward
the Spanish high Pyrenees.

## 2. Refuge des Oulettes de Gaube, 2151 m — Vignemale approach, Hautes-Pyrénées

Below the Vignemale massif (3298 m — the highest point of the French Pyrenees),
the Refuge des Oulettes de Gaube sits above the Lac de Gaube with a direct
view of the north face. This is a refuge with mountaineering character: the
north face of Vignemale — a serious D-grade face — is visible from the terrace,
and the hut hosts guided parties bound for it. The normal route on Vignemale
via the Ossoue glacier is also staged from here. Capacity around 80; staffed
June to October. The approach from Pont d'Espagne (reached by cable car from
Cauterets) takes 3 hours on a well-marked path.

## 3. Refuge de Bayssellance, 2651 m — Vignemale plateau, Hautes-Pyrénées

The highest staffed refuge in the French Pyrenees, at 2651 m on the plateau
below the Vignemale summit. Managed by the CAF, with capacity for approximately
50 guests. Reached from the Refuge des Oulettes de Gaube via the Ossoue glacier
in 3–4 hours, crampons required in early season. The refuge is used for summit
attempts on Vignemale and as a staging point for the high-level traverse
toward Gavarnie. The Ossoue glacier approach from the Spanish side (Refugio
de Góriz direction) is a classic Pyrenean glaciated traverse.

## 4. Refugi d'Estany Llong, 2000 m — Aigüestortes, Lleida

In the heart of the Aigüestortes i Estany de Sant Maurici National Park on
the Spanish Pyrenean side, the Refugi d'Estany Llong serves as a key overnight
on the popular Carros de Foc circuit — a multi-day traverse visiting all nine
refuges in the national park. Capacity around 55; staffed July to October. The
surrounding terrain is characterised by glacial lakes, granite pavements, and
high-quality rock climbing on the surrounding peaks.

## 5. Refugio de la Renclusa, 2140 m — Maladeta massif, Aragón

The primary base camp for the Aneto (3404 m), the highest peak in the
Pyrenees. The Refugio de la Renclusa sits at 2140 m in the Benasque valley
above the village of La Besurta. Capacity around 80; run by the Federación
Aragonesa de Montañismo. The standard Aneto route ascends via the Maladeta
and Coronas glaciers — a 6–7 hour return from the refuge requiring crampons,
ice axe, and basic glacier competence. The Renclusa is busy in July–August
with summit aspirants; booking several weeks in advance is strongly recommended.

## 6. Refuge de Gavarnie, 1580 m — Gavarnie cirque, Hautes-Pyrénées

Below the spectacular Gavarnie cirque — a UNESCO World Heritage geological
formation with Europe's highest waterfall (422 m) — this refuge provides easy
access to one of the most dramatic natural features in France. The valley path
from the village of Gavarnie takes under 2 hours. A natural first base for
exploring the cirque, the Col de Boucharou approach, or the Spanish side via
the Brèche de Roland.

## 7. Refugio de Góriz, 2200 m — Parque Nacional de Ordesa, Aragón

Inside the Ordesa y Monte Perdido National Park, one of the Pyrenees' finest
protected areas, the Refugio de Góriz is essential on the Spanish HRP route.
Capacity around 80; managed by the Spanish Federation of Mountain Sports.
The position between the Monte Perdido massif (3355 m) and the Ordesa canyon
makes it the base for some of the finest high-mountain day routes on the
Spanish side — the Monte Perdido summit, the Brèche de Roland crossing, and
the canyon rim traverses.

## 8. Refuge du Portillon, 2571 m — Luchon, Haute-Garonne

Above Luchon — the traditional Pyrenean spa town — the Refuge du Portillon
sits near the Lac du Portillon at 2571 m, below the Maladeta massif viewed
from the French north side. A serious mountain refuge for parties bound for
the high passes and summits of the Luchon group: the Pic de Maupas, the
Boum du Port, and the cross-border routes to Spain. Capacity around 50.

## 9. Refuge de la Soula, 1900 m — Ariège, French Pyrenees

A quieter and less-frequented refuge in the Ariège section of the Pyrenees,
the Refuge de la Soula serves the Vicdessos valley traverse routes and the
approch to the Pic de Montcalm (3077 m). The Ariège Pyrenees receive fewer
visitors than the central Hautes-Pyrénées section and offer a more solitary
experience on the GR10 and HRP.

## 10. Refuge des Estanyols, 2240 m — Carlit massif, Pyrénées-Orientales

In the Pyrénées-Orientales near the Carlit massif (2921 m), the Refuge des
Estanyols provides access to the lake-dotted high plateau terrain of the eastern
Pyrenees — a landscape of granite, glacial lakes, and high summer meadows that
is among the most distinctive in the range.

## The Haute Route Pyrénéenne (HRP)

The HRP (Haute Route Pyrénéenne) is the high-level traverse of the Pyrenees
from Hendaye on the Atlantic to Banyuls-sur-Mer on the Mediterranean — 800 km
along the spine of the range, covering approximately 45,000 m of elevation
gain across its full length. It is a more demanding and less infrastructure-
dependent route than the GR10 (the French route) or the GR11 (the Spanish
route), crossing some sections without marked trail.

The HRP uses the refuge network as its principal overnight infrastructure;
many of the huts listed above are on or close to the HRP line. The full
traverse takes 45–50 days for a strong party and requires navigation
competence, comfort on mixed terrain above 2500 m, and the ability to
manage a day without a staffed hut on the occasional off-refuge section.
Kev Reynolds's Haute Randonnée Pyrénéenne (Cicerone) is the standard
guidebook.

## Booking and seasons

The main Pyrenean huts are managed by the Club Alpin Français sections in
France and the Federació d'Entitats Excursionistes de Catalunya (FEEC) in
Catalonia. Booking is through the respective club systems online; the CAF
system (camptocamp.org links to many CAF booking pages) allows advance
reservations for most staffed huts. Peak season is July–August; June and
September are quieter and often have better weather in the Pyrenees than
the midsummer period.

The Spanish refugios (Refugio de Góriz, Refugi d'Estany Llong) book through
FEEC (feec.cat) or directly with the individual huts. Prices are generally
slightly lower than French CAF huts at equivalent standards.
