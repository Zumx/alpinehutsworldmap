The Cabane du Mont Fort sits at 2,457 metres on a balcony of alpine pasture above Verbier, its terrace looking straight north across the deep trench of the Rhone valley to the Bernese Alps beyond. For thousands of ski-tourers it is the symbolic opening chapter of the Haute Route, the legendary multi-day traverse from Chamonix to Zermatt. Reached in winter by a short skin from the top of the Verbier lifts, and in summer by a steady walk up from the resort, it combines an unusually accessible position with a genuinely high-mountain outlook. From the deck, as the afternoon light fades, the Grand Combin and the Mont Blanc massif glow on the southern horizon while the lights of Sion flicker far below.

## Where the Haute Route Begins

The classic ski Haute Route can be skied in either direction, but the west-to-east line starting at Verbier is the most popular, and the Cabane du Mont Fort is almost always its first overnight. Parties ride the Verbier lift system up toward the Col des Gentianes, then make a short descent and skin to the hut, arriving with the whole crossing still ahead of them. The following days lead over the Col de la Chaux, across the high glaciated cols toward the Cabane de Prafleuri and the Dix valley, and onward through a chain of remote shelters to Arolla, the Vignettes, and ultimately Zermatt beneath the Matterhorn. The Mont Fort is therefore the hut where the route's mood is set: the gear check, the avalanche-bulletin reading, the first communal dinner among strangers who will become a rope team for a week.

## A Balcony Over the Rhone

Even outside the Haute Route, the Cabane du Mont Fort earns its reputation on the strength of its view. The hut faces almost due north, and the panorama spans an enormous arc of the Pennine and Bernese ranges. To the south rises the Mont Fort itself (3,329 m), accessible by lift and offering one of the great viewpoints of the western Alps; to the east the glaciers of the Grand Désert spread toward the Rosablanche (3,336 m), a popular ski-touring summit; and to the north the valley drops more than two thousand metres to the Rhone, with the Bernese giants — the Wildhorn, the Wildstrubel — closing the far skyline. Sunset from the terrace, with the valley already in shadow and the peaks still lit, is one of the simple pleasures that keeps guests returning.

## Run by the Friends of the Hut

Unlike the strictly club-owned huts of the Swiss Alpine Club that dominate much of the Haute Route, the Cabane du Mont Fort is operated by a local association, the Amis de la Cabane du Mont Fort, and has the feel of a comfortable mountain inn rather than a spartan refuge. With around 90 beds, a warm dining room, and a kitchen reputed among tourers, it offers the half-board arrangement standard to the region: a hearty evening meal, dormitory sleep, and an early breakfast geared to dawn departures. Because it is so easily reached, it also draws day-walkers and lift-riders in summer, lending it a livelier, more sociable atmosphere than the austere glacier huts deeper in the range.

## The Great Valais Hut Network

The Mont Fort is one link in an extraordinary chain of Swiss huts that threads the Valais. To the west, around the Grand Combin (4,314 m), cluster the Cabane de Mille, the Cabane Brunet, the Cabane FXB Panossière beneath the Corbassière glacier, and the high Cabane de Valsorey and Cabane du Vélan used on the alpine summer Haute Route. To the east lie the Cabane de Bertol CAS, perched on its rocky spur with ladders to the door, and the Cabane de Saleinaz CAS in the Trient basin. Higher and farther into the Pennine Alps sit the Britanniahütte above Saas-Fee and the Mischabelhütte clinging to its ridge. The Cabane du Mont Fort opens this whole world: from its terrace you can almost trace the line of huts that carry a tourer all the way to the foot of the Matterhorn.

## Reading the Conditions

The Haute Route is a serious glacial undertaking, and the Mont Fort is where prudent parties take its measure. The crossing demands settled weather, a stable snowpack, and competence in glacier travel and crevasse rescue; the cols between Verbier and Arolla cross terrain where avalanche risk and whiteout navigation are real concerns. Wardens and guides treat the avalanche bulletin as gospel, and many a party has spent an extra night at the Mont Fort waiting for a window rather than committing to the Col de la Chaux in poor visibility. The standard season runs from roughly mid-March to early May, when daylight is long, the snowpack consolidating, and the high cols still skiable. Booking far ahead is essential; spring weekends sell out months in advance across the entire route.

## Summer at the Mont Fort

Out of the ski season the hut takes on a different character. The Tour des Combins and various walking circuits pass nearby, and the Sentier des Chamois traverse links the hut toward the Col Termin and the Grand Désert with a good chance of spotting ibex and chamois on the slopes. The Rosablanche becomes a straightforward summer scramble-and-glacier objective, and the broad terrace makes an idyllic lunch stop for walkers exploring the heights above Verbier. Whether arriving on skis in spring or on foot in August, guests find the same essential combination that has made the hut a classic: an easy approach, a hearty welcome, and a panorama that ranks among the finest in the Valais.

## The Spirit of the First Night

There is something distinctive about the atmosphere of a hut that stands at the very start of a great journey. The Cabane du Mont Fort gathers, on any given spring evening, a remarkable cross-section of the ski-touring world: guided groups working through their final gear checks, independent parties poring over maps and the avalanche bulletin, solo travellers hoping to join a rope team, and a scattering of guides comparing notes on the state of the high cols ahead. Conversation in the dining room is animated and faintly nervous, the talk of people on the threshold of a serious undertaking. Unlike the deeper huts of the route, where exhaustion tends to send everyone to bed early and silent, the Mont Fort has the energy of anticipation rather than fatigue. Veterans of the Haute Route often say the first night carries a particular charge, a sense of the whole crossing still intact and unspoiled ahead, and the Mont Fort is where that feeling is strongest. By breakfast the mood has shifted to focus, headlamps are tested, skins are checked, and one party after another slips out into the cold dark toward the Col de la Chaux and the long road to Zermatt.
