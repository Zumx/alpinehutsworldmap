Seven major national alpine clubs manage the mountain hut networks, trail
maintenance, rescue coordination, and alpine culture across the European
mountains. Understanding how each operates — what the membership buys, which
cards are valid where, and how the reciprocal discount system works — is
practical knowledge for anyone planning a serious multi-day alpine trip. The
[map](/map) shows huts affiliated with these clubs across the range.

## CAI — Club Alpino Italiano

The Club Alpino Italiano was founded in Turin in 1863 and is the oldest alpine
club in Italy. It manages over 700 huts (rifugi) across the Italian Alps and
Apennines through a network of local sections — each section owns and operates
the huts in its area. CAI membership costs approximately €45–55 per year for
adults and is available through any local section.

CAI membership provides: the member overnight rate at all CAI-affiliated rifugi
(typically €5–15 cheaper per night than the non-member rate), liability
insurance for mountain activities, access to the CAI route and map database,
and discounts at partner outdoor equipment retailers. The CAI also manages the
national trail numbering system (the numbered white-red blaze system used
across Italy) and publishes trail conditions reports.

## SAC/CAS — Swiss Alpine Club

The Schweizer Alpen-Club (SAC) or Club Alpin Suisse (CAS) was founded in 1863
(the same year as the CAI, independently) and operates over 150 huts across
Switzerland. SAC membership costs approximately CHF 105–170 annually depending
on category (active member, supporting member, family). All huts are bookable
through the centralized sac-cas.ch system.

SAC membership provides: member rates at all SAC huts (typically CHF 10–20
cheaper per night), technical climbing course discounts, mountain rescue
insurance, access to the SAC topographic map library, and a subscription to
the SAC journal. Swiss hut standards are consistently high — the SAC invests
heavily in renovation and off-grid energy systems.

## ÖAV — Österreichischer Alpenverein

The Austrian Alpine Club (Österreichischer Alpenverein, ÖAV) manages around 230
huts in Austria, from low valley lodges to high glacier outposts above 3000 m.
Annual membership costs approximately €55–70 for adults. The ÖAV also
coordinates the Alpsonline reservation platform, used by both ÖAV and DAV huts.

ÖAV membership provides: member rates at ÖAV and DAV huts, mountain rescue
insurance valid across the Alps, the Bergauf magazine, discounts at partner
outdoor retailers, and access to the Alpenverein digital map service
(alpenverein.de/karte). The ÖAV runs an extensive youth program and subsidizes
climbing walls and outdoor courses for juniors.

## DAV — Deutscher Alpenverein

The German Alpine Club (Deutscher Alpenverein, DAV) is the largest hiking and
climbing club in the world by membership — over 1.4 million members as of 2024.
It operates around 320 huts across Germany, Austria, and South Tyrol. Annual
membership costs approximately €70–85 for adults, with junior categories for
under-18s at significantly reduced rates.

DAV membership provides: member rates at all DAV and ÖAV huts, mountain rescue
insurance, a subscription to the panorama magazine, access to the DAV Summit
Club course catalogue, and the Alpenvereinaktiv digital navigation platform —
which has become one of the most comprehensive trail and hut databases in the
Alps. The DAV member card is the single most widely accepted foreign alpine
club card at Swiss SAC huts.

## FFCAM — Fédération Française des Clubs Alpins et de Montagne

The French federation manages around 120 staffed refuges across the French Alps,
Pyrenees, and Corsica. FFCAM membership costs approximately €25–35 annually
(accessed through a local club, FFCAM being the federation body). The system
is slightly more complex than the German-speaking clubs: you join a local club
(CAF section — Club Alpin Français) that affiliates to FFCAM.

FFCAM/CAF membership provides: member rates at French-affiliated refuges
(typically €3–8 cheaper per night), third-party liability insurance for
mountain activities, and access to the FFCAM refuge booking portal. French
refuges enforce reservation discipline more strictly than most other systems,
particularly on the Mont Blanc massif, and FFCAM member accounts are used
in the booking portal.

## PZS — Planinska Zveza Slovenije

The Slovenian Alpine Association (Planinska Zveza Slovenije, PZS) manages around
170 huts (planinski domovi) across Slovenia, primarily in the Julian Alps and
Kamnik–Savinja range. Annual membership costs approximately €25–35 and is
available through local mountaineering societies (planinskie društva).

PZS membership provides: member rates at all PZS-affiliated huts, participation
in the organized hiking and climbing programme, insurance coverage, and
reciprocal recognition at UIAA member club huts across Europe. The PZS publishes
excellent trail maps of the Julian Alps. Slovenia punches well above its weight
in terms of the technical quality and route variety of its mountain hut network.

## DNT — Den Norske Turistforening

The Norwegian Trekking Association (DNT) operates over 500 huts across Norway,
from staffed lodges to remote self-service cabins stocked with provisions.
Annual membership costs approximately NOK 750–850 for adults, with significant
reductions for families and juniors.

DNT membership provides: a physical cabin key (DNT nøkkel) that opens all
self-service DNT cabins, member rates at staffed huts (typically NOK 100–200
cheaper per night), and participation in DNT's organized hiking programme.
DNT is the organizational backbone of Norwegian outdoor culture — the friluftsliv
tradition of open access to nature is partly expressed through the DNT hut
network, which makes remote terrain accessible without technical climbing
equipment.

## UIAA reciprocity — what it means in practice

The Union Internationale des Associations d'Alpinisme (UIAA) coordinates a
reciprocity agreement between member national clubs. Under this agreement,
membership in any UIAA member club entitles the holder to the member rate
at huts operated by other UIAA member clubs.

In practice this means: a DAV card works at SAC, ÖAV, CAI, and PZS huts.
A CAI card works at ÖAV, SAC, and DAV huts. An SAC card works in Italy and
Austria. The discount amount is generally set at the local club's standard
member rate — it is not a special reciprocal discount, it is the regular
member rate.

The card must be physical and current (valid for the current year) and is
checked at payment. An expired card will be refused. Keep it with your passport
and hiking documents.

## Junior and senior categories

Most clubs offer reduced-rate categories. DAV and ÖAV have junior rates for
under-18s (approximately €20–30) and family rates. SAC has junior and couple
categories. FFCAM has reduced rates for under-25s. DNT has the most accessible
family pricing, with children typically included in a family membership. For
a family planning a two-week alpine hut trip, membership across one or two
clubs saves a meaningful amount compared to non-member rates.
