The Aosta Valley is among the most dramatically enclosed mountain regions in Europe. Bordered to the north by the Mont Blanc massif, to the east by Monte Rosa, and to the south by the Gran Paradiso National Park, this autonomous Italian region packs an extraordinary concentration of high peaks into a valley that is barely 100 kilometres long. The rifugi network here reflects both the Italian alpine tradition — substantial stone buildings with proper kitchens and a strong emphasis on shared meals — and the international character of the clientele, which mixes Italian alpinists with Swiss, French, and northern European climbers and hikers. The ten rifugi below span the full breadth of the valley.

## 1. Rifugio Barbustel, Ayas Valley

At 2,186 metres above the village of Brusson in the Ayas valley, the Rifugio Barbustel sits in a landscape of larch forest and high meadow that provides some of the most beautiful walking in the Val d'Ayas region. The hut is named after the local dialect word for a local bird and is a solid, traditional structure with accommodation for around 50 guests. Routes from here connect to the Colle di Joux and the broader network of trails crossing between the Ayas and the adjacent Valtournenche to the west.

## 2. Rifugio Gabiet, Gressoney

The Rifugio Gabiet stands at 2,345 metres on the Colle Gabiet between Gressoney-la-Trinité and the Ayas valley, accessible by cable car from Gressoney in summer. Its position on the shoulder of the Monte Rosa massif gives exceptional views eastward toward the Swiss Valais and westward across the main Aosta Valley corridor. The hut is well equipped and sleeps around 60 guests. It serves both as a destination in itself — the Lago Gabiet below is a popular objective for summer day-hikers — and as a staging point for more serious routes onto the Rosa plateau.

## 3. Rifugio Alpenzu Grande, Gressoney

At approximately 2,076 metres above Gressoney-Saint-Jean, the Rifugio Alpenzu Grande occupies one of the highest permanently inhabited Walser settlements in the Alps — the Walser were Germanic-speaking settlers who colonised the high valleys of the Aosta region from the Valais in the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries. The rifugio sits in the middle of preserved Walser architectural heritage, and the surrounding hamlets of stone and larch-wood buildings give the approach an unusual cultural depth.

## 4. Rifugio Arp, Valpelline

The Rifugio Arp at around 2,478 metres above Oyace in the Valpelline — one of the major tributary valleys running north from the main Aosta corridor toward the Swiss border — provides a useful midpoint on routes toward the Grand Combin and the Colle du Mont Brûlé. The Valpelline is one of the wilder valleys in the region, with limited road access and a strong sense of remoteness compared to the busier Courmayeur and Cervinia areas.

## 5. Rifugio del Misérin, Gran San Bernardo area

Sitting at 2,580 metres above Chamois in the hills between the main Aosta valley and the Gran San Bernardo pass, the Rifugio del Misérin is notable for two things: its position beside a small mountain lake of exceptional clarity, and the tiny chapel beside it dedicated to Notre-Dame du Misérin, a pilgrimage site since the seventeenth century. The hut sleeps around 40 and is open from late June through September. The surrounding terrain is relatively non-technical, making it accessible to families and less experienced hikers.

## 6. Rifugio Forte Geremia, Champorcher valley

The Rifugio Forte Geremia at around 2,540 metres above the Champorcher valley in the eastern Aosta region sits close to the Gran Paradiso National Park boundary and is used as a base for routes toward the Colle della Rossa and the broader park trail network. The Gran Paradiso area supports one of the largest populations of ibex in Europe — a recovery story that began in the nineteenth century when King Victor Emmanuel II established a royal hunting reserve, which later became the first national park in Italy.

## 7. Rifugio Campolongo, Champorcher

At approximately 1,860 metres in the lower Champorcher valley, the Rifugio Campolongo serves as an entry-level overnight stop for visitors approaching the Gran Paradiso National Park from the eastern Aosta Valley. The valley is less visited than the Cogne or Valsavarenche approaches to the park, and the Campolongo accordingly retains a quiet, local character. The hut opens in summer and accommodates around 40 guests.

## 8. Rifugio Passo del Diavolo, Gran Paradiso

The Rifugio Passo del Diavolo at around 2,745 metres on the Colle di Entrelor in the Gran Paradiso National Park above Cogne provides access to the remote Piantonetto valley on the eastern side of the massif. The hut is a basic but staffed facility sleeping around 35, and its position on a high pass gives superb views toward the Gran Paradiso summit (4,061 metres), which rises steeply to the south. Routes from here cross into the Orco valley in Piedmont.

## 9. Rifugio Mont Fallère, Gran San Bernardo

At approximately 2,480 metres above Saint-Nicolas on the ridge between the main Aosta valley and the Valpelline, the Rifugio Mont Fallère serves the Mont Fallère summit at 3,061 metres — an excellent viewpoint with relatively straightforward routes by Aosta standards. The hut is a modern SAC-style facility with space for around 60 guests and is open from June through September. Mont Fallère is one of the most prominent summits visible from Aosta city, making the hut a popular destination for residents of the valley floor.

## 10. Rifugio Barmasse, Valtournenche

At 2,172 metres above Cheneil in the upper Valtournenche above Chatillon, the Rifugio Barmasse occupies a stunning position on a high shelf looking directly toward the Matterhorn's Italian south face. The Cheneil plateau at around 2,100 metres is accessible by four-wheel drive road from Valtournenche village and serves as a starting point for routes toward the Colle di Valcournera. The rifugio is a comfortable, family-run facility with strong local cooking and space for about 50 guests. The view of the Matterhorn from the terrace is among the finest in Italy.

## Discover the Full Aosta Network

The Aosta Valley's rifugi vary enormously in character, from basic park shelters to large commercial rifugi with full restaurant menus. Many of the best huts are located in valleys with limited public transport, so planning ahead matters.
