The Queyras, tucked against the Italian border in the southern French Alps, is one of the sunniest and driest corners of the whole range, a high regional park where larch forests, dry meadows and pale dolomitic peaks replace the heavy glaciers of the north. Saint-Véran, at around 2040 metres, is often called the highest permanently inhabited commune in Europe, and the valleys around it shelter a string of refuges and gîtes that serve the Tour du Queyras, the GR58. With a Mediterranean clarity of light and weather far more reliable than the high Alps, the Queyras has become a beloved trekking region for those who want long sunlit days and a gentler, pastoral mountain world.

## A dry, sunny high country

What sets the Queyras apart is its climate. Sheltered from Atlantic weather by the massifs to the west, it sits in a pocket of clear southern skies, with hundreds of days of sun a year and a dry, fragrant air. The mountains are not the great icefields of the Mont Blanc or Écrins but sun-baked ridges, marble and schist peaks such as the Pic de Rochebrune (3320 m) and the Pain de Sucre, with larch and Arolla pine climbing high up the slopes. The refuges sit among meadows and forests rather than glaciers, and the walking is long, scenic and friendly, with villages of stone and timber chalets dotting the valleys.

## The Tour du Queyras and the GR58

The Tour du Queyras, following the GR58, is the great walking circuit of the region, a loop of roughly a week that links the main valleys and crosses a series of high cols. It strings together village gîtes d'étape and mountain refuges, so walkers can choose between the comfort of an inhabited hamlet and the wilder feel of a high hut. The circuit crosses passes such as the Col Fromage and the Col de Bramousse, and connects to the GR5 and the cross-border routes into the Italian Piedmont, making the Queyras a hub of long-distance walking in the southern Alps.

## Refuge Agnel

The Refuge Agnel at 2580 metres sits high beneath the Col Agnel, one of the highest paved passes in the Alps on the Italian frontier, near the elegant pyramid of the Pain de Sucre. It is a classic CAF hut, a base for ascents of the Pain de Sucre and the surrounding peaks and a key stage on routes crossing into Italy. The position, in open high pasture beneath the frontier ridge, gives wide views and easy access to the col, and it is busy in summer with walkers and cyclists drawn by the famous pass.

## Refuge de Furfande

The Refuge de Furfande sits at around 2300 metres on the magnificent alpage of Furfande, a high pastoral shelf scattered with old chalets above the Guil valley. It is one of the most atmospheric stops on the Tour du Queyras, a place where the pastoral life of the high Alps survives, with grazing flocks and a sweeping panorama across to the peaks of the Écrins. The approach climbs through larch forest to a balcony of meadow, and the hut's terrace is a celebrated spot for the long southern evenings.

## Gîte d'étape de Saint-Véran

Saint-Véran, the highest commune in the region and one of the highest in Europe, offers gîtes d'étape among its sundial-painted stone and timber houses. Staying in the village is a highlight of the Tour du Queyras, a chance to sleep at over 2000 metres in an inhabited place of remarkable architecture, with its old fountains, fustes of stacked larch and the famous painted sundials. From here walkers head for the Pic de Caramantran and the high cols on the frontier, and the village serves as a comfortable base in the heart of the park.

## Refuge de la Blanche

The Refuge de la Blanche sits at around 2500 metres beneath the frontier ridge above Saint-Véran, near the small Lac de la Blanche and the Pic de Caramantran. It is a quiet high hut on a variant of the circuit, a base for the easy ascent of Caramantran and for crossings of the Col de Chamoussière into the upper valleys. The setting, among bare high pastures beneath the border peaks, has the dry luminous quality that defines the Queyras.

## Refuge du Viso

The Refuge du Viso at 2460 metres lies beneath the great pyramid of Monte Viso (3841 m), the highest peak of the Cottian Alps, which rises just across the Italian border and dominates the eastern Queyras. It is the base for the famous Tour du Viso, the circuit of the mountain that crosses repeatedly between France and Italy, and for the historic Buco di Viso, the oldest tunnel through the Alps, dug in the fifteenth century. The hut sits in wild high country beneath one of the most iconic summits of the southern Alps.

## Refuge Napoléon

The Refuge Napoléon at the Col d'Izoard, at around 2300 metres, is one of a series of huts founded under the bequest of Napoleon to shelter travellers on the high passes. It stands near the summit of the Izoard, one of the most famous cols in cycling, beside the strange lunar landscape of the Casse Déserte with its eroded rock pinnacles. The hut is a comfortable base on the northern edge of the Queyras, well placed for walks among the larch forests and the dramatic scree of the pass.

## Refuge de Bramousse and the cross-border routes

The valleys of the Queyras have long been a corridor between France and Italy, and the refuges and village gîtes near the Col de Bramousse and the Col de Saint-Martin serve walkers crossing the frontier. The cross-border heritage runs deep here, from the Vauban fortifications of Château-Queyras guarding the Guil valley to the Buco di Viso beneath Monte Viso. These huts give access to the long traverses that link the French park to the Italian valleys of the Po and the Varaita, where the same dry southern light and pastoral culture continue across the watershed.

## Wildlife, larch forests and pastoral life

The Queyras regional park, created in 1977, protects a landscape shaped as much by people as by nature. The larch forests, which turn gold in autumn, are among the finest in the Alps, and the dry meadows are rich in butterflies and aromatic herbs. Chamois and ibex roam the high ridges, marmots are everywhere, and the black grouse and the rare ptarmigan live in the high country, while the bearded vulture and golden eagle patrol overhead. The valleys preserve a living pastoral tradition, with flocks brought up to the alpages each summer and the cheeses and honey of the region sold in the villages. This human warmth, the painted sundials and the welcoming village gîtes, gives the Queyras a character quite distinct from the austere high glacier massifs.

## Practical notes for the Queyras huts

The Queyras refuges and gîtes are staffed mainly from June to September, with the dry, stable climate making the walking season long and reliable. The Tour du Queyras takes about a week, with day stages of three to six hours over cols that rarely require any technical skill, making it ideal for first multi-day treks. Reservation through the gîtes or the CAF is advised in high summer. Gateways include Guillestre, Ceillac, Aiguilles and the high village of Saint-Véran, and the circuit connects readily to the GR5 and to the cross-border routes around Monte Viso into Italy.
