The moment a route description says "glacier travel required," the nature of
the objective changes. Not just the gear list — the rope, crampons, ice axe,
and prussik slings — but the way the team moves, the way decisions are made,
and the way hazards are shared. Glacier travel is the single most significant
technical threshold in European alpine hut travel: below it, the approach
to the Monte Rosa Hut from Rotenboden is a long walk; above it, it is a
mountaineering undertaking.

## What makes a glacier dangerous

A glacier is a river of ice moving under its own weight. As it moves over
convexities in the bedrock below, the surface cracks into crevasses —
gaps in the ice that can be a few centimetres or many metres wide, and whose
depth can exceed 30 metres. In winter and early summer, snow bridges form
across crevasses and can hold the weight of a person. As summer progresses,
these bridges weaken. By late summer, many crevasses are open and visible;
in late spring, they may be hidden under firm-looking snow with no surface
indication of the void below.

Falling into an uncovered crevasse while unroped typically means death. Falling
in while roped means a recoverable situation — uncomfortable, potentially
serious, but survivable with a practised rescue protocol.

## Rope spacing

For a two-person party on a snow-covered glacier, the standard rope length
is 30 metres with 8–12 metres of active rope between the two climbers; the
rest is coiled and carried by each person around their body. The coils absorb
the initial shock of a crevasse fall and slow the fall before the rope
arrests it.

For a three-person party, the rope is divided equally — typically 10 metres
between each pair. Four people on a 40-metre rope is a reasonable configuration.

The key principle is that the two people NOT in the crevasse must be far enough
apart that both can anchor and hold before the falling partner reaches the
bottom of the section between them. A rope spacing of 5 metres between four
people on a 15-metre rope defeats the purpose: if one falls, the others are
too close to have time to self-arrest.

## Moving roped

Walking roped on glacier involves keeping the rope from dragging on the snow
(coils in hand, not on the ice) while staying alert to the person ahead. The
standard cadence on easy glacier terrain is a steady walk, with each person
monitoring the rope tension and the surface underfoot.

On a crevassed section, the party slows. Each person watches the snow in front
of them and probes with a pole or ice axe on suspect surfaces. Snow that is
convex — bulging slightly upward — is often concealing a void; snow that sounds
hollow underfoot is an obvious warning. The party moves across suspected snow
bridges quickly, spacing out more to reduce simultaneous loading.

## Crevasse rescue

Every person on a glacier rope should know the crevasse rescue procedure
before leaving the valley. The steps, in sequence: the fallen climber calls
"Fall!"; the other members self-arrest immediately (ice axe pick driven into
the snow, body weight low); once the rope is held, the victim self-rescues if
possible using prussik slings to walk up the rope while the anchor holds;
if self-rescue is not possible, the surface party builds an anchor and performs
a hauling rescue (Z-pulley or drop-C system).

The prussik slings — two loops of 5–6 mm cord, each approximately 60 cm long
— are the crevasse rescue tool that weighs almost nothing and is carried on
the harness at all times. Tying prussik knots should be practised until it is
automatic. This is not theory; it is the thing you do, in the cold, with gloves
on, under stress.

## The ice axe

The ice axe is the primary self-arrest tool on snow and the anchor component
of a crevasse rescue. The standard technique — pick pointing back, shaft flat
against the slope, body weight on the head — arrests a slide on moderate snow
slopes and must be practised until instinctive. Guides running introductory
glacier courses typically spend 45 minutes on a snow slope practising self-
arrest before moving to roped travel.

The ice axe for glacier walking and easy alpinism is a general-mountaineering
axe (60–70 cm, flat adze, spike on the shaft). Technical ice-climbing axes
with curved shafts and steep picks are not suitable and are unnecessarily heavy
for this context.

## Snow bridges: when to cross and when to go around

The decision whether to cross a snow bridge or detour around an open crevasse
is one of the key judgment calls in glacier travel. The assessment looks at
the bridge width, the snow condition (cold and firm vs warm and soft), the
estimated time of day (bridges are more stable in the early morning after a cold
night), and what lies on the other side if the bridge collapses.

The rule for borderline snow bridges is: cross one at a time, at speed, with
the team spread out and the anchor ready. Never weight a bridge simultaneously
with two members of the party.

## Hut approaches requiring glacier travel

Among the classic hut approaches requiring glacier travel are the Monte Rosa
Hut from Rotenboden (F-grade, well-marked), the Cabane des Vignettes CAS
from Arolla (F-grade, heavily crevassed in late season), and the Konkordia
Hütte approach across the Aletsch Glacier. All three are appropriate for
parties with basic glacier training; none is appropriate for parties with no
prior experience of roped glacier travel.

## Reading glaciated terrain

Navigation on a glacier is different from trail navigation because the
terrain changes year to year and the standard mapping often does not
reflect current conditions. The 1:25,000 Swiss or Austrian topographic
maps are accurate for elevation and general crevasse zone locations but
cannot show the specific conditions of a particular day in a particular
season.

The approach is to navigate by understanding the glacier's topography:
crevasse zones typically form where the glacier bends, accelerates down
a steeper section (an icefall), or spreads laterally. The accumulation
zone (high on the glacier) is generally less crevassed than the ablation
zone (lower, where ice melts faster). In June-July, extensive snow cover
conceals crevasses — this is the most dangerous period for hidden holes.
By August-September, crevasses are typically open and visible, making
navigation more predictable but the obstacles more obvious.

Experienced glacier navigators read the snow surface for the subtler
signs: slight depressions, different snow texture, slight shadows that
indicate a concealed void below. This skill develops over multiple glacier
days; it cannot be taught in a classroom.

## Crevasse rescue: the full sequence

When a glacier companion falls through a snow bridge, the priority sequence
is: arrest the rope (team falls flat to distribute weight), confirm the
person is alive and conscious, assess the situation, and begin rescue.
If the person is conscious and uninjured, they can assist from below;
if unconscious, a haul system must lift dead weight.

The standard haul system used in guide courses is the Z-pulley: a 3:1
mechanical advantage system created with the rope, two prussik cords, and
two carabiners. Setup takes 3–5 minutes with practice. The Z-pulley is
taught in every alpine guide course as the minimum standard for a two-person
glacier rope team rescue.

Practice matters more than any amount of theory. Spending 30 minutes in
a controlled setting — on a prepared crevasse, or on a steep snow slope with
a training harness — is what converts the theoretical knowledge into a
practised response.
