On a clear July morning at the Mönchsjochhütte, perched at 3,650 metres above the Jungfraujoch, the first sound of the day is often not the clatter of breakfast crockery but the deep thud of rotor blades echoing off the Sphinx. A small red helicopter swings up the glacier with a net of crates dangling on a long line, drops it on the snow outside the kitchen door, and is gone in ninety seconds. That single load might hold the week's vegetables, a pallet of beer, fresh bread, bottled gas and the post. Almost everything you eat and drink at a high alpine hut arrived the same way, and the economics of that flight quietly shape the price of your dinner.

## Why Flying Is the Only Option

Most serviced huts above roughly 2,500 metres have no road, no cable car for freight and no realistic mule track. A few historic huts were once supplied by porters carrying eighty-kilo loads on their backs, and a handful in the Dolomites and the Mont Blanc range still use small material ropeways — thin cargo cables strung up the mountainside. But for the great majority of high refuges, the helicopter is the supply chain. A flight that takes four minutes replaces what would be a six-hour carry for a team of porters, and it can move a tonne of goods in a morning that no human chain could match.

The dependence is near total. When poor weather grounds the helicopters for a week, as happens every season, wardens ration the menu, run out of fresh produce and apologise for serving the same pasta three nights running. A hut's stockroom is its insurance, and a good warden plans the whole season around predicted flying windows.

## The Anatomy of a Supply Flight

The work is done by light utility helicopters built for mountain flying — the Aérospatiale Lama, long the legend of the Alps, and modern machines like the Airbus H125 and the Kamov used in Italy. They fly with the cargo slung beneath them on a longline rather than packed inside, because an external load is faster to hook and unhook and lets the pilot place it precisely without landing on uneven ground.

A typical rotation works like a shuttle. The helicopter loads at a valley base — Lauterbrunnen, Zermatt, Chamonix or a farmer's field near the trailhead — where ground crew have pre-packed everything into nets and crates weighed to the gram. The pilot lifts, climbs the valley, hovers over the hut's marked landing spot, releases the load, and dives back down empty to collect the next net. Companies such as Air Zermatt, Air Glaciers and Heli Bernina run dozens of these rotations on busy supply days, sequencing several huts on one fuel load.

## What a Load Actually Costs

Flying is expensive, and that cost lands on the menu. Helicopter time is billed by the minute, and a single rotation to a high hut can cost several hundred Swiss francs or euros once you account for fuel, pilot, ground crew and the machine itself. A warden supplying a remote refuge for a season spends tens of thousands on flights alone, which is precisely why a beer at 3,000 metres costs three or four times what it does in the valley and why the menu leans on dense, durable, calorie-rich staples — rösti, polenta, lentil stews, cured meats and hard cheese — that survive storage and justify their flown weight.

Clever wardens minimise flights by ordering in bulk early, freezing what they can, and choosing ingredients with a long shelf life. The romance of fresh salad at altitude is real, but it is also a luxury that each leaf paid airfare to reach.

## Backhaul: Flying the Rubbish Down

The helicopter does not return empty. The same flights that bring food up carry waste, recycling, empty gas bottles and sewage tanks back down. A high hut produces a surprising volume of refuse, and because there is no bin lorry on a glacier, all of it must be flown to the valley for disposal. Many huts compact and bale their rubbish, freeze food waste to stop it rotting, and store everything until a backhaul flight can take it.

This closing of the loop is one of the least visible but most important parts of hut logistics. The cost of removing waste is folded into the same flight budget as the supply run, and it is a major reason huts charge for some things that feel free in the valley, and why they ask guests to carry their own litter back out whenever they can.

## Weather, Risk and the Pilot's Judgement

Mountain supply flying is among the most demanding rotary work there is. Pilots operate close to the helicopter's altitude ceiling, where thin air robs the rotor of lift, in turbulent valley winds and around vertical rock. A longline load swinging beneath the machine adds another variable, and the margin for error near a hut platform built into a slope is small.

Flying happens in the calm of early morning before thermals build, and is called off the moment cloud, wind or poor visibility makes it unsafe. This is why supply schedules are provisional and why a stretch of bad weather quietly empties a hut's larder. The pilots who do this work, often the same crews who fly mountain rescue, are some of the most skilled in aviation.

## How It Shapes Your Stay

Understanding the flight behind your dinner changes how you treat a hut. It explains why the warden asks you to confirm your booking and your meal — every uneaten portion of flown food is wasted money and wasted carbon. It explains why tap water may be limited, why showers cost extra or do not exist, and why the menu is short. And it is the best argument for carrying your own rubbish down: every kilo you remove is a kilo the helicopter does not have to.

Next time a red dot climbs the glacier toward the hut, watch it work. That brief, noisy ballet is the thread that keeps a building habitable in a place humans were never meant to live comfortably, and it is the reason a hot meal and a cold drink are waiting when you arrive.
