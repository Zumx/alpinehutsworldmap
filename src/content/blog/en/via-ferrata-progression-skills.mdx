Via ferrata — iron way — is a form of assisted climbing on fixed infrastructure:
steel cables, iron rungs, and occasionally suspension bridges bolted into
rock faces that would otherwise require technical climbing skills and protection
equipment. Developed in the Dolomites during the First World War for rapid
troop movement and since extended across the Alps and beyond, the via ferrata
network opens vertical terrain to non-technical climbers. Doing it safely
requires specific equipment and an understanding of the grade system.

## The KS (Klettersteig) grade scale

The most widely used grading system for via ferrata is the Klettersteig (KS)
or Hüsler scale, rated from A/KS1 to E/KS5:

KS A (Easy): well-maintained cables on moderate terrain, good footholds, minimal
exposure. Accessible to beginners with basic ferrata equipment. Time commitment
typically 1–3 hours. Examples: most introductory Dolomite routes, the
lower sections of the Averau approach.

KS B (Moderate): steeper terrain with some sections requiring arm strength.
Cable use is regular rather than occasional. Exposure begins. The majority
of tourist-oriented via ferrata routes are in this category.

KS C (Difficult): sustained vertical and near-vertical sections, significant
exposure. Some sections require genuine upper body strength. Cable use is
continuous. A moderate fitness level and previous KS A/B experience are
required. Full energy absorber is strongly recommended.

KS D (Very Difficult): nearly vertical with overhanging sections. Exposure
is serious. The route may involve sections of free climbing between cables.
For experienced climbers only; physical fitness comparable to bouldering V2–3
or sport climbing 5c is typically needed.

KS E (Extremely Difficult): elite sport climbing strength required. These routes
are the domain of dedicated sport climbers and expert alpinists. The cable
is present for emergency fall arrest but cannot substitute for the arm
strength required to progress.

Most via ferrata accessible from huts in the Alps fall in the KS A-C range.
The approach to the Rifugio Pisciadù on the Alta Via 2 involves KS B-C terrain;
the classic Dolomite ferrata routes above the Rifugio Lagazuoi area are
generally A-B grade.

## The double-lanyard energy absorber

A standard harness and karabiner are not adequate for via ferrata. The specific
hazard of a via ferrata fall is the cable-fall: if you are clipped to a cable
and fall, the force generated by stopping on the cable can exceed 15 kN —
well above the threshold for serious internal injury. The energy absorber is
the device that reduces this force to below 6 kN by deploying a rip-strip or
mechanical braking mechanism during the arrest.

The Y-shaped double lanyard (two arms with a central energy absorber) allows
continuous connection: one arm is always clipped to the cable before the other
is moved to the next anchor point. This means you are never unclipped. The
double configuration is not optional on KS B and above — it is the difference
between a managed fall and a ground-fall scenario.

Selection: lanyards with integral energy absorbers (Petzl Scorpio Vertigo,
Black Diamond Via Ferrata Whippet, Kong Air) are lighter than bolt-on absorber
systems. The combined weight of harness plus Y-lanyard absorber is typically
700 g–1.2 kg.

## Helmet

A helmet is non-negotiable above KS A on any via ferrata. The primary hazard
it mitigates is rockfall — other parties above can dislodge stones, and the
fixed hardware itself can shed rock fragments under use. A direct hit to an
unprotected skull from a 50-gram rock at 5 m/s is fatal; the same event with
a certified climbing helmet typically causes nothing worse than a sharp noise.

Mountain helmets (Petzl Sirocco, Black Diamond Vapor, Mammut Skywalker 3)
designed for climbing double as via ferrata helmets and are significantly
lighter than ski or cycling helmets. They are not interchangeable — a cycling
helmet provides no meaningful protection from the impact patterns characteristic
of climbing or ferrata use.

## Gloves

Gloves protect the palms when sliding down cables and gripping iron rungs that
have been heated by sun exposure or covered in rock dust. Light belay gloves
(goat leather or synthetic reinforced palm) are appropriate for ferrata use —
full finger protection with adequate grip sensitivity. The thin knitwear
gloves used for cold-temperature hiking are not suitable.

## Body positioning

The most common beginner error on via ferrata is standing too close to the
rock face — nose against the rock — which creates an awkward, off-balance
position that tires the arms rapidly. The correct position is feet spread
(hip-width or wider), body angled away from the rock face at approximately 30
degrees, weight on the feet rather than the arms, and the cable held for
balance rather than hauling.

Arms tire before legs. Using the cable as a ladder (pulling up hand-over-hand
instead of stepping up) is the fastest route to exhausted forearms on a KS B
route. The rule is: use your feet to climb, use your arms for balance.

## Progression

A sensible progression: begin on a KS A route accessible from a hut — a 2-hour
route with good holds and minimal exposure. Complete a guided introduction if
possible; guides on popular Dolomite or Tyrolean ferrata are available from
local guide services. Progress to KS B after feeling comfortable with the
equipment deployment and the exposure. Build to KS C over multiple routes;
do not attempt D-grade routes without regular climbing training alongside.

## Huts near via ferrata

The Dolomites are the densest concentration of via ferrata in the world. The
Rifugio Lagazuoi, Rifugio Averau, and Rifugio Pisciadù all provide direct
access to routes from their terraces.

## Via ferrata in the Austrian Alps

The Austrian Alps have their own via ferrata network distinct from the
Dolomites — typically less exposed and with shorter individual routes, but
integrated with the ÖAV hut system and well-marked. The Karwendelgebirge,
the Kaisergebirge (Wilder and Zahmer Kaiser near Kufstein), and the
Gesäuse are the primary Austrian via ferrata areas. The Klettersteig grades
in Austrian guidebooks use the same KS A–E scale as the international system
but may be assessed slightly more conservatively.

The Wilder Kaiser above St Johann in Tirol has some of the best intermediate
via ferrata in Austria — routes on the south face of the Kaiser range in
the KS B–C grade that are backed up by the valley huts of the Kaisergebirge.
The combination of a 2-hour approach, a 3-hour ferrata, and a 90-minute
descent to a valley Gasthof for dinner is the classic Austrian via ferrata
day format.

## Helmet fitting and inspection

The helmet is the most important safety item in the via ferrata kit and
also the most frequently neglected in terms of fit and condition. A properly
fitted helmet sits level on the head (not tilted back), with the front edge
two finger-widths above the eyebrow line. The chin strap should be snug
enough that the helmet cannot be pulled off backward; loose chin straps
make the helmet ineffective against an upward fall.

Via ferrata helmets are rated against the same CE EN 12492 standard as
climbing helmets. Inspect the shell annually for cracks, deep scratches,
or delamination — especially after any significant impact. Helmets that
have taken a fall should be retired even if no visible damage is apparent;
the internal foam may be compromised.
