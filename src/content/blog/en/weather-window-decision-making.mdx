The alpine weather window is the most consequential variable in mountain
planning. A stable three-day window makes a difficult route manageable; a
deteriorating forecast makes a straightforward route dangerous. The skill of
reading forecasts, understanding local terrain effects, and executing a
disciplined turnaround when conditions change is more valuable than any
technical climbing ability on the majority of accidents in the Alps.

## The forecast services

MeteoSwiss (meteoswiss.ch) is the Swiss federal meteorological service and
provides the most detailed point-specific forecasts available for Swiss Alpine
terrain. The "local forecast" function allows input of any location with
coordinates and returns an hourly forecast including cloud base, precipitation
probability, wind speed, freezing level, and thunder probability. For any
route involving the Swiss Alps — from the Haute Route to the Monte Rosa
approaches from the Cabane des Vignettes CAS — MeteoSwiss is the starting
point.

Météo-France (meteofrance.com) provides equivalent data for French terrain,
with an excellent mountain bulletin covering the principal ranges: Alpes du
Nord, Alpes du Sud, Pyrenees. The Bulletin de Montagne is issued twice daily
and covers summit conditions, freezing level, and wind forecast at specific
elevations. For Mont Blanc and the French Haute Savoie ranges, Météo-France
is the reference.

Alpenverein Wetter (via the ÖAV and DAV apps and websites) aggregates
ZAMG (Zentralanstalt für Meteorologie und Geodynamik) forecasts for Austrian
alpine terrain into hut-specific forecasts. The ZAMG Bergtour-Wetterinfo
service provides a 3-day mountain forecast for specific Austrian routes and
huts. For a traverse through the Stubai Alps visiting the Bremer Hütte and
Innsbrucker Hütte, this is the relevant service.

## Afternoon convection: the Alpine clock

In the Alps in summer, the typical clear-weather day follows a predictable
pattern. The morning is clear: a high-pressure system allows overnight cooling
that stabilises the atmosphere. Solar heating begins at sunrise. By mid-morning,
cumulus clouds begin to build over the peaks. By early afternoon — typically
13:00–15:00 in high summer — these cumulus towers have developed into
cumulonimbus: the anvil-topped thunderstorm clouds that produce lightning.

The practical implication: be on any exposed summit, ridge, or high col before
12:00 noon in summer. This is the reason Alpine mountaineers depart at 04:00
or 05:00 for summit routes: the summit is reached before the convective window
opens, and the descent is complete before the afternoon storm arrives.

"Afternoon" varies by season and region. In the Alps in July–August, the
convective trigger is typically earliest (13:00–14:00). In September, the
atmosphere is more stable and the afternoon storms are less predictable but
often more severe when they occur. In the Pyrenees, the convective pattern
is similar but shifts slightly later.

## Lightning in the mountains

Lightning in mountain terrain is the direct lethal consequence of ignoring
the convective timeline. The exposed ridge, the summit pyramid, the wet
via ferrata cable are all elevated conducting paths. A direct strike is fatal;
a ground strike nearby can cause injury through step voltage.

The rules:
- Off ridges and summits before 12:00 noon in summer thunderstorm risk.
- If caught on exposed terrain with a storm developing: descend immediately
via the fastest safe route; do not shelter under isolated trees or overhanging
rock; crouch with feet together if a strike is imminent, not lying flat.
- If shelter in a hut or valley is within reach, move toward it. Do not continue
on route during a thunderstorm.

Metal equipment — crampons, ice axes, via ferrata lanyards — does not significantly
increase lightning risk relative to a person standing on the same terrain without
it. The metal does not attract lightning; the terrain and elevation are the risk
factors.

## The turnaround rule

Every party departing for a summit route or high traverse should establish
a turnaround time before leaving the hut. The turnaround time is the moment
at which you begin descending regardless of progress toward the objective.
It is established the night before using the forecast, the route time, and
the convective window. It is not negotiable on the day.

The psychological difficulty of the turnaround is that it requires abandoning
an objective when you are often within reach of it, in a weather window
that has not yet fully closed. The classic accident profile: a party that
reaches the summit 30 minutes after their turnaround time in deteriorating
conditions. They are 30 minutes past the point where they should have started
descent; the storm catches them on the upper face.

Establishing the turnaround time as a commitment the night before — spoken
aloud in the hut, written in a notebook if needed — takes the decision out of
the summit-day judgment that is always compromised by effort, proximity to
the goal, and social pressure.

## Hut keeper forecasts

The hut keeper is the local meteorological expert. At the Rifugio Lagazuoi
or the Konkordia Hütte, the person who has spent 15 or 20 summers watching
the sky from that position knows things that no forecast service captures:
the specific cloud formation over a particular valley that reliably precedes
a storm, the wind direction that means tomorrow will be good, the way the
temperature drops that signals a front 6 hours out. Talking to the hut keeper
the evening before a summit day, in addition to checking the official forecast,
is standard good practice.

## Reading the sky: observational forecasting

Before digital weather forecasting, alpinists relied on observational sky
reading — a tradition still worth knowing even when MeteoSwiss is in the
pocket. The standard indicators: a halo around the moon (ice crystals in
high cirrus — front typically 12–18 hours away), lenticular clouds on the
summit (high-level wind and moisture), cumulus towers building rapidly by
10:00 (convective instability, thunderstorms probable by afternoon), red
sky at night (stable settling air — but not reliable below 2000 m in
summer), and the barometric pressure trend (falling pressure in the last
three hours is more significant than the absolute reading).

The high-pressure wave that produces the best Alpine weather days has a
characteristic: cool and calm at night, gradual cloud development after
midday that does not deepen into cumulonimbus. A high that is beginning
to break down shows cloud arriving from the west or southwest, typically
altostratus sheets rather than cumulus. The difference between a day that
builds and clears versus a day that builds and becomes a storm is visible
in the cloud height and the rate of development.

These observational skills supplement digital forecasting but do not replace
it. The value of observational reading is speed and immediacy: on the face
at 07:00 with no phone signal, reading the sky is the only forecast available.

## Acclimatisation days as weather windows

In multi-day high-altitude objectives, acclimatisation days in the hut
can be used strategically to wait for the right weather window. The team
that spends an extra day at the Hörnlihütte resting and acclimatising while
a weather system passes is in better physiological and psychological shape
for the summit attempt than the team that pushed through in poor conditions
and arrived tired. Patience at altitude is a learned discipline that pays
dividends.

The hut keeper's advice on the typical length of weather cycles in that
specific area — "this kind of front usually clears in 36 hours here" —
is local knowledge that no app provides.
