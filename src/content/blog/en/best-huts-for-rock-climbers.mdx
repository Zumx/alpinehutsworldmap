## Why the Approach Hut Matters

Rock climbing in the alpine zone is fundamentally different from sport climbing at a crag. An alpine rock route may be four hours from the nearest road, on a face that receives morning shade, demanding an early start that only makes sense if you slept within striking distance of the base. The approach hut is not a comfort supplement to the climbing — it is the operational platform that makes the climbing possible.

The best approach huts for rock climbing combine proximity to the objective, reliable early breakfast service, secure storage for gear left behind, and a staff that understands the climbing objectives well enough to provide current conditions information. Many wardens in climbing areas have themselves climbed the routes and can describe the current state of fixed gear, the wetness of crack systems after recent rain, and which sections of a route take unexpected time for most parties.

## Monbijouhütte / Rifugio Carducci, Carnic Alps

Not every classic climbing area is famous. The Carnic Alps along the Austrian-Italian border contain several limestone walls of high quality that are largely known only to Austrian and Italian climbers. The Rifugio Carducci (1928 m), on the Italian side near Sappada, provides access to the Crete di Longerin and the Cima dei Preti. Routes on these walls run from UIAA IV to VII, largely on solid Dolomite limestone with good natural protection.

## Vajolet Hut, Rosengarten (Catinaccio)

The Rifugio Vajolet (2243 m) and the nearby Rifugio Preuss (2243 m) in the Rosengarten group of the Dolomites sit directly below the Vajolet Towers — three limestone needles that constitute one of the most climbed objectives in the eastern Alps. The shortest of the towers, the Torre Winkler, was first climbed in 1887 by Georg Winkler at age 17. Routes on the towers are graded UIAA IV to VII+ and are described in the classic Castiglioni Dolomite guide. The huts are accessible by a 1.5-hour walk from the Grasleitenpasshütte via the Vajolet valley, or by a slightly shorter approach from the Kölner Hütte side.

Beds at the Rifugio Vajolet must be reserved well in advance for July and August. The warden is accustomed to parties starting at 4:30 am for early routes and will arrange cold breakfast boxes upon request.

## Rifugio Torre di Pisa, Brenta Dolomites

The Brenta Dolomites west of Trento are less visited than the Cortina area and contain walls of outstanding character. The Rifugio Torre di Pisa (2669 m) serves the central Brenta massif and gives access to the Via delle Bocchette, a celebrated via ferrata circuit, as well as purely technical rock routes on the Cima Tosa (3173 m) and Cima Brenta (3150 m). Routes on the Brenta limestone are typically more serious in character than equivalent-grade routes in the Cortina Dolomites, with more serious runout and less fixed protection.

## Refuge du Requin, Chamonix Aiguilles

The Refuge du Requin (2516 m) sits at the edge of the Mer de Glace glacier in the Chamonix valley and provides access to some of the most significant multi-pitch granite routes in the Alps. The Aiguilles de Chamonix — including the Aiguille du Midi (3842 m), the Aiguille du Grépon, and the Aiguille des Grandes Charmoz — rise directly above. The granite here is world-famous: coarse-crystalled, rough, and reliable when dry.

The Refuge du Requin is staffed by the CAF from late June to early September and accessed via a 45-minute walk from the Montenvers railway station (reached by a rack railway from Chamonix town). Parties climbing the Grépon or the Nantillons face are expected to leave the refuge before 4 am to reach the approach before the sun hits the serac bands above.

## Biancograt and Coaz Hut, Bernina Massif

The Coaz Hut (2610 m) and the nearby Tschierva Hut (2573 m) in the Engadin above Pontresina serve the Bernina massif — the highest range entirely within Switzerland, with Piz Bernina reaching 4049 metres. The granite and metamorphic rock of the Bernina offers high-altitude rock and mixed routes that see far fewer parties than comparable Mont Blanc terrain. The Biancograt — the northeast ridge of Piz Bernina — is graded AD and is one of the most beautiful sustained ridge routes in the Alps, involving 700 metres of ridge travel on good granite with intermittent snow and ice patches.

## Kaiserschmarrn and the Stripsenjochhaus, Wilder Kaiser

The Wilder Kaiser range north of Kitzbühel in Tyrol is a compact limestone massif of extraordinary climbing density. The Stripsenjochhaus (1577 m) sits in the col at the heart of the range and is surrounded by walls of the Totenkirchl, the Fleischbank, and the Predigtstuhl — all carrying classic routes from UIAA V to IX. The north walls here are among the most serious north-facing limestone walls in the eastern Alps, with routes including the Dülfer Route on the Fleischbank (first ascent 1912, Hans Dülfer) still occupying a prominent position in European climbing history.

The Stripsenjochhaus is an ÖAV hut, staffed mid-June to late October. It has a celebrated kitchen, the eponymous Kaiserschmarrn (a classic Austrian caramelised pancake dessert) being legitimately considered the best in the region.

## Rifugio Comici, Tre Cime di Lavaredo

The Rifugio Lavaredo (2344 m) and the Rifugio Locatelli (2405 m) both serve the Tre Cime di Lavaredo, whose north faces carry some of the most photographed and climbed routes in the Dolomites. The Dülfer Route on the Cima Piccola (UIAA VI), the Comici Route on the Cima Grande (UIAA VI, first ascent 1933 by Emilio Comici), and the modern Hasse-Brandler (UIAA VII+) on the Cima Grande north face are the reference routes of different generations of alpinism. All are approached from the circuit path around the base of the Tre Cime, roughly 15 to 20 minutes from the rifugi.

## Crampon Point, Midi Plan Refuge, Mont Blanc

For alpinists combining technical rock and glacier terrain in the Mont Blanc massif, the Refuge des Cosmiques (3613 m) — adjacent to the Aiguille du Midi cable car top station — provides a base for the Arête des Cosmiques (AD) and for approached to the Mallory Route on the Aiguille du Midi and the traverse to the Vallée Blanche. Its unusual position at a cable car terminal makes it the easiest-to-access high refuge in the Alps.
