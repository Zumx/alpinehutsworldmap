British Columbia's mountain ranges cover an area larger than France, encompassing the Coast Mountains stretching from the US border to Alaska, the Columbia Mountains including the Selkirks and Monashees, and the northern Rocky Mountain ranges that merge into the Yukon. Backcountry infrastructure reflects both this scale and the limited visitor density outside a few well-known zones: huts range from BC Parks emergency fibreglass shelters holding six people to fully catered ski lodges accommodating 25 guests. The Alpine Club of Canada (ACC) maintains a network of huts across BC used by climbers, ski tourers, and summer hikers; BC Parks manages emergency shelters at key locations in provincial parks. The main summer hiking season runs from late July to early October at elevations above 1,800 metres; ski touring extends from December through April at mid-elevations and into June on the highest glaciers.

## 1. Brohm Ridge Chalet, Squamish

Brohm Ridge Chalet sits at roughly 1,500 metres above the Sea to Sky corridor north of Squamish, in the Tantalus Range foothills. The chalet is a privately operated facility with comfortable accommodation for 15 to 20 guests, heated by wood stove, and providing a kitchen for self-catered meals. The ridge offers ski touring through March and hiking from July, with exceptional views of the Tantalus peaks — Tantalus itself tops out at 2,603 metres with permanent glaciation. Access is by snowmobile in winter and on foot or quad trail in summer, making it one of the most accessible overnight alpine options within 100 kilometres of Vancouver.

## 2. BC Parks Emergency Hut, Garibaldi Provincial Park

Garibaldi Provincial Park contains several emergency huts at strategic locations along its backcountry trail system. These fibreglass or timber-frame structures hold six to eight people, are unlocked year-round, and are stocked with basic emergency supplies. The most used examples sit on the approach to the Black Tusk and in the Helm Creek drainage below the Cinder Cone. They are not bookable and should not be relied on for planned overnight accommodation — their purpose is weather rescue — but in practice they frequently provide shelter for walkers caught by the notoriously rapid weather changes in the Coast Mountains.

## 3. Tyax Sky Cabin and Camp, Chilcotin Plateau

Tyax Sky Cabin and Camp operates in the Chilcotin Mountains roughly 240 kilometres north of Whistler, accessible by floatplane from Tyaughton Lake. The operation combines a remote cabin with tent platforms at higher elevation, targeting backcountry skiers in spring and horse-packing and hiking groups in summer. The terrain above the camp includes open alpine meadows and the Spruce Lake watershed, a vast plateau with excellent wildlife viewing. Guided trips are the norm; independent walkers can use the cabin with advance booking through the Tyax Mountain Resort.

## 4. Luc's Cabin, Whistler backcountry

Luc's Cabin in the Whistler backcountry above Rainbow Lake is a compact timber shelter at approximately 1,500 metres, named after a local backcountry skier. It sleeps eight in bunks and is stocked with basic emergency supplies. The approach from the Rainbow Lake trailhead takes three hours in summer. In winter it is a destination for ski tourers using the Whistler backcountry gate system; the terrain above the cabin accesses open bowls on the shoulder of Rainbow Mountain. The cabin is managed jointly by BC Parks and local mountain associations.

## 5. The Tea Hut, Pemberton area

The Tea Hut is a small privately operated warming hut in the alpine zone above Pemberton, catering primarily to ski tourers on day trips from the valley. At around 1,700 metres, it serves hot drinks and snacks through January to April on weekends and holidays. No overnight accommodation is available, but its inclusion here reflects the role of such warming huts in making BC's backcountry accessible to less-experienced visitors. The views south toward the Lillooet Ranges and north to the Pemberton Icefield are excellent on clear winter days.

## 6. Alpine 1, Rogers Pass, Selkirks

Rogers Pass in Glacier National Park is Canada's premier ski mountaineering and backcountry skiing zone, and the ACC's Alpine 1 hut at approximately 2,100 metres is a key staging point for glacier routes on the Selkirk Range peaks. The hut sleeps 12 in dormitory style and is a classic timber-frame construction with metal roof to shed the exceptional snowfall — Rogers Pass averages over 14 metres annually. Access requires navigation through National Parks Canada's Avalanche Safety Order (ASO) system, which restricts backcountry entry to protect the Trans-Canada Highway snow-control programme.

## 7. Alpine 2, Rogers Pass, Selkirks

Alpine 2, the companion hut to Alpine 1 at Rogers Pass, sits slightly higher at around 2,200 metres and accesses a different aspect of the glacier terrain above the pass. The two huts together give ski touring parties the flexibility to split across different objectives on a multi-day Rogers Pass trip. Both are managed by the ACC and require advance booking through the ACC online system. Demand is extremely high for prime season weekends — December to March — and weekend slots in February book out within minutes of opening.

## 8. Blueberry Cabin, Kootenay region

Blueberry Cabin in the Kootenay Mountains sits at around 1,400 metres and is part of a private hut-to-hut network that connects several shelters across the Valhalla Ranges west of Slocan Lake. The cabin sleeps 10 and is self-catered with a wood stove and basic kitchen supplies. Ski tourers use it as a hub for day routes on the open bowls above, while summer hikers access Valhalla Provincial Park trails. The Kootenays receive less tourist traffic than the Sea to Sky corridor, keeping these huts relatively quiet even in peak season.

## 9. Meadow Cabin, Yoho National Park area

Meadow Cabin is a Parks Canada backcountry cabin in the Yoho-Columbia watershed zone, one of several that the agency makes available for reservation through its backcountry permit system. At around 1,600 metres, it sits in subalpine meadow terrain characteristic of the western slope of the Rockies, with open views of limestone peaks. Permits are required for all backcountry travel in Yoho; the cabin has four to six bunks and a propane stove. September visits catch the alpine larch colour that makes the Lake O'Hara area and surrounding drainages so visually remarkable.

## 10. Noyse Lake Cabin, Northern BC

Noyse Lake Cabin is a remote fly-in or long-approach cabin in northern British Columbia's Cassiar Mountains, accessible by floatplane from Dease Lake. It sits at around 1,000 metres above the lake and caters primarily to hunters and fly-fishing clients in late summer and autumn. From a hiking and alpine exploration standpoint, it represents the northern tier of BC's hut network, in a region where infrastructure is sparse and self-sufficiency is mandatory. The Cassiar Mountains rise above 2,700 metres and see remarkably few mountaineers despite terrain comparable to more southerly ranges.
