Every year roughly 10,000 alpine rescue operations take place across the European Alps. Most end happily; many result in invoices that shock the unprepared. A single helicopter sortie in Austria, Switzerland, or Italy can cost between €3,000 and €15,000 depending on flying time, winching complexity, and the number of rescuers deployed. Understanding how rescue insurance works — and which policy actually pays — is as important as packing the right boots.

## How Alpine Rescue Is Organised

Mountain rescue in the Alps is not a single system. In Austria, the Bergrettung operates under regional federations and responds to over 5,000 incidents annually. In Switzerland, Rega (Swiss Air Rescue) runs a fleet of 17 helicopters from 12 bases and is funded almost entirely by membership donations. Italy divides responsibility between the Corpo Nazionale Soccorso Alpino e Speleologico (CNSAS) and regionally contracted helicopter services. Germany leans on ADAC's air ambulance fleet, the largest in Europe, alongside the Bergwacht of the German Red Cross.

The key point is that membership in one organisation — say, the Austrian Alpine Club (Österreichischer Alpenverein, ÖAV) — may include rescue coverage in Austria but not in Italy, where CNSAS transport costs can be billed to the rescued person. Knowing the geography of your coverage matters as much as the policy wording itself.

## ÖRAK: The Austrian Rescue Fund

ÖRAK (Österreichisches Kuratorium für Alpine Sicherheit) is less a direct insurer and more a coordinating body, but the term is widely used by Austrians to mean the Bergrettung coverage bundled into ÖAV or Naturfreunde membership. An ÖAV standard membership (around €65 per year in 2025) includes full helicopter rescue coverage within Austria and partial coverage in South Tyrol and some neighbouring regions, with costs reimbursed after the fact.

The critical detail: "costs reimbursed" means you may pay upfront if the billing is direct-to-patient, then claim back. In practice, Rega and Bergrettung in Austria almost never bill members directly, but smaller helicopter operators in border regions sometimes do. Keep rescue contact numbers and membership cards accessible, not buried in a waterproof pouch at the bottom of a pack.

## ADAC and Its Air Ambulance Reach

Germany's ADAC Schutzbrief, best known as car breakdown cover, bundles a European air-ambulance benefit that has rescued thousands of German hikers abroad. A premium membership tier (roughly €140 per year in 2025, covering a household) includes medically supervised repatriation from anywhere in Europe. Critically, ADAC's benefit is triggered by medical necessity as determined by a physician — a helicopter called because a hiker twisted an ankle and panicked may not be covered; one called because a physician on the ground confirms an orthopaedic injury requiring hospital transport will be.

ADAC operates over 50 yellow air-rescue stations across Germany and responds to Bavarian alpine incidents faster than any other provider. For hikers based in southern Bavaria venturing into the Zugspitze massif, the Allgäu, or the Berchtesgaden Alps, an ADAC membership is close to essential. For those crossing into Austria or Switzerland, it supplements but does not replace local club membership.

## TCS and the Swiss Approach

The Touring Club Suisse (TCS) offers rescue and repatriation coverage through its Assistance membership tiers, starting at around CHF 99 per year. Unlike ADAC, which is primarily a motoring organisation, TCS has long operated alongside Rega and explicitly markets to hikers and cyclists. Its benefits include search-and-rescue cost coverage, mountain rescue transport, and overseas medical evacuation. The upper-tier Assistance Plus product extends coverage globally, which matters for alpinists taking their hut experience to the Himalaya or Andes.

Rega itself runs a separate "Gönner" (patron) membership for CHF 40 per year per person or CHF 70 for a family. A Rega membership does not technically provide insurance — it is a donation model — but in practice, Rega does not bill Gönnerbeitrag members for rescue costs within Switzerland. The psychological and financial protection is real even if the legal structure is unusual.

## Specialist Policies: DAV Summit Club, UIAA, and Global Rescue

The German Alpine Club (DAV) provides a rescue and liability package with full membership (around €65 per year). For serious alpinists, the DAV Summit Club offers expedition-level add-ons covering technical climbs. UIAA insurance, available through affiliated clubs in over 90 countries, fills gaps for international travel.

Global Rescue, an American provider, has gained a following among trekkers heading to Nepal, Peru, and East Africa. It offers a pure evacuation service — field rescue, medical evacuation, and repatriation — at $329 per year for individuals. Unlike insurance, Global Rescue is a membership service that mobilises its own teams and coordinates with local operators. For Himalayan trekking peaks accessible from teahouses and high camps, it is the standard benchmark.

## What a Helicopter Call Actually Costs

A 20-minute search-and-rescue flight with winching in the Swiss Alps typically costs CHF 8,000 to CHF 12,000 when billed commercially. A night search, requiring multiple assets and ground teams, can run to CHF 30,000 or beyond. In Italy, CNSAS officially does not charge for rescue, but transport to hospital by helicopter operated by regional health authorities can generate bills of €3,000 to €7,000.

When no rescue insurance is in place, these costs are borne entirely by the rescued person. Several EU countries have debated mandatory rescue insurance for mountaineers — Bavaria briefly considered it — but as of mid-2026, no mandatory system exists across the Alps. Personal responsibility remains the rule.

## Practical Advice for Trip Planning

Before any hut trip, confirm three things: where your rescue coverage is geographically valid, whether it covers winch operations (some basic policies exclude helicopter winching and cover only vehicle transport), and whether you need to notify the insurer before a rescue or only after. Carry the emergency number for the relevant rescue service — in Austria 140, Switzerland 1414 (Rega) or 144 (medical), Italy 118, Germany 112 — and have them saved offline, since mobile coverage above 2,500 m is patchy.

A bivouac does not suspend your coverage, but self-rescue — turning around when conditions deteriorate — remains the lowest-cost and safest option in every case.
