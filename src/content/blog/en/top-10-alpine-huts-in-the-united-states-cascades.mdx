The Cascade Range of Washington and Oregon is dominated by a chain of
glaciated stratovolcanoes — Rainier (4392 m), Adams, Baker, Hood, Jefferson —
that rise abruptly from Pacific Northwest forest to glaciated summits. The
hut infrastructure in the Cascades is sparse compared to the Alps; the tradition
is one of wilderness camping and guided summit expeditions rather than a
network of staffed overnight facilities. But a handful of high-camp structures
on the major peaks, combined with the 10th Mountain-style warming huts in the
Washington backcountry, constitute a genuine if thin overnight system.

## 1. Camp Muir, 3258 m — Mount Rainier, Washington

Camp Muir on the south slope of Mount Rainier at 3258 m is the principal
high camp on the standard Disappointment Cleaver route to the summit. A stone
shelter (the Muir Hut, capacity approximately 25) maintained by the National
Park Service provides emergency refuge; commercial guide services (Rainier
Mountaineering Inc.) operate a hut complex alongside. Reached from Paradise
(1646 m) in 4–6 hours on the Muir Snowfield. The route from Camp Muir to
the summit (4392 m) takes 4–7 hours; glacier travel with crevasse hazard.
Reservations for the guide camp must be made months in advance.

## 2. Camp Schurman, 3030 m — Mount Rainier, Washington

The alternative high camp on the Emmons/Winthrop Glacier route (the northeastern
approach), Camp Schurman at 3030 m sits on a rock island above the Emmons
Glacier — the largest glacier in the contiguous United States by area. A
small National Park Service hut and ranger station serve the summer climbing
season. The Emmons route is longer but less crowded than the Disappointment
Cleaver. The crevasse hazard on the upper Emmons can be significant and changes
annually.

## 3. Kulshan Cabin (Mount Baker Glacier Hut), 1280 m — Mount Baker, Washington

On the south slopes of Mount Baker (3286 m), the Kulshan Cabin at approximately
1280 m serves as a base area for the Coleman-Deming route — the standard Mount
Baker ascent. The Mazamas (a Portland-based mountaineering club) and the Bellingham
Mountaineers maintain facilities in the area. The Coleman Glacier approach
above requires crampons and rope; crevasse hazard is significant.

## 4. Timberline Lodge, 1830 m — Mount Hood, Oregon

Timberline Lodge at 1830 m on the south slope of Mount Hood (3426 m) is the
most architecturally distinguished mountain lodge in the American Northwest —
a 1937 WPA-era stone and timber building on the National Register of Historic
Places. Although primarily a ski resort lodge, it serves as the staging point
for Mount Hood summit attempts via the South Side route. The summit route
crosses the Palmer Snowfield and the upper crater rim; crampon and ice axe
experience required.

## 5. Silcox Hut, 2160 m — Mount Hood, Oregon

Above Timberline Lodge and reached by the Palmer ski lifts, Silcox Hut is a
1939 stone building used as a warming hut and private event venue. Limited
overnight availability for group bookings; historically used as an overnight
for early-morning summit departures.

## 6. Camp Comfort, 2350 m — Mount Adams, Washington

A high camp site on the south slopes of Mount Adams (3743 m), Camp Comfort
at approximately 2350 m is the standard overnight for the South Spur route.
No permanent hut; tent camping on rocky ground above the treeline. The Adams
summit is a non-technical snow climb in good conditions but a serious
undertaking in winter or mixed conditions.

## 7. Glacier Basin Camp, 1880 m — Mount Rainier, Washington

In Glacier Basin on the northeast side of Rainier, the designated camping
area at 1880 m serves as the approach camp for the Emmons/Winthrop route and
for mountaineers acclimatising before the summit push. A popular intermediate
camp that builds altitude exposure before Camp Schurman.

## 8. Lake Cle Elum Warming Hut, 900 m — Okanogan-Wenatchee NF, Washington

A Forest Service warming hut and ski touring cabin in the Lake Cle Elum
area of the Okanogan-Wenatchee National Forest, used by ski tourers and
snowshoers in winter. Part of a thin but developing system of backcountry
huts in the Washington Cascades.

## 9. Hannegan Hut, 1200 m — Mount Shuksan area, Washington

In the North Cascades near Mount Shuksan and the Canadian border, a seasonal
shelter in the Hannegan area provides support for the approaches to Shuksan
(2783 m) and the Chilliwack River backcountry.

## 10. Crater Lake Rim Shelter, 2100 m — Crater Lake, Oregon

On the rim of Crater Lake National Park in southern Oregon, a historic rim
shelter and several NPS overnight facilities provide the base for ski touring
on the crater rim in winter and exploring the lake terrain in summer.

## Permits and registration for Cascade peaks

All summit attempts on Mount Rainier require a climbing permit, paid through
the National Park Service reservation system (recreation.gov). The permit
fee is approximately $57 per person (2025 pricing) and reserves a spot in
the daily quota. Camp Muir guide service reservations are separately handled
through Rainier Mountaineering Inc. (RMI) and must be made months in advance
for the July–August peak season.

Mount Baker does not currently require a climbing permit for the normal
Coleman-Deming route but does require a day-use fee for entry to the national
forest. Mount Hood's summit routes are permit-free but the trailhead parking
at Timberline Lodge is subject to the Timberline Lodge parking management
system.

## The Pacific Crest Trail connection

The Pacific Crest Trail (PCT) — 4,265 km from the US-Mexico border to the
US-Canada border — passes through the Cascades for much of its Washington and
Oregon sections, connecting several of the areas mentioned above. The PCT
follows the eastern flanks of most Cascade volcanoes, close enough for side
trips to the high camps but not ascending the glaciated upper peaks. Sections
of the PCT in Washington pass through some of the finest wilderness terrain
in the western United States: the Goat Rocks Wilderness below Rainier, the
Glacier Peak Wilderness in the North Cascades, and the volcanic highlands
of the Oregon Cascades.

## Weather in the Cascades

The Pacific Northwest Cascades receive an extraordinary amount of precipitation:
the western slopes of Rainier average over 600 cm of annual snowfall. The
maritime climate means winters are mild but extremely wet; the summer season
is short and the weather window for high-altitude climbing is concentrated
in July–August, with earlier and later attempts subject to storm risk from
Pacific systems.

The National Weather Service avalanche forecast for the Northwest (nwac.us)
is the reference service for winter and spring conditions. It provides zone-
specific avalanche forecasts updated daily during the winter season.

Guides for Rainier summit attempts are provided by Rainier Mountaineering
Inc. (RMI) and Alpine Ascents International — the two licensed guiding
concessions for Rainier summit routes. The guided 3-day summit climb costs
approximately USD 1,200–1,500 per person (2025 rates) and includes rope
teams, equipment use, and high camp guide services. The climbing permit
(approximately USD 57 per person) is separate and required regardless of
whether the ascent is guided.
