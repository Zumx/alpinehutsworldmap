The Georgian Caucasus is one of the great undiscovered mountain regions of the
world. The main range reaches 5047 m at Mount Kazbek and runs through some
of the most dramatically positioned villages in Europe — the stone towers of
Svaneti, the Mestia valley floor, the beehive-tower villages of Tusheti.
The trekking infrastructure is developing rapidly but remains far less developed
than the Alps; what exists is a combination of guesthouses in villages, basic
mountain shelters on high passes, and community-run hiking huts that have been
established with assistance from international hiking organisations and the
Georgian National Tourism Administration.

## 1. Mestia Guesthouses, 1500 m — Mestia, Svaneti

The town of Mestia (1500 m) in the Upper Svaneti valley is the hub for
Caucasus high-mountain travel. It is accessible by small aircraft from Tbilisi
(30 minutes) or by road (8–10 hours through mountain roads). The guesthouse
network in Mestia — primarily family-run, with shared meals and basic rooms —
functions as the primary overnight infrastructure for the Svaneti trekking
season. The Mestia area huts include the famous Ushba shelter at the base
of the Ushba massif (2-day approach from Mestia).

## 2. Ushba Basecamp Huts, 2300 m — Ushba massif, Svaneti

Ushba (4710 m) is the most technically challenging of the Georgian Caucasus
peaks — a dramatic double-summited massif with no easy routes. The basecamp
area at approximately 2300 m in the Dolra valley, approached from Mestia in
2 days, has basic stone shelters used by Georgian and international climbing
expeditions as the staging area. The upper approaches are serious mountaineering
terrain requiring full expedition equipment.

## 3. Becho Pass Shelter, 2368 m — Becho valley, Svaneti

On the classic Svaneti to Elbrus trekking route, the Becho Pass (2368 m)
shelter sits at the Georgian side of the crossing toward the Russian Caucasus
via Becho village. The hut is basic — a stone building with sleeping platforms
maintained by the local community — but provides crucial overnight shelter on
a multi-day route that crosses into the high Caucasus terrain. The Becho valley
itself is one of the most beautiful in Svaneti.

## 4. Tetnuldi Base Camp, 2740 m — Tetnuldi massif, Svaneti

Tetnuldi (4858 m) is one of the highest peaks in the Svaneti area and has
become the most popular technical objective in Georgian mountaineering tourism.
The base camp at approximately 2740 m, reached by 4WD vehicle to 2100 m and
then 2.5 hours on foot, has camping platforms and a basic shelter maintained
by local guides. The mountain has a standard route rated PD+ with some ice
sections.

## 5. Zhabeshi-Adishi Guesthouses, 1900 m — Adishi valley, Svaneti

On the Mestia-to-Ushguli trekking route (one of the most popular multi-day
walks in the Caucasus), the village of Adishi in the upper Adishi valley
(1900 m) provides guesthouses that serve the overnight needs of through-walkers.
The crossing of the Adishi glacier snout — now significantly melted with glacial
retreat — is the most technically demanding section of the route.

## 6. Ushguli Guesthouses, 2200 m — Ushguli, Svaneti

Ushguli (2200 m) is the highest permanently inhabited settlement in Europe —
four interconnected villages at the head of the Enguri river valley, guarded
by the towers of Svaneti's medieval architectural tradition. The cluster of
stone towers is a UNESCO World Heritage site. Guesthouses in Ushguli provide
the endpoint overnight for the classic Mestia-to-Ushguli trek (4 days) and
serve as the base for approaches to the Shkhara massif (5193 m — the highest
peak in Georgia).

## 7. Kazbegi Guesthouses, 1740 m — Stepantsminda (Kazbegi), Mtskheta-Mtianeti

Near Mount Kazbek (5047 m) at the northern end of the Georgian Military Highway,
the town of Stepantsminda (formerly Kazbegi) at 1740 m is the starting point
for the Kazbek ascent. The guesthouses here serve acclimatisation nights and
the approach to the Meteo Station (3675 m) and Betlem Cave Hut on the standard
Kazbek route. A 3-hour walk from Stepantsminda reaches the Gergeti Trinity
Church (2170 m) — one of Georgia's most celebrated buildings, perched on a
promontory above the Military Highway.

## 8. Betlem Cave Hut, 3650 m — Kazbek approach, Kazbegi

A basic shelter at approximately 3650 m on the standard Kazbek ascent route,
used as a high camp by summit parties. The ბეთლემის ქოხი (Betlem's Hut)
is one of the highest maintained shelters in the Caucasus and provides a
cold but functional overnight before the final push to the summit (4–5 hours
from here in good conditions).

## 9. Mestia Mountaineering Base, 1500 m — Mestia

The Georgian National Mountaineering Federation base in Mestia serves as a
coordination and registration point for climbers bound for the high Caucasus
peaks. Guide hiring, equipment rental, and mountain rescue coordination are
managed from here. Dormitory accommodation for independent climbers.

## 10. Tusheti Mountain Shelter Network, 1700–2200 m — Tusheti, Kakheti

In the Tusheti region — a dramatically isolated mountain area accessible only
via the Abano Pass road (one of the steepest roads in the Caucasus) from July
to October — a network of basic shelters supports the growing Tusheti trekking
circuit. The villages of Omalo (1800 m) and Shenako (2200 m) provide guesthouses
of increasing quality as infrastructure investment continues.

## The Svaneti region and its culture

The Mestia area sits in Svaneti — one of the most isolated and historically
significant regions of Georgia. The Svans have maintained a distinct language
(Svan, a South Caucasian language unrelated to Georgian), a unique tower
architecture (the defensive Svan towers, built from the 9th to 13th centuries,
still dominate every village skyline), and a mountain culture that has remained
largely intact because the terrain kept outsiders out for most of history.

Mestia was connected to the Georgian Military Highway by paved road only in
the 1960s; before that, the primary winter access was on foot or horse over
passes that closed from October to May. The isolation preserved the culture;
the recent development of trekking tourism has brought economic change at a
pace that poses challenges for cultural continuity.

The traditional Svan hospitality — guests are offered wine, bread, and salt
on arrival; the toasting tradition at meals follows a formal protocol with
the tamada (toast-master) directing — is found in the guesthouses of Mestia
and the villages along the Mestia-to-Ushguli route. Visitors who engage with
this culture rather than treating the area purely as a trekking access point
find a more rewarding experience.

## Access and practical logistics

Mestia is reached from Tbilisi by a combination of road and air. The drive
takes approximately 9 hours via the Enguri Highway and Zugdidi; the flight
from Tbilisi to Mestia airport takes 40 minutes. The Mestia airport serves
small aircraft (Dash 8 and similar) and is subject to weather cancellations
— the mountains create their own weather at the airport elevation.

Marshrutky (shared minibuses) run from Zugdidi to Mestia several times daily
during the summer season. The road through the Enguri Gorge is scenic and
occasionally precarious; the standard of road maintenance has improved
substantially since 2010 but sections remain rough.
