## Why Avalanche Training Is Specific to Hut Touring

Piste skiing and resort skiing carry low avalanche risk because the runs are patrolled and the terrain is managed. Hut-to-hut ski touring is fundamentally different — it moves through uncontrolled terrain, crosses exposed slopes, and proceeds when conditions allow rather than when patrols have confirmed safety. Every person in an independent touring party must understand avalanche risk assessment, carry the correct equipment, and practise the rescue skills that make the difference between a survival story and a fatality.

This is not optional background knowledge. The IKAR (International Commission for Alpine Rescue) statistics for the Alps indicate that the vast majority of avalanche fatalities in backcountry terrain involve victims who were buried by slides triggered by their own party. The buried person cannot self-rescue. Companion rescue — performed within 15 minutes of burial — is the primary life-saving intervention available. Equipment and training are the prerequisites.

## The Three-Course Framework

Avalanche education in Europe is broadly organised into three tiers, calibrated to experience level:

The first-tier course — typically a one-day or weekend intro — covers transceiver search techniques (single-burial and multiple-burial), probe use, and basic shovelling. It assumes no prior technical background and is appropriate for anyone beginning hut touring. The DAV, ÖAV, SAC, and CAF all run first-tier courses, typically from November through January before the main touring season.

The second tier moves into terrain assessment — the Munter avalanche risk method, slope angle measurement, interpretation of the European Avalanche Danger Scale (1 to 5), reading of the daily bulletin published by national avalanche services (LWD in Austria, SLF in Switzerland, MeteoFrance for France, AINEVA for Italy), and field identification of danger signs including recent avalanche activity, wind loading, and slab instability tests.

The third tier, appropriate for those leading parties or aspiring to guide qualifications, covers expedition-level snow science, search coordination for multiple-burial scenarios, and rescue leadership in complex terrain.

## The European Avalanche Danger Scale

The 1-to-5 scale is shared across all Alpine countries and published daily by national meteorological and avalanche institutions. Understanding it accurately is prerequisite to any go/no-go terrain decision:

Level 1 (Low) is rare and covers stable, settled snowpack. Level 2 (Moderate) covers most touring days after settled cold weather — natural avalanches are unlikely but human-triggered avalanches are possible on steep slopes. Level 3 (Considerable) is the most commonly cited danger level on fatal accident days — avalanches can be triggered by small additional loads on moderately steep terrain. Level 4 (High) means natural and human-triggered avalanches are likely; touring is generally inadvisable above the treeline. Level 5 (Very High) is rarely declared but represents fully unstable snowpack with natural avalanche activity across all terrain.

The statistics are unambiguous: the majority of backcountry fatalities occur at Level 3. Level 3 does not mean it is safe to tour if you stay on safe terrain — it means the safe terrain options are significantly reduced and the consequences of a poor terrain choice are severe.

## Transceiver Techniques: What Practice Actually Means

A transceiver (beacon) in receive mode searches for the electromagnetic signal emitted by a buried transceiver in send mode. Modern three-antenna digital transceivers — models from Mammut, Pieps, Ortovox, and BCA — guide the searcher with a combination of distance indication and directional arrows. The technology is reliable but the technique is not automatic.

Effective beacon search requires that you can reduce distance from 20 metres to 1 metre and initiate a pinpoint search in under three minutes — alone, in cold, with adrenaline active, and potentially with multiple signals from multiple buried people. This requires practice. Carrying a beacon you have never practiced with is only marginally better than carrying nothing — the equipment functions, but the operator does not.

Practice protocol recommended by the ÖAV Safety Institute: 20 minutes of single-burial search practice before every major tour. Bury a beacon in a snowfield near the first hut, time yourself to pinpoint location, and recover it. This takes 20 minutes and genuinely maintains the skill. Annual training day courses are necessary but not sufficient.

## Probe and Shovelling Technique

Probing is the step between beacon pinpoint and shovel excavation. A probe confirms burial depth and location — essential because snow depth may mean the victim is 1.5 metres below the surface and the beam you are shovelling is significant. Probe perpendicular to the slope from your pinpoint reading. Mark the strike point before removing the probe.

Shovelling technique is the least trained skill in companion rescue and the most physically demanding. Strategic shovelling — the conveyor belt method used by DAV rescue trainers, in which two or three shovellers work in rotation, with the front shoveller passing snow backward to relieve the need to lift — is dramatically faster than individual shovelling. Tests conducted by the Swiss Federal Institute for Snow and Avalanche Research (SLF) show that a three-person conveyor belt team excavates to burial depth roughly three times faster than a single shoveller. Practice this with your touring partners before the season.

## Terrain Assessment in the Field

No amount of transceiver practice substitutes for not triggering an avalanche. Terrain assessment is the highest-leverage avalanche safety skill.

Slope angle is the primary variable. Avalanche-prone slopes are typically 28 to 45 degrees — measurable with a clinometer app on a smartphone or a dedicated slope angle card. Slopes below 25 degrees rarely avalanche naturally; slopes above 55 degrees tend to slough continuously rather than loading to release slab avalanches. The dangerous band is the intermediate range, which includes most interesting ski touring terrain.

Exposure — whether the slope faces the wind and loads with transported snow, or faces the sun and develops a crust that may fail under subsequent snowfall — is the second assessment factor. North-facing slopes in the Alps accumulate wind-loaded snow that forms unstable slabs. South-facing slopes in late winter develop a strong crust that may fail dramatically when new snow loads on top of it.

## Courses and Resources

The DAV runs its Lawinenausbildung courses from November through February at training centres across Germany and Austria, including the DAV Summit Club in Munich. The ÖAV hosts multi-day avalanche courses at the Alpine Research Centre Obergurgl in the Ötztal. The SAC courses are available through regional sections. ANENA (France) and AINEVA (Italy) provide equivalent national training programmes.

The RECCO reflector system — a passive reflector sewn into many modern touring garments — does not replace transceiver-probe-shovel but adds a layer for helicopter-based organised rescue operations that can detect reflectors from altitude. It is a complement to companion rescue equipment, not a substitute.
