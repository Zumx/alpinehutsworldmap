The Cordillera Blanca in the Ancash region of Peru is the world's highest
tropical mountain range, with 33 peaks above 6000 m including Huascarán
(6768 m — the highest peak in Peru and the highest in the tropics). The range
runs 180 km through the Andes and is flanked to the east by the Huascarán
National Park — a UNESCO World Heritage site. The mountain hut infrastructure
is modest by Alpine standards but has improved substantially since the 1990s,
with the Refugio Llaca and Refugio Perú providing genuine services for trekkers
and climbers.

## 1. Casa de Guías Huaraz, 3090 m — Huaraz, Ancash

The Casa de Guías (House of Guides) in Huaraz is the organisational hub for
climbing and trekking in the Cordillera Blanca. Operated by the Peruvian
Mountain Guides Association, it provides guide booking, equipment rental,
information on current route conditions, and basic hostel-style accommodation
for climbers staging in the city. Huaraz (3090 m) is the regional capital
and the base for all Cordillera Blanca objectives; the city itself provides
the acclimatisation nights essential before ascending to 4000+ m terrain.

## 2. Refugio Llaca, 4450 m — Quebrada Llaca, Huaraz

The Refugio Llaca (also called Refugio Ishinca in some sources, though the
Ishinca valley is separate) sits at 4450 m in the Quebrada Llaca above Huaraz
— the main road accessible to approximately 4000 m. A stone and concrete
building providing basic dormitory accommodation and meals for acclimatisation
trekkers and parties approaching Ranrapalca (6162 m) and Ocshapalca (5888 m).
The Laguna Llaca at 4450 m, directly beside the refugio, is one of the finest
high-altitude lakes in the Blanca.

## 3. Refugio Perú (Ishinca Valley), 4350 m — Quebrada Ishinca

The Refugio Perú in the Quebrada Ishinca valley is the primary mountain hut
in the Blanca for intermediate-level climbers. At 4350 m, it provides
comfortable sleeping, good meals, and access to three accessible high-altitude
peaks: Ishinca (5530 m, non-technical), Urus (5495 m), and Ranrapalca (6162 m,
glacier approach, AD grade). The refugio accommodates approximately 30 people
and is operated by the Casa de Guías system. Booking in advance is recommended
for July–August.

## 4. Basecamp Chopicalqui, 4650 m — Quebrada Llanganuco

In the Llanganuco valley — the most accessible valley in the Huascarán National
Park, famous for the photogenic Llanganuco lagoons — the base camp area at
approximately 4650 m serves expeditions on Chopicalqui (6354 m) and
Huandoy Norte (6395 m). No permanent hut; tent camping on moraine with
mule support from the valley road.

## 5. Huascarán Base Camp, 4300 m — Quebrada Santa Cruz

The base camp for Huascarán (6768 m) at approximately 4300 m in the Quebrada
Santa Cruz system serves as the main staging area for ascents of both Huascarán
Norte (6664 m) and Sur (6768 m). The standard route (the "normal route") via
the Garganta at 6000 m is rated PD+ to AD but involves serious crevasse hazard
and cold temperatures. No permanent hut at base camp; expedition tents only.

## 6. Refugio Don Bosco, 4300 m — Pisco Valley, Yungay

The Refugio Don Bosco — operated by the Salesians and associated mountain
community organisations — serves the approach to Pisco (5752 m), one of the
most popular introductory glacier climbs in the Andes. Pisco's standard route
is rated PD with crampons required; it is an excellent first high-altitude
glacier objective. The refugio accommodates approximately 25 people and
provides meals; often used as the first overnight stop before the summit.

## 7. Santa Cruz Trek Camps, 3900–4700 m — Quebrada Santa Cruz

The Santa Cruz trek — arguably the finest 4-day trek in the Andes — passes
through the Quebrada Santa Cruz between Cashapampa and Vaqueria, crossing the
Punta Unión pass (4750 m). Camping is the standard overnight method; no huts
exist on the main route, but camp platforms are established at Llamacorral
(3900 m), Jatuncocha (4200 m), and below Punta Unión.

## 8. Alpamayo Base Camp, 4250 m — Quebrada Santa Cruz

The approach to Alpamayo (5947 m) — consistently voted the world's most
beautiful mountain in survey polls for its perfect triangular ice face —
requires a camp at approximately 4250 m and then advanced base camp at 4900 m
near the Alpamayo glacier. A trophy objective for serious mountaineers.

## 9. Artezón Pampa Camp, 4200 m — Llanganuco area

At the base of the Artesonraju (5999 m) — the mountain whose profile inspired
the Paramount Pictures logo — the camp at 4200 m serves mountaineering teams
on a relatively accessible 6000 m objective above the Llanganuco circuit.

## 10. Olleros Trek Shelter, 4200 m — Quebrada Honda

On the Olleros to Chavín trek — one of the pre-Inca routes across the
Cordillera Blanca connecting the Santa valley to the Mosna valley — basic
stone shelters at approximately 4200 m near the Yanashallash pass (4700 m)
provide emergency overnight capability.

## Huaraz as a base

The city of Huaraz (3052 m) in the Santa valley is the service base for
all Cordillera Blanca climbing and trekking. With a population of approximately
120,000, it has evolved from a small Andean city into a dedicated mountaineering
hub with gear rental, guide agencies, porters, mule hire, and a concentration
of climbers from across South America and beyond. The Plaza de Armas has
alpinism gear shops on multiple sides; the Café Andino and similar establishments
provide pre-trip information sharing.

The Casa de Guías (Guides' House) in Huaraz is operated by the AGMP
(Asociación de Guías de Montaña del Perú) and provides the professional
guide certification and pairing service for climbers who want qualified
local guides. For high technical routes — including the Artesonraju northwest
face, the Alpamayo southwest face (possibly the most beautiful route in the
Andes), and the ice routes on Chopicalqui — a certified UIAGM guide is
strongly recommended.

## The Cordillera Negra and valley contrast

The Cordillera Blanca's dramatic glaciated peaks rise on the east side of
the Rio Santa valley (the Callejón de Huaylas); directly opposite rises the
Cordillera Negra — a lower, drier, non-glaciated range that carries the
road north to Chiquián and the Cordillera Huayhuash. The two ranges define
the valley's extraordinary character: glacier-capped 6000 m peaks to the
east, brown volcanic ridges to the west, the Santa river threading between
at 3000 m.

The Cordillera Huayhuash, accessed from Chiquián south of Huaraz, offers the
most dramatic and remote circuit trekking in Peru — the Huayhuash Circuit
(160 km) passes beneath Yerupajá (6634 m), the second highest peak in Peru,
and crosses 10 passes above 4500 m. The hut infrastructure on the Huayhuash
is less developed than the Blanca; most parties camp but a few community-run
lodges provide basic facilities at key stage points.
