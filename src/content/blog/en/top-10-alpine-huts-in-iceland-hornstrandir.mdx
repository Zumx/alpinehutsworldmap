Hornstrandir is the place where Iceland simply stops. The northernmost peninsula of the Westfjords, it was abandoned by its last permanent residents in the 1950s and has since reverted to a strict nature reserve where no roads reach, no cars run, and the only way in is by boat from Ísafjörður. What remains is a coast of vertical bird cliffs, valleys deep in Arctic wildflowers, and one of the densest populations of Arctic fox in the world, here protected and almost tame. Shelter comes from a scattering of converted farmhouses, emergency huts and the cabins of returning summer families. These ten — drawn from Hornstrandir and Iceland's wider off-grid network — are the roofs that make Arctic walking here possible.

## A reserve without roads

Understand first what Hornstrandir is not: it has no marked, maintained trail network in the Alpine sense, no staffed mountain refuges, and no resupply. Routes follow old farm paths and the natural lines of the valleys, crossing rivers that must be forded and high passes — heiði — that hold snow into summer. The classic crossing runs between the bays of Veiðileysufjörður, Hesteyri and Hornvík, taking in the colossal Hornbjarg bird cliff. Boats run only in the short summer window, roughly late June to August, and being weathered in for a day or two is part of the deal. You carry everything; the huts are basic shelter, not hotels.

## 1. Hesteyri, the abandoned village

The natural gateway. Hesteyri was a thriving fishing and whaling settlement until it was abandoned in 1952; today its restored houses serve as summer accommodation and a small guesthouse in the old doctor's house, run by descendants of the families who left. It is the most comfortable base in the reserve and the start point for many crossings.

## 2. Hornvík and Hornbjarg shelters

Hornvík bay sits beneath Hornbjarg, the great bird cliff that rises over 500 metres straight from the sea and teems with guillemots, razorbills and puffins. Basic shelters and the warden's hut here serve walkers tackling the spectacular clifftop walk and the crossing to Látravík.

## 3. Látravík and the Hornbjargsviti lighthouse

The old lighthouse station at Hornbjargsviti, on the remote northeast coast, has been converted to a simple hostel-style hut. Reaching it means crossing high snowfields, and arriving feels like reaching the literal edge of the inhabited world, with nothing but the Greenland Sea beyond.

## 4. Aðalvík and Sæból

The wide bay of Aðalvík, with the abandoned hamlets of Sæból and Látrar, offers another cluster of summer houses and shelter used by walkers on the western crossings. The beaches here are vast and empty, the cliffs alive with birds.

## 5. Reykjarfjörður and its hot pool

On the eastern side of the reserve, Reykjarfjörður is famous for a geothermal swimming pool fed by a natural hot spring — an almost unbelievable luxury after days of cold river crossings. Simple cabins and camping serve walkers completing the longer east-side traverses.

## 6. Grunnavík

A small settlement on the way into the reserve, with restored houses used as summer accommodation. It serves as a quieter alternative gateway and a link on the routes that connect Hornstrandir to the inhabited Westfjords to the south.

## 7. Skálinn við Sveinstind

Beyond Hornstrandir, Iceland's highland huts define off-grid travel in the interior. The shelter near Sveinstindur, on the long Laugavegur and Fjallabak routes, sits among rhyolite peaks and gives a taste of the volcanic interior far from any road.

## 8. Básar á Goðalandi

In the Þórsmörk valley at the southern end of the famous Laugavegur trail, Básar is a popular hut among birch woods beneath the Eyjafjallajökull and Mýrdalsjökull ice caps. It is one of the busier huts in the highlands and the finish of one of the world's great multi-day walks.

## 9. Nýidalur, the central highlands

At around 800 metres in the heart of the Sprengisandur desert route, Nýidalur is one of the most isolated staffed huts in Iceland, a refuge in a vast volcanic emptiness between the Hofsjökull and Vatnajökull glaciers. Reaching it requires a serious highland vehicle or a long crossing.

## 10. Grímsfjall, on Vatnajökull

High on the Vatnajökull ice cap, the Grímsfjall huts sit beside the Grímsvötn volcano in one of the most extreme hut locations on earth — a glacier-research and mountaineering base reached only across the ice, for expeditions prepared for true polar conditions.

## The Icelandic hut system

Iceland's huts are run mainly by Ferðafélag Íslands (the Icelandic Touring Association) and regional clubs, plus private operators. Highland huts on popular routes like the Laugavegur are staffed and must be booked far in advance; emergency and reserve huts are spartan and unstaffed. There is no Alpine-style category system, but the principle is the same: book ahead where you can, carry a stove and food, and never rely on a hut being open or having space. In Hornstrandir specifically, most overnighting is in private summer houses, designated shelters or your own tent.

## Weather, rivers and the Arctic summer

The defining challenges here are weather, water and cold. Even in July, Hornstrandir can deliver sleet, fog that erases the path, and snow on the high passes. Rivers must be forded, sometimes thigh-deep and glacially cold, so carry crossing shoes and know how to read a safe line. Daylight is nearly endless in midsummer, which buys flexibility but no excuse for casual planning. Leave a route plan, respect the fragile tundra by camping only on durable ground, and remember that in a roadless reserve, self-rescue is the baseline expectation.
