Kyrgyzstan's Tien Shan range is one of the world's great mountain systems:
peaks exceeding 7000 m, glaciers of immense scale, and a tradition of
Soviet-era alpinism that produced some of the most technically accomplished
mountaineers of the 20th century. The infrastructure today is a mixture of
Soviet-built climbing camps maintained by Bishkek-based operators, community-
run yurt camps in the lower valleys, and basic shelters established by
mountaineering organisations. Kyrgyzstan requires no trekking permits for
most of its mountains but does require OVIR registration for border-zone areas.

## 1. Ala-Archa National Park Base Lodge, 2100 m — Ala-Archa, outside Bishkek

Ala-Archa National Park, 40 km south of Bishkek, provides one of the most
accessible high-altitude trekking areas in Central Asia. The park entrance
at 2100 m has a basic lodge operated by the Ak-Sai Travel company and the
national park authorities. Day walks extend to the Ratsek hut (3300 m) — the
former Soviet alpinists' camp — and above to the Aksai Glacier and the
5000 m summits above. A permit fee is charged at the park entrance.

## 2. Ratsek Hut (AK-SAI Hut), 3300 m — Ala-Archa

The Ratsek hut complex at 3300 m in the Ala-Archa gorge above Bishkek is
maintained by Ak-Sai Travel and serves as the primary overnight for technical
climbers and acclimatisation trekkers heading for the 4895 m Korona summit
or the Ak-Sai glacier routes. The building dates to the Soviet era and has
been upgraded with sleeping bag accommodation for approximately 30 people.
The view up the Aksai Glacier to the hanging seracs above is one of the finest
mountain panoramas accessible on a single day's walk from a capital city anywhere.

## 3. Khan Tengri Base Camp (North), 3600 m — Inylchek Glacier

Khan Tengri (7010 m) — the "Lord of the Spirits" — is the northernmost 7000 m
peak in the world and one of the most technically demanding. The northern
base camp at 3600 m on the North Inylchek Glacier is operated by Ak-Sai Travel
with a summer camp of sleeping tents, a cook tent, and basic logistics.
Access is by chartered helicopter from the Maidaadyr base (reached by road
from Karakol in 8 hours). The helicopter service is the standard approach;
the overland route via the South Inylchek Glacier from the south takes 5–7 days.

## 4. Pobeda Peak (Jengish Chokusu) Base Camp, 3900 m — Inylchek Glacier

Adjacent to the Khan Tengri camps, Pobeda Peak (7439 m — the second-highest
peak in the former Soviet Union after Communism Peak) base camp is managed
similarly by the Bishkek-based expedition operators. Pobeda is one of the most
serious 7000 m peaks in the world, with extreme weather and technical difficulty.
The camp at 3900 m on the South Inylchek Glacier serves both Pobeda and
Khan Tengri expeditions.

## 5. Lenin Peak Base Camp (Achik-Tash), 3600 m — Trans-Alay Range

Lenin Peak (7134 m) in the Trans-Alay range on the Kyrgyzstan-Tajikistan border
has one of the most established base camp infrastructures in the former Soviet
Union. The Achik-Tash base camp at 3600 m near the Maidaadyr valley is
accessible by road (4WD, 6 hours from Osh), which makes it the most accessible
7000 m base camp in the world. Tents, camp kitchens, and basic facilities are
operated by multiple Bishkek trekking companies during the July–August peak
season. The standard ascent route is rated PD — the most accessible 7000 m
summit for non-technical high-altitude walkers with good acclimatisation.

## 6. Camp 1 Lenin Peak, 4400 m — Trans-Alay

The first of three established high camps on the standard Lenin Peak route,
Camp 1 at 4400 m has platforms for expedition tents and is used by guided
parties over 3–4 days of the ascent. No permanent hut structure; tent camping
only.

## 7. At-Bashy Range Yurt Camps, 2800–3200 m — Naryn Oblast

In the At-Bashy range of central Kyrgyzstan, community-based tourism yurt
camps at 2800–3200 m serve the growing number of trekkers exploring the
central Tien Shan on horseback or on foot. These community-managed camps
provide excellent locally-sourced food and cultural exchange opportunities
that differentiate Kyrgyz mountain tourism from the more expedition-focused
Ala-Archa and Khan Tengri areas.

## 8. Issyk-Kul Trekking Camp, 2400 m — Terskey Ala-Too range

On the southern shores of Lake Issyk-Kul, the Terskey Ala-Too range rises to
nearly 5000 m within 20 km of the lake shore. Several trekking camps operated
by local companies provide overnight bases for multi-day routes into this
relatively untouched range.

## 9. Karakol Valley Huts, 2600 m — Kyrgyz Ala-Too, Karakol

Near the town of Karakol in the Issyk-Kul region, the Karakol valley provides
access to good hiking terrain in the Kyrgyz Ala-Too range. Huts and camp sites
at 2600–3200 m are maintained by local hiking clubs for valley approach treks.

## 10. Uzgen Base Camp, 3100 m — Pamir Alai foothills

In the Pamir Alai mountains in the Osh region of southern Kyrgyzstan, community-
run camp facilities at the foot of the main climbing zones serve the smaller
number of independent mountaineers exploring this less-visited range.

## The community-based tourism and yurt stay network

One of the distinctive features of Kyrgyz mountain tourism is the community-
based yurt stay network, which provides overnight accommodation throughout
the Tien Shan foothills and valley approaches. The CBT (Community Based Tourism)
network, established with Swiss Development Agency support in the early 2000s,
connects individual farms and yurt camps in mountain communities. A yurt stay
includes dinner and breakfast prepared from local ingredients — fresh bread
baked in a tandir oven, yogurt and kurut (dried cheese balls), mutton, and
the omnipresent kymyz (fermented mare's milk).

For multi-day treks in areas without formal hut infrastructure — the Terskey
Ala-Too south of Issyk-Kul, the Chatkal range in the west — the CBT network
provides accommodation that would otherwise require full wilderness camping.
The experience of staying with a Kyrgyz family at 2000–2500 m, with
horses grazing outside and a felt yurt as the sleeping room, is specific to
the Kyrgyz mountain culture and is one of the most memorable aspects of
trekking in this region.

## Permits and access requirements

Kyrgyzstan is one of the more open Central Asian countries for international
visitors; citizens of most Western countries can enter without a prior visa
(electronic visa available at evisa.e-gov.kg). Areas within 30 km of the
Chinese border require a border zone permit obtainable in Bishkek or Karakol.
The Inylchek Glacier area (base camp for Khan Tengri and Pobeda) requires
a border zone permit as it approaches the Chinese border; these are arranged
through licensed Kyrgyz mountaineering agencies.

The Khan Tengri climbing season is July–August. Permit fees for Khan Tengri
are approximately USD 200 per person (peak permit) plus the helicopter
transport to base camp (the glacier approach is impractical without helicopter).
Broad expedition infrastructure exists at Karkara base camp, the helicopter
staging point.
