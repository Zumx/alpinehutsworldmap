Ask experienced Haute Route walkers which single image stays with them longest,
and a remarkable number describe the same thing: arriving on the Otemma Glacier
at dusk, looking ahead across the frozen wilderness, and seeing the lights of
the Cabane des Vignettes CAS burning on a rock island in the middle of the ice.
No road, no cable car, no valley in sight. Just the hut, the glacier, and the
stars beginning to appear behind the Pigne d'Arolla.

The Cabane des Vignettes CAS has been earning that reaction for over a century.

## The position

The hut sits at 3160 m on a rock promontory in the middle of the glacier
system between Arolla and the Col de l'Evêque. It is surrounded on three sides
by glacier ice and approached from the south via the Otemma Glacier from the
Col de Chermontane or from the east via Arolla and the Pas de Chèvres.

The Pas de Chèvres approach — the standard one from Arolla — involves a
descent via a fixed-ladder section onto the Vuibé Glacier, a crossing of the
glacier with crampons and rope required, and then the final walk across ice
to the hut. This access has changed as the glacier has retreated: the ladder
section has been lengthened multiple times to account for the lowering glacier
surface, and route-finding on the glacier itself requires current information
from the hut keeper.

From the north, parties arriving from the Col de l'Evêque or the Grande
Dixence dam area cross the full Otemma Glacier — a longer but more sublime
approach through one of the largest glacier systems in the Pennine Alps.

## Dawn departure

The Cabane des Vignettes is famous among Haute Route regulars not just for the
approach but for the morning departure. The alarm sounds at 04:00. By 04:30,
breakfast is served in silence to a dining room of sleepy mountaineers in
down jackets and headlamps. By 05:00, the first parties are out on the glacier
in the dark, crampons rasping on frozen ice, the headlamp pools small against
the white expanse.

The reason for the early start is the same as at every Haute Route hut: the
Col de l'Evêque (3392 m) or the Pigne d'Arolla summit (3796 m) above should
be reached while the snow is still frozen and before afternoon convective
weather builds. The glacier crossing to the Cabane de Bertol CAS — the next
stage of the Haute Route — involves the Col de Bertol (3268 m) and is best
done on firm morning snow.

## Capacity and comfort

The Cabane des Vignettes CAS accommodates approximately 100 guests, managed by
the SAC Montreux section. The dormitory layout is standard Swiss SAC: side-by-side
mattresses with heavy wool blankets, sleeping liner required. The private room
allocation is small and fills months in advance on popular Haute Route dates.

Dinner is half-board and typically includes soup, a main course, and dessert.
The rifugio is famous — perhaps uniquely so among high-alpine huts — for the
quality of its food relative to the difficulty of supply. Everything arrives
by helicopter. The hut is solar-powered, with the panels visible on the south-
facing rock face below the main building.

SAC member rate applies, with standard reciprocal discounts for UIAA member
club members from other countries. Booking is through the sac-cas.ch system
and is mandatory for any visit from late June onwards.

## The Pigne d'Arolla

The hut serves as base for the Pigne d'Arolla (3796 m), one of the finest
non-technical high summits in the Swiss Alps. The standard route ascends via
the southwest face, involves a short glacier approach and then an easy ridge
to the summit, and takes 2–3 hours from the hut in good conditions. The summit
panorama across the Pennine Alps — Weisshorn, Monte Rosa, Matterhorn, Grand
Combin visible on a clear day — is among the best attainable from a single
overnight stop.

More experienced parties use the hut as a base for the Col de l'Evêque
crossing to the Italian side or the direct ski-touring approach via the
Otemma to the Col du Mont Brûlé (3213 m).

## The image

The photograph taken from the east, across the glacier in golden hour light
with the hut glowing against the rock and the Pigne d'Arolla catching the last
sun, is one of the canonical images of Swiss alpine hut travel. It is reproduced
on SAC material, in guidebooks, and across social media to the point of
familiarity. Arriving at the actual place, having crossed the actual glacier
on foot, remains something different from any photograph.

## Glacier change and current access

The Otemma Glacier and the Pigne d'Arolla glacier system have retreated
significantly since the mid-20th century. The approach from Arolla via the
Pas de Chèvres — fixed ladders onto the Vuibé Glacier — is updated regularly
by the SAC Montreux section as glacier surface conditions change. The ladder
section has been extended multiple times; the current access requires consulting
the hut's website or calling ahead to confirm the specific approach for the
current season.

In early season (June), the glacier may still hold a significant snowpack
that simplifies the approach; in late season (August–September), bare ice
on the lower glacier sections can make crampon technique more demanding. The
standard Arolla approach involves rope travel across the Vuibé and the lower
Pigne glacier; parties should not attempt it without crampons, ice axes, and
basic glacier travel competence.

The hut's Facebook and website are regularly updated with current approach
conditions, including photos. This is one of the few cases in the Alps where
social media genuinely serves a safety function: seeing a photo from the
previous week's approach party tells you what to expect on the ground.

## The broader Val d'Hérens

The Cabane des Vignettes sits within the Val d'Hérens — one of the most
beautiful and least-commercialised valleys in the Pennine Alps. The valley
village of Arolla (1998 m) is a small, traditional resort with a handful of
hotels and guesthouses, no cable car infrastructure, and a walking culture
that is conspicuously different from the Zermatt or Saas Fee experience.
Spending an extra night in Arolla before the Vignettes approach is a pleasure
in its own right — the approach walk through the larch and arolla pine forest
from Les Haudères, 7 km below, is one of the finest valley walks in the Valais.

The SAC Montreux section that manages the Cabane des Vignettes updates the
approach conditions regularly throughout the season. Capacity is approximately
100 guests; SAC members receive the member rate (approximately half the
non-member rate). The hut is open from approximately late June to mid-September.
Reservations through the sac-cas.ch booking system are essential for July
and August; the Vignettes typically books out within days of the reservation
window opening in spring. Early-season (June) dates are more accessible and
carry the additional reward of deep snow on the glacier approaches.
