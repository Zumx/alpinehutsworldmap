The Mont Blanc massif rises above Chamonix as the defining landscape of alpinism, the place where the sport was born and where, at 4808 metres, the highest summit in the Alps still draws climbers from every continent. The French side of the massif is a vertical world of granite spires, hanging glaciers and séracs, threaded by a dense network of refuges run mostly by the French Alpine Club. These are not gentle walkers' huts; many cling to rock ledges reached by ladders and cables, and they exist to launch climbers onto some of the most serious routes in Europe.

## The Chamonix valley as a climbing base

Chamonix is the alpine capital, and its refuges are reached by a combination of the famous lifts and long approaches. The Aiguille du Midi cable car, rising to 3842 metres, the Montenvers railway to the Mer de Glace, and the Téléphérique des Grands Montets place climbers high in the massif within minutes. From these stations the hut paths cross glaciers and granite to refuges that serve Mont Blanc itself, the Aiguille Verte, the Grandes Jorasses and the spires of the Aiguilles de Chamonix. The valley below offers everything from guides to the equipment the routes demand.

## Refuge du Gouter

The Refuge du Goûter at 3835 metres is the highest staffed refuge in France and the key stage on the normal route up Mont Blanc. Rebuilt in 2013 as a striking ovoid of stainless steel and timber clinging to the Aiguille du Goûter, it is an engineering marvel designed to be self-sufficient at extreme altitude. Reaching it means crossing the notorious Grand Couloir, a stone-fall gully that demands speed and an early start, and the hut is reservation-only, with places fiercely contested in the short summer season.

## Refuge des Cosmiques

The Refuge des Cosmiques at 3613 metres sits on the Col du Midi, a short glacier walk from the top station of the Aiguille du Midi cable car. Its accessibility makes it one of the busiest high refuges in the Alps, a base for Mont Blanc by the Trois Monts route, for the Cosmiques Arête, and for countless training climbs. The terrace, looking across the glacier to the séracs of Mont Blanc du Tacul, is one of the most spectacular in the range.

## Refuge du Plan de l'Aiguille

The Refuge du Plan de l'Aiguille at 2207 metres sits at the mid-station of the Aiguille du Midi cable car, beneath the granite needles of the Aiguilles de Chamonix. It is a relaxed, popular refuge serving climbers heading for the spires and walkers on the Grand Balcon Nord, the high traverse that links it to the Montenvers and the Mer de Glace. Its lower altitude and easy access make it a friendly introduction to the massif's hut culture.

## Refuge de la Charpoua

The Refuge de la Charpoua at 2841 metres is a tiny, classic refuge perched on a rock island above the Mer de Glace, beneath the Drus and the Aiguille Verte. Reached by a steep approach with ladders from the Montenvers, it is one of the most atmospheric small huts in the massif, serving the historic routes on the Drus and the Verte. Its limited capacity and dramatic setting make it a coveted overnight for serious alpinists.

## Refuge du Couvercle

The Refuge du Couvercle at 2687 metres sits beneath a huge balanced boulder above the Talèfre glacier, deep in the heart of the massif. Reached by ladders and cables from the Mer de Glace, it serves a wealth of classic routes including the Aiguille du Moine and the Aiguille Verte by the Whymper couloir. The old refuge beside the new one, tucked under its protecting rock, is a beloved relic of an earlier alpine age.

## Refuge de Leschaux

The Refuge de Leschaux at 2431 metres lies on a rock shelf above the Leschaux glacier, facing the immense north face of the Grandes Jorasses, one of the great north faces of the Alps and a stage for many of the most famous climbs in mountaineering history. Small and remote, it is the base for those attempting the Jorasses and the Walker Spur, and its position opposite that wall makes it a place of quiet awe.

## Refuge Albert 1er

The Refuge Albert 1er at 2702 metres overlooks the Tour glacier at the northern end of the massif, above Le Tour and the Col de Balme. It serves the Aiguille du Tour and the Aiguille du Chardonnet and is a popular introduction to glacier mountaineering, with gentler peaks than the central massif. It is also a key staging post on the high-level glacier traverses that cross into Switzerland toward the Trient plateau.

## Refuge de Tre-la-Tete and the southern approaches

At the southern end of the French massif, above Les Contamines-Montjoie, the Refuge de Tré-la-Tête at 1970 metres serves the quieter peaks of the Dômes de Miage and the Aiguille de la Bérangère. This corner of the massif sees fewer crowds than Chamonix and offers magnificent glacier scenery on the approach to the Conscrits hut and the high traverses, a worthwhile alternative for those seeking the massif without the Chamonix throng.

## The birthplace of alpinism

To stay in the Mont Blanc huts is to sleep in the cradle of the sport itself. The first ascent of Mont Blanc in 1786 by Jacques Balmat and Michel-Gabriel Paccard launched the entire culture of mountaineering, and Chamonix has been its capital ever since. The valley swarms each summer with climbers from across the world, and its Compagnie des Guides, founded in 1821, is the oldest in the world and still supplies guides for the classic routes. The refuges carry that history in their names and traditions: the Refuge Albert 1er honours the mountaineering king of the Belgians, and the Vallot shelter high on the Goûter route, a bivouac at 4362 metres, stands as a stark emergency refuge near the summit. The massif has also been a stage for tragedy and rescue on a grand scale, with the high-altitude rescue squad of the PGHM operating helicopters from the valley to pluck climbers from the faces. For all the modernity of the lifts and the rebuilt huts, the essential challenge remains what it was two centuries ago: a long, cold, glaciated climb to the roof of western Europe, undertaken in the dark and finished, if all goes well, in the dawn light on the summit ridge.

## Practical notes for the Mont Blanc huts

The Mont Blanc huts staff mainly from June to mid-September, with the Cosmiques and Goûter dependent on conditions and reservation essential, often months ahead. Nearly every route here is glaciated and serious; rope, crampons, ice axe and the competence to use them are mandatory, and many climbers engage one of Chamonix's many guides. The Grand Couloir crossing to the Goûter is the single most dangerous passage in the range and demands an alpine start. Weather changes are violent and fast; the lifts can strand you above the valley if storms close in.
