The hut is the place where altitude catches up with people. You climb steadily through the day, feeling tired but fine, eat dinner, and then in the long dark hours of a night spent at 3,000 metres the headache arrives, the nausea builds, and sleep refuses to come. Most of the time it is mild and passes. Sometimes it is the first sign of something that can kill within hours. Knowing the difference, in yourself and in the person on the bunk beside you, is one of the most useful skills a mountain traveller can carry.

## Why huts are where it shows

Altitude illness is driven by the fall in oxygen as you ascend, and its symptoms tend to surface several hours after you reach a new height — often overnight, while you sleep, when your breathing slows and oxygen levels dip further. A hut at 2,500 to 3,500 metres is precisely the altitude and precisely the timeframe where acute mountain sickness, or AMS, most commonly appears. You may have felt fine on the climb and wake at two in the morning with a pounding head. This is why the warden, your companions and you yourself should treat the hut night as the moment to stay alert, not to relax your guard.

The cardinal rule, drilled into every mountaineering course, frames everything that follows: if you have symptoms at altitude, assume it is altitude illness until proven otherwise, and never ascend higher with symptoms. That single principle prevents the overwhelming majority of altitude deaths.

## Acute mountain sickness: the common form

AMS is the mild, common end of the spectrum, and most people who sleep high enough will meet it eventually. The hallmark is a headache, usually appearing six to twelve hours after arriving at a new altitude, combined with at least one of: nausea or loss of appetite, fatigue out of proportion to the effort, dizziness, and disturbed sleep. It feels, people often say, like a bad hangover. On its own, mild AMS is not dangerous. The correct response is to stop ascending, rest, hydrate, and treat the headache with simple painkillers. If symptoms settle, you may continue the next day; if they worsen, you descend.

What makes AMS important is not the discomfort but the warning. It signals that your body has not yet adapted, and it is the soil from which the two lethal forms can grow. Taking mild AMS seriously, rather than pushing through it to keep to a schedule, is the whole game.

## HACE: when the brain swells

High-altitude cerebral oedema, HACE, is AMS that has progressed to swelling of the brain, and it is a true emergency. The defining sign is a change in how the person thinks and moves: confusion, unusual behaviour, drowsiness, and above all ataxia — a loss of coordination best tested by asking the person to walk heel-to-toe along a straight line. Someone who cannot walk that line in a straight, controlled way, or who is becoming irrationally sleepy or behaving oddly, may have HACE. Severe, unrelenting headache and vomiting often accompany it.

HACE can progress from first confusion to coma within hours. There is no waiting it out and no climbing through it. The treatment is immediate descent, by any means and without delay, ideally with supplementary oxygen and the medication dexamethasone if a trained person carries it. At the hut, the warden can summon mountain rescue, and a helicopter evacuation may be the fastest route down. The watchword is that descent saves lives where nothing else reliably does.

## HAPE: when the lungs fill

High-altitude pulmonary oedema, HAPE, is the other killer, and it can strike even someone with little or no headache. Here fluid accumulates in the lungs. The early signs are a dry cough, breathlessness far beyond what the exertion warrants, and a marked drop in exercise tolerance — the person who could keep up yesterday now stops every few steps gasping. As it advances, breathlessness appears even at rest, the cough may produce frothy or pink-tinged sputum, the lips and fingertips turn blue, and you may hear crackling or gurgling in the chest. HAPE often worsens at night and is a leading cause of altitude death.

As with HACE, the treatment is descent without delay, with oxygen and rest, and the drug nifedipine where available. Crucially, HAPE and HACE can occur together, and either can follow apparently mild AMS. Anyone who is breathless at rest, confused, or unable to walk a straight line at altitude needs to go down, now.

## How to watch over a bunkmate

The hut setting gives you a quiet advantage: you sleep beside the people you are travelling with, and you can watch them. Notice the companion who skips dinner with a headache and goes quiet, the one whose breathing sounds laboured in the bunk, the one who seems oddly confused on the way to the toilet. Ask directly how someone feels; altitude illness erodes judgement, and a sufferer often plays down symptoms or does not realise how impaired they are. A simple heel-to-toe walk and a few questions can reveal HACE before the person themselves understands what is happening. If you carry a pulse oximeter, a sharply low reading that does not recover with a few deep breaths is a useful red flag, though no gadget replaces watching the person.

## Prevention and the golden rules

Most of this is avoidable. Ascend gradually once above 2,500 metres, aiming to raise your sleeping altitude by no more than 300 to 500 metres a day, and build in rest days. Climb high and sleep low where the terrain allows. Hydrate well, avoid alcohol and sedatives the first nights at a new altitude, and never ignore symptoms to keep to a plan. The drug acetazolamide can aid acclimatisation and is worth discussing with a doctor before a high trip. Above all, remember the three rules that underpin every altitude course: if you feel unwell at altitude, it is altitude illness until proven otherwise; never ascend with symptoms of AMS; and if you are getting worse, or showing signs of HACE or HAPE, descend immediately. The mountain will still be there next year. Make sure you are too.
