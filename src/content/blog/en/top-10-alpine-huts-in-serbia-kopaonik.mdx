Serbia's mountains rise in three distinct zones: the central granitic block of Kopaonik, the eastern limestone ranges of Stara Planina and Rtanj, and the Dinaric ranges in the southwest near the borders with Montenegro and North Macedonia. While none of Serbia's summits exceeds 2,000 m — Midzur on Stara Planina at 1,935 m is the highest point — the mountain landscape is extensive, well-watered, and historically important to Serbian hiking culture, which developed strongly from the late nineteenth century onward. The планинарска кућа (planinarska kuća, mountain house) and the хижа (hizha) form the shelter network, ranging from simple stone shelters to multi-room hostels with full restaurant service. The ten huts below cover the most important mountain areas across the country.

## 1. Х. Руй, Western Serbia

Hut Ruy (х. Руј) sits on the Ruy massif in western Serbia near the border with North Macedonia, at around 1,600 m. The Ruy range is a long forested ridge popular for weekend walking from Vranje and Leskovac. The hut offers basic accommodation and is a base for the traverse of the Ruy ridge southward toward the Vlasina plateau. The forests here contain some of the oldest beech stands in southern Serbia and are important habitat for large raptors.

## 2. Х. Миджур, Stara Planina

Situated on the slopes approaching Midzur (1,935 m), the highest summit in Serbia, хижа Миджур is the principal base for ascents of this peak in the far eastern Stara Planina. The mountain forms the border with Bulgaria, and the view from the summit extends across both countries. The hut has capacity for around 40 people and operates mainly in the summer months from June to September. The trails around Midzur cross high meadows thick with wildflowers in July.

## 3. Поглед, Kopaonik

Поглед — meaning "View" — is a mountain hut near the Kopaonik plateau, one of the most visited mountain areas in Serbia. Kopaonik National Park occupies the highest section of the range, where ski infrastructure from the 1970s and onward coexists with protected forest and meadow habitats. The hut sits on a ridge spur offering a broad panorama across the Ibar valley. It operates year-round due to Kopaonik's dual summer and winter tourism season.

## 4. Копаоник, Central Kopaonik

The Kopaonik hut and lodge complex at around 1,700 m is the central accommodation node on the mountain, positioned within the national park near the ski resort infrastructure. Kopaonik's highest point, Pančićev vrh (2,017 m), is accessible in about two hours. The surrounding plateau landscape, called Suvo Rudište, is open and grassy in summer and ski-serviced in winter. The hut complex has been upgraded repeatedly since the 1980s and today offers modern amenity levels alongside traditional mountain shelter.

## 5. Ртањ, Eastern Serbia

Rtanj (1,565 m) is one of the most distinctive mountains in Serbia — a perfectly pyramidal limestone peak visible for great distances across the Morava valley near Zaječar and Bor. The mountain has an almost cult status in Serbian popular culture, and the trail up the western ridge to the summit cross is one of the most walked paths in the country. The Rtanj hut at around 900 m on the lower slope provides accommodation and serves as the standard trailhead. The summit ascent takes two to three hours from the hut.

## 6. Авала, Central Serbia

Hut Avala serves the mountain of the same name south of Belgrade — at just 511 m, Avala is more hill than mountain, but its proximity to the capital (about 20 km) gives it disproportionate cultural importance. The mountain is forested with oak and beech, crossed by well-marked paths, and topped by a communications tower. The hut near the summit is a popular weekend destination for Belgrade residents and operates year-round. It is the most accessible entry point into Serbian mountain culture for visitors based in the capital.

## 7. Планинарска Кућа Орлово Гнездо, Eastern Serbia

"Eagle's Nest" (Орлово Гнездо) is a mountain house in the Stara Planina region, positioned at mid-elevation on the eastern ranges near Knjaževac. The hut is used by hikers crossing the Stara Planina massif and by nature walkers exploring the river gorges and limestone karst that characterise this part of eastern Serbia. Capacity is modest at around 30 people. The area is notable for its concentration of endemic Balkan flora.

## 8. Lovačka Kuća, Western Ranges

Lovačka kuća (hunting house) shelters appear throughout the Serbian mountain network, originally built by state hunting associations in the mid-twentieth century and later opened to mountaineers. This particular lovačka kuća in the western ranges near the Zlatibor-Tara region offers basic dormitory accommodation and is used as an overnight stop on multi-day forest traverses. The surrounding Tara National Park contains some of the last old-growth Serbian spruce (Picea omorika), an endemic species.

## 9. Planinarski Dom Ski Staza, Kopaonik

The ski trail mountain house (Planinarski dom ski staza) on Kopaonik is positioned near the main piste network and serves both winter skiers and summer hikers following the marked trails across the plateau. The dom has a ground-floor restaurant open to day visitors and dormitory accommodation above for overnight guests. Its central position makes it a useful hub for parties exploring the full Kopaonik plateau across several days.

## 10. Sejnovë, Southwest Serbia

Sejnovë is a mountain shelter near the Golija biosphere reserve in southwestern Serbia, one of the country's largest protected areas and a UNESCO Man and Biosphere site. The Golija massif reaches 1,833 m at Jankov kamen and is covered by some of the densest and least disturbed subalpine forests in Serbia. The hut provides basic accommodation and is the standard base for traverses of the Golija ridge. The reserve is accessible from Novi Pazar and Ivanjica.
