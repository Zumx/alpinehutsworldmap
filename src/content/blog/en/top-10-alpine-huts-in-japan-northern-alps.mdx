Japan's Northern Alps — Kita Alps or Hida Mountains — extend 100 kilometres through Nagano and Toyama prefectures, reaching 3,190 metres at Okuhotakadake and forming the dramatic skyline visible from Matsumoto city. The mountain hut tradition here (yamagoya, literally "mountain huts") is extraordinarily well-developed: staffed huts sit on virtually every major ridge and summit, operate from late July to mid-October, and serve full hot meals including evening dinner (yushoku) and morning breakfast (asagohan). Beds are typically futon mattresses on platforms in communal sleeping halls (zashiki) rather than individual bunks. In August the huts fill to capacity — some sleeping 200 or more people — and walkers are expected to book weeks or months ahead. The Tateyama-Kurobe Alpine Route, the Yari-Hotaka ridge connecting the two main summits, and the Kamikochi basin approaches are the three primary circuits of the Kita Alps. The following ten huts cover key points across these circuits.

## 涸沢ヒュッテ (Karasawa Hyutte), Karasawa cirque

Karasawa Hyutte sits at 2,309 metres in the Karasawa cirque, one of the most famous mountain landscapes in Japan — an amphitheatre of granite that glows orange and red in the autumn foliage of early October, when the rowan and dwarf maple at the cirque rim change colour against permanent snowfields. The hyutte is a large privately operated facility sleeping over 300 guests in peak season, with a restaurant, beer garden terrace, and full meal service. The approach from Kamikochi via the Yokoo valley takes around four hours. It serves as the base for ascents of Okuhotakadake (3,190 m) and Kita-Hotakadake.

## 涸沢小屋 (Karasawa Koya), Karasawa cirque

Karasawa Koya stands directly beside Karasawa Hyutte in the same cirque at 2,350 metres and is the companion hut — slightly smaller, typically less crowded, and operated under different management. The two huts together give walkers a choice of atmosphere and style while sharing the same extraordinary setting. Karasawa Koya's terrace faces directly toward the Hotaka ridge and is considered marginally better for dawn photography of the cirque headwall. It sleeps around 150 and operates from late July to late October.

## 南御室小屋 (Minami-Omuro-Goya), Shiomi-dake approach

Minami-Omuro-Goya at 2,680 metres is positioned on the northern ridge approach to Shiomidake (3,047 m) in the southern sector of the Kita Alps. The hut sleeps 60 and is a quieter alternative to the crowded Hotaka-Yari corridor. The ridge above the hut forms part of the watershed between the Pacific drainage to the east and the Japan Sea drainage to the west — a geographical divide that shapes weather patterns, with summer Pacific monsoon moisture hitting the eastern faces hard while the western sides receive heavier winter snowfall. The hut operates from late July to mid-September.

## 薬師岳小屋 (Yakushidake Koya), Yakushidake summit

Yakushidake Koya sits at 2,701 metres near the summit of Yakushidake (2,926 m) in the Tateyama area of the northern Kita Alps. The mountain is renowned for its broad summit plateau — unusual in a range dominated by steep granite — and the gentle contours attract walkers seeking a less technical route through high-altitude terrain. The hut sleeps 80, operates from late July to mid-October, and provides views north toward the Tateyama Massif. Access from the Oritate trailhead in Toyama prefecture requires two days for most walkers.

## 仙丈小屋 (Senjodake Koya), Senjodake summit area

Senjodake Koya at 2,980 metres is positioned high on the flanks of Senjodake (3,033 m), known as the "Queen of the South Alps" for its graceful glaciated profile. The hut is small — around 40 beds — and sits in a dramatic cirque just below the summit ridge. Access from the Kitazawa Pass on the Lin'no-Michi forest road takes three to four hours. The summit is considered one of the most beautiful in all of Japan's alpine ranges, with a broad plateau ringed by glacial headwalls. The hut is a critical base for summit climbs in the Kita Alps border zone.

## 頂上富士館 (Choujou Fujikan), Mount Fuji summit

While not in the Kita Alps proper, Choujou Fujikan operates at 3,720 metres on the summit rim of Mount Fuji (3,776 m) and serves climbers on the Fujinomiya and Yoshida trails. The summit huts are staffed from early July to early September during the official climbing season. Accommodation is extremely basic — futon mattresses in cramped conditions — but staying here allows walkers to experience the Goraiko (sunrise) from the summit crater rim without the predawn crowds that pack the trails from July onwards. The hut serves instant noodles and hot tea through the night.

## 胸突山荘 (Munatsuki Sanso), Yarigatake approach

Munatsuki Sanso at approximately 2,900 metres sits on the steep final approach section — the "chest-thrust" slope that gives the hut its name — below the summit pyramid of Yarigatake (3,180 m). The summit is nicknamed "The Spear of the Alps" for its sharply pointed granite peak. The hut sleeps 40 and operates as a staging point for walkers making the final chain-assisted scramble to the summit. The views from the approach ridge encompass the full extent of the Kita Alps to the south, toward Kamikochi and Norikura.

## 万年雪山荘 (Mannenyuki Sanso), Tsurugi approach

Mannenyuki Sanso at around 2,400 metres is positioned on the approach to Tsurugidake (2,999 m), the technical peak in the northern Kita Alps considered the hardest of Japan's 100 Famous Mountains (Hyakumeizan). The hut name references the permanent snow (mannenyuki — "10,000-year snow") that fills the north-facing gullies near the hut year-round. Tsurugidake requires fixed-chain scrambling on its final approach and has a genuine accident record; the hut maintains a register for solo parties and provides weather assessment services.

## 御来光山荘 (Goraikou Sanso), Tateyama plateau

Goraikou Sanso at 2,750 metres on the Tateyama plateau provides overnight accommodation for walkers on the Tateyama-Kurobe Alpine Route, one of Japan's most famous mountain traverses crossing from Toyama to Nagano via funicular, ropeway, and trail. The hut name means "sunrise mountain cottage" and the position on the plateau's eastern edge offers exceptional views of the dawn light striking the Tateyama Massif. It sleeps around 60 and operates from late April — when the famous 20-metre Snow Wall on the Tateyama road is at its most dramatic — through October.

## 赤岩八合館 (Akaiwa Hachigou-kan), Tsurugi eighth stage

Akaiwa Hachigou-kan at approximately 2,800 metres is the eighth-stage hut on the standard Tsurugidake ascent route. In Japan's mountain-hut numbering system, gou refers to altitude stages counted from the base; eighth-stage huts typically sit at around 80% of the elevation gain to the summit. The hut sleeps 50 and serves as the final overnight before the technical summit scramble. Because Tsurugidake is a pilgrimage peak as well as a mountaineering objective, the hut sees a wide range of guests — from experienced alpinists to first-time mountain walkers in white pilgrimage garments.
