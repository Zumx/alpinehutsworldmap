Georgia's section of the Greater Caucasus spans the full northern border of the country, from the Likhi Ridge in the west to the Alazani river basin in the east. The highest peaks lie in the central and western sections: Shkhara (5,193 m) on the Svaneti-Kabardino-Balkaria border, Kazbegi (Mkinvartsveri, 5,047 m) dominating the Daryal gorge on the Military Highway, and the complex of peaks around Ushba (4,710 m) above the upper Svaneti valley. Georgian mountain culture has deep historical roots — the highland communities of Svaneti, Tusheti, and Khevsureti maintained tower-village settlements at altitudes above 2,000 m for centuries, and their stone defensive towers remain standing in many valleys. The modern trekking infrastructure is more recent: Soviet-era alplageri at Ushguli and Mazeri established the first organised accommodation, and since the 2000s a growing network of guesthouses, bivouac huts, and purpose-built mountain shelters has opened across the main trekking regions. The ten shelters below represent key points on the most important Georgian mountain routes.

## 1. ბეთლემის ქოხი 3650, Kazbegi

ბეთლემის ქოხი 3650 ("Betlemi Hut at 3650") is a cave shelter and adjacent built refuge below the Betlemi Cave at 3,650 m on the south face of Kazbegi (Mkinvartsveri, 5,047 m). The Betlemi Cave — one of the oldest continuously used religious sites in the Caucasus — contains medieval Georgian liturgical objects and icons placed there by Khevsur priests centuries ago for safekeeping. The hut at this elevation serves as the high camp for Kazbegi summit ascents and is typically reached from the Stepantsminda (Kazbegi town) area in two to three days including acclimatisation.

## 2. Метеостанция Клухорский Перевал, Svaneti Border

The weather station at Klukhorsky Pass (2,782 m) is a Soviet-era installation on the pass connecting the Georgian side of the range to the Dombai valley in Russia (currently not legally crossable due to border status). The station has been used as an emergency shelter by Georgian mountaineers and offers a dramatic perch above the Klukhor glacier. The pass was an important military route in the Second World War and the landscape around it contains the remnants of wartime defensive positions.

## 3. Arkhotis Tavi, Tusheti

Arkhotis Tavi is a shelter and guesthouse point in the Tusheti region of northeastern Georgia, a highland area accessible by one of the most dangerous mountain roads in the Caucasus — the Abano Pass road reaches 2,850 m and is unpaved and extremely narrow. Tusheti's stone tower villages, particularly Omalo, Dartlo, and Chesho, are among the best-preserved examples of medieval Caucasian highland architecture. Arkhotis Tavi serves hikers on the Tusheti circuit and Alazani valley descent routes.

## 4. Mountside, Kazbegi Area

Mountside is a modern mountain lodge and activity centre in the Kazbegi area, positioned in one of the lateral valleys south of Stepantsminda at mid-elevation. The operation focuses on multi-day trekking programmes in the Kazbegi region and offers well-appointed accommodation alongside guide services and logistic support for independent trekkers. The Kazbegi area is the most visited mountain trekking destination in Georgia due to the iconic Gergeti Trinity Church (2,170 m) and the relative accessibility via the Military Highway from Tbilisi.

## 5. ალტიჰატი, Upper Svaneti

ალტიჰატი (Altihati) is a mountain shelter in the Upper Svaneti region, the highest inhabited valley in the Caucasus centred on the UNESCO World Heritage town of Mestia. Upper Svaneti is overlooked by Ushba (4,710 m) — considered by many alpinists the most technically challenging summit in the Caucasus — and backed by Shkhara (5,193 m) and the main Caucasus watershed ridge. Altihati serves trekkers on the multi-day routes connecting Mestia to the upper glacier approaches. The Svan tower villages in the surrounding valleys date from the ninth to twelfth centuries.

## 6. Hill Inn, Roshka Valley

Hill Inn is a guesthouse in the Roshka valley in the Mtiuleti region, near the famous Abudelauri Lakes — three colour-banded glacial tarns (white, green, and black) at around 2,600 m that are among the most visited natural features in Georgia. The path from the village of Roshka to the lakes takes three to four hours. Hill Inn provides accommodation for hikers arriving from the Kazbegi side over the Chaukhi Pass (3,338 m) — a strenuous but spectacular crossing above the basalt columns of the Chaukhi massif.

## 7. აბუდელაურის ტურისტული თავშესაფარი, Abudelauri

Abudelauri Tourist Shelter (აბუდელაურის ტურისტული თავშესაფარი) is positioned near the Abudelauri Lakes at around 2,400 m, providing overnight accommodation for hikers who want to observe the morning light on the lakes and for those doing the multi-day Chaukhi-Roshka-Kazbegi circuit. The shelter is basic but functional, with sleeping platforms and a communal cooking area. It represents the Georgian national parks authority's investment in sustainable trekking infrastructure developed since 2010.

## 8. Felumer, Svaneti

Felumer is a family-run mountain shelter in the Svaneti region on one of the routes connecting the main Mestia valley to the high ridge approaches south of the Caucasus watershed. The name Felumer is of Svan origin, reflecting the distinct Kartvelian language still spoken by the Svan people of Upper Svaneti. The shelter offers simple accommodation and knowledge of local trail conditions that is invaluable for planning ascents of the major Svaneti peaks, where crevassed glaciers and objective hazard require current beta.

## 9. River Land, Svaneti Approaches

River Land is a lodge in the lower Svaneti valley approaches, positioned along one of the river corridors that serve as the hiking entry points from Zugdidi or Kutaisi toward the high country. The lower Svaneti valleys are warmer and more vegetated than the high glacier country and offer outstanding birding in May and June, with species including the Caucasian snowcock, bearded vulture, and various raptors nesting on the limestone cliffs above the treeline.

## 10. Балаганы Кун Иашта, Tusheti Border

Балаганы Кун Иашта are seasonal shepherd shelters (balagany) near the Kuna Iashta summer grazing area in the Tusheti borderland with Dagestan. Balagany are the traditional Caucasian shepherd shelter type — simple wooden or stone-and-timber structures used only during the summer transhumance season — and those at Kun Iashta serve hikers on the less-travelled northeastern Tusheti routes toward the Pirikiti Alazani valley. The area is remote and the trails poorly marked, making local guide hire strongly advisable.
