The Matratzenlager — the word translates literally as mattress store — is one of the defining experiences of alpine travel, and one of the least well explained to first-time visitors. You arrive at a hut after six hours on the trail, pay your bed fee (typically six to twelve euros cheaper than a private room), and are shown into a room that might hold eight people or eighty, depending on the hut. You will sleep on a continuous wooden platform alongside strangers, separated by a single blanket and whatever social norms you can collectively maintain in the dark. It is, when it works, one of the most convivial arrangements in European travel. When it does not, it is one of the least restful nights you will ever spend at altitude.

## The Physical Layout

Most Matratzenlager rooms consist of a central wooden sleeping platform raised 30-50 centimetres from the floor, wide enough to accommodate two rows of sleepers back to back, with a shelf or hook rail running along each wall. In larger huts the platform may span an entire room 10 metres wide and hold 30 or 40 mattresses in a single unbroken row. In smaller, older huts the dormitory is a converted attic with six or eight mattresses under a pitched roof where you cannot stand upright near the eaves.

Each guest receives one blanket (sometimes two in high-altitude huts above 3,000 metres), a pillow, and a pillowcase. You are expected to bring a hut sleeping sheet — a thin cotton or silk liner that fits over the pillow and under the blanket, protecting both you and the shared bedding. Most clubs require this and print the requirement in their booking confirmations, but a surprising number of guests arrive without one and must hire a sheet from the warden at a fee of two to four euros.

## Temperature and Ventilation

Alpine hut dormitories are generally cold. The combination of thick stone walls, single-glazed windows, and a policy of minimal heating (both for cost reasons and because carbon monoxide from gas heaters is a genuine risk in enclosed rooms) means that temperatures at night frequently hover between eight and fourteen degrees Celsius even in July. Experienced hut guests sleep in their base layer. The blankets provided are typically woollen and heavy enough to be adequate, but anyone who runs cold should carry a light sleeping bag or supplement with a down jacket.

Ventilation is a recurring source of tension. The windows in most Matratzenlager are small and open outward on hinges; leaving them open lets cold air in, closing them leads to a warm, humid room that smells of wet wool and athletic effort by midnight. The unspoken convention in most huts is that at least one window stays cracked open regardless of outside temperature. If you find all windows closed when you arrive, opening one quietly is accepted; closing one someone else has opened is not.

## Noise and the 10 PM Rule

Almost every staffed hut in the Alps enforces a Nachtruhe — quiet hours — starting between 9 PM and 10 PM and ending at 5 AM or 6 AM. This is not a suggestion. Wardens will knock on dormitory doors after 10 PM if conversation is audible in the corridor. The rationale is sound: guests attempting summit starts typically rise at 3 AM or 4 AM and need to be asleep by 9 PM to get a useful amount of rest.

The sources of noise that actually disturb sleep are rarely loud conversation. Snoring is far more common and far harder to manage. In a room of twelve people, statistical probability guarantees at least two snorers; in a room of thirty, the combined sound can resemble light machinery. Earplugs are not optional equipment — treat them as essential as your rain jacket. Foam plugs in the 33-decibel reduction range are adequate for most situations; purpose-built sleeping plugs with a flatter attenuation curve are better if you want to hear an alarm while still blocking snoring.

The other persistent noise source is the pre-dawn departure. Guests leaving for a 3 AM summit start will, regardless of their good intentions, make noise: crinkled dry bags, the metallic sound of ice axe against crampon, headlamp switches, whispered conversations that carry. If you are not on a summit schedule, a sleep mask and earplugs handle this; if you are, rehearse your packing the night before so your pre-dawn preparation takes under five minutes.

## Boot Rooms, Wet Gear, and Tracking Mud

Virtually all Alpine huts operate a strict in-out protocol for footwear. You remove your boots in the Schuhraum (boot room) at the entrance and leave them there overnight — wet boots placed on a heated rack if the hut has one, otherwise simply side by side on the floor. You continue into the hut in the hut slippers provided, your own socks, or light camp shoes. Walking through the dining room or dormitory in hiking boots is one of the most reliably irritating things you can do in a hut environment and will draw immediate comment from the warden.

Wet gaiters, rain jackets, and over-trousers belong in the boot room or on the drying rack in the corridor. The drying rack typically has numbered pegs corresponding to your bed number in some huts; in others it is first-come first-served. Do not drape wet kit over another guest's dry gear.

## Unwritten Rules Worth Knowing

Several conventions that regular hut-goers take for granted are never written down anywhere. Reservations made through DAV, SAC, or CAI booking systems assign you a specific bed number, which you should find and claim as soon as you arrive rather than waiting for the warden to show you. Setting an alarm at 3 AM when you plan to wake at 5 AM because you are not sure which is the right start time is inconsiderate; commit to a departure time. Leaving your pack in the dormitory aisle overnight is a hazard to anyone navigating in the dark. And the hut towel — provided in many private rooms but not in most Matratzenlager — should be in your pack if you plan to shower, since the shared bathroom typically has none to spare.

Tipping is neither expected nor unwelcome in most Alpine huts. If the warden has done something notably helpful — revised your route plan because of incoming weather, stayed up late to accommodate a late arrival — a small amount left on the table at breakfast is appropriate. Rounds of schnapps for the dormitory, offered by one guest to others they have met at dinner, are a genuine tradition in Austrian and Bavarian huts and are never turned down.

## Choosing Between Matratzenlager and a Private Room

Private rooms exist in many huts, particularly those at lower elevations on popular trails. They cost significantly more — typically twelve to twenty euros per person more per night — and are worth the premium if you have a scheduled early start, you sleep lightly, or you are travelling as a couple or small group and want a guaranteed bunk-neighbour situation. For solo travellers, the Matratzenlager is usually preferable from a social standpoint: the communal setup encourages conversation in a way that a private room does not, and many of the long-term friendships that form on multi-day Alpine routes begin across a shared dormitory platform.

## Find Huts That Suit Your Sleep Style

If you prefer smaller dormitories with six to ten beds, remote huts at higher elevations generally have fewer total guests and quieter nights; popular valley-access huts with road or cable-car access tend toward larger rooms and more through-traffic.
