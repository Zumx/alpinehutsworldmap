The staffed Alpine hut is inseparable from the person who runs it. A hut keeper
— Hüttenwart in German, gardien de refuge in French, rifugista in Italian —
is simultaneously innkeeper, cook, first-aider, amateur meteorologist, mountain
guide advisor, HR manager, maintenance engineer, and the first person guests
see after a long, sometimes brutal day in the mountains. The quality of a hut
night depends on this person more than on any physical feature of the building.

## Who becomes a hut keeper

The backgrounds are diverse. Many hut keepers are former guides or mountain
professionals who have transitioned to running a hut in middle age, trading
roped climbing for the different demands of hospitality. Some are farmers'
children from mountain valleys who never imagined living elsewhere. A significant
number are couples — the hut-keeper pair is a well-established Alpine
institution, with one partner managing the kitchen and the other handling
guests, maintenance, and the mountain-weather assessment that shapes every
operational day.

In Austria and Germany, the ÖAV and DAV run training courses for new hut
keepers: food hygiene, mountain first aid, basic accounting, weather reading,
and the cultural protocols of the hut. Completion is required before taking
on a lease. In Italy, the preparation is less formalised and more dependent
on apprenticeship — many rifugisti have grown up in the culture, worked for
their parents, and taken over the lease when the time came.

The rifugista of a long-established Italian hut often has a personal
relationship with the mountain that extends back generations. The Rifugio
Lagazuoi family has run the hut at 2752 m above the Falzarego Pass since
the 1960s; the accumulated knowledge of that single position — weather patterns,
seasonal changes, routes, rescue history — is irreplaceable.

## The operating season

Summer season for a staffed Alpine hut typically runs from mid-June to late
September or early October, depending on elevation and snowfall patterns.
This is 15–18 weeks of full operation, often seven days a week, with no
equivalent of the urban weekend. The kitchen is running by 06:00 for early
departures; it closes at 20:30. In between, the hut keeper manages bookings
(now largely via phone and online systems), coordinates helicopter deliveries,
supervises seasonal staff, and deals with the day's events — which at a busy
hut in a popular area can range from straightforward to genuinely complex.

Seasonal staff — typically 2–6 people at a mid-sized hut — are recruited each
year, often from a pool of university students or young mountain enthusiasts
who return season after season. Staff accommodation is typically in the hut
itself, in shared rooms separate from guests.

## Food sourcing and the helicopter

The helicopter is the hut keeper's supply chain. Most high-Alpine huts receive
one or two resupply flights per week in peak season, each carrying 600–800 kg
of provisions. Planning the supply — calculating guest-days, forecasting
consumption, managing perishables at altitude — is a logistics operation
that takes skill to run efficiently.

Some hut keepers supplement helicopter delivery with goods carried up by mule
train or, where a service road or cable car reaches the hut, by vehicle
transport for heavy or non-perishable items. The Dresdner Hütte in the Stubai
Alps is served by the Stubaier Gletscher cable car system, which dramatically
reduces supply cost; huts in truly remote terrain have no such option.

The food menu reflects what arrives: hardy vegetables that travel well
(onions, carrots, cabbage, potatoes), dry and canned goods as staples,
fresh items in the days immediately after a flight. Bread is often baked
on site. Wine and beer travel surprisingly well by helicopter.

## Family versus club management

There is a structural difference between family-operated huts and club-managed
huts in the different Alpine systems. The Swiss SAC and Austrian ÖAV typically
lease huts to a keeper-family under a multi-year contract, with the club
retaining ownership and providing capital for major maintenance and renovation.
The keeper runs the operation, takes the revenue, and pays a lease fee.

In the Italian system, many rifugi — particularly in the Dolomites — are
privately owned rather than club property, giving the rifugista more operational
freedom and full economic ownership. The long-established family rifugi of the
Dolomites are often the most characterful: run with genuine individual identity
rather than within a club-standardised framework.

## What keeps them returning

Ask a long-serving hut keeper what they value about the work, and the answers
converge on a few things: the landscape, the independence, the directness
of the relationship with guests, and the community of people — guides, regular
hikers, mountaineers — who return year after year. The social world of a popular
hut in high season is intense, compressed, and meaningful in a way that is
difficult to replicate in the valley.

The isolation is also part of it. A hut keeper at 3000 m is, for months at a
time, genuinely removed from the normal velocity of modern life. This can be
hard; it is also, for the people who choose it, one of the things they value
most.

## The winter room tradition

Many Alpine huts that are only staffed in summer maintain a Winterraum
(winter room) — an unlocked room accessible year-round, stocked with basic
emergency supplies, a wood or gas stove, and a supply of dry wood or gas
canisters. The winter room exists for exactly the emergencies that close
the staffed hut (storms, avalanche conditions, late-season parties caught
by weather) and is a fundamental part of the alpine safety net.

Using a winter room is generally free for emergency use; the convention is
to leave a contribution in the honesty box (if one exists), to leave more
supplies than you consume, and to restore the room to the state you found
it. Hut keepers who return to their hut at the start of the season expect
to find the winter room used but respected.

Some of the most atmospheric winter room experiences in the Alps are in
huts that are normally dominated by summer crowds: the Rifugio Lagazuoi
in the Dolomites with its wartime tunnel museum, the Bremer Hütte in the
Stubai Alps, and the various Wapta system huts in the Canadian Rockies all
have winter rooms that provide essential emergency infrastructure for the
ski-touring season before the staffed season opens.

## Hut keeper community and training

The hut keeper community across the Alps is small enough that many keepers
know each other personally. In Austria and Germany, the ÖAV and DAV run
a Hüttenwirt training programme that covers food hygiene, first aid, business
management, and mountain rescue coordination. The Swiss SAC has similar
training. The Italian CAI has formal courses for rifugisti that include
traditional cuisine preparation — preserving the regional character of the
food tradition alongside the operational skills.

Regional gatherings of hut keepers — sometimes informal, sometimes
organised by the Alpine clubs — maintain the community bonds. In Tyrol,
the informal network of Hüttenwirte across the ÖAV system means that a
hut keeper whose equipment fails unexpectedly can often borrow or arrange
emergency supplies from a neighbour. The reciprocal trust within the
community is part of what makes the system reliable.
