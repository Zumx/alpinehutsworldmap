In the granite heart of the Pyrenees, where the only Catalan national park spreads its tangle of more than two hundred glacial lakes, a circuit of mountain huts has become a rite of passage for Spanish hikers. The Carros de Foc, or Chariots of Fire, links nine guarded refugis through the Parc Nacional d'Aiguestortes i Estany de Sant Maurici, a loop of roughly 55 kilometres and more than 7,000 metres of cumulative ascent. Walkers carry a small passport, stamped at each hut, and the fastest among them try to complete the full ring in a single punishing day. For most, it is a five or six day journey through a landscape of clear water, scoured rock and weather that turns in an hour.

## The Aiguestortes High Country

Aiguestortes lies in the Catalan province of Lleida, in the comarques of the Pallars Sobirà and the Alta Ribagorça. The name means twisted waters, a reference to the meandering streams of the Sant Nicolau valley near Boí. This is the wettest and greenest corner of the central Pyrenees, with peaks rising above 3,000 metres around the Estany de Sant Maurici and the iconic twin spires of Els Encantats. The bedrock is hard granite and slate, polished by vanished glaciers into smooth slabs and dotted with the lakes, the estanys, that give the park its character.

The park sits between the Aran valley to the north and the Boí valley to the south, with the village of Espot guarding the eastern gateway. Around its fringes lie real places worth knowing: the Romanesque churches of the Vall de Boí, a UNESCO World Heritage site, and the Caldes de Boí thermal springs. The refugis themselves are run mostly by the Federació d'Entitats Excursionistes de Catalunya and the Centre Excursionista de Catalunya, the historic clubs that opened these mountains to walkers a century ago.

## Refugi d'Amitges and the Lake of the Mares

High above the Sant Maurici lake at around 2,380 metres, the Refugi d'Amitges sits among a cluster of lakes beneath the Agulles d'Amitges, slender granite needles beloved of climbers. It is one of the classic Carros de Foc stages and a superb base for ascents of the Pic de Peguera and the Tuc de Saboredo. From its terrace the view runs back across the Sant Maurici basin to the Encantats, a scene reproduced on countless Catalan postcards.

The hut is reached on foot from the Estany de Sant Maurici, itself accessible by the jeep taxis that run up from Espot. Guarded through summer and parts of the ski-touring season, it offers half board, blankets in the dormitory and the brisk, communal rhythm of mountain refuge life: boots off at the door, an early dinner, lights out before the stars are fully out.

## Restanca, Colomèrs and the Northern Stages

On the northern, Aranese side of the circuit, the Refugi de la Restanca stands by its reservoir at about 2,010 metres, a green and sheltered spot reached from the Artiga de Lin or the Aran valley. From here the route climbs to the Refugi de Colomèrs, set in the Circ de Colomèrs, a vast amphitheatre holding dozens of lakes that drain toward the Garona. This is some of the finest lake country in the whole range, and the traverse between the two huts is a highlight of the Carros de Foc.

Further along, the Refugi de Saboredo and the Refugi de Ventosa i Calvell complete the high northern arc. Ventosa i Calvell, beneath the great wall of the Besiberri massif and the Punta Alta, is a launching point for the highest summits in the park. These huts share the same FEEC ethos: simple, warm, well placed, and run by guardians who know every col and every change in the sky.

## Crossing the Cols

The art of the Carros de Foc lies in the passes. Between the huts the route threads a series of high cols, the Port de Ratera, the Coll de Saboredo, the Coll de Contraix and others, many above 2,700 metres and several holding old snow well into July. The Coll de Contraix in particular has a fearsome reputation: a steep, loose descent of granite blocks that demands care and good legs late in a long day.

Walkers tackle the circuit in either direction and split it into stages to suit their fitness. The full loop touches the Refugi d'Estany Llong in the Sant Nicolau valley, the Refugi Josep Maria Blanc beneath the Tuc de Peguera, and the Refugi de Colomina on the southern edge, each adding its own flavour of granite, water and silence. The terrain is rough underfoot, with little true path in places, and route-finding through boulder fields is part of the challenge.

## The Passport and the Spirit of the Circuit

Carros de Foc has its own gentle ceremony. At the start you buy the carnet, the little booklet that records your passage, and at each refugi the guardian stamps it. Complete the ring and you earn a finisher's diploma; race it in under 24 hours and your name joins a roll of honour. The record for the full nine-hut loop has been driven below eight hours by mountain runners, a feat that says as much about the quality of the terrain as the strength of the legs involved.

Yet the soul of the circuit is not speed. It is the rhythm of hut life across a week: the shared tables, the predawn starts under cold stars, the long descents into lake basins where trout rise at dusk. It is one of the most accessible ways to experience a true alpine hut traverse in Spain, demanding fitness and sure feet but no technical climbing.

## Practicalities and Season

The refugis open fully from about late June to late September, with shoulder periods guarded at weekends. Booking ahead is essential in high summer; the huts are small and the circuit popular with Catalan, Spanish and increasingly international walkers. Half board is the norm, and meals lean on hearty mountain fare, escudella stews, lentils, sausage and the inevitable bread and chorizo for the trail.

Weather in Aiguestortes is famously changeable, with afternoon thunderstorms common in July and August. Start early, watch the sky, and respect the park's strict rules: no wild camping, no fires, dogs only in limited zones, and a duty to leave the lakes and slabs exactly as you found them. The taxi services from Espot and Boí shorten the approaches and are the standard way in.

## Beyond the Carros de Foc

The Catalan Pyrenees hold far more than this one circuit. To the east, the Cadí-Moixeró natural park and the high meadows of the Cerdanya offer gentler hut walking, while the Posets-Maladeta region to the west, just over the Aragonese border, guards the Aneto, the highest peak in the Pyrenees at 3,404 metres. Across the Spanish ranges, from the Picos de Europa in the north to the Sierra Nevada in the south, a wider scatter of refuges, refugios and shepherd shelters awaits, places such as Refugio La Chanata, Refugio El Salvador and the high cortijos of Andalucía. Each tells its own story of how Spaniards have sheltered in their mountains.
