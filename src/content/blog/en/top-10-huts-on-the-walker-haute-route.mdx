The Walker's Haute Route — as distinct from the ski Haute Route — is the
summer trekking traverse from Chamonix to Zermatt via the Pennine Alps. At
approximately 200 kilometres with 12,000 m of elevation gain, it takes 12–14
days for a fit party and follows high passes, glacier moraines, and remote
valleys that are largely inaccessible by road. The hut sequence is not identical
to the ski route; the summer route traverses terrain that is accessible only
on foot in snow-free conditions. Each stage ends at a staffed overnight hut or
village with accommodation.

## 1. Refuge d'Argentière / Chamonix valley huts, 1765 m — Chamonix, France

The starting point at Chamonix (1035 m) is reached by TGV from Paris or by
bus from Geneva. The first stage crosses through the Chamonix valley and
ascends to the Col de Balme (2204 m) at the French-Swiss border. Accommodation
the first night is typically in Chamonix centre or the Refuge d'Argentière
above Argentière (1250 m) for parties walking the full route from the lowest
elevation.

## 2. Cabane du Col du Grand Saint Bernard / Bourg-Saint-Pierre area, 2469 m — Valais

On the route variant via the Grand Saint Bernard, the historic hospice at the
Grand Saint Bernard pass (2469 m) — a functioning monastery providing mountain
hospitality since 1049 — serves as one of the most historically resonant
overnight stops in the Alps. Founded by Saint Bernard of Aosta, the hospice
maintained a rescue dog breeding programme (Saint Bernards) for centuries.
Full board; open to all travellers.

## 3. Cabane du Trient, 3170 m — Trient Plateau, Valais

High on the Trient Plateau above Champex-Lac, this SAC hut at 3170 m is the
first genuinely high-alpine overnight on the Walker's Haute Route. Glacier
approach from Champex (3–4 hours). The neighbouring Fenêtre d'Arpette
(2665 m) is one of the most dramatic non-glaciated passes on the route —
a rocky scramble with vertiginous views.

## 4. Cabane des Vignettes CAS, 3160 m — Arolla, Valais

The emotional centrepiece of the Haute Route — a SAC hut perched on a rock
island in the Otemma glacier at 3160 m, reached via the Pas de Chèvres fixed
ladders and a glacier crossing from Arolla. Capacity around 100; early dawn
departure for the Col de l'Evêque crossing. (Full entry in the dedicated Cabane
des Vignettes article.)

## 5. Cabane de Bertol CAS, 3311 m — Bertol ridge, Valais

The most technical overnight on the Walker's Haute Route — reached by fixed
ladders and chains regardless of the season, at 3311 m on a rock pinnacle
above the Bertol glacier. Capacity around 80; departure at 05:00 for the
Tête Blanche crossing.

## 6. Rifugio Brunet, 1900 m — Valpelline, Valle d'Aosta (alternative variant)

On the variant descending to the Italian side from the Col de Valcornère or
Col du Mont Brûlé, Italian rifugi in the Valpelline valley provide overnight
stops before re-crossing into Switzerland.

## 7. Hotel/Guesthouse Zinal, 1675 m — Val d'Anniviers, Valais

The standard stopping village for the Walker's Haute Route mid-section, Zinal
(1675 m) at the head of the Val d'Anniviers provides full hotel and guesthouse
accommodation, restaurants, and supplies. The stage from Arolla to Zinal via
the Col de Torrent (2919 m) is one of the finest days of the whole route.

## 8. Cabane du Mountet, 2886 m — Val d'Anniviers, Zinal

From Zinal, some parties make the effort to ascend to the Cabane du Mountet
at 2886 m above the Mountet glacier — a SAC hut with extraordinary views of
the Obergabelhorn, Zinalrothorn (4221 m), and Weisshorn (4506 m). This is an
optional stage; the standard route continues from Zinal toward Gruben.

## 9. Gruben/Hotel Schwarzsee, 1800 m — Turtmanntal, Valais

The remote village of Gruben in the isolated Turtmanntal is the most
characteristic overnight on the Walker's Haute Route — a small valley that
has changed little over centuries, with the simple Hotel Schwarzsee providing
basic accommodation for through-walkers.

## 10. Zermatt valley accommodation, 1608 m — Zermatt

The final day of the Walker's Haute Route descends from the St Niklaus or
Grächen direction into the Zermatt valley and the endpoint of the traverse.
Arrival in Zermatt below the Matterhorn — visible for the last hour of the
descent — is one of the great finishes in alpine travel.

## Planning the Walker's Haute Route

The standard guidebook for the Walker's Haute Route is Kev Reynolds's
Chamonix to Zermatt (Cicerone), which covers both the summer walking and
the ski variants. The route is typically walked west to east (Chamonix to
Zermatt) to take advantage of the morning light on the eastern approaches
and to finish under the Matterhorn — the psychological destination that pulls
walkers forward across twelve days of effort.

Stage lengths on the Walker's Haute Route range from 5 to 8 hours; the
longest and most serious stages involve glacier moraine terrain and passes
above 2800 m that require an early start. The Fenêtre d'Arpette (2665 m)
and the Col de Torrent (2919 m) are the two most demanding non-glaciated
crossings; parties who prefer to avoid the highest terrain have valley
alternatives at several points.

Acclimatisation is a genuine consideration. Many walkers arrive in
Chamonix from sea level and face the Cabane du Trient (3170 m) within
three days. The standard recommendation is to spend one extra night in
Chamonix or at a lower-elevation overnight before ascending to the high
hut sequence. Altitude sickness at 3000–3300 m is rare but not unknown
for sea-level arrivals who ascend quickly.

Hut reservations on the Walker's Haute Route must be made in advance for
the July–August peak season. The Swiss SAC huts (Cabane du Trient, Cabane
des Vignettes, Cabane de Bertol, Cabane du Mountet) book out within hours
of the online reservation system opening in March. The middle stages
(Zinal, Gruben) involve guesthouses and small hotels that also fill quickly
in peak season. Booking the entire route before departure is strongly
recommended; last-minute walkers frequently find themselves on valley
variants rather than the high route due to full huts.

## Fitness and equipment

The Walker's Haute Route is rated as a moderate-difficult Alpine trekking
route (approximately T3 on the Swiss scale for the main route, with some
sections reaching T4 at the steeper passes and ladder sections at Bertol).
No technical mountaineering equipment is required for the standard summer
route, but glacier-crossing sections should be assessed each season —
the Otemma glacier section has become less straightforward as the glacier
retreats.

Essential kit for the Haute Route includes waterproof clothing of genuine
mountain quality, a sleeping bag (most SAC huts provide blankets but
sleeping bag liners are hygienically standard), trekking poles, and
approach shoes or light hiking boots with a stiff sole adequate for the
high passes. The total pack weight for a well-equipped 12-day traverse
without guided support is typically 12–15 kg for a solo walker.

## The ski Haute Route versus the Walker's Haute Route

The two routes share the same endpoint cities and general direction but
very different terrain. The ski Haute Route (typically March–April) takes
a more direct high-glacier line, crossing the Otemma and Mont Blanc de
Cheilon glaciers in conditions that are impassable in summer. The
Walker's Haute Route follows lower valley approaches and non-glaciated
passes that are snow-free from late June onward. The ski route takes 6–7
days for an expert group; the walking route takes 12–14 days. Both are
considered classics of their respective disciplines.
