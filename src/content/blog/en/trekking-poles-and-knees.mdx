The trekking pole is the piece of equipment most often carried incorrectly
and least often understood. On a multi-day hut route — say, the Stubai
Höhenweg from the Sulzenauhütte to the Nürnberger Hütte, with 800 m of descent
on loose rocky path before dinner — the poles either help your knees significantly
or contribute nothing, depending entirely on how they are used. And on arrival
at the hut, there is one non-negotiable rule that beginners invariably violate.

## The biomechanics of descent loading

On a steep descent, each footfall loads the knee joint with forces of 3–5 times
body weight. Over a 1000 m descent on loose rocky terrain — a normal half-day
on any Alpine hut traverse — this cumulative loading is the primary cause of
knee pain and long-term cartilage wear in mountain walkers.

A pole planted uphill at the moment of foot contact transfers a portion of
this loading from the knee to the shoulder, elbow, and wrist. Studies measuring
knee joint reaction force show reductions of 12–25 percent with properly used
bilateral poles on steep descents. Over multiple days of hut walking, this
reduction compounds: less cumulative loading means less end-of-day knee
soreness and less degradation of the joint over a lifetime of mountain use.

The operative phrase is "properly used." Poles that are the wrong length, used
passively (dragged behind rather than actively planted), or planted at the
wrong moment contribute little. The key is active planting: the pole tip meets
the ground slightly ahead of the foot, at the moment the foot contacts the
slope, and the arm is bent at approximately 90 degrees when the pole is planted.

## Pole length on ascent versus descent

Poles should be longer on descent and shorter on ascent — a 10–15 cm difference
is the standard guideline. On ascent, a shorter pole allows a more natural
upright posture with the arm swing propelling you forward; on descent, a longer
pole allows planting further ahead and creates the mechanical advantage that
actually transfers loading away from the knees.

Many walkers set their poles for uphill and never adjust. Taking 60 seconds
to extend the poles by 10 cm before a major descent is one of the highest-
return maintenance decisions in hut travel.

The correct ascent length: when the pole tip is planted beside your foot
on flat ground, your elbow should be at approximately 90 degrees. The correct
descent length: 5–10 cm longer than the ascent setting.

## The elastic anti-shock debate

Some poles include an internal spring mechanism (typically called "anti-shock"
or "flick-lock elastic") that absorbs vibration on the plant. The debate
about whether this reduces joint loading further or whether it wastes the
mechanical advantage that a rigid plant provides has been running for 30 years.

The current consensus: the spring helps on hard surfaces (rock, frozen
ground) where the rapid deceleration of the pole tip creates a jarring impulse
that would otherwise travel up the arm. On soft surfaces (snow, grass, trail),
the difference is minimal. For hard-surface rocky descents — which is most
of what you encounter on limestone Dolomite terrain or granite Swiss passes —
the anti-shock mechanism provides real benefit.

## Technique on steep and exposed terrain

On very steep or technically demanding sections, poles become a liability
rather than an asset. The upper sections of the Innsbrucker Hütte approach,
via ferrata sections on the Alta Via 2, and scrambling terrain on T4-grade
paths all require hands to be free and available for balance and security.

The solution: collapser or fold the poles and stow them. A pole dangling from
a wrist strap while you scramble is a genuine safety hazard — the strap can
catch on rock and throw your balance at the wrong moment. The time to deploy
and stow poles during a day's walking with mixed terrain is a practiced skill.
Telescoping poles (twist-lock or flick-lock) can be extended or collapsed
in under 30 seconds; folding poles in under 60 seconds.

## The hut slippers rule

Every staffed Alpine hut from the Austrian Schutzhütte to the French refuge
to the Italian rifugio has the same rule: outdoor footwear — boots, trail
runners, crampons, gaiters — stays in the boot room (Schuhraum/local equivalent)
at the entrance. The rule is signposted, universally observed by locals, and
routinely violated by visitors who are not aware of it.

Poles follow the same protocol. They stay in the boot room or in the rack
provided outside the main door. Bringing poles into the dining room, the
dormitory, or the common area is not done. The hut floors are often timber
or tile; steel tips create damage and noise, and the poles themselves create
obstacles in crowded communal spaces.

If your poles have rubber tip protectors (essential for any serious hut
traveller — they protect the tip, reduce noise, and prevent the poles
punching through soft terrain), make sure they are on before the boot room.
If you are uncertain whether poles are welcome inside a particular hut, ask
the hut keeper; the answer is reliably "no, leave them at the door."

## Weight and material

Carbon fibre poles (150–250 g per pole) versus aluminium poles (250–350 g per
pole) is a weight decision with a durability trade-off: carbon is lighter and
stiffer but shatters under lateral stress; aluminium bends but does not
catastrophically fail. For multi-day hut travel on rocky terrain, aluminium is
the more reliable choice unless weight savings are a priority.

Grips: cork grips feel comfortable for most hand temperatures and do not
become slippery when damp. Foam grips are warmer but absorb sweat and odour.
Plastic grips are cold and become slippery with sweat — suitable for easy
summer conditions but not optimal.

## Collapsible versus fixed poles

Collapsible poles (folding or twist-lock) versus fixed-length poles involves
a genuine trade-off. Fixed-length poles are stronger, simpler (no mechanism
to fail), and lighter for a given strength. Collapsible poles can be packed
into a bag, stowed in a pack, or shortened for steep uphill work where full-
length poles are awkward. The stow-ability of collapsible poles is most
useful for via ferrata sections (where poles are a liability) and for air
travel (poles must be checked, not carried on; collapsible poles fit into
standard luggage with fewer issues).

For a multi-day hut traverse with no via ferrata and no air travel, fixed-
length poles sized correctly before departure are lighter and simpler.
For a trip that includes ferrata or requires air checked bags, collapsible
poles are worth the modest weight premium.

## Adjusting length on terrain

The ability to adjust pole length mid-route is undervalued. On steep
ascending terrain, shortening the poles by 5–8 cm prevents the shoulders
from being forced upward; on steep descending terrain, lengthening them
provides a longer reaching point that distributes the shock more effectively.
The optimal length for flat terrain (pole planted with elbow at 90 degrees)
is not the same as for steep technical descents (longer) or steep uphill
(shorter).

Quick-adjust mechanisms (twist-lock, speed lock) that can be changed
during a movement without stopping are worth the minor extra weight and
cost; poles requiring a tool for adjustment are impractical for mid-route
changes.
