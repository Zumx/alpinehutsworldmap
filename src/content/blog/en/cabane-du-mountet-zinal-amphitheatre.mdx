At the very head of the Val de Zinal, where the valley closes into a glaciated cul-de-sac, the Cabane du Mountet stands at 2,886 metres on a rock island above the Zinal glacier, ringed by one of the most spectacular cirques in the Alps. From its terrace the eye travels around an almost unbroken arc of 4,000-metre peaks: the Zinalrothorn, the Ober Gabelhorn, the Dent Blanche, and the long ridge of the Grand Cornier, with the Weisshorn rising behind. Few huts in the entire range place a visitor so completely inside an amphitheatre of ice and rock. The approach is long and the position remote, and that very remoteness is the source of the Mountet's enduring magnetism.

## A Cirque of Four-Thousanders

The Mountet's setting is its whole reason for being. The hut occupies a glacial promontory at the confluence of the Zinal and Mountet glaciers, and the wall of summits enclosing it reads like a roll-call of the Pennine Alps. The Zinalrothorn (4,221 m) and the Ober Gabelhorn (4,063 m) dominate the head of the cirque; the Dent Blanche (4,357 m) lifts its great pyramid to the south; the Grand Cornier (3,962 m) closes the western arc; and away to the east the Weisshorn (4,506 m) shows itself above the intervening ridges. Standing on the terrace at dusk, with the glaciers turning grey-blue and the high faces still catching light, the visitor is surrounded on three sides by some of the most storied mountaineering objectives in Europe.

## The Approach from Zinal

Reaching the Mountet is a commitment in itself. From the village of Zinal at the road-head, the path climbs steadily up the valley, at first through larch and alpine pasture, then across moraine and finally along the flank above the Zinal glacier. The walk takes roughly five to six hours and gains well over a thousand metres, with the final stretch becoming rougher as the trail negotiates the moraine and rock leading to the hut's promontory. This is no lift-served refuge; everyone who sleeps at the Mountet has earned the bed on foot. The length of the approach keeps casual visitors away and gives the hut its characteristic atmosphere of serious purpose, peopled mainly by mountaineers with summits in mind.

## A Classic SAC Mountaineering Base

The Cabane du Mountet is owned and run by the Swiss Alpine Club, and it serves as the launching point for a remarkable concentration of high routes. From here climbers attempt the Zinalrothorn, often by its long and committing ridges; the Ober Gabelhorn; and traverses linking the peaks of the cirque. The hut is also a key staging point on the high-level traverse that links the Zinal valley with neighbouring basins, and parties crossing high cols such as those toward the Mountet's surrounding glaciers use it as a base. With its warden, communal dining, and the typical SAC half-board, the hut functions as a self-contained world at the head of the valley, where the day's rhythm is set entirely by weather, glacier conditions, and the early alpine start.

## Sunrise Without a Summit

One of the Mountet's quiet pleasures is that the sunrise spectacle requires no summit at all. Because the hut sits inside the cirque rather than on a ridge, the first light of day climbs down the faces of the Zinalrothorn and Ober Gabelhorn in full view of the terrace, illuminating the high ice while the valley below remains in deep shadow. A guest can experience one of the great alpine dawns with a cup of coffee in hand, watching the sequence of summits ignite. For those who do want a summit, the surrounding peaks offer objectives across the full range of difficulty, but the hut rewards even the non-climber who simply makes the long walk up to witness the place.

## The Valais Hut Constellation

The Mountet belongs to the dense Swiss Alpine Club network that makes the Valais one of the great hut-touring regions on earth. Across the ridges and valleys lie the Britanniahütte above Saas-Fee and the Mischabelhütte on its airy spur; to the west, around the Grand Combin, cluster the Cabane FXB Panossière, the Cabane de Valsorey, the Cabane du Vélan, the Cabane de Mille, and the Cabane Brunet. Linking huts such as the Cabane de Bertol CAS and the Cabane de Saleinaz CAS thread the routes toward Chamonix and the great traverses. The Cabane du Mont Fort, far to the west above Verbier, opens the ski Haute Route that ultimately reaches this very corner of the Pennine Alps. The Mountet is the wild, deep-set jewel of this constellation, the hut you reach only by walking into the heart of the mountains.

## Planning and Season

A visit to the Mountet should be planned around its long approach and serious surroundings. The hut is typically wardened through the summer mountaineering season, roughly from late June or early July into September, with exact dates dependent on snow and glacier conditions. Anyone venturing onto the glaciers from the hut needs full glacier equipment and the competence to use it, since crevasse hazard is ever-present in the surrounding basin. Even walkers content to reach the hut and turn back should be fit for a long, rough day and prepared for rapid weather changes at altitude. Booking ahead is essential, and a call to the warden for current conditions is the sensible first step before committing to the climb up from Zinal.

## The Long History of a Mountaineering Refuge

The Mountet belongs to the great age of nineteenth-century alpinism, when the Pennine Alps were being explored and their summits claimed for the first time. The Zinal valley and its surrounding peaks were a focus of British and Swiss mountaineering during the golden age, and a hut in this remote basin made the ascents of the Zinalrothorn, the Ober Gabelhorn, and the Dent Blanche far more feasible than the long valley approaches alone allowed. Successive structures have stood on this promontory, each rebuilt and enlarged as the Swiss Alpine Club's network grew and the demands of mountaineers changed. The current hut continues that tradition, a working base rather than a museum, but the sense of history is palpable: the same summits that drew the pioneers still rise unchanged around the terrace, and the routes to them follow much the same lines they did more than a century ago. To sit at the Mountet on a still evening is to occupy a place that generations of climbers have regarded as one of the finest viewpoints in the entire Alpine chain, a feeling reinforced by the very effort required to reach it.
