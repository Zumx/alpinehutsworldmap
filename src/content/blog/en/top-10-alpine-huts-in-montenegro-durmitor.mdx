The Durmitor massif rises in northern Montenegro as a wall of grey limestone, its plateau scattered with eighteen glacial lakes the locals call gorske oci — the eyes of the mountains. At its heart sits the Black Lake, Crno jezero, a short walk from the town of Žabljak, the highest town in the Balkans at around 1,450 metres. Above it the peaks climb past 2,500 metres to Bobotov Kuk, the massif's high point at 2,523 metres. Montenegrin mountain shelter has two faces here: the planinarski dom, the mountaineering house run by hiking societies, and the katun, the traditional summer herding settlement where families still graze sheep and make cheese on the high pastures. Together they make Durmitor and the wilder Prokletije to the south among the finest and least-known walking country in Europe.

## Planinarski dom Škrka

Deep in the heart of the Durmitor plateau, beside the two Škrka lakes, sits one of the massif's classic mountain houses, a base for the demanding traverse beneath Bobotov Kuk. The walk in crosses the high karst, a moonscape of grey limestone pavement and sudden green hollows, and reaching a dom here after a long day is the essence of Balkan hut walking.

## Planinarski dom Šušica

By the long, sinuous Šušica lake on the edge of the massif, the Šušica mountain house serves walkers exploring the quieter western Durmitor. The lake drains underground through the karst in late summer, a reminder that this whole landscape is built on limestone riddled with caves and sinkholes.

## Katun Dolovi Lalevića

The katuns are Durmitor's living tradition. Katun Dolovi Lalevića is one of many summer herding settlements where families move their flocks up to the high pastures from June to September, living in stone-and-timber huts and selling fresh cheese, kajmak, and milk to passing walkers. Sleeping at a katun is the most authentic shelter the region offers — basic, warm, and rooted in a way of life centuries old.

## Etno katun Kolijevka

Some katuns have opened their doors more formally to visitors. Etno katun Kolijevka and similar ethno-villages offer the herding-settlement experience with a little more comfort — proper beds, hearty mountain food, and a base for day walks onto the plateau. They are a gentle way into the katun world for those not carrying full camping gear.

## Planinarski dom Cmiljača and the society houses

A string of mountaineering houses run by Montenegrin hiking societies thread the massif and its approaches — Cmiljača, Vranjak, Praćevac, Suvodo, and Grope among them. These domovi vary from staffed weekend houses to simple locked shelters for which the local society holds a key. They form the backbone of multi-day walking in the region, spaced to break the long traverses across the high ground.

## Planinarski dom Ckara

On the approaches to the plateau, Planinarski dom Ckara is one of the gathering houses where walkers stage before heading high. Žabljak itself, with its guesthouses and the Durmitor National Park visitor centre, is the practical base for the whole massif, and the domovi extend its reach into the peaks.

## Bobotov Kuk and the high bivouacs

The ascent of Bobotov Kuk, the roof of Durmitor, is the great objective. There is no hut on the summit, but the network of plateau domovi and katuns lets walkers reach the base and climb the final via-ferrata-protected scramble in a day. From the top, on a clear morning, you can see across the Tara canyon — Europe's deepest gorge at over 1,300 metres — toward the peaks of Bosnia and Albania.

## Mountain Center Krivi Do

Newer mountain centres such as the one at Krivi Do blend lodging, guiding, and activity bases, reflecting how Durmitor's tourism has grown around rafting the Tara, winter skiing at Žabljak, and summer trekking. They sit between the traditional katun and the modern guesthouse, offering organised access to the massif.

## The Prokletije shelters: Hajla and beyond

South toward the Albanian border, the Prokletije — the Accursed Mountains — are wilder, higher, and far less travelled than Durmitor. Shelters such as the Hajla Mountain Hut serve the peaks above Rožaje and Plav, and the cross-border Peaks of the Balkans trail links Montenegro, Albania, and Kosovo through this range. The katun tradition is even stronger here, with whole valleys still given over to summer herding.

## Katun Reljina and the mountain retreats

Rounding out the picture, retreats like Katun Reljina blend the herding-settlement form with quiet lodging in the high meadows. They are the kind of place where a long traverse can pause for a night of cheese, rakija, and silence under a sky thick with stars, far from any road.

## Food, cheese, and the katun table

Part of the joy of walking Durmitor is eating at a katun. The herding families make their living partly from cheese, and the high pastures produce some of the finest in the Balkans: a firm, salty skorup, the rich kajmak clotted cream, and rounds of sheep and cow cheese matured in the cool stone huts. A walker arriving in the afternoon can usually buy a plate of cheese, bread, and cured pršut ham, washed down with sour milk or a glass of homemade rakija, the fruit brandy that lubricates every Montenegrin welcome. This is not commercial mountain catering but the surplus of a working farm, and eating it connects you directly to the centuries-old transhumance that still shapes these mountains each summer.

## The national park and its lakes

Durmitor became a national park in 1952 and a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1980, recognised for its glacial lakes, its limestone karst, and the Tara River canyon that forms its northern boundary. Eighteen glacial lakes lie scattered across the massif, the Black Lake the most visited but the high tarns of Škrka, Zeleni vir, and others rewarding those who walk further. The park protects bears, wolves, and chamois, and a forest of black pine in the Crna Poda reserve includes trees over four hundred years old. Shelter here sits inside a protected landscape, and walkers are expected to camp only at designated spots, carry out waste, and tread lightly on the fragile high karst.

## When to go

The Durmitor walking season runs roughly mid-June to early October, with July and August the most reliable for the high plateau, when the katuns are occupied and the snow has cleared from the passes. Žabljak's altitude keeps summers cool and nights cold even in midsummer — carry warm layers. The karst holds little surface water away from the lakes, so plan refills carefully on the long plateau crossings.
