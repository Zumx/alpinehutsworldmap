In the Alpine safety network there is a category of structure that saves lives quietly and without fanfare: the emergency bivouac room. It has no warden, no bookings system, no dinner service, and often no electricity. It is left permanently unlocked — or locked by a simple combination known to mountain rescue and published widely — and it exists for one purpose: to provide a place where a party caught by darkness, weather, or injury can survive a night in the mountains without the equipment or conditions required for a fully exposed bivouac.

These shelters are not luxuries. In the documented record of alpine mountain rescue, the ability to reach a nearby emergency bivouac rather than attempt a benighted retreat has preserved lives on hundreds of occasions. Understanding where they are, what they contain, and how to use them correctly is part of the basic knowledge of anyone who regularly spends time in the high mountains.

## What a Biwakschachtel Actually Contains

The standard German term for a minimal emergency shelter is Biwakschachtel — literally bivouac box — and it describes the most pared-down version of the category. A Biwakschachtel is typically a prefabricated metal or fibreglass structure holding between six and sixteen people, placed on a rock ledge or ridge at a critical point in the high mountains. The structure is designed to withstand the extreme weather its site will experience: wind loads in excess of 200 km/h, snow loading of several tonnes per square metre, and temperatures dropping below minus 30 degrees Celsius.

The contents of a functional Biwakschachtel include sleeping boards or bunks along the walls (no mattresses in most examples), woollen or mylar emergency blankets (replaced seasonally), a solid-fuel stove or a small propane cooking unit, a basic food cache (biscuits, instant soup, sugar, tea, sometimes tins of fish or meat), a first-aid kit, a logbook for recording entries, and in modern installations, a radio or satellite communication device. Some newer shelters include a small solar panel powering a locator beacon and an interior light.

Water is the resource that is typically not provided. Melting snow from outside the shelter using the stove is the standard approach in winter or early season; in summer, a nearby stream is usually accessible.

## Where Emergency Bivouacs Are Located

The placement logic for emergency bivouacs follows accident history. The DAV and ÖAV have maintained maps of mountain rescue incidents since the early twentieth century, and the locations where parties most frequently encounter difficulty — high saddles that can be obscured by rapid weather deterioration, long ridges with no natural shelter, sections of route between major huts where the walk-time exceeds a single day — are the places where bivouacs have been sited.

In the Austrian Alps, the densest network of Biwakschachteln exists in the Ötztal, the Stubai, and the Tauern ranges — areas with significant glacier terrain where sudden weather can strand parties on high routes well above the safety of valley huts. The Brandenburger Haus at 3,277 metres in the Ötztal Alps is larger than a typical Biwakschachtel (it holds 18 people in emergency conditions) and was converted from a staffed hut to an unmanned emergency shelter when the route through the area fell out of main-season use. It has emergency heating, a radio, and a food cache that is checked and restocked by ÖAV volunteers twice per season.

In the Swiss Alps, SAC Notunterkünfte (emergency accommodations) are often integrated into hut buildings as a separate always-open section distinct from the main staffed hut. The Jungfraujoch High Alpine Research Station at 3,454 metres includes an emergency shelter available to anyone caught in deteriorating conditions near the Jungfrau plateau, accessible through a separately signed entrance that remains open even when the main research station is locked.

## The Italian Bivacco Network

In Italy, the bivacco network managed by the CAI and its sections is extensive — several hundred bivacchi scattered across the entire Alpine chain from the Maritime Alps to the Julian Alps. They range from converted traditional stone structures to modern fibreglass pods delivered by helicopter to sites above 3,500 metres. The Bivacco Baroni on the Adamello plateau, the Bivacco Lanti near the Cervino (Matterhorn) on the Italian approach, and the Bivacco Gervasutti on the Fréboudze glacier near Courmayeur are among the most heavily used.

CAI bivacchi follow a consistent convention: the door is either permanently unlocked or secured with a standard emergency combination (4 digits, published in CAI guidebooks and in the app sezione by sezione). Inside, the same basic provision applies — sleeping boards, emergency blankets, stove, water vessel, logbook. Using the logbook is not legally required but is strongly encouraged by mountain rescue services: a dated entry recording your party's name, size, departure date, and intended route provides a critical starting point for a search if you do not emerge as planned.

## How to Use a Bivouac Correctly

Arriving at an emergency bivouac is by definition not a planned event, but knowing the correct procedure is useful to rehearse mentally. The first action is to secure the shelter against further weather deterioration by closing and latching the door and any shutters. If multiple parties are present, the floor space should be shared without hierarchy — an emergency shelter is not a hotel and no party has a greater claim to a berth than any other.

Fire discipline is critical. A solid-fuel stove in a sealed metal box at altitude produces carbon monoxide that must be vented through the shelter's chimney or exhaust port; using any form of combustion heating with all vents closed is potentially fatal. Open one vent slightly before lighting; ensure there is visible smoke movement through the exhaust before sealing the shelter against wind. If the shelter has no vent or the vent appears blocked, do not use the stove indoors.

Leave the shelter in the condition you found it or better. Replace any emergency blankets you have used if spare stock is present; replace consumed food from your own supplies if you have surplus; and record your party's details, date, and any repairs or deficiencies observed in the logbook. Mountain rescue personnel check these logbooks and repair reports are the primary mechanism by which shelter maintenance is triggered.

## The Emergency Shelter Is Not a Substitute for Preparation

It is worth stating plainly that the existence of emergency bivouacs does not reduce the need for self-sufficiency in the mountains. The shelters are placed at serious, often remote sites and reaching one in a real emergency — in poor visibility, with a party member who is injured or exhausted — requires that you know its location, that the location is findable in the conditions, and that your party can physically reach it. Parties that rely on emergency shelter as a substitute for adequate clothing, navigation equipment, and physical preparation are not safer for the knowledge; they are more likely to need it.

The appropriate framing is: emergency bivouacs are insurance. Like insurance, knowing they exist reduces anxiety and allows more confident decision-making in ambiguous conditions. Also like insurance, the circumstances under which you claim it are not circumstances you want to be in.
