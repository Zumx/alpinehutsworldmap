The Salzburg Alps form a crescent of mountain ranges that arc from the Berchtesgaden massif on the German border in the north to the Hohe Tauern in the south. The geology here is predominantly limestone, producing deep valleys, dramatic karst plateaus, and vertical walls that attract rock climbers and ridge walkers alike. The Dachstein, rising to 2,995 metres, carries the easternmost permanent glacier in the northern Alps, while the Tennengebirge and Hagengebirge offer more remote terrain that sees far fewer visitors than the better-known ranges to the west. The huts selected below serve different corners of this varied alpine world.

## 1. Kärlinger Haus, Berchtesgaden Alps

The Kärlinger Haus at 1,638 metres sits directly above the Funtensee, a small subalpine lake hidden within the Steinernes Meer plateau of the Berchtesgaden Alps. The lake is famous in German meteorological records as the location that has recorded some of the coldest air temperatures ever measured in Germany. The hut is managed by the DAV and reached by a four-hour approach from Königssee via the Salet landing stage, making it one of the more remote overnight stations in the Berchtesgaden region. It serves as a hub on the Steinernes Meer high traverse and the route toward Saalfelden.

## 2. Gippelhütte, Tennengebirge

Perched at around 1,746 metres on the eastern edge of the Tennengebirge plateau above Werfen, the Gippelhütte is a small, privately operated mountain inn providing access to the labyrinthine karst terrain of one of the least-visited large plateau ranges in the Salzburg Alps. The plateau sits at roughly 2,000 metres and is crossed by a network of waymarked routes, but navigation requires care in fog. The Gippelhütte opens in summer from June through September and accommodates around 30 guests.

## 3. Naturfreundehütte Vinzenz Biedner, Hochkönig

This hut, operated by the Naturfreunde organisation at approximately 1,800 metres on the Hochkönig massif west of Werfen, serves both summer hikers on the Hochkönig Panoramaweg circuit and ski tourers who tackle the broad slopes of the Übergossene Alm — the glaciated summit plateau — in spring. The Vinzenz Biedner hut is a good alternative to the more crowded Matrashaus, which sits directly beneath the Hochkönig summit at 2,941 metres, and provides a quieter introduction to the massif.

## 4. Hochberghaus, Dachstein region

The Hochberghaus stands at approximately 1,340 metres above Rußbach am Pass Gschütt on the northern approaches to the Gosaukamm ridge, a spectacular jagged limestone crest that connects the Gosau lakes area to the Dachstein massif. The hut is accessible in under two hours from the valley and serves as a base for the Gosaukamm traverse, a demanding route across a series of sharp limestone towers that requires sure-footedness and some scrambling experience. The surrounding area is part of the UNESCO Salzkammergut World Heritage Site.

## 5. Techendorfer Alm, Flachau region

The Techendorfer Alm operates at around 1,450 metres in the rolling Radstädter Tauern hills south of Flachau, straddling the boundary between the Salzburg and Styrian Alps. Unlike the dramatic karst massifs further north, the terrain here is rounder, greener, and more reminiscent of the Niedere Tauern style: long ridges with good views and gentle gradients that attract families and trail runners. The alm is a working dairy operation as well as an overnight stop, and guests in summer often arrive on the Tauern Bike Trail that crosses the pass.

## 6. Almgasthof Buchacker, Lungau

Sitting at roughly 1,550 metres in the Lungau, the high plateau region in the south of Salzburg province, the Almgasthof Buchacker represents the tradition of Austrian mountain farming combined with guest accommodation. The Lungau is the driest and sunniest part of Salzburg province, lying in the rain shadow of the Radstädter Tauern, and the surrounding landscape is characterised by broad meadows, scattered farmsteads, and long ridge walks toward the Schladminger Tauern and Nockberge ranges.

## 7. Wildeben, Hagengebirge

The Wildeben shelter on the Hagengebirge plateau, west of Bad Reichenhall and north of Berchtesgaden, is one of the least-visited overnight options in the Salzburg border region. The Hagengebirge — a broad, flat limestone plateau above 1,800 metres — forms a natural barrier between Germany and Austria that is crossed by only a handful of marked routes. The Wildeben is a simple, unstaffed shelter, but its position on the plateau makes it a genuine wilderness stop for experienced hikers comfortable with route-finding in featureless karst terrain.

## 8. Seducker Hochalm, Lungau

The Seducker Hochalm operates at around 1,700 metres in the Seetal Alps south of Tamsweg in the Lungau, providing access to the ridge system connecting the Seekareck and Greilkopf summits. The alm is open in summer and doubles as a working pasture, with cattle grazing the surrounding meadows from June to September. Routes from here connect into the broader Salzburger Lungau Almenweg, a waymarked trail that links a series of high alms across the entire plateau.

## 9. Sistranser Alm, Inn Valley

Though the village of Sistrans lies within the Tyrolean municipality east of Innsbruck, the Sistranser Alm serves trails that cross into the Salzburg border region and provides views down the entire Inn Valley corridor. At around 1,500 metres, the alm is reachable from Sistrans in about 45 minutes and connects to the Patscherkofel ridge system that forms the backdrop to Innsbruck. It is a good introduction to alm culture for visitors staying in the city who want a short but genuine mountain experience.

## 10. Peeralm, Pinzgau

The Peeralm at approximately 1,600 metres sits in the Pinzgau valley system south of Zell am See, on the northern flanks of the Hohe Tauern. The alm is accessed from the Kaprun or Zell am See area and lies within the corridor of paths connecting the Kitzsteinhorn ski area with the lower sections of the Hohe Tauern National Park. In summer, it provides a mid-level stopping point on routes that eventually climb toward the Kapruner Törl and the high terrain of the Glockner group.

## Plan Your Salzburg Alps Traverse

The Salzburg Alps offer some of the most varied hut-to-hut hiking in the eastern Alps, from the remote plateau solitude of the Tennengebirge to the well-trodden Dachstein circuit. Distances between huts vary considerably, and some plateau crossings require navigational confidence in poor weather.
