The Valais contains the densest concentration of 4,000-metre peaks in the Alps — more than 40 summits exceeding that threshold, including the Matterhorn, Monte Rosa, Dom, and Dufourspitze. The SAC hut network built to serve them is correspondingly ambitious, placing staffed overnight stations at altitudes that would seem extraordinary in almost any other mountain range. From the Mischabelhütte above 3,300 metres to the Cabane de Bertol perched on its rocky crest at 3,311 metres, these huts represent the upper limit of what a walking approach can reach on a single day's effort. The ten below trace a circuit through the great valleys on both the Swiss and Italian sides of the main watershed.

## 1. Mischabelhütte, Saas Valley

The Mischabelhütte at 3,340 metres stands on a narrow rocky ridge above the Hohbalm glacier, directly below the Dom (4,545 metres) and the Täschhorn (4,491 metres) — the two highest summits of the Mischabel group. The approach from Saas-Grund takes around five hours and involves a final section over steep, sometimes icy terrain. The hut sleeps about 120 guests when combined with its winter room and is one of the most important bases in the Swiss Alps for parties attempting the Dom, which has the greatest height difference between summit and valley base of any mountain entirely within Switzerland.

## 2. Cabane de Bertol CAS, Val d'Hérens

Few SAC huts have a more dramatic setting than the Cabane de Bertol, positioned at 3,311 metres on a narrow summit of rock above the Glacier du Mont Miné in the Val d'Hérens. The final approach involves a fixed-rope ladder of some 30 metres that climbers and hikers must ascend to reach the entrance — one of the most memorable arrivals in the Alps. The hut sleeps around 80 and serves as a key waypoint on the Haute Route between Chamonix and Zermatt, linking the Dix hut to the east and Les Vignettes to the west.

## 3. Cabane de Valsorey, Grand Combin

At 3,030 metres on the southern approaches to the Grand Combin massif (4,314 metres), the Cabane de Valsorey above Bourg-Saint-Pierre in the Val de Bagnes is the main base for routes on the Combin de Valsorey and the Grand Combin de Grafeneire. The approach from Bourg-Saint-Pierre is around four to five hours and climbs through the remote Valsorey valley to the hut perched on a moraine above the glacier. It sleeps around 100 guests and is staffed from late June through September.

## 4. Cabane du Vélan, Val Ferret

The Cabane du Vélan at 2,642 metres sits above the Swiss Val Ferret near Bourg-Saint-Pierre, well below the glaciated terrain of the Grand Combin but providing a comfortable and accessible overnight base for the standard approach route to the Vélan (3,731 metres). The hut is a pleasant, moderately sized facility with space for around 60 guests and serves as a less-crowded alternative to the busier huts on the Mont Blanc side of the valley.

## 5. Cabane FXB Panossière, Val de Bagnes

Named after the François-Xavier Bagnoud Foundation, the Cabane Panossière at 2,641 metres in the Val de Bagnes above Verbier is the primary approach hut for the Combin de Corbassière and the northern reaches of the Grand Combin massif. The ascent from Fionnay in the lower Val de Bagnes is around three hours. The hut sleeps approximately 90 guests and is a popular stop on the Haute Route Pédestre as well as a base for ski touring in March and April.

## 6. Cabane de Saleinaz CAS, Val d'Arpette

The Cabane de Saleinaz at 2,691 metres above Champex in the Arpette valley provides access to the Saleinaz glacier and routes toward the Trient plateau — a critical section of both the summer and winter Haute Route. The hut is part of the central SAC network and sleeps around 65. The approach from Champex or Praz-de-Fort takes three to four hours and offers exceptional views across to the Aiguilles Dorées and the Swiss flanks of the Mont Blanc massif.

## 7. Cabane Mont Fort, Verbier

The Cabane Mont Fort at 2,457 metres is one of the most accessible SAC huts in the Valais, reachable in around three hours from Verbier or via the cable car to La Chaux followed by a short descent. The Mont Fort summit above at 3,328 metres gives a panorama encompassing the Grand Combin, the Matterhorn, the Dents du Midi, and the Mont Blanc massif. The hut is frequently used as an introduction to overnight stays in the Swiss Alps for visitors based in Verbier.

## 8. Cabane de Mille, Val de Bagnes

Sitting at 2,473 metres on the southern flank of the Bagnes valley above Le Châble, the Cabane de Mille is a smaller facility with space for around 50 guests, positioned on a spur overlooking both the main valley and the Grand Combin to the north. The hut is part of the Mont Blanc to Matterhorn trail network and offers an alternative overnight to the better-known Verbier circuit.

## 9. Cabane Brunet, Fully

The Cabane Brunet at 2,103 metres lies in the remote Fully area west of Martigny, in the hills above the Rhône valley that form the western extremity of the Valais Alps. The terrain here is far less glaciated than the central Valais and the approach through the Forêt de Fully — a nature reserve protecting some of the most diverse flora in Switzerland — is botanically rich, with orchids and rare grasses on the south-facing limestone terraces. The hut sleeps around 50 and is open in summer.

## 10. Rifugio Ottorino Mezzalama, Ayas Valley

The Mezzalama at 3,036 metres on the Italian side of the Monte Rosa massif, above the Ayas valley in the Aosta region, is named after Ottorino Mezzalama, who led the first ski traverse of the Alps in 1929. It serves as a key overnight on the famous Mezzalama Ski Race route and is positioned on the southern approach to the Colle delle Cime Bianche. The hut sleeps around 80 and is reached by a four-hour approach from Champoluc. The ski descent from the hut through the upper Ayas cirque in spring is one of the finest in the western Alps.

## Plan Your Valais Hut Circuit

The Valais hut network is extraordinary in its ambition and its difficulty — approaches are serious, altitude is high, and weather can deteriorate rapidly. Many of the huts require crampons and ice axe skills even in summer. Before you go, check current conditions on the SAC website and examine your planned route in detail.
