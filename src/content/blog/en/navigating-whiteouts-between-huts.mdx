There is a particular kind of fear that arrives when the cloud comes down on a glacier and the world dissolves into uniform white. Sky and snow merge into a single luminous blank, your own feet seem to float, and the hut you left an hour ago and the one you are heading for both vanish as if they never existed. This is whiteout, and on the high traverses that link alpine huts it is one of the most serious situations a competent party can face. Getting from one refuge to the next in it is a skill built on preparation, instruments and discipline, not luck.

## What a Whiteout Actually Does

A whiteout is the loss of all visual reference. In thick cloud or falling snow over a snowfield, there is no horizon, no shadow, and no contrast to tell up from down or near from far. The brain, deprived of cues, invents them, and the invented version is usually wrong. Walkers in whiteout routinely veer in a slow curve without noticing, mistake a gentle rise for a cliff edge, or step off a cornice they never saw.

The danger is compounded on glaciated terrain, where crevasses hide under snow bridges and the safe line between two huts may be only metres wide. A whiteout does not merely make navigation hard; it removes the feedback that normally corrects a small error before it becomes a fatal one. Respecting that is the first survival skill, and it begins with the willingness to stay put or turn back when the option exists.

## The Compass Is Not Optional

In a true whiteout the compass becomes your primary instrument, and using it well demands practice before you need it. The method is to take an accurate bearing from the map between known points — hut to col, col to hut — and then follow it precisely, trusting the needle over every instinct that insists you are drifting. Because a person cannot walk a straight line by feel alone, the team leapfrogs: one member walks ahead to the limit of visibility, the other lines them up on the bearing and waves them left or right until they are exactly on line.

This is slow and demands total discipline. The temptation to abandon the bearing because it feels wrong is constant and must be resisted, since the feeling is the very error the compass exists to defeat. A small adjustable correction for magnetic declination, set before the trip, keeps the bearing honest. Practising this on an easy day in fog, long before a real glacier whiteout, is what separates a controlled crossing from a guess.

## Wands, Cairns and the Marked Way

On heavily travelled glacier routes the mountains themselves help, if you know what to look for. Many classic hut-to-hut glacier crossings are marked in season with wands — thin bamboo poles topped with flagging tape — placed by guides and wardens to mark the safe line through crevassed ground. In whiteout these wands become a lifeline, each one just visible from the last, and losing the line between two of them is a moment to stop rather than wander.

Lower down, painted waymarks, cairns and the worn track of previous parties serve the same purpose, though all can vanish under fresh snow. The prudent navigator treats marked routes as an aid, not a guarantee: wands fall over, cairns get buried, and tracks fill in within minutes of a blizzard. The marks supplement the compass and the map; they never replace them, and a party that relies on footprints alone is one snowfall from being lost.

## Handrails, Catching Features and Aiming Off

Good navigators do not just point at a target; they build a route from features that catch them when they err. A handrail is a linear feature you can follow — a moraine ridge, a rock band, the foot of a slope, a stream line — that keeps you on track without precise bearings. A catching feature is something unmistakable beyond your target, like a valley wall or a steep drop, that tells you if you have gone too far.

Aiming off is the subtlest of these. Rather than steer straight for a hut that you might miss to either side in poor visibility, you deliberately aim to one known side of it, so that when you reach a handrail you know with certainty which way to turn. Arriving at a stream and knowing the hut lies upstream is far safer than arriving and not knowing whether to go left or right. These classic techniques, taught by every mountaineering club, turn navigation from a gamble into a sequence of certainties.

## The GPS Question

Modern GPS and phone mapping have transformed whiteout navigation, and pretending otherwise is foolish. A waypoint set on the next hut, or a recorded track of the route, lets a device show your position and heading even when the eyes give nothing. For many parties this is now the first line of navigation, and it has prevented countless epics. Used well, it confirms the compass work and removes much of the guesswork.

But electronics fail in the cold, batteries die, and screens crack, exactly when you most need them. The disciplined party carries GPS and the map-and-compass skills to continue without it, treating the device as a powerful aid rather than a sole dependency. Spare batteries kept warm against the body, a backup device, and the habit of cross-checking the screen against a bearing are what keep technology a help rather than a trap.

## Knowing When Not to Go

The most important whiteout skill is the judgement that prevents the whiteout crossing altogether. A hut warden's forecast, a deteriorating sky, and the simple question of whether the next leg crosses crevassed or corniced ground should all feed a calm decision about whether to set out. Many alpine accidents begin with a party leaving a perfectly safe hut into worsening weather because they had a schedule to keep.

There is no shame in a rest day, in retreating to the hut you just left, or in waiting out a front that will clear by afternoon. Wardens see this constantly and respect it; the experienced parties are the ones who stay. The mountains reward patience, and the next hut will still be there tomorrow, which is more than can be said for the party that pushes into a whiteout it did not have the tools to survive.
