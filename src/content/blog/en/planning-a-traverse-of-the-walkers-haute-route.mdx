The Walker's Haute Route runs roughly 200 kilometres from Chamonix beneath Mont Blanc to Zermatt beneath the Matterhorn, climbing and descending some 14,000 metres of cumulative ascent along the way. Unlike the glaciated ski Haute Route that shares its name, this is a summer walking line, but it is no gentle trek: it crosses eleven or more high passes, brushes the foot of great glaciers, and threads a chain of mountain huts and village inns that demand to be booked, sequenced and understood long before you set off. Get the logistics right and it is one of the finest two weeks on foot in Europe.

## The shape of the route

Most walkers take twelve to fourteen days, starting in Chamonix at around 1,000 metres and finishing in Zermatt at 1,600 metres, with the highest point on the standard variant at the Col de Prafleuri or the Augstbordpass, both above 2,800 metres. The line runs west to east through three regions: the Mont Blanc massif, the Valais valleys of Switzerland, and the high country beneath the Pennine Alps that ends at the Matterhorn. The walk passes through or near Argentière, Trient, Champex-Lac, Le Châble, Verbier, Arolla, Les Haudères, Zinal, Gruben and St Niklaus before the final climb to Zermatt.

The accommodation alternates in character. Some nights are spent in proper alpine huts run by the Swiss Alpine Club; others in village hotels, dortoirs and gîtes. This mix is the route's defining logistical feature, because the two types are booked differently and fill at different rates.

## The first stages: Chamonix to the Swiss border

From Chamonix the classic opening crosses to Argentière and over the Col de Balme at 2,191 metres into Switzerland, descending to Trient. An alternative high variant takes the Fenêtre d'Arpette at 2,665 metres, a steeper and more spectacular crossing past the Trient Glacier that should only be attempted in good conditions and clear of snow. Either way the early nights are typically in valley accommodation — the Auberge in Trient, then Champex-Lac, a pretty lake village with hotels and a dortoir. These first stages are about building rhythm and altitude tolerance before the route turns serious.

Booking here is straightforward: village hotels and gîtes take reservations months ahead and rarely fill completely, though the popular spots in July and August warrant early enquiry.

## The middle: the Grand Combin country and the high cols

The heart of the route lies in the Valais. From Le Châble a long climb leads toward the Grand Combin massif and one of the trip's logistical decisions: the higher, wilder Cabane de Louvie and Cabane de Prafleuri line, or a lower route through Verbier. The Cabane de Prafleuri at 2,624 metres is a true SAC-style hut in remote terrain, and from it the route crosses the Col de Prafleuri and the dramatic dam and reservoir of the Lac des Dix.

This stretch reaches the Cabane des Dix or, more commonly for walkers, continues to Arolla via the Col de Riedmatten or the adjacent Pas de Chèvres, where iron ladders bolted to the rock ease the final drop. Arolla at 2,000 metres is a key resupply and rest point. These high huts are the ones that fill fastest, often weeks ahead in peak season, and they are the reason the whole itinerary must be locked in early: a single fully booked hut can force a rerouting of several days.

## The final third: Arolla to Zermatt

From Arolla the route descends to Les Haudères and Évolène, then climbs again over the Col de Torrent at 2,916 metres or the Col du Tsaté toward the Moiry valley, where the Cabane de Moiry sits spectacularly above its glacier. The line then drops to Zinal in the Val d'Anniviers, a classic mountain village, before the long haul over the Forcletta or the Meidpass to the German-speaking Turtmanntal and the hamlet of Gruben.

The penultimate crossing is the Augstbordpass at 2,894 metres, leading down to St Niklaus or Gasenried in the Mattertal. The final day traverses the high balcony path with its first head-on views of the Matterhorn, the Weisshorn and the Dom, ending in Zermatt. Saving the Matterhorn for last is the route's masterstroke; the mountain reveals itself only in the final hours.

## Practical logistics that make or break the trip

Book the high huts first, in the order you will reach them, and only then fill in the village nights around them. Most SAC huts take online or phone reservations and require confirmation; many ask for a deposit and most expect to be paid in Swiss francs, so carry cash, as card payment is unreliable in remote refuges. Half board is the norm in huts and strongly recommended, since carrying two weeks of food is neither practical nor necessary. A sleeping-bag liner is mandatory in the huts, and an SAC or reciprocal alpine club membership earns the member discount on every hut night.

Resupply points at Champex, Le Châble, Arolla and Zinal allow you to buy snacks and replace worn kit. Several stages can be shortened or escaped via valley buses and the postbus network if weather closes a pass or a body needs a rest day, so build at least one buffer day into a two-week plan.

## Season, fitness and conditions

The walking season runs from roughly late June to mid-September, with the high cols often holding snow into early July and the huts closing from mid to late September. July and August offer the most reliable weather but the busiest huts; early September can be glorious and quieter, with a real risk of an early snowfall on the passes. The route demands genuine hill fitness — repeated 1,000-metre-plus climbs day after day — and sure-footedness on steep, rocky and occasionally exposed ground, including the ladders near the Pas de Chèvres. It is not technical mountaineering, but it is sustained, serious walking at altitude, and it rewards those who arrive trained and have done their logistical homework.
