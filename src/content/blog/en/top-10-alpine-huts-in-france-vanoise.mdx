The Vanoise, France's first national park, fills the great mountain block between the Tarentaise and the Maurienne, a country of glaciers, marmot-filled meadows and the highest reintroduced ibex population in the Alps. Its summits gather around the Grande Casse (3855 m), the highest peak wholly within the park, and the long ice of the Glaciers de la Vanoise that gives the classic circuit its name. The refuges here, mostly run by the Fédération Française des Clubs Alpins et de Montagne, range from busy crossroads huts on the GR55 to remote glacier bases, and together they make the Vanoise one of the finest hut-to-hut regions in Europe.

## The GR55 and the Tour des Glaciers

Two great walking lines structure the Vanoise. The GR55 cuts north to south straight through the heart of the park, crossing the Col de la Vanoise beneath the Grande Casse, while the Tour des Glaciers de la Vanoise loops around the central icefields in a four- to six-day circuit. Both string together a sequence of refuges spaced a comfortable day apart, so walkers can move from hut to hut carrying only a light pack. The trailheads at Pralognan-la-Vanoise, Termignon and Val d'Isère feed walkers into a network that has been refined over decades into one of the most reliable trekking circuits in the French Alps.

## Refuge du Col de la Vanoise

The Refuge du Col de la Vanoise, also called the Refuge Félix Faure, stands at 2516 metres on the pass of the same name, directly beneath the north face of the Grande Casse and beside the small glacial lakes of the col. It is the busiest and most famous hut in the park, a great crossroads where the GR55 and the Tour des Glaciers meet, and a base for the voie normale on the Grande Casse. From Pralognan the climb takes around three hours, and the hut's terrace looks straight across to the Glacier de la Grande Casse.

## Refuge de l'Arpont

The Refuge de l'Arpont at 2309 metres sits on the long traverse above the Maurienne, on the Tour des Glaciers, with a celebrated view across the valley to the Dent Parrachée and the line of hanging glaciers on the eastern flank of the massif. Rebuilt in modern eco-conscious form, it is one of the most comfortable huts in the park, and its position on a shelf of alpine pasture makes it a classic spot to watch the evening light move across the ice. Marmots are everywhere on the approach, and ibex graze the slopes above.

## Refuge de la Femma

The Refuge de la Femma at 2367 metres lies deep in the wild Vallon de la Rocheure, one of the quietest corners of the Vanoise, on a variant of the great circuit. It is a haven for wildlife, with ibex and chamois often visible from the door and bearded vultures patrolling the cliffs, and the surrounding meadows are among the richest for alpine flowers in the park. The remoteness that keeps the crowds away is precisely its appeal, and the long valley approach is part of the experience.

## Refuge du Plan du Lac

The Refuge du Plan du Lac at 2364 metres above Termignon enjoys one of the great panoramas of the Vanoise, a full sweep across the Maurienne to the Dent Parrachée and the central glaciers. Reached by a short walk from a high road, it is an accessible hut popular with families and with photographers chasing the alpenglow, yet it sits squarely on the Tour des Glaciers and serves as a fine first night for walkers starting the circuit from the Maurienne side.

## Refuge de l'Aiguille Doran and the Col du Palet

The Refuge du Col du Palet at 2587 metres lies on the GR55 north of the Col de la Vanoise, on the high crossing toward Val d'Isère and Tignes. Set among bleak, beautiful high pastures beneath the Pointe du Grand Pré, it is a typical GR55 staging hut, simple and well placed for the long traverse. From here the route drops toward the Lac de Tignes, linking the heart of the park to the great ski valleys of the Tarentaise.

## Refuge de la Leisse

The Refuge de la Leisse at 2487 metres stands in the open Vallon de la Leisse on the GR55 between the Col de la Vanoise and the Col de la Leisse. It is a wild, exposed hut in a broad glacial valley where ibex and marmots are constant company, and it serves as a key link in the north-south traverse. The setting, ringed by the Pointe de la Sana and the surrounding peaks, gives a strong sense of the scale of the Vanoise high country.

## Refuge de la Valette

The Refuge de la Valette at 2590 metres clings to a balcony above Pralognan, on the Tour des Glaciers, with a direct view onto the Glaciers de la Vanoise and the séracs spilling from the Grande Casse and the Dôme de Chasseforêt. The steep approach from the valley is rewarded by one of the most spectacular glacier outlooks in the park, and the hut is a favourite second night on the circuit, perfectly placed to watch the icefields catch the morning sun.

## Refuge de l'Aiguille Grive and the high glacier camps

Above the walking refuges, the Vanoise holds true mountaineering bases. The Refuge du Fond d'Aussois at 2324 metres and the small huts beneath the Dôme de Chasseforêt serve roped parties heading onto the Glaciers de la Vanoise, the gentle but crevassed icefield that lends itself to a first glacier traverse. The Dôme de Chasseforêt (3586 m) and the Pointe de l'Échelle are popular ski-mountaineering and summer glacier objectives, and the high camps fill in spring with skiers crossing the great plateau of ice that gives the whole region its name.

## Wildlife, the ibex and the national park

The Vanoise was created in 1963 as France's first national park, and its founding purpose was the protection of the Alpine ibex, then reduced to a tiny remnant on the Italian side of the frontier in the Gran Paradiso. Today the two parks share a vast protected zone across the border, and the Vanoise holds one of the largest ibex populations in the Alps; the animals are often seen at close range near the high huts, unbothered by walkers. Chamois, marmots and the reintroduced bearded vulture share the high country, and the meadows below the refuges blaze with gentian, anemone and the rare alpine flora the park protects. Strict rules apply throughout: dogs are forbidden, bivouac is limited, and the flowers may not be picked. This pastoral and wild character, with summer flocks still grazing the lower alpages and the cheeses of Beaufort and Tarentaise on the hut tables, gives the Vanoise a richness beyond its glaciers.

## Practical notes for the Vanoise huts

The Vanoise refuges are staffed mainly from mid-June to mid-September, with the high glacier season for skiers running through spring. Most walking huts are reached in two to four hours, and the GR55 and the Tour des Glaciers can be combined into circuits of four to seven days. Reservation through the Club Alpin or the park's booking system is essential in high summer, when the Col de la Vanoise hut in particular fills quickly. Glacier routes from the high camps require rope, crampons and ice axe, while the main circuits are demanding but non-technical walks. Pralognan-la-Vanoise, Termignon and Modane are the principal gateways.
