Graubünden is the largest canton in Switzerland and the emptiest, a maze of more than a hundred and fifty valleys where Romansh is still spoken alongside German and Italian. This is the country of the Swiss National Park, the only national park in the nation, and of the Bernina massif, the easternmost four-thousanders in the Alps. The huts here lie farther from roads and lifts than almost anywhere else in the country, demanding longer approaches and rewarding them with a wilder, quieter mountain experience. The names on the doors carry the music of three languages, sometimes all in one valley.

## Why Graubunden huts feel different

The huts of Graubünden serve a more dispersed, less crowded mountain world than the Valais or the Oberland. Many sit at the head of long side valleys reached by Postbus and a half-day walk, far from any cable car. The Rhaetian Railway, a UNESCO World Heritage line, threads the canton and delivers walkers to trailheads at Pontresina, Bergün and the Albula and Bernina passes. The result is a hut network that feels exploratory: you earn your bed with your legs, and the huts reward you with solitude that the busier western ranges can no longer offer.

## Tschiervahütte

The Tschiervahütte at 2584 metres sits in the heart of the Bernina massif above Pontresina, looking up at the Biancograt, the slender ice arête of Piz Bernina (4049 m) that is one of the most beautiful lines in the Alps. The hut is the classic base for the Bernina, the only four-thousander in the Eastern Alps, and the approach up the Tschierva glacier moraine is a magnificent walk in its own right, framed by Piz Roseg and Piz Morteratsch.

## Bovalhütte

The Bovalhütte at 2495 metres overlooks the Morteratsch glacier, the largest in the Bernina range, reached by an easy walk from the Morteratsch railway station up the glacier valley. Markers along the path show the dramatic retreat of the ice over the past century, making the approach a lesson in climate change. The hut serves Piz Palü (3900 m) and Piz Bernina from the eastern side and is one of the most popular in the canton.

## Coazhütte

The Coazhütte at 2610 metres lies in a wild amphitheatre at the head of the Roseg valley, ringed by the glaciers of Piz Roseg, Piz Glüschaint and the Sella peaks. The approach from Pontresina up the Val Roseg, often done by horse-drawn carriage in summer, is one of the loveliest valley walks in Switzerland. The hut, with its rounded modern form, sits amid a landscape of ice and rock that feels genuinely remote despite its accessibility.

## Chamanna Jenatsch

The Chamanna Jenatsch at 2652 metres stands in the Val Bever in the central Albula Alps, on a high traverse route between the Engadine and the Julier pass. Romansh in name like so many Graubünden huts, it serves a quieter range of grass-and-scree peaks far from the glamour of the Bernina. It is a key stage on long traverses of the canton and a base for Piz d'Err and the surrounding three-thousanders.

## Kesch-Hütte

The Kesch-Hütte at 2625 metres beneath Piz Kesch (3418 m) is one of the busiest huts in central Graubünden, a hub on the famous ski tour from Bergün to the Engadine and on summer treks across the Albula Alps. The modern hut, rebuilt to a high environmental standard, sits in open alpine terrain with the glaciated north face of Piz Kesch rising directly above, a fine objective for both climbers and ski-tourers.

## Chamanna Tuoi

The Chamanna Tuoi at 2250 metres lies at the head of the Val Tuoi above Guarda, the postcard Engadine village of the Schellen-Ursli story. It is the base for Piz Buin (3312 m), the peak that gave its name to the sun cream and the highest in the Silvretta range on the Swiss side. The walk up from Guarda through larch woods and high pasture is gentle and beautiful, making this one of the most approachable hut hikes in the lower Engadine.

## Lischanahütte

The Lischanahütte at 2500 metres sits high above Scuol in the Lower Engadine, beneath the dolomitic walls of Piz Lischana and Piz San Jon. This corner of Graubünden, close to the Italian and Austrian borders, sees few foreign visitors, and the hut offers a genuinely off-the-beaten-track experience among pale limestone peaks more reminiscent of the Dolomites than the granite Bernina.

## Chamanna Cluozza

The Chamanna Cluozza at 1882 metres is the only hut inside the Swiss National Park, set deep in a forested valley where wildlife is strictly protected and you are likely to see red deer, ibex and bearded vultures. The park's rules are absolute: no leaving the marked paths, no fires, no picking anything. The hut is a rare chance to sleep within a fully protected wilderness and a model of low-impact mountain hospitality.

## Hut-to-hut tours across the canton

Graubünden rewards those who string its refuges into longer journeys, and several classic multi-day tours cross the canton. The Bernina high route links the Tschiervahütte, the Coazhütte and the Bovalhütte beneath the four-thousander, a glaciated tour for experienced parties. The Senda Sursilvana and the Via Engiadina offer gentler valley-balcony walking through larch forest and Romansh villages such as Sent, Ftan and Guarda, sleeping in inns and the lower huts. In winter the canton becomes one of the great ski-touring arenas of the Alps: the route from Bergün over the Kesch-Hütte to the Engadine is a rite of passage, and the Silvretta traverse from the Jamtalhütte and Wiesbadener Hütte on the Austrian side crosses into Swiss territory around Piz Buin. The Walser communities who settled these high valleys in the Middle Ages left a legacy of timber architecture and place names, and many huts stand on or near the old droving and trading routes that once crossed the passes. Walking these tours, you sense a mountain culture that has been continuously inhabited for centuries, quite different from the purely sporting world of the western Alps, and the food in the huts reflects it, with capuns and pizzoccheri appearing on menus alongside the usual rösti.

## Practical notes for the Graubunden huts

Graubünden huts staff mainly from late June to mid-September, with some Bernina huts opening earlier for spring ski-touring. Approaches are generally longer than in the western Alps, so plan for full days and study the Postbus and Rhaetian Railway timetables carefully, as they are the lifeline of the region. The Bernina huts require glacier skills for the peaks but not always for the approach. Reservation through the SAC system is advised, and a little Romansh courtesy, a greeting of allegra, goes a long way in the eastern valleys.
