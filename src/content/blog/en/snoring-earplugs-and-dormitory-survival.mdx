Lights out is at ten in most Alpine huts, and within twenty minutes the matratzenlager has become a soundscape. Somewhere down the row a man who walked nine hours from the valley is producing a rasping, two-stroke snore. Two bunks over, a zip is being opened with surgical slowness, as if quiet effort cancelled the noise. The first night in a shared dormitory is a rite of passage that almost nobody warns you about, and the difference between a wrecked night and a workable one comes down to a handful of small, learnable habits.

## What a matratzenlager actually is

The German word matratzenlager literally means "mattress camp," and that is exactly what you get: a long, low platform, sometimes a single continuous mattress and sometimes a row of thin pads, where eight, twenty or forty people sleep side by side under shared blankets. You will meet it under different names across the Alps. In Italy it is the camerata, in France the dortoir, in Switzerland the same Matratzenlager. The classic CAI, DAV and SAC huts almost all run on this model, because cramming bodies efficiently is the only way a building perched at 2,500 metres can shelter everyone who shows up. Private rooms exist in valley-edge huts and a few modernised refuges, but at altitude the dormitory is the default, and reserving early rarely changes that.

You are issued a wool blanket or a duvet and a pillow. You are not issued sheets. Almost every hut association in the Alps now requires a personal liner — a Hüttenschlafsack or sleeping-bag liner — for hygiene, and many will refuse the blanket if you turn up without one. A silk liner weighs around a hundred grams and packs to the size of an apple; a cotton one is cheaper and warmer but bulkier. This is the single most important piece of kit nobody mentions in the brochures.

## The earplug question

Earplugs are not optional, and not all earplugs are equal. The cheap foam cylinders sold at pharmacies give a nominal noise reduction rating of around 30 decibels, which sounds enormous until you remember that a heavy snorer peaks at 80 to 90 decibels, well into the range of a passing lorry. Foam works, but only if you roll it tight and hold it in place while it re-expands; jammed in carelessly it falls out by two in the morning.

Wax earplugs mould to the ear canal and seal better for many people, at the cost of being fiddly in cold fingers. Reusable silicone flanged plugs are durable and easy to clean but rarely match foam for raw attenuation. The pragmatic answer is to carry two pairs of good foam plugs — one to use, one for when the first goes missing in the blanket — and to consider them as essential as your headlamp. Some experienced hut-goers add a thin beanie pulled down over the ears, which both muffles sound and keeps the head warm in an unheated room.

## Choosing your bunk

If you arrive early enough to choose, position matters more than comfort. A spot at the end of a row gives you one fewer neighbour and a wall to turn toward. Avoid the bunk directly beside the door: it catches every late arrival, every midnight trip to the toilet, and the cold draught each time the door opens. Lower bunks save you a ladder descent at three in the morning but sit in the path of foot traffic; upper bunks are quieter but hotter, since heat rises into a roof space that may already be stuffy.

The genuinely strategic move is to identify the snorers before lights-out. People are remarkably honest about it at dinner if you ask lightly, and a heavy snorer will often volunteer to take an end spot out of courtesy. Putting two bunk-lengths between yourself and the loudest sleeper does more for your night than any earplug.

## The unwritten etiquette

Hut dormitory culture runs on a few shared rules that newcomers learn fast. Pack your rucksack before lights-out, never during the night; the sound of nylon and buckles at five in the morning is the cardinal sin of the dortoir. If you must leave early for an alpine start, lay everything out the evening before and carry it to the boot room to dress. Use the red light or a dimmed setting on your headlamp, never the full beam, which sweeps across twenty faces like a searchlight.

Phones go on silent and, ideally, stay in the pack; a glowing screen at midnight is as disruptive as noise. Keep conversation to a whisper after lights-out, and accept that the person below you can hear everything. Most of all, remember that everyone in the room is tired, and that tomorrow you may be the one snoring. The tolerance you extend tends to come back to you.

## Managing your own sleep

Altitude itself works against deep sleep. Above roughly 2,500 metres many people experience periodic breathing, where respiration speeds, slows and briefly pauses in cycles through the night, sometimes waking you with a gasp. This is normal and not dangerous in a healthy acclimatised person, but it fragments rest. Going easy on the dinner wine helps, since alcohol both worsens periodic breathing and relaxes the throat muscles that cause snoring in the first place. Hydrate through the afternoon rather than gulping water at bedtime, which only guarantees a cold trip to the toilet.

Keep your body temperature stable. Huts are often unheated overnight, and a thin liner under a shared duvet can run cold; sleeping in a base layer and dry socks makes a real difference. If you run hot under the heavy hut blankets instead, peel down to the liner alone. A small inflatable or foam pillow gives better neck support than the lumpy communal one, and an eye mask shuts out the inevitable headlamp.

## When you simply cannot sleep

Some nights nothing works, and that is fine. A wakeful night before a summit is rarely the disaster it feels like; you can climb perfectly well on broken rest, and the adrenaline of an alpine start papers over a lot. Lying still with your eyes closed delivers most of the physical recovery of true sleep, so resist the urge to fight it. Slow, deliberate breathing settles both your nervous system and, conveniently, the rhythm that triggers snoring.

If you are chronically unable to share a room, plan around it: book the rare huts with small bedrooms, choose quieter mid-week nights, or build in a valley hotel between long hut stretches to reset. But for most walkers the dormitory becomes, surprisingly quickly, part of the pleasure — the shared groan when the snoring starts, the whispered solidarity, the dawn when forty strangers shoulder their packs and head into the same cold blue.
