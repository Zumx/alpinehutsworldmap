The network of staffed alpine huts that now stretches from the Sierra Nevada to the Norwegian Jotunheimen did not grow organically from local custom. It was deliberately planned, financed, and built by a cluster of voluntary associations founded within a single decade in the 1860s, each responding to the explosion of mountaineering activity that followed the first ascent of the Matterhorn in 1865. Understanding those organisations — their rivalries, their overlapping territorial ambitions, and their eventual cooperation — explains why the hut system exists in its current form and why it remains fundamentally a membership-based public service rather than a commercial hospitality network.

## The First Generation — SAC and CAI

The Swiss Alpine Club (SAC, Schweizer Alpen-Club) was founded in Olten on 19 April 1863, the oldest of the major Alpine clubs by a margin of two years. Its founding document spoke explicitly of building permanent shelters at useful points in the mountains, and the first SAC hut — a simple stone shelter on the Faulhorn above Grindelwald — was taken over from its previous owner within the first year. By 1870 the SAC had a dozen structures across the Bernese, Glarus, and Graubünden Alps.

The Club Alpino Italiano (CAI) was founded in Turin in 1863 by Quintino Sella, a politician and mineralogist who had made the first ascent of Monte Viso the same year. The CAI grew rapidly through the 1870s as the unified Italian state channelled nationalist energy into mountain exploration, and its section system — local chapters with territorial responsibility for specific massifs — became the template that almost all other clubs would follow. The first purpose-built CAI hut, the Rifugio Vittorio Emanuele II at 2,732 metres in the Gran Paradiso massif, was completed in 1884 and still stands.

## The German and Austrian Response

The German Alpine Association (Deutscher Alpenverein, DAV) and the Austrian Alpine Association (Österreichischer Alpenverein, ÖAV) were founded jointly in Munich in 1869 before splitting into separate national bodies in 1873. The split was partly administrative and partly a reflection of the Austro-Prussian tensions of the period, but the two clubs maintained close operational ties and divided responsibility for hut construction across the Eastern Alps along rough geographic lines. The ÖAV took primary responsibility for the Ötztal, Stubai, and Zillertal Alps; the DAV focused on the Berchtesgaden, Allgäu, and Karwendel regions.

The scale of German-Austrian hut building between 1875 and 1914 was extraordinary. By 1913 the combined membership of the DAV and ÖAV exceeded 120,000, and the two clubs jointly maintained more than 600 huts and shelters. Many of these were substantial two- or three-storey stone buildings with space for 40-80 guests, a dining room, a warden's apartment, and a separate equipment store. The Rheinwaldhorn Hut in Graubünden, the Hannoverhaus on the Hochalmspitze, and the Berliner Hütte in the Zillertal — built in 1879 with a dining room that seated 60 — all date from this period and remain in operation today.

## The Club Alpin Français and the Western Alps

The Club Alpin Français (CAF) was founded in Paris in 1874, a decade after the British Alpine Club (1857) had demonstrated that a metropolitan membership base could fund ambitious infrastructure in remote mountains. The CAF concentrated its early building programme in the Dauphiné, the Pyrenees, and the Mont Blanc massif, regions that the Swiss and Italian clubs had left largely to local guides. The Refuge du Goûter on the normal route to Mont Blanc was taken over and enlarged by the CAF in 1880; the Refuge des Écrins in the Massif des Écrins was built in 1891. By 1900 the CAF maintained 57 huts, a number that would triple by 1930.

## The Impact of the First World War

The First World War disrupted the hut network profoundly. The Dolomites and the Julian Alps became active front lines, and dozens of huts — including Austrian-owned facilities now inside Italian territory — were either destroyed in fighting or rendered inaccessible by new international borders drawn at Versailles and Saint-Germain-en-Laye. Italian law banned German and Austrian Alpine clubs from owning property on Italian soil, transferring hundreds of DAV and ÖAV huts to the CAI overnight in 1923. The political renaming of huts in South Tyrol — German names replaced with Italian translations — created resentments that persisted for decades and were only partly resolved by the Italian-Austrian state treaty of 1969.

The interwar period also saw a democratisation of hut use. Before 1914, hut guests were overwhelmingly male, upper-middle-class, and accompanied by professional guides. During the 1920s and 1930s, the growth of workers' alpine associations in Germany, Austria, and Italy, combined with the development of Via Ferrata routes that allowed less experienced climbers to reach exposed terrain safely, brought a much broader social profile to the huts. Matratzenlager — large dormitory rooms with shared mattresses — became standard rather than exceptional, and hut fees were deliberately kept low enough that factory workers could afford a weekend in the mountains.

## Post-War Reconstruction and the Modern Era

Reconstruction after 1945 was financed partly by membership dues, partly by national and regional government grants, and in some cases by the German government as partial restitution for huts lost in Italy. The DAV alone rebuilt or substantially renovated more than 200 huts between 1948 and 1960. Helicopter supply, which became practical in the Alps during the 1950s, transformed the logistics of both construction and provisioning: stone and timber gave way to steel-frame construction kits that could be airlifted to sites above 3,000 metres, and seasonal stocking of food and fuel no longer required summer-long pack-mule trains.

The environmental pressures of the late twentieth century brought a new set of obligations. Alpine clubs were early adopters of composting toilets, solar panels, and low-phosphate cleaning products — not from commercial incentive but because their own membership increasingly demanded it and because the clubs owned the watersheds around their huts and could see the effects of pollution firsthand. The DAV introduced mandatory environmental standards for all its huts in 1992, and by 2010 the majority of huts above 2,000 metres operated without a mains grid connection.

## What the Clubs Own Today

The DAV currently maintains 322 huts and 178 alpine shelters, making it the single largest operator of mountain accommodation in the world. The ÖAV has 231 huts, the SAC 152, the CAI more than 700 (spread across hundreds of local sections), and the CAF around 160. Each club sets its own membership fee and its own discount structure for affiliated members of other clubs, and a reciprocity agreement dating to 1909 — updated regularly — means that a DAV member receives the same discounted hut fee at a SAC or ÖAV facility as at a DAV hut, and vice versa.

This network, representing more than a century of coordinated construction across six countries and four main languages, is what makes multi-day mountain travel in the Alps fundamentally different from comparable experiences in other mountain ranges. The huts are not hotels that happen to be at altitude; they are the physical infrastructure of a voluntary movement that decided, in the 1860s, that the mountains should be accessible to anyone willing to walk to them.
