Jotunheimen — the Home of the Giants — is the highest mountain region in Scandinavia and one of the most dramatic in northern Europe. Galdhøpiggen (2,469 metres) and Glittertind (2,464 metres) are the two highest summits in Norway, and both rise from a landscape of glaciers, tarns, and ridges that is compactly arranged between the Sognefjord in the west and the Gudbrands valley in the east. The DNT (Den Norske Turistforening — Norwegian Trekking Association) has maintained a network of huts throughout Jotunheimen since the 1870s, and many of the current lodges occupy positions that have been used for overnight shelter for well over a century. The season runs from late June through September at most staffed huts, with self-service facilities available at other times. These ten represent the core of the Jotunheimen network.

## 1. Gjendebu, Lake Gjende

The Gjendebu lodge at approximately 984 metres at the western end of Lake Gjende is accessible only by the regular boat service that crosses the 18-kilometre lake from Gjendesheim, or by a full day's walk from Memurubu. It is a large, comfortable DNT lodge sleeping around 100 guests and serves as the western terminus of the famous Bessegen ridge traverse — one of the most walked mountain routes in Norway. The surrounding terrain includes routes toward the Kalvehøgde and the passes leading into the Utladalen valley.

## 2. Glitterheim, Glittertind approach

The Glitterheim lodge at around 1,384 metres at the foot of Glittertind is the primary base for ascents of Norway's second highest peak. The approach from Gjendesheim or from Spiterstulen is around five to seven hours depending on route. Glitterheim sleeps approximately 100 guests in summer and is one of the most substantial facilities in the Jotunheimen network. The surrounding terrain includes the Veo glacier — now significantly reduced from its extent in the nineteenth century — and routes connecting to the western Jotunheimen trail system.

## 3. Leirvassbu, Leirdalen

At 1,400 metres in the Leirdalen valley at the head of the Sognefjord drainage, Leirvassbu is a large lodge sleeping around 130 guests and one of the most important nodes in the western Jotunheimen network. It provides access to the Smørstabb glacier and routes toward the Store Smørstabbtinden group. The lodge is open from late June and is reached by road from Lom — a four-wheel drive track that makes it one of the more easily accessible high lodges in the region.

## 4. Memurubu, Lake Gjende

Memurubu at approximately 987 metres sits on the northern shore of Lake Gjende at the midpoint of the Bessegen traverse. The lodge serves both the Bessegen ridge hikers and parties departing into the interior of Jotunheimen toward the Bessvatnet lakes and the high terrain north of the Gjende corridor. Memurubu is accessible by boat from Gjendesheim and sleeps around 90 guests. The view from the dining room across Gjende toward the Glittertind massif is one of the classic panoramas of Norwegian mountain scenery.

## 5. Spiterstulen, Galdhøpiggen approach

The Spiterstulen lodge at 1,100 metres in the Visdalen valley is the standard starting point for ascents of Galdhøpiggen (2,469 metres) — the highest summit in Scandinavia. The lodge is accessible by private car via a toll road from Lom and sleeps around 120 guests in the main building plus additional capacity in a series of smaller annex buildings. On clear summer days, several hundred people complete the Galdhøpiggen summit circuit, which takes around six to eight hours return. A guided glacier route via the Svellnosbreen is also available.

## 6. Fondsbu, Valdres

The Fondsbu lodge at approximately 1,070 metres at the head of Tyin lake in the Valdres region is one of the most westerly of the main Jotunheimen lodges, positioned at the junction of routes heading east into the mountain core and north toward the Sognefjell plateau. The lodge is reached by road from Årdal and is a comfortable facility sleeping around 80 guests. It serves as a starting point for the Hurrungane massif to the west — the sharpest and most dramatic rock peaks in Jotunheimen.

## 7. Olavsbu, western Jotunheimen

The Olavsbu self-service hut at approximately 1,400 metres in the western Jotunheimen between Gjendebu and the Hurrungane area is one of the simpler DNT facilities, providing basic dormitory shelter without staffed catering. It sits on the high plateau south of the main Gjende corridor and is used primarily by parties on multi-day traverses of the western high terrain. The surrounding area is particularly good for spotting reindeer in summer.

## 8. Eidsbugarden, Bygdin

At approximately 1,060 metres at the eastern end of the Bygdin lake, the Eidsbugarden lodge is one of the longest-established in Jotunheimen, with roots in the nineteenth century summer tourism that first brought visitors from Christiania (Oslo) to the mountains by horse and carriage. It is accessible by boat across Bygdin from Bitihorn and by road from Valdres. The lodge sleeps around 70 guests and serves as a base for the Galdhøpiggen approach via the eastern route.

## 9. Gjendesheim, Bessegen east

Gjendesheim at approximately 987 metres at the eastern end of Lake Gjende is the busiest starting point for Bessegen ridge hikers and the main ferry terminal for the lake boat service. The lodge sleeps over 100 guests and includes a large restaurant serving the considerable day-visitor traffic in July and August, when thousands of people attempt the Bessegen ridge — a five-hour traverse along a narrow spine of red quartzite between two lakes at different elevations. The ridge was described by Henrik Ibsen in the 1867 play Peer Gynt.

## 10. Juvasshytta, Galdhøpiggen

The Juvasshytta at 1,841 metres is the highest staffed lodge in Norway and the highest starting point for Galdhøpiggen ascents. It sits directly on the Juvass glacier and provides guided glacier walks as well as the jumping-off point for the shortest route to the Galdhøpiggen summit — around two and a half hours on the guided glacier tour. The lodge is accessible by toll road from Lom and is a popular destination for families and groups who want the summit experience with a shorter approach than the Spiterstulen route.

## Navigating Jotunheimen's Hut Network

Jotunheimen's lodges vary considerably in size, character, and accessibility. The large valley lodges — Spiterstulen, Leirvassbu, Gjendesheim — can be reached by car or boat, while the interior huts require multi-day approaches on foot. DNT membership provides reduced rates at all lodges and at the unstaffed self-service huts, where users leave payment on an honesty basis.
