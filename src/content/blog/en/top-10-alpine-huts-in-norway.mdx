The Norwegian Trekking Association (Den Norske Turistforening, DNT) manages
over 500 mountain huts across Norway — a mix of staffed lodges, unmanned
self-service cabins stocked with basic provisions, and unstaffed no-service
shelters. The DNT hut network is built on the friluftsliv tradition of
democratic access to the mountains, and prices for members are among the most
reasonable of any alpine club system in Europe. The ten huts below anchor
the finest routes in Jotunheimen and the surrounding highlands. All are
visible on the [map](/map).

## 1. Spiterstulen, 1100 m — Northern Jotunheimen

The valley base for Galdhøpiggen (2469 m) — the highest point in Norway and
Scandinavia — and Glittertind (2464 m), Norway's second highest. Spiterstulen
is a privately operated mountain lodge (not DNT-affiliated) at road access
level, making it the most accessible high-mountain base in Jotunheimen.
Capacity over 100, with full board accommodation, guided glacier walks on
the Svellnosbreen, and summer snow routes to both summits. The guided route
to Galdhøpiggen from Spiterstulen crosses the Svellnosbreen glacier; guided
trips are strongly recommended for the crevassed sections.

## 2. Glitterheim, 1384 m — Northern Jotunheimen

DNT staffed hut on the approach to Glittertind (2464 m) from the eastern
Jotunheimen, reached from Sota Seter on the northern park road. Capacity
around 120. The approach to Glittertind from Glitterheim avoids the glacier
crossing required from Spiterstulen — a more straightforward route in summer
when the snowcap is reduced. The hut is staffed from late June to mid-September.
Mountain weather in Jotunheimen changes fast; a clear morning can become a
whiteout by early afternoon.

## 3. Memurubu, 1008 m — Lake Gjende, Jotunheimen

On the northern shore of the iconic Lake Gjende — the green, glacially fed
lake at the heart of Jotunheimen — Memurubu is accessible by boat from
Gjendesheim or by a full day's walk over the Besseggen ridge. The Besseggen
ridge is the most walked route in Norway, with up to 40,000 walkers annually
navigating the knife-edge between Gjende (984 m) and Bessvatnet (1373 m).
Memurubu is the end point of Besseggen and serves both ridge walkers and
parties continuing west into the Jotunheimen interior. DNT-staffed, capacity
around 100.

## 4. Gjendebu, 990 m — Western Lake Gjende, Jotunheimen

At the western end of Lake Gjende, accessible by boat from Gjendesheim or
after a full day traversing the length of Gjende's shore. Gjendebu is the
western anchor of the Gjende lake route and the starting point for routes
into the remote western Jotunheimen — Kvitskard, Leirvassbu, and ultimately
Sognefjellshytta. DNT-staffed, capacity around 70. This is deeper wilderness
than the Besseggen area; parties should carry navigation tools and be
self-sufficient for weather delays.

## 5. Krossbu, 1295 m — Sognefjell, Jotunheimen

On the Sognefjell mountain road (Route 55) at 1295 m — one of the highest
public roads in Norway — Krossbu is a staging point for ski touring and
summer glacier routes on the Smørstabbreen and Fannaråken. Not a DNT hut but
a private mountain lodge with a long alpine tradition. Road access makes it
a reliable provisioning stop on Jotunheimen traverses. The Fannaråken summit
(2068 m) offers views across the entire Jotunheimen massif.

## 6. Sognefjellshytta, 1413 m — Sognefjell

Adjacent to Krossbu on the Sognefjell road, Sognefjellshytta is the classic
Norwegian fjell lodge in full form — warm, wood-panelled, with an emphasis on
traditional Norwegian cooking (fårikål, kjøttkaker, homemade bread) and a
culture of communal table-sharing. This is not a wilderness experience —
road access means day visitors — but the surrounding plateau and the
Fannaråken ridge are genuine high mountain terrain.

## 7. Rondvassbu, 1160 m — Rondane National Park

The central hut of Rondane — Norway's first national park and the setting for
Henrik Ibsen's Peer Gynt — Rondvassbu sits on the shore of the Rondvatnet
lake between the two main peaks, Rondslottet (2178 m) and Storsmeden (2016 m).
DNT-staffed, capacity around 130. The Rondane plateau is more arid and exposed
than Jotunheimen — reindeer herds move across the plateau in summer and
autumn. Routes across the Rondane main ridge require careful weather awareness
above the treeline.

## 8. Trolltunga Camp Area Huts — Hardangervidda approaches

The Trolltunga trail from Skjeggedal to the Trolltunga rock formation (1180 m)
passes through a set of DNT self-service huts on the Hardangervidda plateau.
These are unstaffed self-service cabins stocked with canned goods, firewood,
and basic utensils — payment made via the DNT honesty system (cash or online).
The Trolltunga route itself is longer and more demanding than social media
typically represents — a 22–27 km round trip with 800 m elevation gain,
typically done as a very long day hike or as an overnight from the plateau huts.

## 9. Tyin Filefjell, 1076 m — Filefjell

On the plateau between Jotunheimen and Valdres, the Tyin Filefjell lodge sits
at the Tyinvatnet lake at 1076 m. Road access from Route 53. Historically an
important route between eastern and western Norway; the King's Road crosses
here. Private lodge, not DNT, but integrated into route planning for
Jotunheimen west-to-east traverses and winter ski tours.

## 10. Skogadalsbøen, 1035 m — Utladalen, Northern Jotunheimen

Deep in the Utladalen valley gorge — one of the deepest and most dramatic
valleys in Norway — Skogadalsbøen is a DNT staffed hut accessible only on
foot, with no road access. Reached from Øvre Årdal in 7–8 hours via the
Vettisfossen waterfall (the highest free-falling waterfall in Norway at 275 m)
or via longer routes from the Jotunheimen interior. Capacity around 80.
The remoteness gives this hut a distinctive atmosphere.

## Self-service versus staffed in the DNT system

DNT huts fall into three categories: staffed (betjent), self-service (ubetjent
med nøkkel), and no-service (ubetjent uten nøkkel). Self-service huts require
a DNT member key (available on membership), are stocked with basic provisions,
and operate on an honesty system for payment. They extend the network deep into
terrain where staffing year-round would be uneconomical. A DNT membership
card and the DNT cabin key unlock a walking network that covers almost the
entire Norwegian highland.

## Jotunheimen in context

Jotunheimen (the Home of the Giants) is the highest mountain range in
Scandinavia and the heartland of the Norwegian alpine tradition. The range
contains Norway's two highest peaks — Galdhøpiggen (2469 m) and Glittertind
(2464 m) — along with the extraordinarily dramatic Hurrungane massif in the
west, including Store Skagastølstind (2405 m), the most technically demanding
summit in Norway.

The landscape character of Jotunheimen is distinctive: rounded glaciated
summits with some sharp ridges, wide U-shaped valleys, and the chain of
glacier-green lakes (Gjende, Bygdin, Tyin) that give the range its navigational
spine. The DNT hut network is dense enough that experienced walkers can cross
Jotunheimen from the eastern valley road at Otta to the western fjordland
without a tent — sleeping in staffed and self-service huts throughout. This
crossing (approximately 7 days west-to-east or east-to-west) is the backbone
Jotunheimen route.

## Norwegian distances and weather

Norwegian trail times are calculated on a different rhythm from Alpine
routes — the terrain is lower but often boggy, the weather is maritime and
changeable, and the distances between huts are longer than in the densely
networked Swiss or Austrian Alps. A stage time of 7–9 hours between Jotunheimen
huts is normal, and the wind at ridge level in Norwegian conditions is
genuinely serious. A hut-to-hut trip in Jotunheimen requires the same
weather-awareness as an alpine route, just focused on windchill, rain, and
fog rather than thunderstorms and rockfall.

The Norwegian Meteorological Institute's yr.no is the reference forecast
service for all Norwegian outdoor planning — free, excellent, and updated
every hour for specific locations. Download the yr.no app before leaving home
and use it daily.
