South Tyrol — Südtirol to its German-speaking majority, Alto Adige on the Italian road signs — is the most linguistically layered province in the Alps. Annexed by Italy after the First World War, it kept its Tyrolean dialect, its Knödel and Speck, and its dense network of Schutzhütten built by the German and Austrian alpine clubs before 1914. Today those huts carry double names, are run under Italian law, and serve a clientele that switches mid-sentence between German and Italian. The terrain ranges from the glaciated Ortler-Cevedale massif in the west, through the Texel and Stubai frontier ranges, to the pale towers of the Dolomites in the east. The ten huts below trace that span, and the cultural seam that runs through all of it.

## 1. Plosehütte – Rifugio Plose, Eisack Valley

The Plosehütte at around 2,446 metres on the Plose above Brixen (Bressanone) is one of the most accessible and rewarding huts in the province. A cable car from the valley brings walkers to within an easy stroll, and the terrace looks straight across to the Geisler and Peitlerkofel peaks of the Puez-Geisler Nature Park, among the most photographed in the Dolomites. The hut is comfortable and family-friendly, with a kitchen turning out the full Tyrolean repertoire — Schlutzkrapfen, Kaiserschmarrn, barley soup. It makes an ideal first night for anyone easing into the South Tyrolean hut world.

## 2. Martellerhütte – Rifugio Martello, Ortler-Cevedale

High in the Martell valley at around 2,610 metres, the Martellerhütte sits at the foot of the Cevedale glacier in the Stelvio National Park, Italy's largest. This is serious glacier country: the hut serves climbers heading for the Cevedale at 3,769 metres and the long traverse of the Ortler-Cevedale massif, where the highest peak, the Ortler itself, reaches 3,905 metres. The walk in from the Martell valley head is a steady climb through moraine and the last larches. The hut is run in the disciplined SAC-Italian style, with an early breakfast for rope teams and a strict turnaround for glacier travel.

## 3. Lyfialm – Malga Lifi, Martell Valley

Lower in the Martell valley, the Lyfialm (Malga Lifi) at around 2,165 metres is a working alpine dairy that doubles as a refreshment stop and simple overnight. It sits in the heart of the Stelvio park, where the Martell valley is famous for its high-altitude strawberry cultivation and its summer wildflower meadows. The alm serves its own cheese and yoghurt, and the surrounding trails give gentle walking with the Cevedale glaciers as a constant backdrop. It is the soft counterpoint to the austere Martellerhütte higher up the same valley.

## 4. Rifugio Sauch, Trudner Horn

The Rifugio Sauch sits in the wooded uplands of the Trudner Horn (Monte Corno) Nature Park in the far south of the province, at around 1,360 metres. This is a gentler, more pastoral South Tyrol than the glaciated west — mixed forest, clearings, and a mild Mediterranean tinge that allows chestnuts and even the occasional vineyard at lower altitudes. The Sauch is an unpretentious family hut, popular with walkers exploring the quiet Trudner Horn and with cross-country skiers in winter. It shows the province's softer, lower-altitude face.

## 5. Rifugio Prato Rosso, Eggental

At around 1,750 metres above the Eggental (Val d'Ega), the Rifugio Prato Rosso — the red meadow — takes its name from the alpenrose that flushes the slopes crimson in early summer. The hut looks toward the Latemar massif, one of the classic Dolomite ranges, whose pale pinnacles glow at sunset in the famous Enrosadira, the alpenglow that gave the Dolomites their UNESCO World Heritage fame. The Prato Rosso is a comfortable base for walks into the Latemar and an easy introduction to Dolomite scenery for families.

## 6. Rifugio Serot, Eastern Valleys

The Rifugio Serot lies in the quieter eastern uplands of the province, a modest staffed hut serving walkers on the local trail network at around 1,600 metres. Huts like the Serot rarely make the guidebooks, but they are the backbone of South Tyrolean walking: solid, hospitable, well-fed, and embedded in working pasture. The surrounding country offers undemanding day walks through larch and stone-pine, with the higher frontier ranges visible to the north — a reminder of how broad the province's altitude range really is.

## 7. Schutzhütte on the Stubai Frontier

Along the watershed where South Tyrol meets the Austrian Stubai Alps, a chain of Schutzhütten serves the high traverse routes that thread the frontier ridge. These huts — many built by German alpine club sections before 1914 and now operated from the Italian side — sit above 2,500 metres in glaciated terrain, with the Zuckerhütl at 3,507 metres as the range's high point. They are the classic bases for the Stubai high route, a multi-day glacier traverse that crosses repeatedly between the two countries on a single ridge.

## 8. Rifugio Ghedina, Dolomite Approach

The Rifugio Ghedina lies in the Dolomite country toward the Cortina frontier, a comfortable hut serving the pale-rock landscape that draws walkers from across the world. The Dolomites' distinctive towers — the product of ancient coral reefs uplifted and weathered into pinnacles — surround huts like the Ghedina with some of the most spectacular scenery in the Alps. It makes a relaxed base for the via ferrata and the lower walking trails, with the full Tyrolean kitchen and the easy bilingual hospitality typical of the province.

## 9. Rifugio Campolongo, Dolomite Passes

Set near one of the high Dolomite passes, the Rifugio Campolongo serves the network of routes linking the great Dolomite valleys — Badia, Fassa, and beyond. The Dolomite passes are among the most dramatic road and trail crossings in Europe, and huts positioned on them give immediate access to the Sella, the Sassolungo, and the surrounding towers. The Campolongo is a practical waypoint for both walkers and the cyclists who climb these legendary passes, with hearty food and a constant rotation of mountain travellers.

## 10. Rifugio Carota, Forested Foothills

The Rifugio Carota occupies the wooded lower country of the eastern province, a small hut at modest altitude that anchors a web of family trails through spruce and larch. South Tyrol's foothill huts are easily overlooked beside the glacier bases of the Ortler, but they carry the same Tyrolean character — generous portions, scrubbed wooden interiors, and a steady local clientele. The Carota is the kind of hut where the province's everyday mountain culture, rather than its postcard summits, comes most clearly into focus.
