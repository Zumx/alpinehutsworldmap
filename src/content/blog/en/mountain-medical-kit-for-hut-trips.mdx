A multi-day hut trip in the Alps places you, for periods of two to eight hours between huts, in terrain where the nearest medical facility is a helicopter ride away and the nearest help is whoever is walking the same path. Most medical events in the alpine environment are minor: blisters, twisted ankles, dehydration, sunburn. But the consequences of being unprepared for even a minor event multiply quickly in cold, wet, or high-altitude conditions. A well-constructed kit weighing 400-500 g prevents most minor events from becoming major ones.

## The Philosophical Baseline

The first principle of a hiking medical kit is that it should treat what you can treat competently and stabilise what requires professional care. This is not the same as carrying every item from a pharmacy shelf. Carrying ibuprofen you don't know how to use safely is less valuable than carrying nothing but knowing that ibuprofen taken before a long descent reduces knee inflammation. The medical kit and the knowledge to use it are not separable.

The second principle is weight discipline. A 2 kg first-aid kit carried on a five-day traverse will be left behind on day two when a pair of trekking poles would have been more useful. Every item in the kit should have a specific intended use case and should be the minimum weight version of that item available.

## Blister Management

Blisters are statistically the most common medical event on multi-day hut trips and the one most often handled poorly. A friction blister on the heel that is not managed on day one becomes a wound on day two and a hike-ending problem on day three.

The kit should contain: a roll of Leukotape P (or equivalent rigid sports tape), Compeed blister pads in multiple sizes, a sterile lancet for draining intact blisters, and a small amount of Betadine or equivalent antiseptic wipe. Leukotape is preferred over moleskin because it adheres firmly to moist skin and does not roll or bunch. Applied as a preventive layer over hot spots at the end of day one, it prevents blister formation entirely in most cases.

The correct technique for a formed blister: pierce at the base with a sterile lancet, drain by gentle pressure, leave the roof of the blister intact (it is a sterile protective layer), dry the area, apply Betadine, and cover with a Compeed pad cut to size. Taping over the pad with Leukotape prevents the pad from rolling off during the next day's descent.

## Wound Care

Alpine terrain produces wounds primarily from falls on rock and scree. The wound care component of the kit should treat lacerations up to approximately 3 cm that do not involve major vessels, and abrasions that are not full-thickness.

Required items: sterile wound closure strips (Steri-Strips or equivalent), sterile dressing pads (5x5 cm, two minimum), a roll of 5 cm cohesive bandage (Coban), antiseptic wipes, and a pair of nitrile gloves. Closure strips suffice for most clean lacerations; they require clean, dry skin to adhere, which is the main challenge in rain. The cohesive bandage holds dressings in place over knuckles and joints where standard adhesive dressings fail.

For head wounds, which bleed disproportionately to their severity: direct pressure for five minutes stops most scalp lacerations regardless of size. The priority after stopping bleeding is assessing for concussion — confusion, pupil inequality, persistent headache, or nausea after a head impact warrants immediate descent and medical evaluation.

## Altitude Sickness

Acute mountain sickness (AMS) is a genuine risk on Alpine hut trips involving significant altitude gain. The standard AMS symptom set — headache combined with at least one of nausea, fatigue, dizziness, or difficulty sleeping — begins when unacclimatised individuals ascend above 2,500 m rapidly. In the Alps, this primarily affects visitors flying to Zurich or Munich and reaching a 3,000 m hut on the same day.

The pharmaceutical response is ibuprofen (400 mg) or paracetamol (500-1,000 mg) for headache relief, combined with halting further ascent. Acetazolamide (Diamox, 125-250 mg twice daily) is a prophylactic and mild treatment; it requires a prescription in most European countries and should be discussed with a physician before the trip. The definitive treatment for AMS that is not responding to rest is descent — every 300-500 m of descent produces measurable symptomatic improvement.

The kit should contain paracetamol (10 tablets minimum) and ibuprofen (10 tablets minimum). Ibuprofen is preferable for altitude headache on the evidence available; paracetamol is the safer option when the patient has stomach problems or a history of ulcers.

## Musculoskeletal Injuries

Ankle sprains are the most common significant musculoskeletal injury on mountain terrain. The immediate management follows the RICE protocol: rest, ice (improvised from snow in the Alpine environment), compression with a cohesive bandage, and elevation when possible. Most grade 1-2 lateral ankle sprains can be walked off with taping support; a figure-of-eight support applied with Leukotape around the ankle joint provides meaningful stability for descent.

A trekking pole is the most important orthopaedic aid in the kit — not in the first-aid bag but in your hand. A single pole reduces ankle joint loading on descent by 15-25% as measured by force-plate studies. Two poles reduce it more. Carrying them in a pack on flat terrain and deploying them on descents is the simplest prophylactic measure available.

The cohesive bandage in the wound care section doubles as a sprain support. A knee brace for patellofemoral pain (hiker's knee) is worth carrying if this is a known personal issue; generic unbranded neoprene knee sleeves weigh 80 g each and provide meaningful compression and warmth.

## Hypothermia and Cold Injury

The kit should include one emergency foil bivouac bag (Highlander or equivalent, 80 g) that wraps a seated person in reflective material. This is the first line of hypothermia treatment when a party member cannot continue, shelter is not available, and evacuation is being organised. The foil bag is not a sleeping bag replacement but a heat retention device that buys time.

Ibuprofen is the correct medication for mild frostbite (superficial white patches on extremities) once the affected area is protected from further cold — it reduces the inflammatory damage during rewarming. Do not rub frostbitten tissue; do not rewarm frostbitten feet if the person still needs to walk to safety, because walking on thawed tissue causes vastly greater damage than walking on frozen tissue.

## Complete Kit Summary

To keep the kit under 500 g: Leukotape (1 roll), Compeed (10 varied), sterile lancet (2), Betadine wipes (5), wound closure strips (1 pack), sterile dressings 5x5 (2), cohesive bandage 5 cm (1 roll), antiseptic wipes (5), nitrile gloves (1 pair), paracetamol 500 mg (10 tabs), ibuprofen 400 mg (10 tabs), foil bivouac bag (1), safety pins (4), and a small set of instructions that you have read before leaving home.

## Plan Your Route, Know Your Evacuations

The medical kit enables independent management of minor events; it does not substitute for knowing where each hut is, what the rescue number is for the country you are in, and at what point a situation requires calling for help rather than managing further. [Open the map](/map) to plan your traverse, identify the huts at each stage, and understand which sections are most remote — the map makes clear where a two-hour walk to shelter exists versus a six-hour commitment to a hut on the far side of a pass. Build that knowledge into your trip planning before you leave.
