Northern Lombardy's alpine terrain is one of the least internationally known sections of the southern Alps, yet it contains some remarkably varied mountain groups. The Orobie Alps run east–west above Bergamo and the Valtellina, their southern scarps rising directly above the Po plain in a wall that is visible from Milan on clear winter days. To the northeast, the Stelvio National Park encompasses the Ortler-Cevedale group, with its permanent glaciers and some of the highest passes in Europe. Between these lie the Adamello-Presanella group and the Camonica valley, all served by a network of rifugi that draws primarily Italian visitors and sees comparatively little international traffic. The ten huts below offer an introduction to this underrated region.

## 1. Alpe e Rifugio Legnone, Orobie

At approximately 1,740 metres on the Alpe Legnone above Delebio in the lower Valtellina, the Rifugio Legnone sits near the summit of the Legnone (2,609 metres), the isolated limestone pyramid that forms the western anchor of the Orobie. The Legnone rises sharply above Lake Como and offers views that encompass the full arc of the Alpine chain from Mont Blanc to the Adamello. The rifugio is a modest, welcoming facility open in summer and provides straightforward access to the summit via waymarked paths.

## 2. Baita dell'Alpino, Bergamo Alps

The Baita dell'Alpino at around 1,760 metres in the Bergamo Alps above Serina or the Val Seriana serves as a classic example of the Baita style — a converted high pasture building operating as a mountain inn. The Bergamo Alps are characterised by rounded pre-alpine summits and deep forested valleys, making them a popular destination for day hikers and cyclists from the Bergamo and Brescia area. The Baita offers accommodation and a traditional Lombard mountain menu.

## 3. Rifugio Ghedina, Orobie

The Rifugio Ghedina at around 2,010 metres in the central Orobie above Valbondione in the upper Val Seriana provides access to the Rifugio Curò area and the broad lake-studded plateau in the heart of the Orobie Alps. The Orobie here are dominated by dark metamorphic rock — schist and gneiss — giving them a moody, rugged character quite different from the limestone of the western Alps. The hut sleeps around 40 and is open from June through September.

## 4. Lyfialm - Malga Lifi, Stelvio region

The Malga Lifi at approximately 1,900 metres in the upper Stelvio National Park area above the Pejo valley in Trentino — bordering the Lombardy section of the park — is a traditional malga (high alpine pasture farm) that operates as a rifugio in summer. The name Lyfialm reflects the South Tyrolean German-speaking tradition of the area. The surrounding terrain gives access to routes toward the Cevedale glacier and the paths crossing into the Ortler group.

## 5. Martellerhütte - Rifugio Martello, Martell Valley

At approximately 2,610 metres in the Martell valley above Latsch in South Tyrol, the Martellerhütte sits at the foot of the Zufrittspitze and within the Stelvio National Park. The hut carries a bilingual name reflecting the German-Italian character of the area and is maintained by the AVS (South Tyrolean Alpine Club). It sleeps around 50 and is used primarily as a base for ski touring in spring and glacier routes on the Cevedale-Ortler chain in summer.

## 6. Rifugio Baitone, Adamello

The Rifugio Baitone at 2,449 metres in the Adamello group above the Val Camonica near Ponte di Legno sits beside the Lago del Baitone, a small reservoir in the glaciated terrain of the Adamello plateau. The Adamello massif is the largest non-Himalayan glaciated area in the central Alps and was a major theatre of mountain warfare in World War One, when Italian and Austro-Hungarian forces fought at altitude for three years. Numerous artefacts from this conflict have been recovered as the Adamello glacier has retreated. The Baitone hut sleeps around 60 and is open from July through September.

## 7. Rifugio Coca, Orobie

The Rifugio Coca at 1,892 metres above Valbondione in the eastern Orobie is one of the most visited rifugi in the Bergamo Alps, positioned beside a large reservoir that services the valley's hydroelectric infrastructure. The terrain above the hut is more remote, climbing to the main Orobie watershed ridge at around 2,600 metres with views across the Valtellina to the Bernina and Disgrazia groups. The hut is operated by the CAI Sezione di Bergamo and sleeps around 70 guests.

## 8. Rifugio Tagliaferri, Orobie

The Rifugio Tagliaferri at 2,400 metres is one of the highest staffed huts in the Orobie, positioned in the central section of the chain above Valbondione with direct access to the main ridge and routes crossing into the Valtellina. The hut commemorates Eugenio Tagliaferri, a Bergamasco alpinist active in the late nineteenth century, and is managed by the CAI Bergamo. It sleeps around 40 and serves as a key waypoint on the multi-day Orobie Alta Via traverse.

## 9. Rifugio Lissone, Orobie

At approximately 2,160 metres in the eastern Orobie above Valbondione, the Rifugio Lissone is a modern facility rebuilt by the CAI section of Lissone following the original structure's deterioration. It provides access to the Passo di Segnale (2,820 metres) and routes across the main Orobie watershed toward the Valtellina. The hut accommodates around 50 guests and is open from June through September.

## 10. Rifugio Curò, Orobie

The Rifugio Curò at 1,914 metres above Valbondione is the central hub of the upper Orobie trail network, positioned beside the Lago Barbellino and serving as the gathering point for routes leading to the Rifugio Barbellino above (2,130 metres) and the broader lake plateau. The hut is named after Ettore Curò, a local industrialist who funded its construction in the early twentieth century. It sleeps around 90 guests and is the largest and busiest hut in the central Orobie, with a full kitchen and comfortable dormitory accommodation.

## Explore Lombardy's Hut Network

Lombardy's rifugi are concentrated in three main mountain groups — the Orobie, the Adamello-Presanella, and the Stelvio-Ortler — each with its own character and season. The Orobie are accessible from late May, the Adamello plateau from July, and the Ortler group from mid-June through September.
