The Sierra Nevada of California runs 640 km from the Tehachapi Pass to the
Cascade Range boundary in the north, forming the "Range of Light" (John Muir's
phrase) — a vast granite batholith rising to Whitney (4421 m, the highest point
in the contiguous United States) and containing Yosemite, Kings Canyon, and
Sequoia National Parks. The overnight infrastructure for mountain travel here
is thinner than the Alps or even the Cascades: a few genuine huts, a handful
of high-country lodges open in summer, and the dispersed wilderness camping
that is the Sierra's dominant mode of overnight travel.

## 1. Pear Lake Ski Hut, 2865 m — Sequoia National Park, California

The Pear Lake Ski Hut — operated by the Sequoia Natural History Association
under National Park Service permit — is one of the few genuinely Alpine-style
overnight huts in the Sierra Nevada. Located at 2865 m in Sequoia National
Park, it accommodates 10 people in winter (mid-January through April) and
is accessed by a 7-mile ski or snowshoe approach from Wolverton. A wood-burning
stove, cooking equipment, and sleeping bunks make it a full self-service hut
in the DNT tradition. Reservations are by lottery; demand far exceeds supply
and applications are made months in advance. In summer, the area is accessible
by trail; the hut is not available for summer overnight use.

## 2. Muir Trail Ranch, 2300 m — High Sierra, San Joaquin River headwaters

Muir Trail Ranch in the middle of the John Muir Trail (accessible only by
foot or horse from the nearest trailhead, 2 days in any direction) is a hot
spring resort that provides resupply services for JMT through-hikers. Cabins,
hot spring pools, and a resupply system (buckets dropped off before the trail
and collected at the ranch) make it the most significant infrastructure point
on the 340 km JMT. Not a traditional mountain hut — a working ranch that serves
wilderness travel logistics.

## 3. Rae Lakes Basin Camps, 3360 m — Kings Canyon National Park, California

The Rae Lakes basin in Kings Canyon National Park is the most celebrated
overnight location on the John Muir Trail — a chain of four lakes at 3360–3400 m
below the Glen Pass with the Cathedral Range visible to the north. Wilderness
permits are required; camping at designated sites in the basin. No hut, but
the lakes and surrounding terrain are among the finest wilderness camping in
the western United States.

## 4. Bishop Pass Camp, 3600 m — Inyo National Forest, California

On the approach to the Bishop Pass (3585 m) from the South Lake trailhead
above Bishop, the campsite near Long Lake at 3600 m serves JMT through-hikers
and climbers approaching the Palisades — including the North Palisade (4341 m),
the Clyde Variation, and related routes. No fixed hut; wilderness camping.
The Palisades are the technical climbing destination of the Sierra Nevada.

## 5. Bighorn Plateau Camp, 3700 m — Sequoia National Park

On the high cross-country route through the heart of Sequoia, the Bighorn
Plateau at approximately 3700 m provides one of the most exposed and dramatic
campsites in the Sierra Nevada — a flat plateau above the treeline with Whitney
visible to the southeast. Accessible only cross-country; no trail.

## 6. Guitar Lake Camp, 3960 m — Whitney area, Sequoia National Park

The standard summit-day base camp for Mount Whitney via the JMT approach,
Guitar Lake at 3960 m provides flat camping in a spectacular glacial cirque.
The summit of Whitney from Guitar Lake takes 4–5 hours via the JMT. No fixed
shelter; wilderness camping with bear canisters required.

## 7. Whitney Portal, 2550 m — Lone Pine, Inyo County

The trailhead for the main Whitney Trail at 2550 m has a small store and
camping area that serves as the base for day and overnight hikers on the
22-mile Whitney round trip. The Portal Store (seasonal) provides basic
food and emergency supplies.

## 8. Cottonwood Pass Camp, 3660 m — Inyo National Forest

On the southern approach to the JMT via Horseshoe Meadows, the Cottonwood
Pass campsite at 3660 m provides a high-country entry into the Sierra for
parties avoiding the Whitney Portal crowds.

## 9. Mammoth Lakes High Country Camps, 2800–3400 m — Inyo National Forest

The Mammoth Lakes area provides the densest backcountry access in the Sierra
outside Yosemite, with multiple wilderness camping zones above the ski resort.
The Duck Pass and Convict Lake approaches provide routes into the high country
above 3000 m.

## 10. Glacier Lodge, 2400 m — Big Pine Canyon, Inyo County

At the end of the Big Pine Canyon road, Glacier Lodge (a private mountain
resort) serves as the trailhead staging area for the approach to the Palisade
Glacier — the southernmost active glacier in the Sierra Nevada and the
traditional ice-climbing training ground for California alpinists.

## The John Muir Trail

The John Muir Trail (JMT) is a 340 km long-distance trail from Yosemite
Valley to the summit of Mount Whitney, passing through the heart of the
Sierra Nevada at elevations predominantly above 2700 m. It was completed
in 1938 and named for the Scottish-American naturalist John Muir, whose
advocacy in the 1890s and 1900s was instrumental in establishing the national
park system in California. The JMT is widely considered the finest long-
distance trail in the contiguous United States.

Unlike European Alpine trails, the JMT has no hut infrastructure in the
traditional sense: wilderness camping is the standard mode. Permits are
required for all overnight travel in the Wilderness areas of Yosemite,
Ansel Adams, John Muir, and Sequoia-Kings Canyon national parks and forests.
The permit quota system for the JMT is highly competitive; the Yosemite
entry permit (the most constrained) requires an application up to six months
in advance through recreation.gov. Approximately 40% of permits are released
two weeks before the trip date for last-minute applicants.

## Bear canister requirements

All overnight wilderness travellers in the High Sierra are required to use
a bear-resistant food canister. The black bears of the Sierra Nevada are
habituated to human food and will destroy a tent or backpack to access
food that is not properly stored. Bear boxes (metal lockers) are installed
at some popular camps; where no bear box exists, a hard-sided approved
canister is the required storage method. Failure to comply can result in
significant fines and forced evacuation from the wilderness.

The bear canister requirement adds approximately 1.2–1.8 kg to the pack
weight for a standard 7-day section of the JMT. Lighter weight canisters
(e.g., BearVault BV500, Ursack) that meet the Sierra Nevada requirements
are available; the Ursack (a Spectra fabric bag with an internal aluminium
liner) is the lightest compliant solution at around 340 g.

## Whitney permits

Mount Whitney's main trail from Whitney Portal is the most heavily permitted
day hike in the United States. The permit quota for the main Whitney Trail
overnight zone is 60 people per night (plus 100 day hikers); the permit
lottery opens in February for the May–October season. Demand for July and
August dates is enormous; applicants who are rejected in the main lottery
may attempt the permit lottery for the October dates (less crowded) or
approach via the JMT (which uses the JMT permit system instead).
