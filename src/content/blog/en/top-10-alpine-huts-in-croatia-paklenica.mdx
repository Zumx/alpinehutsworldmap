There is no other range in Europe quite like the Velebit. It runs for nearly 150 kilometres along the Croatian coast, a great wall of grey limestone that rises almost straight from the Adriatic so that, from the right ledge, you look down on islands and turquoise sea while standing among karst peaks and beech forest. At its southern end lies Paklenica National Park, the country's premier climbing destination, where the Velika and Mala Paklenica gorges cut deep canyons into the massif. Croatian mountain culture is built around the planinarski dom (mountain home) and the smaller kuća (hut), maintained by the Croatian Mountaineering Association (HPS). These ten are the shelters worth planning a Velebit traverse around.

## A range between sea and stone

The defining experience of the Velebit is contrast. Mornings can be Mediterranean — warm rock, the smell of sage and pine — while a few hours of climbing bring you onto a windswept plateau where the bura, the fierce northeasterly wind, can drop temperatures and visibility without warning. The premier-protected route is the Premužićeva staza, a remarkable dry-stone trail built in the 1930s that traverses the most rugged parts of the range on an even, walkable gradient. Most Velebit huts are unstaffed or only seasonally staffed, often with a key system through the HPS, so self-sufficiency and a reliable water plan matter on this porous limestone where surface streams barely exist.

## 1. Paklenica mountain hut, Velika Paklenica

The hub of the national park. Reached on a straightforward walk up the Velika Paklenica gorge past the famous climbing walls of Anića kuk, the staffed Paklenica hut sits in beech forest and serves as the base for ascents toward Vaganski vrh, the range's high point at 1,757 metres.

## 2. Struge, beneath Sveto brdo

High on the southern Velebit, the Struge shelter sits at around 1,400 metres beneath Sveto brdo (Holy Mountain), the second peak of the park. It is the classic overnight for the long traverse to Vaganski vrh, with one of the great Adriatic sunrises waiting at the summit above.

## 3. Planinarska kuća Pljuskara

A small HPS hut on the northern Velebit reaches, set among the forested folds below the high karst. Quiet, unpretentious and a useful link on the long ridge route, it is the kind of hut that rewards walkers who want the Velebit without the crowds of the Paklenica gorges.

## 4. Planinarska kuća Korita

Set on the northern Velebit near the access road that climbs from the coast, Korita serves as a comfortable base for day exploration of the high plateau and the botanical reserves where the endemic Velebit degenia flower grows. It makes a gentle introduction to the range.

## 5. Planinarski dom Hahlić

On the Učka and Risnjak side of the Croatian mountains, the Hahlić mountain home above the Kvarner gulf gives a different flavour — closer to Rijeka, set in forest, and a popular weekend objective with views over the islands of the northern Adriatic.

## 6. Planinarski dom Klek

Above Ogulin in the Velika Kapela range, the Klek hut sits beneath the unmistakable rock dome of Klek, a peak wrapped in Croatian folklore about witches gathering on its summit. It is one of the most beloved beginner climbing and walking objectives in the country.

## 7. Planinarska kuća Trišnjica

A modest hut in the Velika Kapela, the wooded range that links the Velebit toward the Plitvice country. It serves walkers on the long-distance routes that thread the Dinaric forests, where bear, wolf and lynx still range through some of the wildest woodland in this part of Europe.

## 8. Planinarska kuća Rudi Jurić

Tucked in the karst hills inland from the coast, this small HPS shelter is typical of the network that makes multi-day Dinaric walking possible — basic, welcoming, and a roof when the bura blows or the summer storm rolls off the sea.

## 9. Lujčekova hiža

A characterful hut in the Gorski kotar, the green mountainous interior of Croatia where rainfall is high and the forest dense. Cooler and wetter than the coastal Velebit, the Gorski kotar offers a contrasting alpine experience among fir, beech and limestone sinkholes.

## 10. Planinarska kuća Mlaka

A quieter shelter on the Velebit approaches, useful for breaking the long northern stages of the Premužićeva staza. Like most huts on the range, it asks for self-sufficiency in return for solitude and a front-row seat on the great limestone wall above the Adriatic.

## Climbing in Paklenica

Paklenica is, above all, a climbing park. The Velika Paklenica gorge holds hundreds of routes from gentle slabs to long, serious multi-pitch lines on Anića kuk, a 350-metre wall that draws climbers from across Europe each spring and autumn. The big-wall season avoids the searing coastal summer; April to June and September to October are the sweet spots, when the rock is cool and the gorge throws shade through the afternoon. Free-climbing festivals and a long tradition of Croatian alpinism centre on the park, and even non-climbers come simply to walk the gorge floor and watch the action on the walls.

## Seasons, water and the bura

Plan around the wind and the water. The bura is the dominant Velebit hazard, capable of making exposed plateau crossings genuinely dangerous, so check forecasts and be ready to retreat into the forest. Water is the other constant problem: the karst swallows rainfall, leaving few reliable springs, so carry more than you think you need and confirm cistern availability at each hut before you set out. Spring and autumn are ideal for the high traverses; summer is hot and best reserved for the higher, shadier sections. The Premužićeva staza, walked over several days hut to hut, remains one of the finest and least-known long routes in Mediterranean Europe.
