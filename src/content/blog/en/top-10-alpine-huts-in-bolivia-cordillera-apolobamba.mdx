The Cordillera Apolobamba is the part of the Bolivian Andes that almost nobody reaches. It runs along the Peruvian border north of Lake Titicaca, a chain of glaciated peaks over 6,000 metres tall that draws condors by the dozen and trekkers by the handful. There are no roads to most of it, no telephone signal, and very few buildings of any kind. What passes for a refuge here is often a shepherd's stone hut, a community building in a village like Pelechuco or Curva, or a simple cabaña built for the trickle of walkers crossing the range. Compared with the Alps, the infrastructure is minimal and the altitude unforgiving — the trekking floor sits above 4,000 metres. This is shelter at its most elemental, set against some of the emptiest high country on the continent.

## Refugio Campo Alto Roca

High on the approach slopes, Refugio Campo Alto Roca serves climbers and trekkers staging for the bigger peaks. A campo alto — high camp — is exactly what it sounds like: a basic shelter near the snowline where you sleep before an alpine start. At these elevations the cold is constant and the nights are long, and a roof, however rough, is worth the effort of carrying less.

## Refugio Nuevo Llaulini

In the heart of the Apolobamba trek, Refugio Nuevo Llaulini is a community-built shelter that gives walkers a dry night between high passes. The classic traverse links villages such as Curva, Lagunillas, and Pelechuco, crossing passes near 5,000 metres, and a refuge like Llaulini breaks the most exposed stretch of the route.

## Cabaña Museo Federico Nielsen Reyes

Named for the Bolivian historian, this cabaña-museum reflects the way culture and shelter intertwine in the Andes. The Apolobamba is also home to the Kallawaya, the itinerant healers whose knowledge of Andean medicinal plants earned UNESCO recognition. Lodging here is as much an encounter with living tradition as a place to sleep.

## Refugio Huayna Potosi and the high camps

South of the Apolobamba proper, in the Cordillera Real near La Paz, Huayna Potosi at 6,088 metres is the most climbed 6,000-metre peak in Bolivia, and its refuge system is the best developed in the country. The base Refugio Huayna Potosi sits by the Zongo Pass dam at around 4,700 metres, while the higher Refugio Huayna Potosi Alto Campo, near 5,200 metres, is the launch point for the summit. Many first-time high-altitude climbers cut their teeth here precisely because the shelter chain is so good.

## Cabaña Laguna Vizcachani

Beside one of the cold glacial tarns that dot the range, Cabaña Laguna Vizcachani is the kind of lakeside shelter that makes a long trekking day finish well. The vizcacha — a rabbit-like rodent of the rocks — gives the place its name, and you will likely see them sunning themselves on the boulders at dawn.

## Refugio El Alto

Named for the sprawling city above La Paz that serves as the gateway to the whole region, Refugio El Alto is the kind of staging post where parties gather, sort gear, and acclimatise before heading into the cordillera. El Alto airport, at over 4,000 metres, is itself one of the highest international airports in the world.

## Cabaña Laguna Copaiba and the lake shelters

The high Andean lakes are a recurring feature of shelter here. Cabaña Laguna Copaiba is one of several lakeside cabañas that double as fishing camps and trekkers' rest stops. The water is glacier-fed and astonishingly clear, and the silence at these altitudes is total but for the wind.

## Refugio Doña Elvira and the family shelters

Much Andean hospitality runs through families rather than clubs. Places like Refugio Doña Elvira and the various community cabañas — Cabaña La Espartana among them — are run by local people who open their homes and outbuildings to walkers. Expect a hot soup, a stack of blankets, and conversation in a mix of Spanish, Aymara, and Quechua.

## Refugio Vista Panoramica and Refugio de Yunga Pampa

On the descent from the high passes, the land drops fast toward the Amazon basin, and shelters such as Refugio Vista Panorámica Huayna Potosí and Refugio de Yunga Pampa mark the transition. Yunga refers to the warm, humid montane forest below the puna grassland — within a day's walk you can go from glacier to cloud forest, one of the great elevation gradients of South America.

## Refugio Luna Verde and the trekkers' outposts

Smaller outposts like Cabaña Luna Verde round out the network, often little more than a single room with a wood stove. They exist because the Apolobamba traverse, though seldom walked, is one of the finest high routes in the Andes, and even a minimal roof transforms a brutally exposed crossing into something achievable.

## The condors and the wildlife

The Apolobamba is one of the surest places in the Andes to see the Andean condor, the largest flying bird in the world, with a wingspan reaching over three metres. They ride the thermals along the ridges in numbers, and the range was given protected status partly to safeguard them; the Apolobamba National Integrated Management Natural Area covers a vast swathe of the cordillera. Walkers also share the high puna with vicuñas — the wild relatives of the alpaca, prized for their fine wool — and with the vizcachas that sun on the rocks. The lakes hold introduced trout, and the high grasslands shelter the rare Andean cat. Sleeping in a remote refuge here means waking to a soundscape of wind, distant flocks, and almost nothing else.

## Vicuñas, llamas, and the pastoral life

Shelter in the Apolobamba cannot be separated from the herding life that sustains it. The villages of Curva, Pelechuco, and Antaquilla are home to communities who graze llamas and alpacas on the high bofedales — the boggy cushion-plant meadows that stay green year round and feed the herds through the dry season. Many refuges and cabañas double as part of a working estancia, and the people who keep them are herders first and hosts second. Buying woven goods, sharing coca leaves, and respecting the rhythms of the agricultural calendar are part of travelling well here. The Kallawaya healing tradition, still practised around Curva, adds a layer of cultural depth found in few other mountain ranges.

## Planning a crossing

The dry season, roughly May to September, is the only sensible window; the wet months bring deep cloud and impassable mud. Acclimatise in La Paz or El Alto first, hire an arriero with mules in Curva or Pelechuco, and carry everything you cannot guarantee finding — fuel, most food, a proper sleeping bag rated well below freezing. Permits and a local guide are wise given how remote and trackless the range becomes.
