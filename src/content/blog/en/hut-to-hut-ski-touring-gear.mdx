## The Fundamental Tension: Safety Weight vs. Comfort Weight

Hut-to-hut ski touring sits in a gear calculus that pure mountaineering and pure resort skiing both avoid. You are moving through serious avalanche terrain with a loaded pack, but you are also sleeping in a warm hut with a kitchen, a boot drier, and a blanket. The gear decisions that matter are therefore the ones that trade safety function for weight reduction, not the luxury items — those are easy to leave home.

A hut tour with seven people and a guide carries a different risk profile than a four-person group travelling independently through unfamiliar terrain. The lists below assume a competent independent party of two to four people on a route like the classic Haute Route (Chamonix to Zermatt, 7 days), the Ortler Hochtouren in the Stelvio region, or a shorter but serious traverse in the Silvretta or Ötztaler Alps.

## Skis and Binding Systems

For hut-to-hut touring, skis in the 80-to-90 mm underfoot range strike the appropriate balance between flotation in unpacked snow and manageable weight. Full-fat freeride skis above 100 mm are heavy and make the uphill segments punishing without offering proportionate benefit on tracked or variable spring snow. Race-oriented touring skis below 75 mm underfoot are efficient on the ascent but underpowered in the descents that multi-day Haute Route terrain demands.

Tech (pin) bindings are the standard for serious hut touring — lighter than frame bindings, efficient in tour mode, and sufficient in descent mode for the controlled skiing that alpine terrain requires. Frame bindings suit beginners and those prioritising downhill security. Hybrid systems have closed the gap in both weight and performance. Whatever system you choose, ski crampons (Harscheisen) are not optional — a six-day traverse will encounter at least two or three sections of icy morning crust or wind slab where climbing without them is impractical.

## Skins and Skin Adhesive

Climbing skins should match the ski width to within a few millimetres — skins that are too narrow lose grip at the edges on traversing slopes; skins that overhang damage the ski base over time. Nylon-mohair blend skins offer a better glide-to-grip ratio than pure nylon and are worth the additional cost for a week-long tour.

Carry a small amount of skin-specific adhesive wax in case the glue degrades in cold temperatures — skin glue loses adhesion rapidly below minus 15 Celsius, and an early March tour in the Engadin or the Aosta Valley will encounter such temperatures. A skin repair strap (a simple buckle device) weighs almost nothing and handles the common failure of glue detachment on a steep ascent.

## Avalanche Safety Gear: Non-Negotiable and Non-Reducible

Every member of an independent ski touring party must carry a digital three-antenna avalanche transceiver (beacon), a probe of at least 240 cm length, and an aluminium shovel with a T-grip handle. These three items are not per-group items — they are per-person items. A buried skier cannot self-rescue; the only practical avalanche rescue is a fast companion rescue, and it requires three independent sets of equipment working simultaneously.

Avalanche airbag packs add a meaningful layer of protection and have become lighter (around 2 kg for the airbag system) in recent years. Their benefit is greatest in soft avalanches and in situations where the deployment decision is made before the avalanche accelerates. They do not replace transceiver-probe-shovel and should be considered an addition, not a substitution.

Carry the avalanche safety equipment on your person or in your pack's designated safety compartment, accessible in under 10 seconds.

## Navigation and Emergency Communication

A GPS device (handheld or watch-integrated) with the relevant maps loaded offline is a primary navigation tool in whiteout conditions. AV maps at 1:25,000 loaded on a Garmin Fenix or an Outdoor phone cover the most serious touring areas of the Alps. A paper backup of the key day's route — printed from eAV+ or Swisstopo — adds negligible weight and provides a reference that does not depend on battery life.

A mobile phone with local SIM or a roaming data plan covers emergency calls in most hut-to-hut terrain in the Alps. Coverage gaps exist — the Valais high terrain and parts of the Ötztal have dead zones. A PLB (personal locator beacon) or satellite communicator (Garmin inReach, SPOT) removes the coverage dependency and is strongly advised for routes with significant glacier terrain or extended sections without phone signal.

## What the Hut Provides

Understanding what a staffed alpine hut provides in winter/spring ski touring season prevents you from carrying unnecessary duplicate items:

Most ÖAV, DAV, and SAC huts staffed for ski touring provide mattresses and blankets or duvets. A sleeping bag liner is recommended rather than a full sleeping bag — it adds 150 to 200 grams and covers the hygiene gap. Indoor hut slippers or camp shoes (crocs, lightweight sandals) save the boot drier from having to handle all your damp gear at once. Most huts have drying rooms adjacent to the boot room, usually heated.

Meals — dinner and breakfast — are provided at most staffed huts. Carry lunch for the trail (energy bars, dried fruit, salami, hard cheese). A water bottle or thermos for hot drinks on the ascent; the hut will fill it in the morning.

## Clothing and Layering

The ski touring layering system: a merino or synthetic base layer, a light insulating mid-layer (100-weight fleece or thin down jacket), and a waterproof hardshell. In March and April, the hardshell sees less use than in November-December touring, but carry it. A lightweight insulating jacket for summit stops and cold descents should fit over the hardshell.

Helmet: mandatory for any glacier terrain and for descents on which falls have serious consequences. Sun protection at altitude is severe — SPF 50 minimum on all exposed skin, glacier glasses with full lateral UV protection.

## Rope, Harness, and Crevasse Rescue

On any route crossing crevassed glacier, each party member must carry a harness and know how to use it. A 30-metre 8 mm half rope is the touring standard — lighter than a full mountaineering rope and adequate for a two to four person party's crevasse rescue system. Prussik loops (two per person), ice axe, and crampons complete the glacier travel kit. The ice axe also serves as a self-arrest tool on steep firn descents above the huts.
