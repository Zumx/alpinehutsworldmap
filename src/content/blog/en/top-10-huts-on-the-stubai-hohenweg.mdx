The Stubai Höhenweg is one of the great hut circuits of the Austrian Alps —
a 60-kilometre traverse of the Stubai Alps that connects eight mountain huts
across terrain ranging from glaciated passes to flower-filled high valleys.
Starting and finishing in the Stubai valley, the route takes 5–7 days and gains
over 9000 m of elevation across its stages. It is more demanding than the
Tour du Mont Blanc but more accessible than the Haute Route; it is the
archetype of Austrian ÖAV circuit hiking at its best.

## 1. Sulzenauhütte, 2191 m — Stubaital, Tirol

The Sulzenauhütte at 2191 m in the main Stubai valley approach is the classic
first or last overnight on the circuit. It sits above the Sulzenau waterfall
in a spectacular glacial valley with direct views of the Wilder Freiger
(3418 m). Owned by the ÖAV Nuremberg section; capacity around 130. Full half-
board service from mid-June to end of September. The approach from Ranalt in
the Stubai valley takes 2.5–3 hours. The terrain above the hut — the Sulzenau
Glacier and the Pfaff Saddle — provides access to the high circuit.

## 2. Nürnberger Hütte, 2297 m — Stubaital, upper circuit

On the eastern circuit above the Stubai valley floor, the Nürnberger Hütte
at 2297 m serves as a high-level base with access to the Wilder Freiger
normal route and the Trögler. ÖAV Nuremberg section; capacity around 100.
The classic Nürnberger panorama — Wilder Freiger, Wilder Pfaff, and the
Zuckerhütl (3505 m, the highest peak in the Stubai Alps) on the right —
is one of the great hut terraces in Tyrol.

## 3. Bremer Hütte, 2413 m — Schrandele area, Tirol

On the high section of the Höhenweg above the Pinnistal valley, the Bremer
Hütte at 2413 m is a classic ÖAV Schutzhütte with capacity for approximately
85 guests. The approach from the Pinnistal valley through the forest and up
the Schrandele takes 3–4 hours. The stage between the Bremer and the
Innsbrucker Hütte crosses the Pflerscher Höhenweg at elevations above 2800 m —
the highest terrain of the standard circuit.

## 4. Innsbrucker Hütte, 2369 m — Brennertal approach, Tirol

The Innsbrucker Hütte at 2369 m above the Padaster valley serves the western
stages of the Höhenweg and provides access to the Habicht (3277 m) — the
landmark summit visible from Innsbruck. ÖAV Innsbruck section; capacity around
100. The stage to the Dresdner Hütte crosses the Stubaier Gletscher ski area
and is the most infrastructure-rich section of the circuit.

## 5. Dresdner Hütte, 2308 m — Stubaier Gletscher, Tirol

At the foot of the Stubaier Gletscher ski area's main cable car system, the
Dresdner Hütte at 2308 m is the most accessibly reached of the Höhenweg huts —
a cable car from Mutterberg brings guests to just below the hut. Capacity
around 100; full restaurant service and a terrace with views of the ski area
glacier above. The contrast between the ski resort infrastructure and the
traditional Höhenweg character here is one of the circuit's interesting tensions.

## 6. Neue Regensburger Hütte, 2286 m — Windachtal, Tirol

On the stage between the Dresdner and the circuit's return to the Sulzenau
area, the Neue Regensburger Hütte at 2286 m provides a quieter and more
traditional overnight in the Windach valley. ÖAV Regensburg section; capacity
around 70. The route toward the Freiger Ferner glacier approach passes above
the hut.

## 7. Starkenburger Hütte, 2237 m — Sellrain, Tirol

Slightly off the main Höhenweg on the Sellrain connection, the Starkenburger
at 2237 m provides an alternative approach or exit stage for the circuit from
the north. ÖAV capacity around 50; useful for parties extending the circuit
into the Sellrain Alps.

## 8. Wildebachhütte, 1793 m — Pinnistal valley

A lower-elevation hut in the Pinnistal valley that serves as a connection
point between the Bremer Hütte stage and the valley. Less used by through-
hikers but popular with day visitors from the valley.

## 9. Kaserstatthütte, 1837 m — Stubaital, lower section

A valley-level ÖAV hut serving the lower Stubai valley and the approach from
Schönberg. More accessible than the circuit huts and used for non-technical
day walks.

## 10. Schlicker Alm, 1612 m — Stubai foothills

A traditional Tiroler Alm (high pasture) guesthouse below the Höhenweg
circuit, serving the approach from Fulpmes in the lower Stubai valley. The
cattle farming character of the Stubai foothills is visible here at its
most authentic.

## Planning the Stubai Höhenweg circuit

The standard guidebook is Stubai Rucksack Route by Cicerone (Kev Reynolds),
or the ÖAV's own Stubaier Höhenweg route description. The circuit is
typically completed in 5–7 days, with most parties taking 6 days and a
rest day option at the Sulzenauhütte or the Dresdner Hütte. The direction
of travel — clockwise or anticlockwise — makes little difference in terms
of difficulty, but the clockwise direction (starting with the Sulzenauhütte
and ending via the Dresdner) is the most common.

The Stubai Höhenweg season runs from mid-June to early October; the exact
opening dates of each hut are posted on the ÖAV (Österreichischer Alpenverein)
website each spring. A late-season traverse (September) offers the best
chance of settled weather and autumn colour in the lower valleys, but
the highest sections — the Pflerscher Höhenweg above 2800 m — may carry
early snowfall from mid-September.

ÖAV membership entitles members to the member's rate at all ÖAV huts,
which is approximately 50% less than the non-member rate. German Alpine
Club (DAV) members receive reciprocal rates. International visitors who
plan more than three nights in ÖAV huts per year generally find it
economically worthwhile to purchase a temporary membership.

## The Zuckerhütl and summit options

The Stubai Höhenweg does not require glacier travel or technical climbing
on the standard circuit, but the surrounding terrain offers excellent
options for those who want to extend the experience. The Zuckerhütl
(3505 m) — the highest summit in the Stubai Alps — can be ascended from
the Dresdner Hütte with crampons and ice axe, typically as a pre-dawn
start before the day's Höhenweg stage. The Wilder Freiger (3418 m) from
the Nürnberger Hütte and the Habicht (3277 m) from the Innsbrucker Hütte
are similar aspirations: achievable for fit trekkers willing to hire a
guide or climb with an experienced companion.

The Stubaier Gletscher above the Dresdner Hütte is one of the most
accessible glaciers in the Tirol for general visitors — a cable car from
Mutterberg runs a year-round ski operation that also provides summer
tourists with glacier access to 3160 m. Walking on the glacier is possible
on the marked summer glacier path from the top cable car station, providing
an easy introduction to glacier terrain for those who have not previously
been on glacial ice.

## Characterising the Stubai Alps

The Stubai Alps differ from the Ötztal Alps (immediately to the west) in
being slightly lower, more hospitable in character, and less glacier-
dominated. The valleys — Stubai, Sellrain, Pinnistal — are well-served
by public transport from Innsbruck, which is unusual for a major alpine
circuit. The Stubaital Bus connects Innsbruck with Fulpmes and Ranalt
in the valley (30–40 minutes); the circuit start and end are therefore
easy to reach without a car. For walkers based in Innsbruck, the Stubai
circuit is accessible on the first morning without an overnight stay
elsewhere.
