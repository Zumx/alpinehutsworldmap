The Dolomites contain more than 250 staffed rifugi, yet a handful transcend their practical function as overnight shelters and become destinations in their own right. Five of the most celebrated — Lagazuoi, Auronzo, Fanes, Scotoni, and Nuvolau — each occupy a distinct kind of grandeur, whether that means perching above a wartime tunnel system, staring at the North Face of the Tre Cime, or standing as the oldest stone-built hut in the entire range. Together they illustrate how varied the Dolomite rifugio experience can be, even within a mountain group that covers barely a hundred kilometres from end to end.

## Rifugio Lagazuoi — History Under Your Feet

At 2,778 metres on the saddle between the Falzarego Pass and the summit plateau of Lagazuoi, this rifugio owes much of its fame to what lies directly beneath it. During the First World War, Austrian and Italian engineers drove more than four kilometres of galleries into the mountain, including a shaft built to pack an enormous explosive charge that detonated on 20 June 1917, blowing the top off the Austrian position. Today visitors can descend those same tunnels with a headlamp in about 45 minutes, emerging at the base of the rock face near the cable-car station at Falzarego.

The hut itself has 70 beds spread across private rooms and a Matratzenlager, a terrace that faces the Tofane group, and a reputation for exceptionally good food for its altitude. The cable car from Passo Falzarego (2,105 m) makes it accessible to non-climbers, and the Tiroler Höhenweg and Alta Via 1 both pass directly through the saddle, ensuring a cosmopolitan mix of day visitors, long-distance trekkers, and guided climbing parties throughout the July-to-October season.

## Rifugio Auronzo — The Gateway to the Tre Cime

Few rifugi on earth are positioned with more visual impact than Auronzo, which sits at 2,333 metres on a broad ledge immediately below the southern walls of the Tre Cime di Lavaredo. The three towers — Cima Grande (2,999 m), Cima Ovest (2,973 m), and Cima Piccola (2,857 m) — fill the view from every window. On clear mornings the rock turns from deep grey to orange to cream as the sun rises behind the Cadini group to the east.

The hut is reachable by a paved toll road from Misurina, which means it receives thousands of visitors per day in high season and is deliberately less intimate than more remote alternatives. It has around 160 beds, a large restaurant, a shop, and a first-aid point. Its strategic position makes it the natural start for the classic circumnavigation of the Tre Cime — roughly 9.5 kilometres and 500 metres of ascent — a loop that also passes the Rifugio Lavaredo and the Rifugio Locatelli. Despite its crowds, an early-morning weekday arrival before the toll road opens brings a near-total silence that the afternoon never delivers.

## Rifugio Fanes — Solitude in the Heart of a Legend

Rifugio Fanes lies at 2,060 metres inside the Parco Naturale Fanes-Sennes-Braies, a protected plateau landscape of wide meadows, limestone pavements, and dolines that the surrounding peaks — Cima Scotoni, Col Bechei, and Cima delle Dieci — enclose like an amphitheatre. The Ladin-speaking communities of the Val Badia have long associated this plateau with the mythological kingdom of the Fanes, and the hut carries that cultural weight lightly but perceptibly in its decor and the bilingual Italian-Ladin menus.

Access requires at least two hours on foot from any direction: the most popular approach climbs from Pederü in the Val Badia via the Ütia de Börz, gaining roughly 700 metres. The hut offers around 90 beds and is a central node on the Dolomites' Alta Via 1, meaning a majority of guests are mid-trek rather than day visitors. The result is a communal atmosphere around long shared tables that is rare in more accessible huts. The terrain above the hut, including the demanding Via Ferrata dei Leoni to the summit of Cima Scotoni (2,874 m), rewards those who can spend two nights rather than one.

## Rifugio Scotoni — Cliff-Edge Drama Above Lago di Limides

Just below the Passo di Limo at 1,985 metres, Rifugio Scotoni clings to a rock ledge above the small green lake of Limides, with the sheer 800-metre south face of Cima Scotoni rising behind it. The hut was built in the late 1960s and expanded in the 1990s; it now sleeps around 80 guests in rooms and dormitories. Its terrace is arguably the finest balcony view in the entire Val Badia sector, with the Lagazuoi massif directly across the valley and the smooth limestone walls of the Fanis group overhead.

The hut serves as a natural waypoint between the Rifugio Lagazuoi circuit and the Fanes plateau, and it appears on the itinerary of the Alta Via 1 on the third day of most guided tours. Rock climbers use it as a base for the classic routes on the Scotoni south face, a wall developed extensively by Italian climbers in the 1950s and 1960s and still regarded as one of the serious Dolomite faces. The kitchen is small but reliable, and the warden family has been running the rifugio for more than three decades.

## Rifugio Nuvolau — The Oldest Stone Hut in the Dolomites

Standing at 2,575 metres on the summit of Monte Nuvolau above the Cinque Torri group and the Falzarego Pass, the Rifugio Nuvolau holds a distinction no other hut in these mountains can claim: it was built in 1883, making it the oldest stone-built rifugio in the Dolomites. The original structure survives largely intact, a compact two-storey building of pale Dolomite limestone with a corrugated-iron roof that can be seen from the Falzarego road on clear days.

The ascent from the cable-car station at Cinque Torri takes approximately 45 minutes over steep but path-worn ground, gaining 280 metres. The summit platform offers a 360-degree panorama that on cloudless mornings includes the Marmolada glacier (3,343 m), the Civetta, the Pelmo, and on exceptional days the summit cross of the Gran Sasso in Abruzzo, more than 500 kilometres to the south. The hut has only 20 beds, making advance booking essential in August, but the small capacity is precisely what preserves its character: dinners are shared at a single long table, the warden pours the house wine generously, and by nine o'clock the lights are out.

## Planning a Circuit That Links All Five

A five-day walking circuit connecting all the rifugi described here is entirely feasible between late June and early October. A standard itinerary starts at Passo Falzarego (cable car to Lagazuoi, night 1), descends via the wartime tunnels to the Valparola road, crosses the Fanes plateau to Rifugio Fanes (night 2), traverses to Rifugio Scotoni via the Passo di Limo trail (night 3), continues north through the Cadini di Misurina to Rifugio Auronzo (night 4), and returns via Misurina and a shuttle bus to Falzarego for the ascent to Nuvolau (night 5). Total distance is approximately 65 kilometres with 3,800 metres of cumulative ascent. All five huts require advance booking for bed-nights; Auronzo in particular fills its private rooms by March for the August weekends.
