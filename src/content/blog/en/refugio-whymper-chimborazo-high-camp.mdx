There is a curious distinction attached to the summit above the Refugio Whymper: because the Earth bulges at the equator, the peak of Chimborazo is the point on the planet's surface that lies farthest from its centre. At 6,263 metres it is not the highest mountain by altitude above sea level, but measured from the core of the Earth it reaches higher than Everest. The Refugio Whymper sits at around 5,000 metres on the volcano's flank, a stone shelter that takes its name from Edward Whymper, the English alpinist who made the first ascent in 1880 with the Italian guides Jean-Antoine and Louis Carrel.

## The Farthest Mountain from the Centre of the Earth

Chimborazo dominates the central Ecuadorian Andes, rising in solitary bulk above the Reserva de Producción de Fauna Chimborazo near the city of Riobamba. It is a long-dormant volcano, snow-capped and glaciated, whose summit was for a time believed to be the highest mountain in the world before the Himalaya were measured. Edward Whymper, already famous for the first ascent of the Matterhorn in 1865, came to Ecuador specifically to study the effects of altitude and to climb its great volcanoes. His successful ascent of Chimborazo with the Carrel cousins, themselves Matterhorn veterans, is one of the landmark events of Andean mountaineering.

The reserve around the mountain is home to herds of wild vicuña, reintroduced to the high paramo, and the road that skirts its base is among the highest in Ecuador. The combination of altitude, history and that unique geophysical claim makes Chimborazo a magnet for climbers from around the world.

## Two Refuges on the Flank

Chimborazo has two refuges stacked on its lower slopes. The Refugio Carrel sits at around 4,800 metres and is reached by road; from there a steep walking trail of perhaps half an hour climbs to the Refugio Whymper at roughly 5,000 metres. The lower hut serves day visitors and acclimatising trekkers, while the higher Whymper refuge is the true climbers' base, the place where summit teams rest before the midnight start. Both are stone buildings designed to withstand the ferocious winds that scour the exposed flank.

The Whymper refuge offers dormitory bunks, a kitchen and dining area, and the spartan comforts of any high-altitude base. Conditions are basic and cold, and water and supplies must be carried up. The naming of these two huts after Whymper and Carrel deliberately commemorates the partnership that first reached the summit, a fitting tribute on the very mountain they conquered.

## The Summit Climb

The ascent of Chimborazo is a serious high-altitude glacier climb, harder and more committing than neighbouring Cotopaxi. The standard route, the Whymper or Normal route, climbs through loose volcanic rock and scree before gaining the glacier, where crampons, ice axe, rope and crevasse-rescue competence become essential. Rockfall on the lower section is a recognised hazard, and the route has been altered over the years as the glacier has retreated and the mountain has shed loose stone.

Summit day starts around 11 PM or midnight from the refuge. The climb gains more than 1,200 metres to the summit plateau, which holds two main tops: the Ventimilla summit and, slightly higher and farther on, the Whymper summit, the true high point. The full round trip can take eleven to fourteen hours and demands good acclimatisation, fitness and steady conditions. It is widely regarded as one of the most demanding of the accessible 6,000-metre peaks.

## Acclimatisation and the Ecuadorian Build-Up

No one should arrive in Ecuador and head straight for Chimborazo. The mountain sits at the top of a logical progression of Ecuadorian peaks that climbers use to acclimatise. A typical build-up begins on the Rucu Pichincha above Quito, moves to the Iliniza Norte at 5,126 metres, and includes an ascent of Cotopaxi from the Refugio José Ribas before the final and hardest objective of Chimborazo. Each step adds altitude and time for the body to adapt.

This staged approach is why Ecuador is so prized: within a single two- or three-week trip, a fit and competent mountaineer can climb a sequence of high volcanoes culminating in the farthest point from the Earth's centre. Quito, at 2,850 metres, is the natural hub, and acclimatisation begins simply by living there for a few days.

## Ecuador's Volcano Refuges

The Refugio Whymper belongs to a network of mountain refuges that thread through Ecuador's volcanic spine. On Cotopaxi the Refugio José Ribas serves the snow line; the Refugio del Volcán Pichincha guards the peaks above the capital; and the Tungurahua Refugio and the Refugio de Montaña Cotopaxi Cara Sur extend the system to other volcanoes. Beyond the high country, in the Amazonian foothills, refuges and eco-lodges such as the Cabañas Oyacachi and the lodges around the Sumaco massif round out a remarkably varied set of mountain shelters.

This network, much of it built and maintained by climbing clubs, makes the great equatorial volcanoes accessible to a wide range of mountaineers in a way that few other ranges can match.

## Practical Notes

Chimborazo lies within a protected reserve, and a certified guide is required for any glacier ascent. The most stable climbing seasons are generally December to January and the drier window from June to September, though high winds can shut the mountain down at any time. The summit is significantly colder and windier than Cotopaxi, so a robust layering system, a windproof shell, warm mitts, goggles and high-protection glacier glasses are all essential.

Above all, respect the altitude. Chimborazo is the high point of an Ecuadorian climbing trip in every sense, and it rewards only those who have built up to it patiently.
