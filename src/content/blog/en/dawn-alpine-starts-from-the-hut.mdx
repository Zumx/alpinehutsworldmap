The alarm goes at half past two. In the dark dormitory, headtorch beams crisscross the bunks, zips rasp, and from below comes the clatter of the warden setting out flasks of coffee and baskets of bread. Outside the windows the glacier glows faintly under the stars, and the air that leaks through the door is sharp with cold. Within an hour a line of small lights will be threading up the moraine in single file. This is the alpine start, the pre-dawn departure that defines a summit day in the high mountains, and learning to do it well is one of the quiet rituals of serious mountaineering.

## What the Alpine Start Is

The alpine start is the practice of leaving the hut or bivouac in the dark, often between two and four in the morning, to be high on the route by sunrise. It is not bravado or an arbitrary tradition; it is a precise response to how the mountain changes through the day. The same slope that is safe and firm at four can be lethal by noon, and the alpine start exists to put the climber in the right place at the right time, when conditions are at their best and the dangers at their lowest.

For anyone new to the high mountains, the first alpine start is a strange experience: dressing in the cold dark, eating breakfast when the body wants sleep, and walking out into a black landscape lit only by a small lamp. But the logic becomes obvious with experience, and the early hours often turn into the most magical of the day, with the summit glowing pink at dawn while the valleys far below are still drowned in shadow and the world feels entirely your own.

## Why So Early: The Snow

The first and oldest reason for the alpine start is the snow. Overnight, when temperatures drop below freezing, the snowpack hardens into a firm, stable surface that holds the weight of a climber and resists sliding. This frozen crust is what makes glacier travel and snow slopes safe and efficient: crampons bite cleanly, snow bridges over crevasses stay strong, and steep snow can be climbed quickly. As the sun rises and the day warms, that same snow softens, turning to heavy slush that swallows the leg to the knee, weakens snow bridges, and dramatically raises the avalanche risk.

This is why the timing is so unforgiving. A snow slope crossed safely at five in the morning may be a wet-snow avalanche trap by eleven, and a firm snow bridge at dawn may collapse under the same climber on the way down at midday. The alpine start is built around being on the steep, snowy, glaciated ground while it is still frozen and being off it, or back on safe terrain, before the afternoon thaw turns the mountain dangerous.

## Why So Early: Rockfall, Storms and Time

Snow is not the only reason. On rocky routes, the freeze-thaw cycle is a constant hazard: ice that cements loose blocks in place overnight melts as the sun hits the face, releasing rockfall down gullies and couloirs through the late morning and afternoon. Climbing a couloir at dawn, while everything is still frozen solid, is far safer than being caught in it when the sun has loosened the stones above.

Then there is weather. In summer the Alps run on a daily rhythm of building heat that often breaks into afternoon thunderstorms, lethal for anyone exposed on a ridge or summit. The alpine start buys the time to reach the top and begin descending before the clouds build, getting climbers off the high, exposed ground before the storm arrives. Finally there is simple arithmetic: a long route with a summit and a descent needs many hours, and starting before dawn leaves a margin of daylight and safety that a late start throws away.

## The Night Before: Preparing for a Smooth Departure

A good alpine start is made the evening before. The experienced climber lays everything out before sleeping: boots and gaiters by the bunk, harness with crampons and ice axe clipped ready, headtorch with fresh batteries within reach, water bottle filled, and the pack repacked so nothing needs hunting for in the dark. Clothes for the cold first hour are set aside, and the route, the timings, and the turnaround time are agreed with partners so no decisions need making at half past two.

Equally important is the body. Eating well at dinner, drinking enough, and getting to sleep early all matter, because the alpine start steals hours from the night and the climb demands energy on little rest. Filling water bottles and preparing breakfast or grabbing what the warden has left out the night before saves precious minutes. The smoothest departures look almost wordless: people who prepared the evening before simply rise, dress, eat, and walk out, while the unprepared rummage and delay in the dark.

## Hut Etiquette in the Small Hours

Leaving a shared hut at three in the morning is a test of consideration, because while you are starting your summit day, dozens of others may be sleeping. Good etiquette is unspoken but firm: pack your bag in the corridor or boot room rather than the dormitory, keep your headtorch dipped and your voice to a whisper, avoid rustling plastic bags and slamming doors, and never switch on the main dormitory lights. The careful climber moves like a shadow, gathering gear quietly and slipping out without waking the room.

Wardens usually support the early starters, setting out a simple breakfast the night before or rising to brew coffee, and the courteous response is to be quick, tidy, and grateful. Sign out if the hut has a board, so that in an emergency someone knows you are on the mountain. The unwritten rule cuts both ways: those staying in bed accept some inevitable noise, and those leaving keep it to the absolute minimum. A well-run hut at four in the morning is a quiet, purposeful place, full of people moving softly toward the dark.

## Walking into the Dark

The first steps from the hut are disorienting: a small circle of headtorch light, the crunch of boots on frozen ground, the cold biting at fingers not yet warmed by movement. Navigation in the dark demands attention, following cairns, paint marks, or a track of earlier bootprints, and it is easy to lose the route in the featureless black, so most parties move close together and check the map and altimeter often. Within an hour the body warms, the rhythm settles, and the first grey light begins to seep into the sky ahead.

Then comes the reward that keeps climbers loving the alpine start despite the brutal hour: the dawn. The summits catch the first sun and burn orange while the climber is still in shadow, the cold eases, and the whole purpose of the early rise becomes self-evident. To stand high on a frozen slope as the sun breaks over a sea of peaks, hours of safe climbing already behind you, is one of mountaineering's great experiences, and it is available only to those willing to leave the hut while the rest of the world sleeps.
