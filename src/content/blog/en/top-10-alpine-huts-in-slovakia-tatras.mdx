The High Tatras — Vysoké Tatry — rise abruptly from the Central European plains along the Slovak-Polish border, forming the smallest alpine range in the world to exceed 2,600 metres. Compressed into a massif barely 26 kilometres long and 15 wide, the peaks concentrate a remarkable density of glacial lakes, steep granite ridges, and shelters that have served mountaineers since the mid-nineteenth century. Slovak mountain shelters are called chaty (singular chata), and several have operated continuously for over a century, maintained today by the Slovak Mountain Rescue Service and private operators. The hiking season runs from late June to early October, though some lower huts open from May; winter skiing brings visitors through March at the resort-adjacent shelters. This guide covers ten of the most significant places to sleep in the Slovak Tatras, with notes on altitude, access, and what makes each one worth the effort.

## 1. Téryho chata, Malá Studená dolina

Perched at 2,015 metres in the Malá Studená dolina (Small Cold Valley), Téryho chata is the highest permanently staffed mountain hut in Slovakia and one of the most photographed. Built in 1899 and named after the Hungarian mountaineer Edmund Téry, the stone building overlooks five glacial tarns known as the Five Spis Lakes. The approach from Tatranská Lomnica takes around four hours on red-marked trail. The hut sleeps roughly 24 in dormitory style, serves hot meals, and fills up weeks in advance for July and August weekends. Views east toward Lomnický štít are superb at dawn.

## 2. Chata pri Zelenom plese, Green Lake

Chata pri Zelenom plese sits at 1,551 metres beside the Zelené pleso (Green Lake), one of the deepest glacial lakes in the Tatras at over 20 metres. The hut has operated since 1944 and was rebuilt after fire damage; it now offers comfortable rooms in addition to dormitory beds, a full restaurant, and sauna access — unusual luxury for a mountain shelter. Access from Tatranská Lomnica is straightforward along a well-graded path in about two hours. It makes an excellent base for routes toward Veľký Mlynár and the Jahňací štít ridge.

## 3. Horský hotel Popradské pleso, Poprad Lake

At 1,494 metres in the southern Tatras, the Horský hotel Popradské pleso occupies one of the most scenic positions in the whole range, reflecting the granite peaks of Rysy and Mengusovské štíty in the lake's still surface. A rack railway ran here until the 1970s; today the easiest access is a two-hour walk from Štrbské Pleso resort. The hotel dates from the early twentieth century and has been modernised into a full mountain hotel with en-suite rooms, a restaurant, and terrace seating. At this altitude, larch forests give way to dwarf pine, and the transition creates memorable autumn colour in September.

## 4. Chata pod Soliskom, Solisko Ridge

Directly accessible by gondola lift from Štrbské Pleso, Chata pod Soliskom stands at 1,840 metres on the shoulder of Predné Solisko and acts as the starting point for popular ridge walks toward Furkotský štít. Because of the lift, it receives heavy day-tripper traffic in summer and serves as a ski-touring destination in winter and spring. The hut itself is a solid timber building with a self-service restaurant and limited overnight beds; it does not require the same booking lead time as more remote shelters. The panorama across the Štrbské Pleso basin and west toward the Liptov plain rewards even a brief visit.

## 5. Rainerova chata, Zelené pleso basin

Rainerova chata, at approximately 1,300 metres in the lower Zelená dolina, predates most High Tatras shelters — it was established in 1908 by the Hungarian Carpathian Association, and its original stone walls survive within the later extensions. The hut serves as a transit point for walkers ascending toward Chata pri Zelenom plese or branching east toward the Kopské sedlo pass into Poland. Accommodation is in multi-bunk rooms; the kitchen produces hearty Slovak dishes including kapustnica soup and grilled trout. Outside the peak July-August window, the trails here are quiet enough to encounter red deer crossing the valley floor.

## 6. Zbojnícka chata, Veľká Studená dolina

Zbojnícka chata translates loosely as the Outlaw Hut, referencing the folk legends of mountain brigands that permeate Tatra culture. Situated at 1,960 metres in the Veľká Studená dolina (Great Cold Valley), it is one of the more challenging huts to reach, requiring a steep four-to-five-hour ascent from Tatranská Lomnica. The building has 27 beds, basic meals, and a wood-burning stove that feels essential in the evening even in August. From the hut, experienced hikers access routes to Pyšný štít (2,363 m) and the scenic Priečne sedlo saddle traversing to the adjacent valley. Advance reservation is essential from mid-July.

## 7. Chata Erika, Tatranská Kotlina area

Chata Erika sits at a lower elevation on the eastern periphery of the national park near Tatranská Kotlina, providing a comfortable and less crowded base for exploring the eastern valleys. The building is a traditional timber chata with a cosy interior, moderate room capacity, and a garden terrace ideal for long summer evenings. Trails from here connect to the Čierna hora ridge and the quieter border region near the Polish Belianske Tatry. It attracts families and walkers preferring a gentler pace rather than the extreme terrain of the central massif.

## 8. Chata Hermanka, Belianske Tatry

The Belianske Tatry are a protected sub-range east of the main massif where access is more restricted to preserve karst cave systems and rare flora. Chata Hermanka, at around 1,450 metres, is one of the few overnight options in this zone and serves walkers on the permitted educational path through the limestone plateau. The views north toward Poland and south over the Poprad basin are expansive and the summer wildflower meadows include protected species of gentian and saxifrage. Overnight capacity is limited and the hut requires advance booking even outside peak season.

## 9. Schronisko pod Durbaszką, Polish-Slovak border zone

Although technically on the Polish side of the Tatras watershed, Schronisko pod Durbaszką at 1,274 metres is closely linked to the Slovak trail network through the Kopské sedlo border crossing and is regularly used by walkers doing cross-border traverses. The hut belongs to the Polish Tatra Society (PTT) and provides dormitory and private room accommodation with meals. The cross-border E3 long-distance route passes nearby, and the combination of Slovak and Polish Tatras viewsheds — visible from the ridge above — makes it a worthwhile addition to any High Tatras itinerary.

## 10. Križkovská chata, Mlynická dolina

Križkovská chata sits in the Mlynická dolina at roughly 1,700 metres, an intermediate position making it accessible to moderate hikers but still firmly in the high mountain zone above the treeline. Stone walls and a corrugated metal roof give it the utilitarian look common to shelters built in the postwar Czechoslovak period. The hut is a staging point for ascents of Bystrá and Ľadový štít and sees a steady flow of climbers in summer. Food is basic but generous; the staff operate in conditions that require helicopter supply drops when the access trail is snowbound in late spring.
