The Mönchsjochhütte sits at 3,650 metres on a saddle of rock and ice above the Jungfraujoch, and for most of its life the warden could rely on a simple fact: the ground it stood on was frozen solid and would stay that way. That assumption no longer holds. Across the high Alps, the permafrost that has cemented summit ridges and rock spurs for thousands of years is thawing, and the huts anchored into it are beginning to shift, crack and, in a few alarming cases, slide. The future of the highest refuges is now a question of engineering, money and hard decisions about what can be saved.

## What Permafrost Does for a Hut

Permafrost is ground that stays below zero year-round, and in the high Alps it acts as a structural binder. It glues together the loose blocks of a frost-shattered ridge, holds boulders in place on steep slopes, and gives foundations something solid to grip. Many of the most spectacularly sited huts — perched on cols, spurs and rock teeth above 3,000 metres — owe their stability entirely to this frozen cement rather than to bedrock.

When the permafrost warms past zero, that binding fails. The blocks that the ice held together begin to move independently, slopes that looked permanent start to creep, and the rockfall season lengthens and intensifies. A hut bolted to such ground inherits every one of these movements. Cracks appear in walls, doors jam, terraces tilt, and in the worst cases whole structures are left straddling a slowly opening fissure.

## The Most Exposed Refuges

Some failures are already famous. The Refuge du Goûter on the normal route up Mont Blanc, rebuilt in 2013 at 3,835 metres as a futuristic steel ellipse, was designed in full knowledge of a destabilising slope and anchored deep into rock. The older Rifugio Carrel on the Matterhorn's Italian ridge and the various bivouacs of the Valais have all needed remedial work as their surroundings loosen.

Perhaps the most quoted case is the Refuge de l'Aigle in the Écrins, rebuilt in 2014, and the long monitoring of the Aiguille du Midi area above Chamonix, where guides report routes changing year on year. The Capanna Regina Margherita on the Signalkuppe, at 4,554 metres the highest building in Europe, sits on rock that is itself warming. Wardens at these altitudes now watch their own walls the way sailors watch the sky.

## How Movement Is Measured

Clubs no longer simply hope for the best. The Swiss, Italian, French and Austrian alpine clubs increasingly install monitoring on their highest huts: crack gauges across suspect joints, borehole thermometers that record ground temperature several metres down, and survey markers re-measured by GPS each season. Some sites carry tiltmeters that report in near real time, so that a sudden acceleration triggers an inspection before a season opens.

The data are sobering. Borehole records from sites such as the Stockhorn ridge and the Jungfraujoch show permafrost warming steadily, with the depth of summer thaw creeping downward. For a hut, the practical question becomes whether movement is slow and uniform, which can be managed, or differential, where one corner sinks faster than another and the whole frame is wrenched.

## Engineering the Response

Where a hut is worth saving, the engineering is heroic. Foundations are driven deep through the active thawing layer into colder rock below, sometimes with micro-piles grouted many metres down. Structures are made deliberately light and articulated, so they can flex with small movements rather than crack. The Goûter's steel skeleton is the showcase example: a building designed to be partly demountable, insulated to keep its own heat from thawing the ground beneath it.

That last point matters. A warm, occupied hut radiates heat into its foundations, accelerating the very thaw that threatens it. Newer designs lift the building clear of the ground on ventilated stilts, or insulate the underside heavily, so the structure no longer cooks its own footing. Composting toilets, solar arrays and careful thermal design all reduce the heat and waste that a high hut pushes into fragile surroundings.

## When a Hut Cannot Be Saved

Not every refuge can be rescued, and the clubs have begun making the hardest choice of all: relocation or abandonment. A hut whose access ridge has become a daily rockfall gallery may be moved to a safer shoulder, even if that means losing a historic site. Bivouacs, being small and often prefabricated, are sometimes simply helicoptered to new ground. Larger huts face demolition when repair costs outrun their value.

These decisions carry emotional weight. A hut is rarely just a building; it is a century of summit registers, club history and personal memory. Closing or moving one is a kind of mountain bereavement, and clubs handle it carefully, consulting members and often preserving names and artefacts even when the original walls come down.

## What It Means for the Hut-Goer

For those who walk and climb, the changes are already visible. Approach routes shift as moraines collapse and glaciers shrink away from their old margins, leaving steep, unstable slopes where there was once an easy snow ramp. Ladders and fixed ropes appear on sections that used to be a simple walk. Guidebook times become unreliable, and prudent parties check recent conditions reports from the warden rather than trusting an old description.

The honest message is that the highest huts are now managed assets in a changing landscape, not fixed points on the map. Booking ahead, reading current conditions, respecting closures imposed for instability, and accepting that a beloved refuge may one day be gone are all part of moving responsibly through the high Alps of the coming decades.
