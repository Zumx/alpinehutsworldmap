Trentino occupies a pivotal position in the Italian Alps — the autonomous province connects the Dolomites in the east with the glaciated Adamello-Presanella group in the west, and the Brenta Dolomites in its northern sector represent the only major Dolomite massif west of the Adige river. The Brenta is geologically and scenically exceptional: a compact group of vertical towers, deep couloirs, and connecting ledge systems that form one of the finest via ferrata networks in the Alps. The Rifugio network here is dense and well-maintained by the SAT (Società degli Alpinisti Tridentini), and most of the huts described below are associated with the celebrated Bocchette vie ferrate that traverse the high terrain of the Brenta.

## 1. Rifugio Tuckett e Sella, Brenta Dolomites

The Rifugio Tuckett e Sella at 2,272 metres in the western Brenta above Campiglio is named jointly after Francis Fox Tuckett, the English alpinist who first explored the Brenta in the 1860s, and Quintino Sella, the Italian statesman who co-founded the Italian Alpine Club in 1863. The hut is the central hub of the Bocchette route system and one of the busiest rifugi in the Dolomites, sleeping over 100 guests in summer. The approach from Madonna di Campiglio takes around two hours, and routes diverge from the hut to almost every significant peak in the western Brenta.

## 2. Rifugio Alimonta, Brenta Dolomites

Standing at 2,580 metres in the upper Brenta cirque above the Rifugio Tuckett, the Rifugio Alimonta is positioned directly on the Bocchette Alte route — the demanding high traverse of the Brenta that crosses narrow ledges and fixed-rope sections at over 2,800 metres. The hut was rebuilt in 1990 and sleeps around 70 guests. Its position gives superb views of the main Brenta group including the Cima Tosa, Cima d'Ambiez, and the dramatic Campanile Basso, considered one of the hardest rock pillars in the Alps.

## 3. Rifugio Brentei, Brenta Dolomites

The Rifugio Brentei at 2,182 metres in the Nardis valley on the eastern side of the Brenta above the Vallesinella waterfalls is the starting point for the classic ascent of the Campanile Basso (2,883 metres), a needle of rock first climbed in 1899 by Carlo Garbari and his companions after several failed attempts by leading European alpinists. The hut sleeps around 80 and is reached from Campiglio in about three hours. It is a genuine working mountaineering base, with a notice board listing current route conditions and a regular turnover of climbing parties.

## 4. Rifugio XII Apostoli, Brenta Dolomites

The Rifugio XII Apostoli at 2,489 metres in the high cirque of the eastern Brenta is named after the twelve rocky spires that form a jagged skyline above the hut. It sits on the Bocchette Centrali route and is one of the most dramatically positioned rifugi in the Brenta, enclosed on three sides by vertical rock and approached by a long, sustained path from the Malga Flavona. The hut sleeps around 70 and is one of the older SAT structures, having been rebuilt several times since the original shelter was established in the nineteenth century.

## 5. Rifugio Casinei, Brenta Dolomites

At 1,825 metres in the Vallesinella above Madonna di Campiglio, the Rifugio Casinei is the lowest and most accessible of the major Brenta huts, situated at the valley head where the paved road ends and the trail network begins. It functions as both a destination for day hikers exploring the Vallesinella waterfalls area and a staging post for overnight guests heading higher into the Brenta. The hut sleeps around 50 and is open from June through October, with a particularly busy July and August.

## 6. Rifugio Pedrotti alla Tosa, Brenta Dolomites

The Rifugio Pedrotti alla Tosa at 2,491 metres is one of the most important high huts in the Brenta, positioned close to the foot of the Cima Tosa (3,173 metres) — the highest summit in the Brenta group. The hut was founded by the SAT in 1891, making it one of the oldest rifugi in Trentino, and has been rebuilt and expanded several times. It sleeps around 80 guests and serves as the main base for ascents of Cima Tosa and for the Bocchette Alte route in both directions.

## 7. Rifugio Agostini, Brenta Dolomites

The Rifugio Agostini at 2,410 metres in the southern sector of the Brenta above the Val d'Ambiez is a quieter alternative to the busier central huts. Named after the Italian alpinist Ippolito Agostini, who made several significant first ascents in the area, the hut sits at the foot of the Cima d'Ambiez and is reached from San Lorenzo in Banale in about four hours. The southern Brenta sees less traffic than the Campiglio approaches and rewards visitors with a more contemplative alpine experience.

## 8. Rifugio Cornisello, Presanella group

At 2,124 metres in the Val Nambrone west of Madonna di Campiglio, the Rifugio Cornisello sits at the threshold of the Presanella group, the glaciated range that lies between the Brenta and the Adamello. The Presanella (3,558 metres) is one of the highest peaks in Trentino and carries small glaciers on its northern flanks. The Cornisello hut is reached in about two hours from the valley and provides access to routes through the Cornisello couloir system toward the Presanella summit.

## 9. Rifugio Grostè, Brenta Dolomites

The Rifugio Grostè at 2,442 metres on the Passo Grostè north of Madonna di Campiglio is reachable by cable car from the resort, making it one of the most accessible high-altitude rifugi in the Brenta. Despite this accessibility, the terrain above is genuinely challenging — the Grostè is a launching point for the Bocchette Alte and for routes on the northern peaks of the Brenta including the Cima Brenta and the Castelletto Inferiore. The hut sleeps around 50 and is open in summer and winter.

## 10. Rifugio Spinale, Madonna di Campiglio

The Rifugio Spinale at 2,104 metres above Madonna di Campiglio is accessible by cable car and marks the lower boundary of the Dolomiti di Brenta UNESCO World Heritage Site. It functions as a restaurant and overnight stop rather than a mountaineering base and is popular with summer visitors who want the Brenta panorama without the commitment of a long approach. The Spinale chairlift extends the ski area from Campiglio and the hut is busy in both summer and winter.

## Planning the Brenta Traverse

The Bocchette routes that link the major Brenta rifugi are some of the most rewarding multi-day via ferrata circuits in the Alps, but they require appropriate equipment — harness, ferrata kit, and helmet — as well as physical fitness and comfort with exposed terrain. The season typically runs from late June through mid-September, and hut reservations are essential in July and August.
