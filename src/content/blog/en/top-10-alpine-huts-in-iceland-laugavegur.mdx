Iceland's highland interior is one of the most geologically violent landscapes
accessible on foot in Europe — active volcanoes, geothermal fields, obsidian
lava deserts, glaciers, and the strange coloured rhyolite hills of the
Landmannalaugar area. The Laugavegur (Hot Springs Way) trekking route connects
Landmannalaugar in the north with Þórsmörk in the south over 55 kilometres
of extraordinary terrain. The huts along the route — operated by Ferðafélag
Íslands (FÍ, the Iceland Touring Association) and private operators — are
essential infrastructure in a landscape where wild camping is permitted but
extreme weather is normal.

## 1. Landmannalaugar Huts, 600 m — Fjallabak Nature Reserve

The starting point of the Laugavegur, Landmannalaugar is accessible by 4WD
bus from Reykjavík (4–5 hours) and sits in a natural geothermal area where
a hot spring flows from beneath a lava field into a bathing pool. The FÍ hut
complex at Landmannalaugar — Landmannalaugar — provides sleeping bag
accommodation, cooking facilities, and basic meals for up to 200 people across
several buildings. The iconic hot spring pool is directly beside the huts.
The coloured rhyolite hills surrounding the area — red, green, yellow, ochre —
provide the most photographed walking terrain in Iceland.

## 2. Hrafntinnusker Huts, 1100 m — Laugavegur, Day 1

At 1100 m on the Laugavegur's highest terrain, the Hrafntinnusker hut complex
sits in a landscape of obsidian (hrafntinnur — raven flint) and fumaroles.
The terrain here is above the snowline for much of the hiking season; the
route from Landmannalaugar involves crossing several snowfields. FÍ-operated
huts with sleeping bag accommodation for approximately 80 people. Meals
service in peak season.

## 3. Álftavatn Huts, 540 m — Laugavegur, Day 2

Descending from the high volcanic terrain to the glacial lake Álftavatn
(Swan Lake), the huts at this stage of the Laugavegur provide a dramatic
contrast: the volcanic highlands behind, the lake and river delta ahead, and
the Mýrdalsjökull glacier visible to the south. The FÍ hut at Álftavatn
accommodates approximately 60 people. The river crossings in this section —
bare-foot glacier-melt streams — are one of the Laugavegur's most discussed
practical challenges.

## 4. Emstrur (Botnar) Huts, 490 m — Laugavegur, Day 3

The huts at Emstrur, also known as Botnar, sit in a lunar landscape of black
sand deserts and canyons sculpted by glacial outwash. The Markarfljót canyon
near here — up to 200 m deep, carved by a glacial outburst flood — is one of
the most dramatic features of the route. FÍ huts for approximately 70 people.
The final approach to Þórsmörk on Day 4 is the most technically demanding
section of the standard Laugavegur (river crossings, steep descent).

## 5. Þórsmörk (Volcano Huts / Básar), 200 m — Þórsmörk

The endpoint of the Laugavegur is actually a cluster of three separate
properties in the Þórsmörk valley: the FÍ hut at Langidalur, the Volcano Huts
(a private operator), and Básar á Goðalandi. All sit in a birch scrub forest
valley surrounded by three glaciers. Þórsmörk is accessible by super-jeep
from Reykjavík (3 hours) making it possible to arrive or depart from the
south end without walking the full Laugavegur.

## 6. Fimmvörðuskáli Hut, 1060 m — Fimmvörðuháls Route

On the dramatic ridge connecting Þórsmörk with Skógar on the south coast,
the Fimmvörðuskáli (Fimmvörðuskáli hut) sits at 1060 m between the
Mýrdalsjökull and Eyjafjallajökull glaciers. The Fimmvörðuháls route (24 km,
1 day or 2 with overnight) became internationally famous after the 2010
Eyjafjallajökull eruption created fresh lava fields in its upper section.
FÍ-operated; sleeping bag accommodation for 80.

## 7. Hólaskjól Hut, 615 m — Þórsmörk area

A smaller FÍ hut in the broader Þórsmörk area, providing a quieter alternative
base. The valley terrain around Hólaskjól is excellent for day walks in a
more solitary setting than the busier huts.

## 8. Nýidalur Hut, 720 m — Sprengisandur highland route

On Iceland's Sprengisandur highland route — a four-wheel-drive track crossing
the interior desert — Nýidalur provides a remote overnight stop in the barren
heart of Iceland. Not on the Laugavegur but significant for those exploring
the broader highland network. The Iceland interior is a proper wilderness;
Nýidalur is the only settlement for 150 km in any direction.

## 9. Drekaskáli Hut, 630 m — Askja caldera area

Near the Askja caldera and the Víti crater lake, Drekaskáli is the base hut
for one of Iceland's most dramatic geological features — the Askja volcanic
system that produced a massive eruption in 1875. The 4WD track to Askja from
Mývatn takes 3–4 hours; the hut accommodates approximately 50 people.

## 10. Laugafell Hut, 700 m — Highland interior

A remote FÍ hut with natural geothermal hot pots on the western slopes of
the Kerlingarfjöll area, Laugafell is accessible by 4WD bus in summer and
by ski in winter across the highland plateau. One of the more isolated huts
in the FÍ network.

## Icelandic highland access restrictions

Iceland's highland interior (hálendi) is designated a protected zone with
restricted vehicle access. F-roads — the high-clearance tracks that connect
the highland destinations — are open only when the ground has dried
sufficiently after the winter snowmelt, typically from late June. Driving
on closed F-roads damages the fragile highland vegetation and is a significant
fine-carrying offence enforced by the Environment Agency of Iceland.

The main huts of the Laugavegur (Landmannalaugar, Hrafntinnusker, Álftavatn,
Emstrur, Þórsmörk) are served by scheduled 4WD bus services from Reykjavík
during the open season. These buses are the primary access method for trekkers
without their own vehicle; the services run by Reykjavik Excursions (re.is)
and Trex are reliable and pre-bookable online.

For those with a rental 4WD vehicle: note that Icelandic rental car insurance
typically excludes damage resulting from river crossings — and several approaches
to highland huts involve unbridged river crossings. Confirm your insurance
coverage before attempting any river crossing in a rental vehicle.

## Iceland's geological context

The Laugavegur route traverses terrain that is among the youngest and most
geologically active on Earth. Iceland sits on the Mid-Atlantic Ridge — the
boundary between the Eurasian and North American tectonic plates, which are
separating at approximately 2.5 cm per year. The rift valley is visible in
Þingvellir, north of Reykjavík; the volcanic activity that builds Iceland
faster than erosion destroys it is expressed in the route's fumaroles,
geothermal fields, lava flows, and caldera lakes.

The 2010 Eyjafjallajökull eruption — which disrupted European air traffic for
six days — is visible from the Fimmvörðuháls extension of the Laugavegur, where
the new lava fields created by the eruption are crossed on the traverse. These
fields are less than 20 years old; the rock is still geologically newborn,
black and raw, with virtually no vegetation. Walking across them is a rare
experience of geology in real time.
