The Gran Paradiso is the only mountain wholly within Italy to exceed 4,000 metres, rising to 4,061 metres at the heart of a national park that is itself a landmark in European conservation. The land was a royal hunting reserve of the House of Savoy in the nineteenth century, set aside to protect the last alpine ibex from extinction; in 1922 King Victor Emmanuel III gave it to the state, and it became Italy's first national park. Today some of the densest ibex and chamois populations in the Alps graze its high pastures, and the network of huts — many descended from the old royal hunting lodges and gamekeepers' paths — gives walkers and climbers a way into one of the great wildlife landscapes of the range. The ten below circle the massif from its famous valleys to its quiet eastern flank.

## 1. Rifugio Vittorio Emanuele II, Valsavarenche

The Rifugio Vittorio Emanuele II at around 2,732 metres above Pont in the Valsavarenche is the classic base for the normal route up the Gran Paradiso, and one of the busiest high huts in Italy. Named for the king who created the reserve, the modern dome-shaped building sleeps over 150 and fills nightly in summer with rope teams setting out before dawn for the glacier and the Madonna statue on the summit. The walk up from Pont takes around two and a half hours through pasture grazed by ibex, which are so habituated here that they wander among the huts at dusk.

## 2. Rifugio Federico Chabod, Valsavarenche

A quieter alternative on the same flank, the Rifugio Federico Chabod at around 2,750 metres also serves the Gran Paradiso normal route but by a slightly longer and less crowded approach. Named for an Aostan historian and statesman, the hut is modern, comfortable, and beautifully sited beneath the Laveciau glacier. Many climbers ascend by the Chabod and descend to the Vittorio Emanuele, making a satisfying traverse of the mountain's north-western face. The terrace at sunset, with the glacier turning gold, is one of the finest in the park.

## 3. Rifugio del Misérin, Champorcher

On the eastern edge of the park above the Champorcher valley, the Rifugio del Misérin at around 2,580 metres stands beside a clear mountain lake and a seventeenth-century pilgrimage chapel. The terrain here is gentle — broad pasture, low passes, and easy walking that suits families and those exploring the park's quieter side. The Misérin sleeps around 40 and opens from late June through September; the early-July pilgrimage festival fills it weeks ahead. From here, routes cross westward into the heart of the park.

## 4. Rifugio Forte Geremia, Champorcher

The Rifugio Forte Geremia at around 2,540 metres sits close to the eastern park boundary above the Champorcher valley and serves routes toward the Colle della Rossa and the network of trails that lace the park's eastern third. This is excellent ground for wildlife: the Champorcher valley supports large herds of ibex, and the gentle high pastures above the hut are among the best places in the park to watch them in the cool of early morning. The hut accommodates around 40 and makes a good base for a multi-day eastern loop.

## 5. Rifugio Passo del Diavolo, Cogne Side

The Rifugio Passo del Diavolo at around 2,745 metres sits on the Colle di Entrelor above Cogne, giving access to the remote Piantonetto valley on the Piedmont side of the massif. A basic but staffed shelter sleeping around 35, it commands a superb view of the Gran Paradiso summit rising to the south. The Cogne valley, with its old magnetite mines and its broad meadows below the Gran Paradiso, is the park's best-known approach, and the Diavolo pass takes walkers from its busy trails into genuinely wild country.

## 6. Rifugio Campolongo, Eastern Approach

At around 1,860 metres in the lower Champorcher valley, the Rifugio Campolongo is an entry-level overnight stop for walkers approaching the park from the east. Less visited than the Cogne or Valsavarenche gateways, it keeps a quiet, local character and accommodates around 40 through the summer. For those building toward the higher glacier huts, or for families wanting a single straightforward night beneath the peaks, the Campolongo is an honest, unhurried base with home cooking.

## 7. Rifugio Sella, Valnontey

The Rifugio Vittorio Sella at around 2,584 metres above the Valnontey near Cogne is one of the most beloved huts in the entire park, occupying a former royal hunting lodge in a high amphitheatre of pasture surrounded by glaciers. It is famous for its ibex: at dawn the animals come down to graze and lick the mineral-rich ground right beside the building. The hut sleeps over 100 and is reached by a steady two-hour walk from Valnontey. It is the single best place in the Alps to see ibex at close range.

## 8. Rifugio Barbustel, Northern Flank

The Rifugio Barbustel at around 2,186 metres sits on the northern flank of the broader Gran Paradiso region in the Mont Avic area, anchoring routes that link the park to the neighbouring nature reserve. Named for a local dialect word for a bird, the solid stone hut sleeps around 50 amid larch forest and high meadows scattered with small tarns. It is a quieter corner, away from the summit crowds, where the focus is on long traverses and the rich birdlife of the lower altitudes.

## 9. Rifugio Arp, Western Valleys

At around 2,478 metres, the Rifugio Arp guards a high valley running toward the frontier ridge on the park's western edge, in markedly wild terrain with long approaches and little road access. It is a useful midpoint on routes pushing toward the high passes, sitting in country where the old hunting and herding paths still thread between pastures. Expect simple food, a small dormitory, and the deep quiet that defines the park away from its famous central valleys.

## 10. Rifugio Pavillon, Northern Approaches

The Rifugio Pavillon sits in the high country toward the northern margins of the broader massif, serving walkers on the routes that connect the Gran Paradiso region with the Mont Blanc approaches across the Aosta valley floor. From positions like this the full sweep of the Italian high Alps comes into view — the Gran Paradiso to the south, the Mont Blanc massif to the north — and the hut makes a logical pivot for walkers linking the two great ranges on a long, ambitious traverse of the region.
