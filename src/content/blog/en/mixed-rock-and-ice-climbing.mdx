The term "mixed climbing" covers terrain where rock, ice, and snow exist in
combination on a single pitch or route. On a classic Alpine route in summer,
mixed sections typically appear in the form of ice-glazed rock ledges, frozen
cracks, a frozen couloir between rock bands, or a summit ridge where névé meets
exposed stone. The skills required are different from either pure rock climbing
or pure ice climbing — and they are the gateway to the harder alpine routes
that start from the highest huts in the Alps.

## The alpine ice spectrum

Pure ice climbing on dedicated water-ice routes (frozen waterfalls, ice gullies)
uses vertical ice-climbing technique: frontpoints on both feet pressing into
the ice surface, two technical tools swinging and placing alternately, the
body balanced in a triangular relationship between the three points of contact.
This is the environment for which modern technical tools — aggressively curved
shafts, ergonomic grips, steeply raked picks — were designed.

Alpine ice climbing on glaciated mountain routes is less steep and less dramatic
than vertical ice but requires its own judgment. The moderate ice faces and
couloirs of the Bernese Oberland, the approach to the Hörnlihütte's upper
ridge sections, and the standard routes on objectives like the Allalinhorn
(4027 m) above the Britanniahütte all involve alpine ice at 35–55 degrees
where frontpointing is appropriate but the pace is faster, the terrain is longer,
and the consequences of a fall are more serious than on a contained ice route.

The general mountaineering axe (a B-rated tool with a flat or mildly angled
pick) is the right tool for alpine ice, not the specialised technical ice tool.
Double-axe technique — both hands holding tools — is used on steeper sections;
single axe with the second hand free is sufficient on moderate alpine ice.

## Crampon choice

For mixed alpine terrain, the choice is between 12-point general mountaineering
crampons with horizontal front points and 12-point crampons with vertical
frontpoints (designed for ice climbing). In practice, a 12-point crampon with
monopoint or dual frontpoint and a slightly aggressive front geometry works
on both rocky mixed terrain and moderate ice.

The critical crampon specification for mixed terrain is rigidity of the
platform: a rigid crampon (designed for stiff mountaineering boots) performs
better on hard ice and on small rock holds than a semi-rigid crampon designed
for flexible trekking boots. Boot-crampon compatibility must be verified before
the route; a crampon that bends with the boot on hard ice is unsafe.

## Dry-tooling and M-grades

Dry-tooling is the technique of placing ice-tool picks into rock cracks, holes,
and edges to climb mixed ground without ice. It was developed in the 1980s and
1990s as a training discipline and evolved into a competition sport and an
extreme alpine style. The M-grade scale (M1 through M15+) rates the difficulty
of dry-tooling moves independently of the surrounding alpine context.

In an alpine mountaineering context, dry-tooling at M2–M4 grades describes
the standard mixed terrain encountered on winter and early-season alpine routes:
placing a pick behind a flake, hooking a crampon point over a small edge,
swinging onto an icy shelf. This level of technique is accessible after a
weekend of practice on a dedicated mixed or ice route; it does not require
competition-level athleticism.

Routes like the east face of the Bremer Hütte approach in the Stubai Alps,
or the access to the Innsbrucker Hütte via the Stubai Ridge in spring, provide
low-grade mixed terrain that introduces the techniques naturally.

## The rack for mixed alpine routes

On a mixed alpine route of moderate difficulty (AD–D), the standard rack is:
a small set of wires (sizes 1–7), two or three small cams, four to six ice
screws (60 or 80 mm length, screw-in type), a deadman snow anchor, 60 m half-
ropes or a 50 m single dry-treated rope, and a standard alpine rack of
carabiners, slings, and belay/rappel devices.

Ice screws on alpine routes serve primarily as running protection on steep ice
sections and as component parts of V-thread anchors for rappelling off ice.
Placing a screw efficiently — in under 60 seconds, one-handed, on a vertical
section — is a practiced skill worth developing before the objective.

## The mental transition

Climbers who come from sport or traditional rock climbing to alpine mixed
terrain often find the mental transition harder than the physical one. On a
sport route, all variables are controlled: the rock is clean, the route is
marked, the gear is pre-clipped or well-protected. On a mixed alpine route,
conditions change daily, ice quality varies by temperature, rock holds conceal
under ice, and the consequence of a mistake is measured in mountain terms.

The recommended progression is systematic: guided introductory alpine course,
then moderate summer glacier routes from huts like the Britanniahütte or
Rifugio Gnifetti, then winter or spring routes as experience and confidence
develop.

## The North Face classics

The great north faces of the Alps — the Eiger Nordwand (3970 m), the Matterhorn
Nordwand, and the Grandes Jorasses Walker Spur — represent the upper limit of
alpine mixed climbing that has preoccupied mountaineering history since the
1930s. They share a common character: extended mixed terrain, highly variable
and often poor rock, complex glacier and serac approach hazards, and commitment
once the route is above the lower third.

The Eiger Nordwand is approached from the Alpiglen pasture below the face,
sometimes with a night in the small bivouac shelter at the foot of the wall.
No permanent hut serves the face itself; retreat from the upper sections in
deteriorating conditions has historically resulted in fatalities, which is part
of what makes the Eiger a byword for the consequences of poor timing decisions.

The modern approach to these routes involves advanced weather forecasting and
the willingness to commit only when a multi-day stable window is confirmed.
The mountains themselves have not changed; the forecasting has improved enough
that parties who are disciplined about timing have a significantly better safety
record than climbers of the 1950s–1980s who relied on barometric trends alone.

## Training routes from huts

Several high huts serve as bases for training routes that develop the mixed
climbing skill set. The Britanniahütte above Saas Fee provides glacier access
to the Allalinhorn (4027 m) — the standard guided introductory glacier peak
in the Swiss Alps, with moderate mixed terrain at the summit. The Rifugio
Gnifetti at 3611 m in the Monte Rosa massif serves the Liskamm and Castor-
Pollux approaches. The Hörnlihütte directly serves the Matterhorn's Hörnli
Ridge — the standard route that, while technically not extreme, requires
good competence in alpine terrain and reliable weather judgment.

A progression through these more accessible objectives builds the competence
base for the grade-AD and D routes that require full technical mixed skills.
