The word hut covers an enormous range, from a hotel-like mountain inn with a restaurant and hot showers to a tin box bolted to a ridge that sleeps four and holds nothing but a foam mat and a logbook. Turning up at the second expecting the first is one of the classic mountain mistakes, and the categories the Alpine clubs use to grade their huts exist precisely to prevent it. The Swiss Alpine Club and the German Alpine Club each maintain a grading scheme that tells you, before you commit, what level of service, comfort, and self-reliance a given hut demands. Learning to read these categories is one of the most useful planning skills a hut-goer can acquire.

## Why Categories Exist

A hut category is a promise and a warning at once. It tells you whether a warden will cook your dinner or whether you must carry a stove; whether blankets are provided or you need a full sleeping bag; whether you can pay by card or must bring exact cash for an honesty box. Misreading it has real consequences — arriving at an unstaffed shelter expecting a meal, or carrying a heavy tent to a fully serviced inn that made it pointless.

The clubs developed these gradings to manage expectations across networks of hundreds of huts that differ wildly in altitude, access, and purpose. A category is shorthand for a whole bundle of practical questions, and once you understand the system you can glance at a hut's classification and know roughly what awaits, what to pack, and how to behave.

## The Serviced Hut: Warden, Meals, Beds

At the comfortable end sits the serviced or staffed hut — bewirtschaftet in German, gardé in French, custodito in Italian. During its open season this hut has a resident warden and usually a team, who cook a hearty evening meal and breakfast, sell drinks and snacks, manage the sleeping arrangements, and oversee safety and rescue coordination. Sleeping is typically in a shared dormitory on mattresses with provided blankets or duvets, with the personal sleeping bag liner as the one item you must bring.

These are the huts most walkers picture: places like the Berliner Hütte in the Zillertal, with its grand nineteenth-century dining hall, or the busy SAC huts on the Haute Route. The larger serviced huts can feel almost like mountain hotels, with private rooms alongside the dormitory, drying rooms, and sometimes hot showers. The essential character is that you arrive, are fed, and sleep without carrying camping equipment — the model that makes hut-to-hut walking so light and so popular.

## The Self-Service Hut: Open but Unstaffed

A step down in service, and up in self-reliance, is the self-service hut. Here the building is open and equipped — a kitchen with cooking facilities, gas or a stove, pots and crockery, bunks with blankets, and lighting — but there is no warden. You let yourself in, often with a club key or a code, cook your own food (which you must carry, though some self-service huts keep a stocked larder of tins and staples), clean up after yourself, and pay into an honesty box or by a self-registration system.

This model is the backbone of the Norwegian DNT network, where selvbetjent huts are stocked with food paid for on trust, and it appears across the Alps too, particularly for huts that do not justify a full-season warden. The self-service hut asks more of the visitor — you are cook, cleaner, and caretaker for the night — but rewards with solitude, flexibility, and lower cost. The unwritten rule is to leave it as you found it or better, refill the water, replace any wood burned, and lock up.

## The Bivouac: Shelter at Its Most Basic

At the spartan extreme is the bivouac hut, the biv — biwakschachtel in German, bivacco in Italian. This is an unstaffed emergency or basic shelter, often a small metal or wooden box placed high on a route where no full hut is feasible, sleeping perhaps four to twelve people. It provides walls, a roof, bunks or platforms, and usually mattresses and a few blankets, but no warden, no food, often no running water, and frequently no reliable means to cook beyond what you carry.

The Italian Alps in particular are dotted with these austere boxes, many placed by CAI sections on high traverses and climbing approaches. A bivouac is never booked — it operates on first-come, and the iron law is that no one in genuine need is turned away. Using one means carrying everything: sleeping bag, stove, fuel, food, and water or the means to melt snow. The bivouac is the purest expression of mountain shelter, a place to survive a night high on the mountain rather than to be hosted.

## Winter Rooms and Seasonal Status

Cutting across the categories is the question of season. Most serviced huts are staffed only during a core season — broadly summer for walking huts, spring for the ski-touring huts of the Haute Route — and outside that window they change character entirely. Many keep a winter room (Winterraum) open: a part of the building left unlocked and unstaffed, functioning as a self-service or bivouac-style shelter with bunks and blankets but no meals and no warden.

This seasonal shift is a frequent trap. A hut that is a comfortable serviced inn in August may be a cold, foodless winter room in October, and a walker who books expecting dinner will be disappointed. Always check the staffing dates, not just the category, because the same building can be three different things across the year — serviced in season, self-service in shoulder season, and a locked shell with only a winter room in deep winter.

## How to Read a Category Before You Go

Reading a hut's classification well is a matter of asking the right questions. Is it staffed during your dates, or only open as a winter room? Are meals provided, or must you cook and therefore carry a stove and food? Are blankets supplied, so a liner suffices, or is it a bivouac demanding a full sleeping bag? Can you book and pay by card, or is it first-come with an honesty box needing cash? Each answer flows from the category, and together they determine what goes in your pack and how you plan the day.

The clubs publish this information on each hut's page, and the symbols and terms — bewirtschaftet, selbstversorger, Winterraum, biwak, gardé, custodito, incustodito — repay learning. A walker who reads them fluently plans accurately, packs correctly, and is never caught out by a hut that turns out to be something other than imagined. The category is the single most important fact about a hut after its location, and it should be the first thing you check.
