At 5,636 metres, Pico de Orizaba — the Aztecs called it Citlaltépetl, the Star Mountain — is the highest peak in Mexico and the third-highest in North America after Denali and Mount Logan. It is also a near-perfect dormant volcano, its summit cone wrapped in the Jamapa Glacier, the largest body of ice in the country. Most climbers approach from the north, through the highland village of Tlachichuca in Puebla, where four-wheel-drive trucks grind up a brutal dirt track to a single stone refuge perched at the edge of the moraine. The mountains here are not the granite spires of the Alps; they are volcanic giants, dry and dusty at their bases, fiercely cold and crevassed above. The huts that serve them range from one famous high-altitude shelter to a scatter of valley lodges and ecotourism cabins that make the long approach humane.

## Refugio Piedra Grande

This is the hut everyone means when they talk about Orizaba. Piedra Grande sits at roughly 4,260 metres on the north side, a long aluminium-clad shelter reached by the dirt road from Tlachichuca. Inside are tiered wooden sleeping platforms for around sixty people, no mattresses, no heating, and no charge — it is a free, first-come refuge maintained loosely by the climbing community. Most parties arrive by truck in the afternoon, spend a night or two acclimatising, and leave at one or two in the morning for the summit push up the Jamapa Glacier. Bring everything: stove, water, a serious sleeping bag. Nights here can drop well below freezing, and the thin air at over 4,200 metres makes the first night restless.

## RAMSA and the Tlachichuca lodges

Tlachichuca is the logistics hub for the whole north side, and the name climbers learn first is Servimont, run in the old Reyes family soap factory — though the wider RAMSA operation handles much of the truck transport up to Piedra Grande. These outfits offer bunk beds, hot meals, and storage at around 2,600 metres, letting you sleep low, sort gear, and stage the four-wheel-drive shuttle. Acclimatising for a night in town before going high is sensible practice on a peak this big.

## Albergue de Laguna Seca

On the approach side, the Albergue de Laguna Seca offers simple bunkroom lodging in the high country between the farmland and the volcano. It is the kind of staging post that turns a frantic single-day attempt into a graded ascent, with cold clear nights and the cone of Orizaba filling the skyline at dawn.

## Cabaña de los Bubus and Complejo Primavera

Lower on the slopes, family-run cabins such as Cabaña de los Bubus and the Complejo Primavera cater to weekend walkers and acclimatisation hikes rather than summit teams. They are warm, walled, and a useful introduction to the altitude for anyone planning to push higher later in the trip. Expect home cooking, woodsmoke, and the dry pine forest of the eastern Sierra Madre.

## Tamandua and the eco-cabins

The slug "ecotourism" attaches to a growing number of small operations around the volcano. Tamandua and the Centro Ecoturístico Las Guacamayas market themselves to nature visitors as much as alpinists, offering cabin nights, guided walks, and a base for the lower forest trails. They are reminders that Orizaba is not only a climbing objective but a protected natural area within the Pico de Orizaba National Park.

## ASIRMI, PENIN and the guide cooperatives

Local guiding and rescue cooperatives such as ASIRMI and PENIN are woven into the hut ecosystem here. On a glaciated 5,600-metre volcano with rockfall, crevasses, and altitude that has killed unprepared parties, hiring a registered local guide is not bureaucracy — it is the difference between a hard day and a dangerous one. These groups also coordinate transport and basic shelter for clients on multi-day programmes.

## Hacienda del Bosque and the highland stays

A short distance from the access roads, places like the Hacienda del Bosque 3a Sección and the Centro Ecoturístico PAKAL TSIX A provide more comfortable highland lodging for parties who want a real bed before and after the climb. They sit in the agricultural belt around 2,500 to 3,000 metres, ideal for the gradual altitude gain that a serious summit attempt demands.

## Mesa del Tirador and the south approach

The south side of Orizaba, reached from the Veracruz town of Coscomatepec, is wilder and far less travelled. High points like the Mesa del Tirador serve the occasional party attempting the southern routes, which are longer, looser, and rockier than the standard glacier line. There is no equivalent of Piedra Grande here; shelter is improvised, and self-sufficiency is total.

## Cenit and the modern cabins

Newer ventures such as Cenit, billed as a Descanso de los Dioses, and a string of named cabañas including Cabañas San Diego, blend mountain lodging with the comfortable cabin-getaway market that has grown around Mexico's high volcanoes. They are best understood as acclimatisation and rest options rather than alpine refuges proper.

## Hacienda la Soledad and Posada El Roble

Rounding out the network, valley-floor stays such as Hacienda la Soledad and the Posada El Roble give arriving climbers a soft landing after long travel from Mexico City or Puebla. From here the pattern is the same as it has been for a century: rest low, drive high, sleep cold at Piedra Grande, and start for the summit in the dark.

## What a night on the mountain is like

The rhythm of an Orizaba climb is unlike anything in the Alps. There is no warden, no cooked dinner waiting, no menu of Kaiserschmarrn — Piedra Grande is a cold concrete-and-metal box that you make liveable yourself. Parties stake out a stretch of the wooden platform, unroll mats and bags, and fire up stoves to melt snow or boil the water hauled up by truck. The afternoon fills with the clatter of crampons being fitted, the hiss of MSR burners, and the strained breathing of people who flew in from sea level a day or two before. Sleep comes hard at 4,260 metres; most lie awake listening to the wind hammer the metal cladding. The summit alarm goes at midnight, and by one in the morning headlamps are bobbing up the scree toward the labyrinth and the glacier above. It is austere, communal, and unforgettable, and it is the reason the hut, for all its discomfort, is beloved.

## Getting there and the logistics

Reaching Orizaba is half the expedition. From Mexico City it is roughly four to five hours by road to Tlachichuca, usually via Puebla, and from the village the only way up to Piedra Grande is by sturdy four-wheel-drive truck arranged through the local operators. The dirt road climbs more than 1,600 metres in a couple of hours of jolting, dusty switchbacks, and in poor conditions the trucks may stop short, adding a walk. Booking transport and a refuge slot in advance through Servimont, RAMSA, or a guiding cooperative is strongly advised in the busy October-to-March window, when weekend crowds from Puebla and Mexico City fill the access roads and the hut alike.

## A note on conditions

Orizaba's climbing season runs roughly October to March, the dry months, with the glacier hardest and most reliable in the deep winter. The Jamapa Glacier has retreated noticeably in recent decades, exposing more rock and ice on the upper cone, so route conditions change year to year. Carry a rope, crampons, and an ice axe, and respect the altitude — the summit is higher than any peak in the contiguous United States.
