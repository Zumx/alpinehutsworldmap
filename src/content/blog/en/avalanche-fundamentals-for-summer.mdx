Most people associate avalanches with winter ski touring — and rightly so, since
dry-slab avalanches in winter conditions account for the majority of
avalanche fatalities. But the hiker crossing a high snow slope in July on the
approach to the Sulzenauhütte, or traversing below a cornice en route to the
Cabane des Vignettes CAS in early season, faces real snow-stability hazards.
Summer avalanche conditions are different from winter, but they are not
inherently safer. Understanding the mechanisms helps make better decisions.

## Wet snow avalanches

The dominant summer snow hazard is the wet snow avalanche. As the snowpack
warms through spring and early summer, water percolates downward through the
layers. When it reaches an ice crust or impermeable layer, it lubricates the
interface and the entire saturated slab can release. Wet snow avalanches are
typically slower-moving than dry slabs, but they are extremely dense and carry
enough momentum to sweep a person off a ridge or into a bergschrund.

The melt-freeze cycle is the key mechanism to understand. During a cold clear
night, the surface snow refreezes into a hard crust. In the morning, this
crust provides excellent travel conditions and reasonable stability. As solar
heating builds through the day — typically from mid-morning onwards on south
and east-facing slopes — the surface softens and the snowpack becomes unstable.
This is why hut-based alpinists depart at 03:00–05:00: not just to avoid
afternoon convective storms, but to cross snow terrain while it is frozen and
stable.

The practical rule is: be off significant snow slopes by 10:00 on warm sunny
days. If you are still on a major snow face at noon with the snow balling under
your crampons and your boots punching through, you are in elevated hazard
territory.

## Residual cornices

Winter and spring wind action builds cornices — overhanging snow formations
on the leeward side of ridges — that persist well into summer. A cornice that
looks solid from below may be a hollow shell, undercut by warm air and solar
radiation. Cornices fail unpredictably. The hazard is twofold: the person
walking along the cornice edge who falls through, and the person below who is
hit by the falling mass.

The rule when traversing ridgelines in late spring or early summer is to stay
several metres back from the edge on the windward side. "Several metres" means
more than it intuitively feels — the fracture line of a collapsing cornice
can be a metre or more back from the visible edge overhang. On well-marked
routes like the approach to the Riemannhaus in the Berchtesgaden Alps, the
cornice hazard on the upper traverse is known and signposted in season; on
less-frequented terrain, there are no signs.

## Spring conditions and late-season snow patches

The transition season — roughly May through July depending on elevation and
aspect — carries the highest wet-avalanche frequency in the Alps. Route-finding
on a traverse like the one approaching the Bremer Hütte in the Stubai Alps
can involve significant snow sections with runout zones below. The question
to ask is not "will this slope avalanche" but "if this slope avalanches while
I am on it, where does the runout go and can I survive it?"

Late-season isolated snow patches — July or August remnants in couloirs and
below north-facing cliffs — are generally stable (they have had months to
consolidate) but may conceal a hard ice base. Crampon technique on these
patches matters more than avalanche assessment.

## The glacier wet-snow hazard

Glaciers in summer present a specific variant of wet-snow hazard. Seracs
(ice towers) warm and destabilise over the course of a warm day, and serac
collapse events — sometimes releasing vast quantities of ice and snow — are not
predictable. The Refuge du Gouter approach is notorious for the Grand Couloir,
a gully swept by rockfall and occasional ice debris from the serac zone above.
Crossing it rapidly and early is the standard tactic — not eliminating the
hazard but minimising exposure time.

The glaciated approaches to huts like the Konkordia Hütte above the Aletsch
Glacier require an assessment of icefall exposure on warm afternoons. Guides
and experienced alpinists time their moves through exposed sections.

## Carrying a beacon in summer

The question of whether to carry avalanche transceivers (beacons) in summer
is contested. On a groomed walking trail to a low-elevation hut, a beacon
is unnecessary kit. On a high-alpine traverse involving significant snow slopes
in May or June, it is a reasonable precaution — particularly if the route
involves slopes that would meet the definition of avalanche terrain in any
season.

The simpler and more universally applicable tools for summer are terrain
awareness, timing discipline, and the willingness to turn around when conditions
are not right.

## Assessing a slope before crossing

A practical field assessment of a snow slope requires checking several
factors simultaneously. Slope angle is the starting point: wet snow
avalanches occur predominantly on slopes between 25 and 45 degrees.
Slopes below 25 degrees generally don't develop the gravitational stress
to release; slopes above 45 degrees tend to slough continuously rather
than building up slab potential.

Aspect and time of day interact: a south-facing slope at 3000 m at
11:00 on a clear July day is likely to be in the afternoon instability
window; the same slope at 07:00 after a cold night is probably safe.
An east-facing slope warms first in the morning and may be unstable by
09:00; west-facing terrain is safer in the morning and dangerous in
the afternoon.

The final check is the runout: where does the avalanche debris go if
the slope releases? If it runs into a terrain trap (a gully, a cliff
edge, a bergschrund) rather than onto open flat ground, the consequences
of a small slide are much worse. A shallow wet slide in open terrain
may be survivable; the same slide channelled into a narrow gully is not.

## Forecasts and information sources

The European Avalanche Warning Service (avalanches.org) aggregates
forecasts from 25 services across the Alps and provides standardised
danger ratings. While predominantly focused on winter conditions, the
service also publishes spring (melt-freeze cycle) forecasts and specific
glaciated route advice for major peaks during the summer.

Country-specific services: MeteoSwiss (avalanches in Switzerland),
Zentralanstalt für Meteorologie und Geodynamik (ZAMG, Austria), Servizio
Valanghe Italiano (SVI, Italy), and Météo-France all publish avalanche
bulletins during the relevant season. In spring (April–June), these
services extend their coverage to include the wet-avalanche and cornice
season specifically.

## What to carry in summer for snow terrain

The minimum snow-terrain kit for a summer hut approach that involves
significant snow depends on the elevation and time of year. For most
T3 routes in the Alps in July–August, crampons and an ice axe are
not required but are worth carrying in June or after a late-season
snowfall. The decision threshold: if any section of the route involves
a slope where a slip could result in an uncontrolled slide of more
than 50 m, an ice axe and basic arrest technique are warranted.

Ten-point or twelve-point crampons with anti-balling plates are the
standard summer snow kit. Lightweight aluminium crampons designed for
walking rather than technical ice climbing save weight and are adequate
for the compacted or slushy snow encountered on most summer approach
terrain. Bringing them and not needing them is a minor inconvenience;
needing them and not having them is a significantly worse scenario.
