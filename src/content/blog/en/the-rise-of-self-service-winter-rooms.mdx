When the warden locks the main door of an alpine hut at the end of September and walks down to the valley for the winter, the building does not go entirely dark. A small side door, often marked with a simple sign and a key kept in a code box, opens into the Winterraum — the winter room — a spartan, unheated annexe left open for anyone who needs shelter in the cold months. Inside there are bunks, blankets, a wood stove, a few pots, and a logbook. There is no warden, no menu, no payment desk. There is only a tin box on the wall and the quiet expectation that you will leave money in it and the room as you found it. This small institution is one of the most quietly remarkable features of the alpine hut system.

## What a Winter Room Actually Is

The winter room is the part of a serviced hut that stays accessible when the hut itself is closed. Most Alpine Club huts in Switzerland, Austria, Germany, Italy and France maintain one, precisely because the mountains do not empty in winter — ski tourers, snowshoers and ice climbers need somewhere to sleep, and in a storm a winter room can be the difference between a survivable night and a deadly one. The room is deliberately basic: a stove and firewood, a stack of woollen blankets, a stocked emergency shelf, and bunk space ranging from four places in a tiny bivouac annexe to twenty or more in a large hut.

Crucially, it is never staffed. You let yourself in, you look after yourself, and you take everything out again. The winter room asks more of its guests than any serviced hut, and gives back a kind of solitude that the busy summer building never offers.

## The Honesty Box and a Culture of Trust

At the heart of the winter room is the honesty box — a locked metal tin, often bolted to the wall by the logbook, into which you post the overnight fee in cash. Nobody checks. Nobody counts heads against payments. The whole arrangement runs on the assumption that people who travel into the winter mountains are the kind who pay what they owe even when no one is watching, and remarkably, the assumption largely holds.

The fees are modest, typically a little less than a staffed summer night, and members of the Alpine clubs — the SAC, DAV, ÖAV, CAI and their reciprocal partners — pay a reduced rate. Many huts now supplement the tin with a bank-transfer slip or a payment code, but the cash box endures as the symbol of the system. It is a small daily referendum on mountain honesty, and the clubs report that most people quietly vote the right way.

## Why They Have Spread

Winter rooms are not new, but their number and use have grown markedly as winter mountain travel has boomed. Ski touring has surged from a niche pursuit into a mass sport, snowshoeing has opened the winter hills to walkers, and a warming climate has pushed serious mountaineers toward the colder, more reliable conditions of the shoulder seasons. The clubs have responded by improving winter quarters — better insulation, solar lighting, clearer signage and online occupancy information — turning what was once a bare survival shelter into genuine, if simple, lodging.

There is an economic logic too. A hut that offers a year-round winter room earns a little revenue and keeps its place in tourers' plans through the off-season, while the unmanned model costs almost nothing to run. The result is a network of huts that never fully closes, quietly extending the alpine season across the whole calendar.

## The Etiquette That Keeps It Working

A winter room survives only because its users police themselves, and the unwritten code is strict. You bring your own sleeping bag, because the blankets are few and shared. You burn wood sparingly and, where you can, replace what you use or carry up a log. You cook on your own stove, wash up, and pack out every scrap of rubbish, because there is no bin and no flight to remove it. You leave the room cleaner than you found it, the blankets folded, the stove cold and swept, the door firmly shut against snow and animals.

Above all, you pay. The temptation to skip the tin when no one is watching is exactly the temptation the whole system depends on people resisting. Guests also sign the logbook, recording who is in the mountains and where they intend to go — a small act that has helped rescuers more than once when a party failed to return.

## Reading the Room Before You Rely on It

A winter room is a wonderful resource, but it is not a hotel and should never be treated as a guaranteed bed. Capacity is limited, popular rooms on classic ski-touring lines like those of the Berner Oberland or the Silvretta can fill on a fine weekend, and there is no booking and no warden to make space. Wise tourers carry the means to be self-sufficient, check club websites for the room's condition and any winter closures, and always have a fallback plan.

Conditions vary enormously. Some winter rooms are warm, well-equipped and almost cosy; others are a freezing concrete cell with damp blankets and a temperamental stove. Knowing which you are heading for, and arriving with the gear and the attitude to cope with the worse case, is the mark of an experienced winter traveller. The room rewards the prepared and punishes the complacent.
