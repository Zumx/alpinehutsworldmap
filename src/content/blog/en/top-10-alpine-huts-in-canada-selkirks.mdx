The Selkirk Mountains rise in a tangle of glaciers and granite west of the Canadian Rockies, separated from them by the deep trench of the Columbia River. At their heart lies Rogers Pass, the 1,330-metre saddle that the Canadian Pacific Railway crossed in 1885 and that opened these mountains to climbers a generation before the Rockies were widely explored. This was where North American mountaineering was born: Swiss guides were brought to Glacier House near the pass in the 1890s, and peaks such as Mount Sir Donald (3,284 metres) became the proving ground for a new sport. Today the area lies within Glacier National Park, and the Alpine Club of Canada (ACC) maintains a cluster of huts that give modern alpinists access to the same glaciers and ridges. These ten shelters and bases form the backbone of climbing and ski touring in the Selkirks.

## Rogers Pass and the birth of a sport

The story of the Selkirks begins with the railway. Glacier House, a CPR hotel built beside the Illecillewaet Glacier in 1886, became the first true alpine resort in North America, and the Swiss guides stationed there made the early ascents of Sir Donald, Mount Bonney, and the Hermit peaks. The hotel is long gone, but its meadow trails survive, and the tradition of guided climbing in these mountains continues through the ACC and the local guiding companies. Rogers Pass is now famous worldwide for its winter ski touring and for one of the largest mountain avalanche-control programmes on earth, run from the Trans-Canada Highway that threads the pass.

## Asulkan Hut

The Asulkan Hut sits at around 2,100 metres at the head of the Asulkan Valley, roughly a six-kilometre climb from the Illecillewaet trailhead near Rogers Pass. It is one of the most popular ACC huts in the Selkirks, sleeping around twelve, and serves as a base for climbs of the Dome, Mount Jupiter, and the surrounding Asulkan peaks, as well as for spring ski descents of the glacier. The position, perched above the valley with the Illecillewaet névé spread out beyond, is among the finest of any hut in the range.

## Wheeler Hut

The Wheeler Hut, named for Arthur Oliver Wheeler, co-founder of the Alpine Club of Canada in 1906, stands close to the Illecillewaet campground at the foot of the valley around 1,250 metres. Larger and more accessible than the high huts, it sleeps around thirty and serves as a winter ski-touring base and a staging point for parties heading higher into the Asulkan and Illecillewaet basins. Its low elevation and easy access make it a popular introduction to the Selkirk hut system.

## Glacier Circle Cabin

The Glacier Circle Cabin is a remote, historic shelter at around 1,900 metres on the far side of the Illecillewaet névé, reached by a long traverse over the glacier or a demanding approach from the Beaver Valley. It sleeps only a handful and is one of the more committing destinations in the range, set beneath the great icefalls of the Deville névé. The cabin is a survivor of an earlier era of Selkirk exploration and is prized by ski tourers undertaking the full Glacier National Park traverse.

## Sapphire Col Hut

The Sapphire Col Hut is a small emergency-style shelter perched at around 2,560 metres on the col between the Asulkan and Illecillewaet drainages. It is one of the highest huts in the Selkirks and offers minimal comforts — a roof, walls, and a place to wait out weather — but its position on the divide gives unrivalled access to the surrounding glaciated peaks. It is most often used by ski tourers crossing between the two valleys on multi-day high routes.

## Hermit Meadows and the Hermit peaks

Hermit Meadows, reached by a steep switchbacking trail from the highway near Rogers Pass, sits at around 2,000 metres beneath the Hermit Range. There is no hut here in the conventional sense but rather a designated alpine camping area used as a base for climbs of Mount Tupper, Mount Rogers, and the Swiss Peaks. The meadows are a classic Selkirk objective, and the contrast between the roadside start and the high alpine arena above is achieved in only a few hours.

## Mount Sir Donald and the northwest arête

Mount Sir Donald (3,284 metres) is the iconic summit of the Selkirks, a sweeping pyramid of quartzite visible from Rogers Pass. Its northwest arête is one of the classic alpine rock climbs of North America, typically done from a bivouac or high camp on Uto-Sir Donald col rather than from a hut. The peak was first climbed in 1890 by Swiss guides from Glacier House, and it remains the defining objective for climbers visiting the range. The approach and the climb make for a long, committing day in serious alpine terrain.

## Fairy Meadow and the Adamants

North of Rogers Pass, beyond Glacier National Park, the ACC's Fairy Meadow Hut serves the spectacular Adamant Range and the Gothics. Reached by helicopter from the Columbia Valley, it sleeps around twenty and is the base for one of the most coveted ski-touring and climbing weeks in the Canadian backcountry, surrounded by granite spires and the Granite Glacier. Its remoteness and the quality of the surrounding terrain make it one of the prize destinations of the broader Selkirk region.

## The home of glacier mountaineering

The Selkirks hold a special place in the climbing history of North America, because it was here that the idea of guided glacier mountaineering first took root on the continent. The Swiss guides employed by the Canadian Pacific Railway brought the techniques and equipment developed in the Alps — rope, ice axe, and crampons — and trained a first generation of Canadian alpinists. Many of the region's classic lines cross hanging glaciers and iced couloirs that still demand these skills today, and the Alpine Club of Canada continues to run instructional camps where beginners learn the basics of crevasse rescue and rope work. For the visitor this means the Selkirks offer a rare blend: easily reached valley huts for those who want to walk, and serious high-alpine terrain for those who want to climb. To stand on the porch of the Asulkan Hut and look out over the Illecillewaet névé is to look out over the very birthplace of an entire sport, and that continuity of tradition is part of what makes the range so compelling.

## Seasons, avalanche risk, and access

The Selkirks have two distinct seasons. Summer brings glacier mountaineering and rock climbing from July through September, when the snow bridges are firmest and the approaches clear. Winter and spring bring world-class ski touring, but the avalanche hazard at Rogers Pass is severe and access to much of the backcountry is governed by a permit and closure system tied to the highway's artillery avalanche control. Anyone travelling here in winter must register, carry full avalanche safety equipment, and understand the daily bulletin. Glacier National Park entry fees and ACC membership rates apply at the huts.
