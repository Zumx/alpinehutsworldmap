The Écrins massif, the rugged heart of the Dauphiné, is the most alpine of France's national parks and the only major massif in the country with no road crossing its interior. Its summits cluster around the Barre des Écrins (4102 m), the southernmost four-thousander in the Alps, and the savage profile of La Meije (3984 m), long the last great peak of the Alps to be climbed. The refuges here are run by the French Alpine Club and tend to be more austere and remote than those of Chamonix, serving a wilder, less crowded mountaineering arena where the approaches are long and the glaciers are real.

## The Ecrins as a wilderness park

Unlike the lift-served massifs of the northern Alps, the Écrins keeps its interior roadless and largely wild. Access comes from valley villages such as Ailefroide, La Bérarde, La Grave and Vallouise, from where long footpaths climb to refuges that feel genuinely cut off from the modern world. The national park status forbids new development, so there are no cable cars carrying you to the high huts; you walk in, often for hours, and that effort is precisely what gives the Écrins its character of a true mountain wilderness.

## Refuge des Ecrins

The Refuge des Écrins at 3175 metres sits on a rock spur above the Glacier Blanc, the largest glacier in the southern Alps, and is the base for the Barre des Écrins, the highest summit of the massif. Reached by a long approach from Pré de Madame Carle above Ailefroide, past the lower Glacier Blanc refuge, it is a serious high-altitude hut where roped parties prepare before dawn for the classic glacier and ridge route to the Barre and the Dôme de Neige.

## Refuge du Promontoire

The Refuge du Promontoire at 3092 metres clings to a rock rib directly beneath the south face of La Meije, the most coveted and difficult of the great Écrins peaks. Reached from La Bérarde up the wild Vallon des Étançons, it is the launch point for the traverse of La Meije, one of the classic grande course traverses of the Alps. The hut's position, pressed against the foot of that immense wall, is among the most dramatic in the range.

## Refuge du Glacier Blanc

The Refuge du Glacier Blanc at 2542 metres is the lower of the two refuges on the approach to the Barre des Écrins, set beside the snout of the retreating Glacier Blanc. It is a popular and accessible hut, reached in a few hours from Pré de Madame Carle, and serves both as a staging post for the Écrins hut higher up and as a fine destination in its own right for walkers wanting to reach the edge of the glacier world without serious mountaineering.

## Refuge du Pelvoux

The Refuge du Pelvoux, also known as the Refuge Lemercier, at 2704 metres above Ailefroide, serves the Mont Pelvoux (3946 m), one of the first great peaks of the massif to be climbed. The approach up the Sialouze cirque is steep and demanding, and the hut serves climbers heading for the glaciated routes on the Pelvoux and the surrounding peaks. It is a quieter alternative to the busy Écrins refuge across the valley.

## Refuge du Sele

The Refuge du Sélé at 2511 metres sits in a high cirque above Ailefroide, beneath the peaks of Les Bœufs Rouges and the Pic Coolidge. It is a base for rock and mixed routes in a less-frequented corner of the massif, valued by climbers who want the Écrins atmosphere without the crowds that gather on the Barre. The approach climbs steadily through the upper Vallouise, with the glaciers of the Ailefroide group filling the head of the valley.

## Refuge Temple-Ecrins

The Refuge Temple-Écrins at 2410 metres lies in the Vallon de la Pilatte above La Bérarde, serving the Pic Coolidge and the routes of the central massif. Reached on foot from the roadhead village, it is a comfortable, well-placed hut that gives access to a range of climbs of varied difficulty, and it sits on one of the great hiking traverses that link the refuges of the Écrins into a multi-day tour.

## Refuge de la Pilatte

The Refuge de la Pilatte at 2577 metres stands at the head of the Vallon de la Pilatte above La Bérarde, facing the magnificent semicircle of glaciers and peaks that includes Les Bans and the Ailefroide. The retreat of the Pilatte glacier has dramatically changed the approach in recent years, and the hut is a stark witness to the transformation of the high mountains. It serves serious glacier routes and is a highlight of the GR54 tour of the Écrins.

## Refuge du Chatelleret and La Berarde

The Refuge du Châtelleret at 2225 metres in the Vallon des Étançons is the first hut on the approach to La Meije from La Bérarde, the great roadhead village at the heart of the massif. La Bérarde itself, devastated by flooding in 2024, has long been the mountaineering capital of the Écrins, and the Châtelleret remains a key staging post beneath the towering north face of La Meije, on the way to the Promontoire higher up.

## Wildlife and the high pastures

The Écrins is not only a climbers' arena but one of the great wildlife refuges of the Alps, and the walk to its huts passes through a living landscape. Ibex, hunted nearly to extinction in the nineteenth century, were reintroduced and now graze openly on the slopes above the refuges, often unconcerned by passing walkers; chamois move across the screes, and marmots whistle from the high meadows. Overhead, the golden eagle patrols the cirques, and the bearded vulture, reintroduced to the region in recent decades, has begun to breed again on the cliffs. The national park, created in 1973, protects more than ninety thousand hectares of this high country, and its rules are strict: dogs are forbidden, camping is limited to bivouac near the huts, and the flora, including rare alpine flowers such as the king of the Alps and edelweiss, is fully protected. The lower huts sit among summer pastures where shepherds still bring flocks up for the season, and the cheeses of the valleys, the bleu du Queyras and the tomme, find their way onto hut tables. This combination of serious mountaineering above and rich pastoral life below gives the Écrins a depth that the purely glaciated massifs lack.

## Practical notes for the Ecrins huts

The Écrins huts staff mainly from June to September, with the high glacier huts dependent on conditions that have shifted markedly as the glaciers retreat. Approaches are long, frequently three to five hours, and there are no lifts to shorten them, so good fitness is essential. The summit routes from the Écrins and Promontoire huts require full glacier and alpine skills, rope, crampons and ice axe. Reservation through the French Alpine Club is necessary in summer, and the GR54, the tour of the Oisans, links many of these refuges into one of the finest multi-day treks in France.
