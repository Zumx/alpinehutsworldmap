Romania's Carpathian Mountains describe an arc across the country from north
to east to south, enclosing the Transylvanian plateau. The highest peaks
reach 2544 m (Moldoveanu in the Fagaras) — modest by Alpine standards but
contained in a landscape that combines alpine terrain with dense beech forest,
medieval villages, and extraordinary biodiversity including the last brown
bear and wolf populations in continental Europe outside Russia. The cabana
network — Romania's version of the Alpine hut — is modest in quality by
Western European standards but improving, and the routes between huts are
well-marked and compelling.

## 1. Cabana Mălăiești, 1720 m — Bucegi Mountains, Prahova

Below the Bucegi plateau edge in the Prahova valley area, the Cabana Mălăiești
serves the classic approach routes from the Prahova valley to the Bucegi
plateau. The hut sits at 1720 m in the forested zone below the rocky plateau
rim, with trails ascending to the plateau in 2 hours. Capacity around 60;
the hut has been renovated and provides reasonable comfort for the region.
The surrounding terrain offers walks through the distinctive karstic and
conglomerate geology of the Bucegi.

## 2. Cabana Babele, 2206 m — Bucegi Plateau, Dâmbovița

On the Bucegi plateau itself at 2206 m, Cabana Babele is accessible by cable
car from Bușteni (the most visited mountain cable car in Romania) and sits near
the famous Babele rock formation — naturally sculpted conglomerate figures
that are one of the most recognisable images in Romanian mountain culture.
Capacity around 80; year-round operation via cable car access. The nearby
Sfinxul (Sphinx) rock formation is 400 m to the northeast. The plateau walks
to the Omu peak (2505 m — the highest point in the Bucegi) take approximately
3 hours from here.

## 3. Cabana Caraiman, 2025 m — Bucegi, Prahova

Below the cross on Caraiman Peak (2384 m) — a 28-metre iron cross erected in
1928 as a WWI memorial and visible from the Prahova valley below — the Cabana
Caraiman provides access to one of the most visited summit crosses in Romania.
The ascent from the cabana to the cross takes approximately 1.5 hours on a
well-marked path. The Cabana Caraiman is accessible by cable car from Bușteni
(shared with the Babele cable car line). Capacity around 50.

## 4. Cabana Diham, 1320 m — Bucegi foothills, Prahova

A lower-elevation cabana in the forested foothills of the Bucegi, Cabana Diham
serves as a gentle starting point or finishing point for multi-day routes in
the Bucegi. At 1320 m it is below the rocky plateau but provides an excellent
base for forest walks, spring wildflower routes, and the beginning of ascents
to higher terrain. The Cabana Saua Diham nearby provides an alternative at
a slightly higher elevation.

## 5. Cabana Bârcaciu, 1560 m — Fagaras Mountains, Sibiu

In the northern foothills of the Fagaras — Romania's highest range and the
most demanding alpine terrain in the country — the Cabana Bârcaciu serves as
the primary starting hut for the Fagaras main ridge traverse. The ridge route,
running approximately 75 km from Transfagarasan Highway to Zarnesti, involves
sustained terrain above 2000 m with frequent exposure and limited bail-out
options. The cabana is the recommended first night before committing to the
ridge. Capacity around 40.

## 6. Cabana Podragu, 2136 m — Fagaras ridge, Arges

On the Fagaras main ridge at 2136 m, the Cabana Podragu is the highest staffed
cabana on the ridge traverse and provides access to the summit of Moldoveanu
(2544 m) — Romania's highest point — in approximately 3 hours. The ridge
walking from Podragu in either direction is exposed and dramatic, with the
glacial lake Lacul Podragu directly below the hut.

## 7. Refugiul Bâlea Tunel, 2038 m — Fagaras, Transfagarasan

Near the south portal of the Transfagarasan tunnel at 2038 m, this refuge
provides year-round access via the tunnel road (open in summer) and serves
parties climbing in the Bâlea area. The Bâlea Lake (2034 m) and the Bâlea
ice hotel (a winter attraction) are nearby. The cable car from Cârțișoara
provides winter access.

## 8. Cabana Postăvaru, 1604 m — Postăvaru Massif, Brasov

Above the city of Brașov, the Cabana Postăvaru is one of the most accessible
mountain cabane in Romania — reached by cable car from Poiana Brașov ski
resort or on foot from the forest above Brașov. The surrounding Postăvaru
massif offers a day's walking circuit above the treeline. Popular as a
weekend destination for Brașov residents.

## 9. Cabana Cheile Turzii, 520 m — Turda Gorge, Cluj

A lower-elevation cabana in the dramatic Turda limestone gorge — technically
not alpine terrain but one of Romania's great walking destinations, with sheer
cliffs up to 300 m and extensive rock climbing routes. The gorge walk takes
3–4 hours return; the cabana provides overnight facilities for those exploring
the gorge in detail.

## 10. Cabana Cozia, 1577 m — Cozia Massif, Vâlcea

In the southern Carpathians above the Olt valley, the Cabana Cozia serves the
Cozia massif — a compact, forested range with an interesting ridgeline traverse
to the Cozia summit (1668 m) and views across the Olt river valley and the
monastery of Cozia below. Less visited than the Bucegi and Fagaras, offering
a quieter Romanian mountain experience.

## The cabana tradition

The Romanian cabana (plural cabane) tradition developed in the late 19th
and early 20th centuries under the influence of both Austro-Hungarian
mountain tourism (the Transylvanian Carpathians were part of the Habsburg
Empire until 1918) and a distinct Romanian national romanticism about the
Carpathian landscape. The Eminescu poetry tradition and the image of the
Carpathians as the soul of Romania gave the mountains a cultural resonance
beyond recreational use.

The cabane network was maintained through the communist period (1947–1989)
under state management, then partially privatised after 1989. The quality
varies considerably: some cabane are well-maintained private operations
with good food and facilities; others are functionally preserved but
unrenovated. The Cabana Mălăiești (2150 m) in the Bucegi is among the
most characterful — a stone building with a long history and a loyal
following among Romanian mountaineers.

## Transylvania and mountain access

The major Carpathian mountain areas are accessible from Transylvanian cities:
Brașov (gateway to the Bucegi and Piatra Craiului), Sibiu (gateway to the
Făgăraș), and Cluj-Napoca (gateway to the Apuseni limestone ranges).
Bucharest is 150 km from the Bucegi via the Prahova valley — the most
popular mountain access from the capital.

The mountain train to Sinaia (1–2 hours from Bucharest) and the cable car
from Sinaia to the Bucegi plateau (1400 m to 2000 m) makes the Bucegi the
most accessible high terrain in Romania. Cabana Babele and the Sphinx rock
formation at 2216 m are reached from the upper cable car station in a
30-minute walk — the most visited spot in the Romanian mountains.
