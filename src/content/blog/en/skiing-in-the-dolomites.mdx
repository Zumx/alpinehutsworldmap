The Dolomites in winter are one of the great paradoxes of Alpine skiing: the
most famous pistes in the world (the Sella Ronda circuit, Val Gardena's
Ciampinoi descent) sit within the same UNESCO World Heritage landscape that
also offers some of the most serious ski touring in the eastern Alps. The
Marmolada glacier (3343 m) rises from the south end of the Dolomite block as
the only true glacier in the range; the Sella Ronda connects four valleys
in a circuit that can be skied in both directions on prepared runs. Understanding
both layers of this landscape helps plan a more complete Dolomite ski experience.

## The Sella Ronda

The Sella Ronda is the landmark of Dolomite piste skiing: a circuit around
the Sella massif connecting the four valleys of Alta Badia, Val Gardena, Val
di Fassa, and Arabba in approximately 25 km of ski runs (and 45 km of
total connected terrain). The circuit can be completed in one day by
intermediate and above skiers using the colour-marked directions (orange for
anticlockwise, green for clockwise).

The route links 26 connected lifts. The terrain is predominantly blue and
red-graded, with several black sections optional for those seeking steeper
ground. The mountain restaurants and rifugi at lift stations — including the
Rifugio Plan Boè above the Sella group at 2873 m — provide refreshment stops
that are as much a part of the Sella Ronda experience as the skiing itself.

In good snow conditions (typically January through March), the circuit takes
4–6 hours of actual skiing, allowing time for lunch and a relaxed pace.
Queues at the key interconnections (particularly the Passo Sella to Val
Gardena connection) can be significant on clear weekends.

## Marmolada and glacier skiing

The Marmolada (3343 m) is the highest Dolomite summit and carries the only
significant glacier in the range — the Marmolada Glacier, which has retreated
substantially in recent decades. The summit cable car from Malga Ciapela
in the Val di Fassa reaches approximately 3265 m, and glacier skiing from the
top to Malga Ciapela offers around 1300 m of vertical.

The glacier skiing on Marmolada is subject to snow conditions that vary
considerably: early season (December–January), the glacier surface is typically
skiable; by late season (March–April), the lower sections may be closed due
to crevasse exposure. Current closure information is posted by the lift company.

The southern face of the Marmolada also holds the most significant piece of
First World War mountain history in the Dolomites: the Città di Ghiaccio
(City of Ice) — an elaborate system of tunnels and barracks driven into the
glacier itself by Austrian forces between 1916 and 1917. Nothing visible
remains on the surface, but the historical significance of the terrain is
noted at the base station.

## Refugio access in winter

Several Dolomite rifugi accessible in summer by marked trails become ski-touring
objectives in winter and early spring. The Rifugio Lagazuoi (2752 m) is reached
by cable car from the Falzarego Pass regardless of season; it serves as a
winter touring base for routes on the Lagazuoi massif. The Rifugio Averau
(2416 m) is accessible on skis from the Cinque Torri area and provides an
alternative base for tours above Cortina d'Ampezzo.

Ski touring in the Dolomites differs from the western Alpine norm: the terrain
is more broken and rocky, requiring careful navigation and a higher tolerance
for mixed and rocky sections in the approaches. The rewards — unique sculptured
rock formations in a winter setting, with the possibility of returning to a
full-service rifugio at the end of the day — are specific to this landscape.

## Practical notes for Dolomite skiing

The Dolomiti Superski pass covers 12 areas and 1200 km of connected pistes
across the Dolomite block, including the Sella Ronda circuit. It is the largest
single ski area pass in the world by piste kilometre count.

Base villages for the Sella Ronda circuit include Corvara/Alta Badia, Santa
Cristina and Ortisei in Val Gardena, Canazei in Val di Fassa, and Arabba in
the Belluno Dolomites. Each has its own character; the Val Gardena villages
are the most internationally renowned, the Alta Badia villages the most
traditionally Ladin-speaking.

Snow quality in the Dolomites is highly variable by aspect and elevation.
The northern and eastern aspects hold powder longer; the south-facing terrain
above the Marmolada and the lower-elevation Val di Fassa valley slopes
soften quickly in late-season sunshine.

## Ski touring in the Dolomites: what to know

Off-piste skiing and ski touring in the Dolomites requires awareness of the
specific hazards of the terrain. The Dolomite rock — calcium carbonate coral
reef limestone — is generally concealed under snow in good conditions but
rapidly exposed in early and late season. Ski edges and ski bases suffer
significantly from rock contact; carrying a repair kit and expecting to
service equipment more frequently than on pure snow terrain is standard.

Avalanche risk in the Dolomites is generally moderate — the terrain is less
extreme than the western Alps — but exists on the steeper couloirs and north-
facing bowls that hold powder after snowfall. Checking the South Tyrol/Trentino
avalanche bulletin before off-piste days is standard practice.

The Lagazuoi massif above the Falzarego Pass has become a popular ski-touring
area for parties staying at the Rifugio Lagazuoi (accessible by cable car
in winter). The terrain includes a descent through the Armentarola valley
toward San Cassiano — 10 km of off-piste run — that is one of the most
popular backcountry descents in the Dolomites.

## The Sellaronda on skis

The Sella Ronda's charm for experienced intermediate skiers is the combination
of distance, vertical, and cultural density in a single day. Doing the circuit
clockwise (green markers), the sequence is: Alta Badia → Campolongo Pass →
Arabba → Sella Pass → Canazei → Gardena Pass → Val Gardena → back to Alta
Badia. Each pass has a ski-hotel or rifugio for lunch; the Rifugio Plan Boè
above the Passo Sella is the standard high lunch stop.

The timing challenge: the circuit takes 4–6 hours of actual skiing; weather
deterioration in the early afternoon can close sections of the circuit and
leave parties stranded in a valley with limited transport options. Starting
early (8:30 at the first lift), maintaining a forward pace, and abandoning
the lunch stop ambition if conditions change are the keys.

## Dolomite ski schools and guides

The Dolomite valleys — Val Gardena, Alta Badia, Cortina d'Ampezzo — have
among the finest ski instructor traditions in Europe. The val Gardena ski
school, based in Ortisei, trains Ladin-culture instructors from childhood;
the multi-language environment (Ladin, German, Italian, and English are all
spoken in a single lesson group) is distinctive. For off-piste ski touring
in the Dolomites, the Cortina Guide service and local mountain guide
associations provide guided access to the more serious terrain above the
pisted areas.

Ski touring in the Dolomites — particularly the routes around the Lagazuoi
and Tofana areas — requires rock-skiing kit (stiff skis, Dynafit or similar
tech binding, reinforced base) for the inevitable rocky sections in poor snow
years. Dolomite rock underfoot is not alpine powder skiing; it is a different
activity.
