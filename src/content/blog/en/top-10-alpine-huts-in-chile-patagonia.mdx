Chilean Patagonia stretches from the Lakes District in the north to the Tierra
del Fuego archipelago in the south, and the mountain landscape changes
dramatically across this distance: the granite towers and walls of the Torres
del Paine, the volcanic peaks and lakes of the Araucanía region, and the
ancient beech forests that clothe the lower mountain slopes everywhere. The
refugio network is a combination of CONAF-managed facilities in the national
parks, private operations in the Torres del Paine circuit, and mountaineering
huts in the Argentine-Chilean border zone.

## 1. Refugio Frey, 1700 m — Bariloche, Río Negro (Argentina side)

Although technically in Argentina rather than Chile, the Refugio Emilio Frey
in the Cerro Catedral area above Bariloche is so central to Patagonian mountain
culture that it belongs on any regional list. The hut sits at 1700 m below
the extraordinary granite towers of Cerro Catedral (2388 m) — a major rock
climbing destination with over 200 routes. Capacity around 90; the approach
from the Lago Gutiérrez road takes 2.5–3 hours. CONAF-connected but privately
run; full meals service in season.

## 2. Refugio Grey, 50 m — Torres del Paine W Circuit

On the W Circuit of Torres del Paine National Park, Refugio Grey sits at the
shore of Lago Grey with the Grey Glacier calving face visible across the lake.
Operated by Vertice Patagonia; capacity around 100 in dormitory and private
room combination. The approach from the Paine Grande camping area takes 4–5
hours along the lakeshore. The glaciological change at the Grey Glacier —
active calving, floating icebergs, visible retreat — is one of the most dramatic
climate-change indicators in South America. Pre-booking 6 months in advance
is required for peak season (November–March).

## 3. Refugio Chileno, 380 m — Torres del Paine W Circuit

On the approach to the Torres del Paine viewpoint, Refugio Chileno sits at
approximately 380 m above sea level (though surrounded by peaks far higher)
halfway up the Valle del Ascencio. Operated by Fantastico Sur. The Torres
viewpoint is 2 hours above; most parties ascend at dawn from Refugio Chileno
for the sunrise light on the towers. The refugio is the most coveted overnight
booking on the W Circuit.

## 4. Refugio Las Torres, 100 m — Torres del Paine base

At the base of the Valle del Ascencio, Refugio Las Torres (and the associated
Hotel Las Torres) is the main accommodation hub for the east end of the W
Circuit and the O Circuit. Access is by 4WD from Puerto Natales (3 hours).
Multiple accommodation grades from luxury hotel to basic refugio dormitory.

## 5. Refugio Paine Grande, 50 m — Torres del Paine central

The largest refugio complex in Torres del Paine, at the junction of the W
Circuit routes toward Grey and Cuernos, with a catamaran ferry connection
from the park entrance. Capacity around 130; full meals service; the social
hub of the W Circuit mid-point. Wind conditions at Paine Grande are extreme
— gusts exceeding 100 km/h are not uncommon in summer.

## 6. Refugio Otto Meiling, 1350 m — Cerro Tronador, Río Negro (Argentina)

Below the summit of Cerro Tronador (3478 m) — the highest volcano in Patagonia
— the Refugio Otto Meiling sits at 1350 m on the Argentine side of the Andes.
Named after a German-Argentine mountaineer who was central to Patagonian
alpinism in the 20th century, the hut serves as base camp for Tronador's
various summit routes. Capacity around 80; the approach from Pampa Linda takes
3 hours.

## 7. Refugio Toni Rohrer, 1200 m — Osorno volcano, Los Lagos

On the flanks of Osorno volcano (2652 m) above the Lago Llanquihue, the
Refugio Toni Rohrer provides the base for Osorno summit attempts. The upper
mountain carries a permanent ice cap; crampons and ice axe are required above
the snowline at approximately 1800 m. The glacier skiing on Osorno's south
slopes is a Patagonian specialty.

## 8. Refugio El Caulle, 1700 m — Puyehue area, Los Ríos

Near the Puyehue volcano complex (which erupted significantly in 2011), the
Refugio El Caulle serves trekkers in the Volcán Puyehue National Park area.
The landscape in this zone has been dramatically altered by the 2011 eruption,
with new lava fields and ash deposits changing the route network.

## 9. Refugio de Montaña Cerro Provincia, 2100 m — Río Clarillo, Maule

Near Santiago in central Chile, the Cerro Provincia refugio at 2100 m is one
of the most accessible mountain huts from the capital — reached in 3–4 hours
by car and then 2 hours on foot from the Río Clarillo National Reserve. The
Andes above Santiago carry snow from May to October; the refugio serves ski
touring and summer hiking seasons.

## 10. Refugio Tinquilco, 750 m — Huerquehue National Park

In the Araucanía region, Refugio Tinquilco sits at the entrance to the
Huerquehue National Park near Pucón. The park's trail network passes through
old-growth lenga beech and coihue forest to high-altitude lakes. One of the
most scenically accessible refugios in Chilean Patagonia.

## The Torres del Paine circuit in detail

The W trek in Torres del Paine takes 4–5 days and covers three major arms
of the park: the Valle del Francés (Los Cuernos glacier valley), the
Mirador Las Torres (the base of the granite towers), and Lago Grey (the
blue iceberg-calving glacier lake). The circuit trek (Q trek or O trek)
adds the less-visited western and southern sections of the park, adding
3–4 days and a significantly wilder experience. The circuit section carries
fewer walkers than the W, and the CONAF (Corporación Nacional Forestal)
refugios on the circuit are smaller and more basic.

CONAF permits for the circuit are issued in limited numbers and are
separate from the W permits. Independent camping is permitted at designated
sites; the CONAF refugio system and the private Vertice Patagonia and
Fantastico Sur networks each manage different sections. Confirming which
sections of your planned route are served by which operator — and booking
the appropriate system — is essential before departure.

## Patagonian weather: what to expect

Patagonian weather at Torres del Paine (50°S) is driven by the Southern
Ocean westerlies — some of the most consistent and powerful winds on Earth.
The "Patagonian wind" is not a metaphor: gusts of 100+ km/h in the valley
between the Torres and the Cuernos are routine on wind days. Trekking in
wind that makes forward progress difficult is part of the experience rather
than an aberration.

Rain at Torres del Paine averages approximately 600 mm annually at the
park visitor centre, distributed throughout the year. The driest and calmest
period is December through February (Southern Hemisphere summer), but no
month is reliably dry. Waterproof gear of genuine quality — not fashion
waterproofing — is the baseline kit. The refugio kitchens are crowded
with wet gear at the end of every day regardless of season.

## Booking and permits

CONAF park entry fees apply to all visitors; fees for international visitors
are approximately USD 21–27 (2025 rates) per person. All trek route overnight
bookings must be made through either the Fantastico Sur system (manages
the Las Torres and Eastern W sections) or Vertice Patagonia (manages the
Refugio Grey and western sections) or CONAF directly for the circuit campsites.
Online booking is essential; walk-in capacity is essentially zero in the
November–February peak season.
