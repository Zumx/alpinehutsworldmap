Aconcagua (6961 m) in the Argentine province of Mendoza is the highest peak
in the Americas, the highest outside Asia, and one of the Seven Summits. It
attracts approximately 5,000 permit-holding climbers per year during its season
(November–March), making it the most commercially developed high-altitude
mountain outside Nepal. The camp system — permanent structures and designated
camp areas on two main ascent routes — is the most elaborate of any mountain
in the Americas.

## 1. Plaza de Mulas, 4370 m — Normal Route Base Camp

The main base camp for the Normal Route (the most popular Aconcagua ascent
via the northwest face), Plaza de Mulas at 4370 m is a year-round staffed
camp with commercial tented accommodations, a mountain rescue post (rescatistas),
medical facility, satellite communication, and a remarkable range of services
for a location 40 km from the nearest road. Operated under permits issued by
the Mendoza provincial authorities. The approach from Puente del Inca (2720 m)
takes 2 days via the Horcones valley. Capacity in the commercial tented camps
collectively runs to several hundred people in peak season.

## 2. Plaza Argentina, 4200 m — Polish Glacier Route Base Camp

The base camp for the Polish Glacier Route (the southeast face route, more
technically demanding than the Normal Route and involving glacier climbing
on the Polish Glacier) sits at 4200 m in the Vacas valley. The approach from
Los Penitentes takes 3 days via the Relinchos valley. Plaza Argentina is quieter
than Plaza de Mulas and serves a smaller, more technically experienced clientele.
Basic park warden facilities and designated camping.

## 3. Plaza Francia, 4200 m — South Face Base Camp

The base camp for the south face approaches — among the most technically
demanding routes on any Seven Summit — at approximately 4200 m near the snout
of the French Glacier. The south face of Aconcagua is a 3000 m face with
routes rated D to ED; not accessible to non-technical climbers. Plaza Francia
is used primarily by serious alpinists.

## 4. Camp 1 (Normal Route), 5050 m — Nido de Cóndores

The standard acclimatisation camp on the Normal Route at 5050 m — Nido de
Cóndores (the Condors' Nest) — is a wide flat area with wind-sheltered spots
behind rock walls built by climbers over decades. Most guided teams spend 2
nights here as part of the rotation acclimatisation protocol (ascend high,
descend to base, re-ascend). No permanent hut; tent camping only.

## 5. Camp 2 (Normal Route), 5560 m — High Camp / Casa de Piedra

A secondary camp at 5560 m on the Normal Route, sometimes called Casa de
Piedra for a large boulder that provides partial wind shelter. The terrain
above here becomes more demanding, with the Canaleta couloir (loose scree
at very high altitude) and the final ridge to the summit.

## 6. Camp 3 (Normal Route), 6000 m — White Rocks / Piedras Blancas

The high camp on the Normal Route at approximately 6000 m, typically used
only by teams making a summit push. The altitude effects here — poor appetite,
disrupted sleep, reduced reasoning — are pronounced. Guides emphasise that the
decision to proceed from Camp 3 requires honest self-assessment.

## 7. Refugio Berlin (Historic), 5970 m — Normal Route

The famous "Berlin" hut, once located at approximately 5970 m on the Normal
Route, was a metal structure used by summit parties from the 1980s through
the 2000s. Severely damaged by storms and later removed, it is now part of
Aconcagua's mountaineering history rather than a functional shelter. It is
mentioned in virtually every historical account of Normal Route ascents and
its removal marked a change in how the upper mountain is managed.

## 8. Camp 1 Polish Glacier Route, 5000 m — Polish Glacier lower slopes

The first camp on the Polish Glacier Route at approximately 5000 m establishes
a base for the technical glacier climbing above. The Polish Glacier (or Polish
Traverse) involves 45–55 degree glacier ice — crampons, ice axes, and rope
management are required.

## 9. Refugio Emilio Frey (Mendoza side), 1700 m — Cerro Catedral, Bariloche

In the broader Argentine Patagonia region near Bariloche, the Refugio Emilio
Frey (distinct from the similarly named hut discussed in the Chile/Patagonia
section) serves as the primary hut for the granite walls of Cerro Catedral
above Lago Gutiérrez. Capacity around 90; the primary technical climbing hut
in Argentine Patagonia.

## 10. Casa de Guías Mendoza, 720 m — Mendoza city

The guides' association offices and mountain support infrastructure in Mendoza
city serve as the logistical base for all Aconcagua expeditions. All permits,
guide contracts, and equipment checks begin here or at the park entrance at
Puente del Inca.

## Permits and logistics

All Aconcagua climbers and trekkers must hold a permit issued by the
Mendoza Provincial Parks authority (aconcagua.mendoza.gov.ar). Permit
fees are tiered by season and by activity: full climbing permits for the
peak season (December 15 – January 31) are the most expensive, currently
approximately USD 900 per person. Trekking-only permits (allowing access
to Plaza de Mulas but not the summit) and low-season climbing permits
are available at reduced rates.

The permit system includes a mandatory medical examination fee. All
climbers must have a physical examination on arrival at the park entrance;
those showing signs of inadequate cardiovascular fitness, pre-existing
altitude sickness, or other disqualifying conditions may be denied entry.
The park medical staff at Plaza de Mulas can also order descent for any
climber whose condition deteriorates.

Guided services are not mandatory but are strongly recommended for
independent climbers attempting the mountain for the first time. Several
Argentine and international guide companies operate full-service expeditions
including mule transport, camp setup, high-altitude cooking, and summit
guide support. The guided trip cost is typically USD 5,000–9,000 depending
on the season and service level.

## Acclimatisation schedule

Aconcagua's altitude demands a structured acclimatisation programme.
The standard guided approach takes 18–21 days from the trailhead to
summit and back, including multiple rotation days (climbing higher then
returning to base camp to sleep lower). The Rothlisberger rule — "climb
high, sleep low" — is fundamental at Aconcagua. The failure rate for
climbers who attempt to summit in fewer than 14 days from sea level is
significantly higher than for those who take 18–21 days.

Acute mountain sickness (AMS), high-altitude pulmonary edema (HAPE), and
high-altitude cerebral edema (HACE) are genuine risks above 5000 m.
The Aconcagua mountain rescue service (rescatistas) operates a helicopter
rescue system for medical emergencies; the cost of helicopter rescue is
covered by mandatory insurance that is part of the permit fee. Climbers
without separate high-altitude rescue insurance are advised to check their
permit coverage carefully.

## The summit: what to expect

The summit of Aconcagua (6961 m) can be reached in good conditions by
a fit, well-acclimatised non-technical climber on the Normal Route. The
final 2000 m from Nido de Cóndores to the summit via the Canaleta couloir
takes 8–12 hours round trip from Camp 2. The Canaleta — a steep, loose
scree couloir at extreme altitude — is universally described as the most
dispiriting section of the climb: slow, difficult, and physically taxing
at 6500–6900 m. The summit is wide and flat; in clear conditions, the
Pacific Ocean is visible to the west and the Pampas to the east. The
descent from the summit back to Plaza de Mulas takes most teams 2 days.
