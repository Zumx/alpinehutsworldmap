The Tour du Monte Rosa is one of Europe's great long-distance hut circuits —
a 180-kilometre traverse around the Monte Rosa massif crossing between
Switzerland and Italy at six different passes. At elevations consistently above
2000 m and with several stages crossing glaciated terrain, it is more demanding
than the Tour du Mont Blanc and significantly more serious than most European
long-distance routes. The reward is a perspective on the Monte Rosa massif —
the highest multi-summit group in the Alps outside the Mont Blanc range —
from every angle.

## 1. Rifugio Quintino Sella al Felik, 3585 m — Val d'Ayas, Valle d'Aosta

On the Italian approach to Monte Rosa, the Rifugio Quintino Sella al Felik
at 3585 m is the standard Italian-side staging hut for the Zumsteinspitze,
Signalkuppe, and the Capanna Margherita. CAI-managed; capacity around 100.
The hut sits above the Felik Glacier and serves parties acclimatising before
the high Monte Rosa summits. The evening view from the terrace takes in the
entire Italian side of the massif.

## 2. Rifugio Capanna Giovanni Gnifetti, 3647 m — Val Sesia, Piedmont

The second highest CAI rifugio in Italy (after the Capanna Margherita above),
the Gnifetti hut at 3647 m serves the Valsesia approach to Monte Rosa via
the Indren cable car. Capacity around 80; the Gnifetti was founded in 1889
by the CAI section of Varallo. From here, the ascent to the Rifugio Capanna
Regina Margherita on Punta Gnifetti takes 4–5 hours via the Lys Glacier.

## 3. Rifugio Capanna Regina Margherita, 4554 m — Punta Gnifetti, Monte Rosa

The highest hut in Europe at 4554 m, perched on the summit of Punta Gnifetti
in the Monte Rosa massif. (Full entry in the dedicated Capanna Margherita
article.) The CAI's flagship high-altitude hut; a mandatory milestone on any
Monte Rosa circuit.

## 4. Monte Rosa Hut, 2883 m — Gorner Glacier, Wallis

The architecturally celebrated SAC Monte Rosa Hut (see dedicated article) at
2883 m above Zermatt, providing the Swiss-side base for the Monte Rosa circuit.
The Swiss approach to the massif — from Rotenboden via the Gorner Glacier —
makes the Monte Rosa Hut the pivotal overnight on the western circuit stages.

## 5. Rifugio Riffelhaus / Riffelalp Resort, 2222 m — Zermatt area

The resort and small rifugio on the Riffelalp terrace above Zermatt (accessible
by cogwheel railway from Zermatt) provides a comfortable lower-elevation stage
on the circuit. The Matterhorn and Monte Rosa massif are both visible from
here; the Gornergletscher panorama is outstanding.

## 6. Täschalp / Täsch Hütte area, 2000 m — Täschtal, Wallis

On the Swiss circuit stage above the Täsch valley, high alm accommodation
provides the overnight between Zermatt and the Saas valley crossing. The
Täschtal is one of the lesser-known valleys of the Zermatt area, with a
traditional character.

## 7. Britanniahütte, 3030 m — Saas Fee, Wallis

Above Saas Fee, the Britanniahütte at 3030 m on the Hinter Allalin glacier
(reached by cable car) provides one of the finest acclimatisation overnights
in the circuit. The surrounding 4000 m peaks — Allalinhorn, Strahlhorn,
Rimpfischhorn — are all accessible in day ascents from here.

## 8. Rifugio Cingino, 2260 m — Antrona valley, Piedmont

On the Italian approach from the Antrona valley into the southern Monte Rosa
flanks, the Rifugio Cingino serves the most remote section of the Tour. The
Antrona valley retains a traditional alpine character with limited tourist
infrastructure.

## 9. Rifugio Città di Busto, 2480 m — Macugnaga, Piedmont

Above Macugnaga — the Italian village that looks directly at the enormous
east face of Monte Rosa (the biggest mountain face in the Alps at over 2700 m)
— the Rifugio Città di Busto at 2480 m serves the Monte Rosa east face
climbing approaches and the Tour du Monte Rosa circuit.

## 10. Rifugio Zamboni-Zappa, 2070 m — Macugnaga, Piedmont

A lower approach hut at 2070 m for the Macugnaga side of the circuit, the
Zamboni-Zappa serves parties ascending from the Macugnaga valley toward the
east face and the connecting stages toward the Italian summit ridges.

## Difficulty and timing

The Tour du Monte Rosa is rated a demanding alpine trekking route: most stages
involve 1200–1800 m of elevation change, several involve glacier crossings
at passes of 3000+ m, and the section involving the Capanna Margherita at
4554 m requires full altitude management. The full circuit takes 11–14 days
for fit, experienced mountain walkers. Several shorter versions are possible:
the 7-day Swiss-side circuit omitting the Italian high routes, or the 5-day
Italian-side circuit below the Margherita.

For the full circuit including the Capanna Margherita, the acclimatisation
profile matters enormously. A realistic schedule: arrive in Zermatt, spend
night 1 at the Monte Rosa Hut (2883 m) for acclimatisation, then proceed on
the circuit with the high Italian huts (Gnifetti, 3647 m, then Margherita,
4554 m) after several intermediate nights.

Weather on the Monte Rosa massif is variable in all seasons. The massif is
high enough to generate its own cloud and precipitation; the Italian and Swiss
sides can have completely different conditions simultaneously. Carrying a
flexible schedule — the ability to wait an extra day at a hut for a
weather window to cross a high pass — is essential for the full circuit.

## The Macugnaga east face

The east face of Monte Rosa as seen from Macugnaga is one of the great
mountain walls in the Alps. At approximately 2700 m of vertical from the
Macugnaga valley to the summit cluster, it is the largest mountain face in
the Alps — comparable in scale to the south face of Aconcagua or the Rupal
Face on Nanga Parbat. Seeing it from the Rifugio Città di Busto above
Macugnaga at dusk, with the last light on the ice walls above, is one of
the most spectacular views accessible from a staffed overnight hut in Europe.

The face's serious climbing routes (rated ED and above) are among the most
serious in the Alps; the approaches from the Italian huts below are accessible
to experienced mountaineers.

## The five highest summits of the Alps

The Monte Rosa massif contains five of the ten highest peaks in the Alps.
The Dufourspitze (4634 m) is the highest summit in Switzerland and the
second highest in the Alps after Mont Blanc; the Nordend (4609 m), Zumsteinspitze
(4563 m), Signalkuppe (4554 m — site of the Capanna Margherita), and
Parrotspitze (4432 m) complete the cluster. All five can be reached from
the Italian huts in favourable conditions by experienced mountaineers.

This concentration of 4000 m peaks accessible from a hut system with
full-service accommodation makes the Monte Rosa massif unique in the Alps:
no other area combines the altitude of the main Himalayan foothills with
the infrastructure of a staffed overnight network at 3600–4554 m. The
comparison with the Karakoram base camps is instructive — at those altitudes,
the accommodation is a tent; at Monte Rosa, it is a working rifugio with
hot food and a dining room.

## Booking the Italian huts

The Italian rifugi on the Monte Rosa massif — Rifugio Quintino Sella,
Rifugio Gnifetti, and Capanna Margherita — are operated by the Club Alpino
Italiano (CAI) sections and Monterosa Ski company. Booking is through the
individual rifugi by phone or email (Italian preferred; basic English
usually understood) or through the CAI national booking system where
available. The high-season (July–August) dates fill quickly; the Capanna
Margherita in particular is in high demand for the overnight experience
at 4554 m.
