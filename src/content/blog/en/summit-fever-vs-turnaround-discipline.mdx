## The Pattern That Repeats

Mountain accident reports from the DAV, SAC, ÖAV, and the PGHM (the French mountain rescue gendarmerie) share a pattern across hundreds of incidents. A party sets out with a specific summit objective. Conditions deteriorate or the pace falls behind schedule partway through. The party continues anyway. The accident occurs on the descent — tired, in deteriorating weather, on terrain they might have managed fine in the morning.

The post-incident language is consistent: they were already three-quarters of the way there; they didn't want to have come all this way for nothing; the weather looked like it might hold a bit longer; turning around felt like failure. None of these are irrational thoughts. All of them are traps.

The cognitive failure that alpine accident investigators call "summit fever" — and that psychologists studying high-risk decision-making call "goal commitment bias" — is not a character flaw particular to reckless individuals. It is a predictable human response to sunk investment. Understanding it structurally, rather than dismissing it as something that happens to overconfident beginners, is the prerequisite for avoiding it.

## Sunk Cost in the Mountain Context

The sunk cost fallacy in economic decision-making describes the error of continuing a losing course of action because resources have already been invested. The investment is gone regardless of what you decide next; the only rational question is whether the future action is worth its future costs and risks.

In the mountain context, the sunk costs are vivid and physical — three hours of climbing, significant elevation gain, the cost of the booking at the approach hut, the planning effort, the annual leave taken. These are real investments. But the summit is not owed to you in proportion to them, and the terrain does not adjust its risk level based on how much you have already spent to get there.

Studies of mountaineering fatalities in the Alps by accident researchers from the DAV Safety Research division consistently find that a high proportion of fatal accidents occur on the descent — particularly in the afternoon, when parties have already exceeded their planned turnaround time. The peak death window on days with weather deterioration is between 2 pm and 5 pm, in conditions that were foreseeable from the morning.

## Time-Based Turnaround Rules

The oldest and most respected decision framework in alpine guiding is the time-based turnaround. Before leaving the hut, set a specific time by which you must begin descending, regardless of summit position. This time is derived from the known length of the descent, the sunset or weather window, the condition of the most difficult section on the way down, and your party's realistic pace.

The rule is only useful if it is set before departure — at the hut, with coffee, in the calm of the morning. The turnaround time set on the ascent, when the summit is visible and the weather is fine, is the only honest one. The turnaround time reassessed at 2 pm on a narrowing ridge with incoming cloud is inevitably optimistic.

Guides on the Matterhorn typically operate a 9 am turnaround from the summit — if you are not at or past the summit of the Hörnli Ridge by 9 am, you turn around regardless of your position. The summit is 100 metres away? You turn around. This apparent rigidity is the result of decades of accident patterns on that specific mountain.

## Weather Decision Frameworks

The 3x3 filter, developed by Werner Munter as part of his avalanche decision-making system and subsequently adapted to general alpine risk assessment, provides a structured framework for the go/no-go decision at three levels: the regional level (what is the forecast showing across the range?), the route level (what specific terrain characteristics apply to today's objective?), and the local level (what is the current observation — snow texture, wind speed, cloud development, temperature?) telling you?

Each level adds or subtracts from the overall risk picture. The filter is not a checklist that outputs a binary answer — it is a structuring tool that forces the decision-maker to consider each level explicitly rather than collapsing all risk judgement into a single gut impression.

The key insight from the Munter framework is that local conditions — what you can see and feel right now — tend to be given too much weight relative to regional and route-level information when humans make intuitive risk assessments. A clear morning and a confident feeling on a technically straightforward ridge creates an optimism that the regional forecast (afternoon convective storms, 70% probability from 14:00) should counter. Explicit attention to each level of the filter prevents the local signal from overwhelming the broader picture.

## Group Dynamics and the Turnaround Decision

Turning around is harder in a group than alone. The social dynamics of a climbing party push toward continuation: no one wants to be the person who caused the retreat; a dominant member's confidence is contagious; the newer or less experienced members calibrate their own assessment to the apparent certainty of the stronger member. Research on alpine accident clusters — incidents involving groups of three or more — shows that group decision-making under time pressure reliably underestimates risk relative to the assessments any single member would make independently.

Guiding practice addresses this with a pre-agreed leadership structure: the guide's turnaround decision is not subject to group vote. Recreational parties often lack this structure, which means someone in the group must consciously take on the role of articulating the turnaround case even when doing so feels socially costly.

## What Guides Actually Do

Experienced alpine guides describe their decision-making less as the application of a framework and more as continuous reassessment — a running comparison between their plan and reality. A guide leading a party on the Lagginhorn (4010 m, Saas-Fee) may note at each stage whether their actual pace matches the planned pace, whether weather signals match the forecast, and whether group energy is following the expected curve. If two of those three are negative, the guide begins managing the turnaround decision actively rather than deferring it.

The experienced guide's advantage is not superior courage or superior fitness — it is a calibrated internal clock, built from hundreds of descents, that accurately predicts how much time a descent will take. This calibration is the basis of every sound turnaround decision.

## Building the Habit Before You Need It

The decision framework is most powerful when practiced on low-stakes days. Setting a turnaround time on a straightforward summer walk — and following it when you reach that time without having reached your objective — builds the habit and removes the cognitive novelty from the situation when the stakes are higher. Most experienced Alpine walkers describe specific incidents early in their careers when they turned around unnecessarily, by a cautious reading, and later understood that the practice of turning around was itself the skill they were developing.
