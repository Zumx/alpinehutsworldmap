The French Alps hold some of the most demanding and rewarding mountain terrain
in Europe, and the refuge network that serves them reflects that character:
serious, well-run, often booked months ahead, and uncompromising about safety
and reservation discipline. Most major refuges are operated by the FFCAM
(Fédération Française des Clubs Alpins et de Montagne) or affiliated local
clubs. The ten below anchor the most significant routes in the range. All are
plotted on the [map](/map).

## 1. Refuge du Goûter, 3835 m — Mont Blanc Massif

The main staging hut for the Voie Normale on Mont Blanc, reached from Les
Houches via the Tête Rousse or the Goûter couloir (one of the most
statistically dangerous passages in European mountaineering, due to rockfall).
The current building, opened in 2013, replaced an infamous structure that was
seriously overcrowded. Capacity is now around 120 with a highly regulated
timed-booking system. Book through the FFCAM portal months in advance for
July and August. Weather on the Goûter ridge can deteriorate from clear to
full storm within two hours — always check the Chamonix météo forecast the
day before.

## 2. Refuge des Cosmiques, 3613 m — Aiguilles de Chamonix

Perched on the Aiguille du Midi ridge above Chamonix, accessible by cable car
or by foot up the Mer de Glace and Géant glacier. The Cosmiques serves as
base for routes on the Aiguille du Midi, the Arête des Cosmiques, and the
Mont Blanc du Tacul. At 3613 m, acclimatization nights here are standard
preparation before attempting Mont Blanc. Run by CAF Chamonix with capacity
for around 100 guests. Half-board is compulsory; the dining room looks directly
onto the Brenva face.

## 3. Refuge Albert Premier, 2702 m — Chamonix Aiguilles Rouges

The base hut for the Aiguilles Rouges and a key overnight on the Tour du Mont
Blanc approaching from Argentière. At 2702 m it is accessible enough for a
day hike from the Charamillon cable car station, which makes it unusually
popular for a high refuge. Guardian-staffed from June to September, capacity
around 140, with half-board and a packed lunch service. The approach from Le
Tour to the Fenêtre de Balme is a reliable route even in early summer.

## 4. Refuge du Glacier Blanc, 2550 m — Écrins

The approach hut for the Barre des Écrins (4102 m) and Dôme de Neige, reached
from the Pré de Madame Carle roadhead above Vallouise. The 2550 m elevation
makes acclimatization less demanding than the Mont Blanc huts. The route from
the roadhead follows the Glacier Blanc moraine and takes roughly 2–3 hours.
Capacity around 130; FFCAM-affiliated, with a second nearby hut (Refuge
Cézanne) offering overflow. Routes from here cross crevassed glaciers requiring
rope and glacier travel competence.

## 5. Refuge du Promontoire, 3092 m — Écrins

Base hut for the Meije (3982 m), one of the last great Alpine peaks to be
climbed (first ascent 1877). The Meije is a serious technical objective and the
refuge reflects this — it attracts guided parties and competent independent
alpinists rather than casual hikers. Located above La Grave and accessible from
the La Grave–Les Deux Alpes cable car followed by a foot approach. The guardian
knows the Meije routes intimately; ask for current conditions.

## 6. Refuge du Soreiller, 2720 m — Écrins (Vercors/Oisans border)

Tucked into the Valjouffrey valley south of the Écrins core area, the Soreiller
serves routes on the Aiguille Dibona (a celebrated granite tower popular for
rock climbing) and the access to the Sirac massif. FFCAM-affiliated, capacity
around 40, with a reputation for good food and a quieter atmosphere than the
Mont Blanc refuges. The approach from Valsenestre takes 2.5 hours.

## 7. Refuge de l'Aigle, 3450 m — Écrins

One of the highest non-glacier refuges in the Écrins, on the crest of the
Barre des Écrins massif north ridge. Reached from the Plan de Valfourche or
from the Glacier du Lautaret approach. Serves routes on the Ailefroide and
Pelvoux (3946 m). A serious approach requiring crampons and ice axe in most
conditions — this is not a hike-up. Capacity around 60; guardian-staffed from
July through August.

## 8. Refuge Vallot, 4362 m — Mont Blanc Massif

Not an alpine club refuge in the traditional sense — the Vallot is an emergency
refuge and meteorological station maintained by the Chamonix authorities. It
sits on the shoulder of Mont Blanc at 4362 m and serves as a genuine emergency
shelter for parties caught by sudden weather. It is not bookable, not staffed,
and not intended for planned overnight stays. Sleeping there by choice rather
than necessity is discouraged and generates bad conditions for subsequent
emergency users. The 2-metre-square sleeping area is for emergencies only.

## 9. Refuge du Carro, 2760 m — Vanoise

In the Haute Maurienne near Val Cenis, the Refuge du Carro serves the Route
des Grandes Alpes long-distance route and circuits in the southern Vanoise.
This is quieter terrain than the Mont Blanc or Écrins massifs — the Vanoise
National Park restricts vehicles and infrastructure, keeping the environment
unusually pristine. Capacity around 50, FFCAM-affiliated.

## 10. Refuge de la Charpoua, 2841 m — Mer de Glace, Chamonix

A small, characterful refuge on the Mer de Glace lateral moraine above
Chamonix, with direct views of the Aiguilles and the retreating glacier below.
Used primarily as a base for rock routes on the Aiguille Verte and Aiguille
Droites. Capacity around 55. The approach is via the Montenvers railway and
then on foot across the glacier — glacier access changes year on year as the
Mer de Glace retreats; check current conditions with the refuge before
planning the approach.

## Planning across the French ranges

The French Alps cover a large geographic and difficulty range — from moderate
Vanoise circuits suitable for strong walkers to serious glacial routes in the
Écrins requiring full alpinist equipment. Book FFCAM refuges early (April for
July–August dates), always call ahead the day before to confirm, and check
météo-france mountain forecasts the evening before any glacier approach.

## The Écrins — France's alpine heartland

The Parc National des Écrins, centred on the Barre des Écrins (4102 m), is
the southernmost massif in the Alps with a significant permanent ice cap. The
routes here are more serious and less trafficked than the Mont Blanc massif —
you will not encounter the guided queues of the Goûter route. The FFCAM
refuges serving the Écrins are smaller, the approaches longer, and the
terrain more demanding, but this also means genuine mountain wilderness is
available within a relatively short drive from Grenoble or Gap.

The Refuge du Glacier Blanc is the standard staging point for Écrins summit
routes; the Refuge du Promontoire below the Meije is for experienced alpinists
only. The Écrins huts generally have excellent food by the standards of remote
French mountain huts — the Refuge Cézanne and Glacier Blanc huts in particular
are well-provisioned for their accessibility level.

## The Mont Blanc crowds problem — and the solution

The Mont Blanc massif in July and August is the most visited high-altitude
terrain in the world. Queues on the Goûter route, full huts, and the
associated fatigue of heavily used glaciers are all real. The solution most
guides and experienced alpinists apply: go in late June or early September,
target routes on the Italian or Swiss sides of the massif rather than the
Goûter, and use the quieter huts — Refuge des Cosmiques, Refuge de la
Charpoua — as bases for routes that see a fraction of the Goûter traffic.

The Aiguilles Rouges, the Argentière glacier, and the Aiguille Verte north
face routes from the Charpoua are excellent objectives with significantly
less competition for hut beds than the Normal Route infrastructure on the
main summit. The quality of the climbing is comparable; the queues are not.
