The Alta Via 1 is the Dolomites' original long-distance high route: 120
kilometres from Lago di Braies in the north to Belluno in the south, crossing
the range from the Sexten Dolomites to the Belluno Prealps across terrain that
alternates between demanding path, via ferrata, and open ridge walking. The
route was established in 1966 and has become one of the defining hut-to-hut
trails in the Alps. The hut sequence below covers the core overnights.

## 1. Rifugio Lagazuoi, 2752 m — Falzarego Pass, Ampezzo Dolomites

The midpoint landmark of the Alta Via 1, the Rifugio Lagazuoi at 2752 m above
the Falzarego Pass is reached by cable car or by the WWI tunnel route from
below. The family-run rifugio has capacity for approximately 80 guests and
serves some of the finest food on the high route. The WWI tunnel museum
extends through the Lagazuoi massif — a unique living history of the Italian
Front. The sunset terrace faces west across the Tofane and Marmolada.

## 2. Rifugio Nuvolau, 2575 m — Cinque Torri area, Ampezzo

On the Nuvolau summit at 2575 m, the Rifugio Nuvolau is the oldest rifugio
in the Dolomites — established in 1883 — and one of the most spectacularly
positioned. The summit plateau provides a 360-degree Dolomite panorama. Reached
from the Ra Gusela chairlift or on foot from the Cinque Torri area in 2 hours.
Capacity around 25; popular and books quickly.

## 3. Rifugio Averau, 2416 m — Cinque Torri, Ampezzo

Just below the Nuvolau on the Averau summit at 2416 m, the Rifugio Averau
provides more capacity (approximately 60) and a similar panoramic position
in the same area. Many Alta Via 1 walkers choose the Averau for the larger
dormitory and the Nuvolau for a dawn walk up to the smaller summit.

## 4. Rifugio Adolfo Sonino al Coldai, 2190 m — Civetta area, Belluno

Below the north face of the Civetta (3220 m) — the "Wall of the Dolomites"
— the Rifugio Coldai at 2190 m provides one of the most dramatic overnight
positions on the southern Alta Via 1. The Civetta north face is visible in
its entirety from the hut terrace. Capacity around 70; approached from Alleghe
in 3 hours. The stage from Coldai to the Rifugio Vazzolèr traverses terrain
below the great north walls.

## 5. Rifugio Attilio Tissi, 2250 m — Civetta massif, Belluno

On the west side of the Civetta at 2250 m, the Rifugio Tissi provides
excellent access to the Civetta group and the continuation south on the Alta
Via 1. Capacity around 60; the hut is named after Italian mountaineer Attilio
Tissi who pioneered several of the routes above.

## 6. Rifugio Bruto Carestiato, 1834 m — Zoldo Alto, Belluno

On the lower southern section of the Alta Via 1 above the Zoldo Alto valley,
the Rifugio Carestiato at 1834 m provides a mid-stage overnight in the less-
frequented southern Dolomite section. Capacity around 50; the surrounding
terrain of the Moiazza and Civetta southern foothills is quieter than the
Cortina area.

## 7. Rifugio Bosconero, 1457 m — Zoldo valley, Belluno

A lower-elevation overnight in the forested zone above the Zoldo valley,
the Bosconero serves the transit sections of the Alta Via 1 between the high
dolomite terrain and the Belluno foothills. Less dramatic than the high
positions but pleasant in the forest character.

## 8. Rifugio Fanes, 2060 m — Fanes Plateau, Ampezzo

In the upper section of the Alta Via 1 near Lago di Braies, the Rifugio Fanes
on the Fanes plateau at 2060 m provides access to the Fanes-Senes-Braies
Nature Park — a less-visited but extraordinarily beautiful section of the
northern Dolomites. Capacity around 80.

## 9. Rifugio Seekofel / Rifugio Biella, 2327 m — Pragser Wildsee area

Near the Pragser Wildsee (Lago di Braies) — the northern starting point of
the Alta Via 1 — the Biella rifugio provides the first or last high-elevation
overnight on the route. The Seekofel ridge above gives views across the entire
northern Dolomite panorama.

## 10. Rifugio 7th Alpini, 1502 m — Belluno plain approach

Near the southern end of the Alta Via 1 above Belluno, the 7th Alpini rifugio
marks the transition from high Dolomite terrain to the Venetian foothills.
A satisfying final overnight before the descent to Belluno and the bus or
train return north.

## Practical planning for the Alta Via 1

The Alta Via 1 is typically walked from north to south (Lago di Braies to
Belluno) over 10–12 days. Northbound is possible but the prevailing weather
is better for a south-facing descent, and the majority of the waymarking and
guidebooks assume the north-to-south direction.

The route is graded T2–T4 depending on section, with a handful of more
demanding sections around the Civetta area and the via ferrata approaches to
certain ridgeline viewpoints. A standard hiking boot with ankle support and
a 30-35 litre pack are appropriate; no technical climbing equipment is required
for the standard route.

Booking rifugi in advance is essential for July and August. The peak season
fills the key huts (Rifugio Lagazuoi, Rifugio Nuvolau, Rifugio Coldai) weeks
in advance. Bookings are made directly with each rifugio by phone or email;
there is no central reservation system for the Alta Via 1 network. Italian
language capability is helpful for telephone bookings; most rifugisti speak
at least basic English but telephone bookings in Italian are smoother.

The Dolomiti Superski lift network provides cable car access to several huts
on the route, including the Rifugio Lagazuoi (Falzarego cable car) and the
Rifugio Nuvolau (Ra Gusela chairlift). This access means that some sections
can be walked in reverse or shortened if a party needs to adjust for weather
or fitness. The cable car infrastructure also means that the Alta Via 1 core
passes through areas that are busy with day visitors, particularly around
Cortina d'Ampezzo; the character of the route shifts noticeably between the
busier cable-car zones and the quieter central sections around the Civetta.

## The Civetta: the Alta Via's centrepiece

The Civetta massif (3220 m) is the route's most dramatic landmark. Its
northwest face — a 1200 m wall of vertical and overhanging Dolomite limestone
— was described by Paul Preuss in 1911 as "the wall of walls" and is still
considered one of the great challenge faces in the Alps. The Via Tissi (TD)
and the Solleder-Lettenbauer Route (ED) on the northwest face are among the
most celebrated climbs in the Dolomites.

The Alta Via 1's passage through the Civetta area passes well below the
summit on the accessible trekking ground; the rifugi serving this section
(Rifugio Coldai at 2191 m and Rifugio Tissi at 2250 m) provide views of
the northwest face's upper sections from the terrace. The combination of
proximity to a great face and comfortable rifugio hospitality is one of the
Alta Via's particular pleasures.

## Starting from Lago di Braies

The northern starting point of Lago di Braies (Pragser Wildsee, 1494 m)
is one of the most visited lakes in the Dolomites — a turquoise glacial
lake surrounded by forest and Dolomite peaks that has become an iconic
social media image. This popularity means the starting point carries a
significant day-visitor crowd in July and August; the first morning of
the Alta Via 1 begins in this commercial tourist environment before
gradually ascending to quieter terrain.

Parking at Lago di Braies is limited and restricted; public transport from
the Bruneck/Brunico station (RegioExpress to San Candido, then bus) is the
recommended access approach in peak season.
