Piedmont occupies the south-western corner of the Italian Alps, and its name — literally the foot of the mountains — understates the case dramatically. Three of the great frontier massifs press against its borders: Monte Rosa to the north-east, the Gran Paradiso group to the north-west, and the Maritime Alps where the chain finally bends down toward the Ligurian Sea. Between them lie the high valleys of the Walser, German-speaking settlers who crossed the passes from the Valais in the thirteenth century and built timber-and-stone hamlets at altitudes the Latin valley folk avoided. The rifugi of Piedmont reflect this layered identity: glacier bases on the Rosa, ibex-country shelters along the Gran Paradiso boundary, and forgotten military posts above the southern valleys. The ten below trace that arc from the Walser high country down to the Maritime ridge.

## 1. Rifugio Gabiet, Gressoney Valley

At roughly 2,345 metres on the Colle Gabiet above Gressoney-la-Trinité, the Rifugio Gabiet is one of the principal gateways to the Monte Rosa massif from the Lys valley. A summer cable car lifts walkers most of the way, leaving a short approach to a comfortable hut sleeping around 60. The position is superb: eastward the ground falls toward the Swiss Valais, while westward the Lys glacier system climbs toward the Punta Gnifetti and the Capanna Regina Margherita at 4,554 metres, the highest staffed hut in Europe. The Lago Gabiet below the terrace draws day-walkers, but the rifugio's real role is as a launch point for the long glacier routes onto the Rosa plateau.

## 2. Rifugio Alpenzu Grande, Gressoney-Saint-Jean

The Rifugio Alpenzu Grande sits at about 1,776 metres on a terrace above Gressoney-Saint-Jean, in the middle of one of the best-preserved Walser settlements in the Alps. Reached on foot in around an hour, it occupies a cluster of dark larch-wood houses and stone granaries that are themselves a protected cultural landscape. The cooking leans heavily on Fontina and local mountain butter, and the terrace looks straight across the Lys valley toward the Rosa. It is a gentle introduction to the Walser high country, open roughly from June into October, and a useful first night for anyone working slowly toward the glacier huts above.

## 3. Rifugio Barbustel, Mont Avic Natural Park

Although administered from the Aosta side, the Rifugio Barbustel at around 2,186 metres serves walkers crossing between the Lys valley and the Mont Avic Natural Park, and it anchors the eastern end of a popular high traverse. Named for a local dialect word, the hut sleeps roughly 50 in a solid stone building set among larch and high meadow dotted with small tarns. The surrounding park protects one of the densest stands of mountain pine in the western Alps, and the trails radiating from Barbustel give some of the quietest walking in the whole Monte Rosa hinterland.

## 4. Rifugio del Misérin, Champorcher

At about 2,580 metres above the Champorcher valley, on the boundary of the Gran Paradiso National Park, the Rifugio del Misérin stands beside a small, exceptionally clear lake and a chapel that has drawn pilgrims since the seventeenth century. The hut sleeps around 40 and opens from late June through September. The terrain here is forgiving — broad pasture and gentle passes rather than ice — which makes the Misérin a favourite with families and with walkers crossing eastward into Piedmont's Orco valley. The pilgrimage festival in early July fills every bunk weeks ahead.

## 5. Rifugio Passo del Diavolo, Gran Paradiso

The Rifugio Passo del Diavolo sits at around 2,745 metres on a high col in the Gran Paradiso group, linking the Aosta valleys with the Orco valley on the Piedmont side. It is a basic but staffed shelter sleeping roughly 35, and its position on the pass gives a clean line of sight to the Gran Paradiso summit at 4,061 metres rising to the south. This is prime ibex country: the species was hauled back from near-extinction inside the old royal hunting reserve that became Italy's first national park in 1922, and dawn from the Diavolo pass often reveals herds grazing the screes.

## 6. Rifugio Forte Geremia, Orco Valley

Above the Orco valley at around 2,540 metres, the Rifugio Forte Geremia sits close to the Gran Paradiso park boundary and serves routes toward the Colle della Rossa and the western park trails. The Orco is the classic Piedmont approach to the massif — quieter than the Aosta valleys, with the granite domes of the Vallone di Piantonetto offering some of the finest crag climbing in the region. The hut accommodates around 40 and makes a logical base for a multi-day loop combining ibex watching with non-glaciated summits.

## 7. Rifugio Campolongo, Eastern Approach

The Rifugio Campolongo at roughly 1,860 metres is an entry-level overnight stop on the eastern approaches to the Gran Paradiso, in country less travelled than the better-known Cogne and Valsavarenche routes. It keeps a quiet, local character and accommodates around 40 guests through the summer season. For walkers building fitness before the higher huts, or for families wanting a single straightforward night out, the Campolongo is an honest and unpretentious choice with home cooking and an early-rising routine.

## 8. Rifugio Arp, Walser High Valleys

At around 2,478 metres, the Rifugio Arp guards a high tributary running up toward the frontier ridge, in terrain that feels markedly wilder than the busy resort valleys. Road access ends low, so the walk in is long and the sense of remoteness real. The hut is a useful midpoint on routes pushing toward the high passes, and it sits in classic Walser ground where the old transhumance paths still thread between summer pastures. Expect simple food, a small dormitory, and the kind of silence that has become rare in the Alps.

## 9. Lago Nero Shelter, Maritime Foothills

Tucked among the southern valleys, the shelter above Lago Nero — one of several alpine tarns of that name — gives walkers a base in the lower-altitude but rugged country where the Piedmont Alps begin their long descent toward Liguria. The lake itself, dark against grey scree, is a popular day objective, and the surrounding trails connect to old smugglers' and military paths that once watched the French frontier. It is gentler ground than the Rosa, but no less characteristic of the Piedmont mosaic.

## 10. Rifugio Forte beneath the Maritime Ridge

The southern Maritime Alps are studded with abandoned forts and barracks from the era when this was the contested border between Italy and France, and several have been restored as rifugi. These stone strongholds, perched on cols above 2,000 metres, now shelter hikers on the long-distance routes that cross the Maritime chain. The position is dramatic, the history palpable, and the views stretch on clear days all the way to the distant glint of the Mediterranean — a fitting end-point for a region that runs from glacier to sea.
