Japan's Southern Alps — the Minami Alps or Akaishi Mountains — run north-south through Yamanashi, Shizuoka, and Nagano prefectures, with ten peaks above 3,000 metres and the highest summit in Japan outside Mount Fuji: Kitadake at 3,193 metres. Less accessible than the Northern Alps and with fewer gondola shortcuts, the Minami Alps maintain a genuine remoteness; walkers typically reach them by bus from Hirogawara or Ikawako-daimon after a two-hour drive from Kofu or Shizuoka. The main walking season runs from early July to early October, with snowfall possible above 3,000 metres in late September. Mount Fuji (3,776 m), 70 kilometres east of the Southern Alps, is technically in its own volcanic category but shares the yamagoya tradition of the Japanese Alps: wooden huts at each numbered stage of the four main trails provide food, shelter, and supplemental oxygen for altitude-affected climbers. The official climbing season is July and August; outside these dates, the summit huts are closed and conditions can be severe.

## 銀明館 (Ginmeikan), Kitadake approach

Ginmeikan at around 2,230 metres is positioned on the lower Kitadake approach from Hirogawara, one of the valley-entry huts that provide the first overnight for walkers entering the Minami Alps from the north. The building sleeps 60 and serves full hot meals. The surrounding dense fir and beech forest creates a transition atmosphere before the trail breaks into the open terrain above 2,500 metres. From Ginmeikan, the standard ascent of Kitadake takes a further day via Shirane-Oike and the north ridge.

## 日ノ出館 (Hinoidekan), Kitadake mid-elevation

Hinoidekan at approximately 2,500 metres is a mid-elevation overnight stop on the Kitadake (3,193 m) ascent, sitting in the krummholz zone where the forest begins thinning to scattered dwarf conifers and open terrain. The hut sleeps 50 and provides views east toward the Yatsugatake range visible across the Kofu basin on clear days. Access to the summit from Hinoidekan is a three-to-four-hour ascent, typically attempted as a predawn start for Goraiko (sunrise) views from the highest non-volcanic peak in Japan.

## 山口山荘 (Yamaguchi Sanso), Kaikoma approach

Yamaguchi Sanso at 2,450 metres sits on the approach to Kaikomagatake (2,967 m), a peak known for its steep granite profile visible from the Kofu basin below. The sanso (mountain lodge) sleeps 40 and is a quieter facility than the main Kitadake huts. The Kaikomagatake approach from Tsugaike involves a sustained steep ascent through larch forest. The summit views west toward the Konsei ridge and north toward the Hokutodake group reward the effort of the approach.

## ブナハウス内山 (Buna House Uchiyama), beech forest camp

Buna House Uchiyama is a low-elevation shelter at around 1,500 metres in the broad-leaved beech forest zone of the southern Minami Alps. It caters to walkers approaching the southern peaks — Hijiri-dake (3,013 m) and Kamiyama (3,000 m) — from the Shizuoka side via Ikawa. The building is modest, sleeping 20, and the surrounding mature beech (buna) forest for which it is named is a protected example of the broadleaf woodland that once covered the lower Minami Alps more extensively before twentieth-century forestry. Spring visits in late May see spectacular beech leafing.

## 五合目金懸小屋 (Gougome Kanakake Koya), Mount Fuji fifth stage

Gougome Kanakake Koya operates at the fifth stage (2,400 m) of the Fujinomiya trail on Mount Fuji's southern flank, in Shizuoka prefecture. The Fujinomiya trail is the most direct route to the summit and the most popular among climbers from the Osaka-Kyoto-Nagoya direction. Fifth-stage huts serve as the entry point for climbers who drive up to the trailhead; from here the summit is four to five hours by the trail. The hut provides equipment rental, food, and a manned rest area. July and August nights at the fifth stage involve large queues of climbers preparing for overnight summit ascents.

## 大崩山荘 (Oomineyama-so), Omine approach, Shizuoka

Oomineyama-so at around 1,800 metres is positioned on an approach trail in the southern sector of the Minami Alps near Shizuoka prefecture, serving walkers on the less-frequented southern ridges. The lodge sleeps 30 and represents the quieter side of the Southern Alps experience — on a weekday in August, a walker might have the hut largely to themselves. The southern Minami Alps receive heavier rainfall from Pacific typhoons than the more sheltered northern section, and the vegetation reflects this with lush undergrowth and frequent cloud forest conditions.

## 東京屋 (Tokyo-ya), Yoshida fifth stage, Mount Fuji

Tokyo-ya operates at the fifth stage of the Yoshida trail (2,300 m) on Mount Fuji's northern flank in Yamanashi prefecture. The Yoshida trail is the most popular of Fuji's four routes and the only one with two staffed huts at the fifth stage. Tokyo-ya is a landmark building with a long history of serving climbers; the name references the Tokyo hiking clubs that historically used this approach. From here, the Yoshida trail's series of numbered stations — sixth through eighth and a half — leads to the summit crater in five to seven hours.

## 半蔵坊 (Hanzabo), Fuji seventh stage

Hanzabo at 3,010 metres on the Yoshida trail's seventh stage represents the point where most walkers begin to feel the effects of altitude on Mount Fuji. The hut provides supplemental oxygen for purchase — a practical necessity for perhaps 10% of summit climbers — along with hot meals, stamp services (collecting official Fuji stamps is a tradition on each trail), and emergency weather shelter. The seventh stage is also where the descent trail branches off from the ascent, separating the uphill and downhill flows of the enormous August crowds.

## 見晴館 (Miharashi-kan), Fuji eighth stage

Miharashi-kan at 3,350 metres on the Fujinomiya trail's eighth stage is one of the highest permanently staffed buildings in Japan outside of observatory and research facilities. The name means "viewing hall" — from this point on clear days, the horizon extends across the Pacific Ocean to the south and west. The hut sleeps 80 in cramped but warm conditions and provides the last hot meal available before the summit. Walkers who time their ascent to arrive here around midnight can rest and then continue to the summit for the 4:45 AM Goraiko in August.

## 敬神小屋 (Keishin Koya), Subashiri trail, Mount Fuji

Keishin Koya at around 2,700 metres on the Subashiri trail (the eastern Fuji route) is a smaller, quieter facility than its Yoshida and Fujinomiya counterparts, reflecting the Subashiri trail's lower traffic volume. The Subashiri approach through the old-growth forest of the Fuji-Hakone-Izu National Park lower slopes is considered the most atmospheric of the four ascent routes, and the hut retains a traditional wooden character rather than the utilitarian metal construction of the heavily rebuilt western trail huts. Summer crowds on this trail are a fraction of the Yoshida numbers.
