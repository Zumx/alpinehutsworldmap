The Laugavegur trek — Hot Springs Way — is Iceland's most famous walking route,
and its fame is entirely justified. Fifty-five kilometres of extraordinary
landscape from the rhyolite highlands of Landmannalaugar to the birch-forested
valleys of Þórsmörk: geothermal fields, obsidian lava deserts, glacial rivers,
blue-green glacier tongues, and one of the most concentrated displays of
volcanic geology accessible on foot anywhere on Earth. The FÍ (Ferðafélag
Íslands) huts along the route provide the infrastructure that makes this
terrain accessible to people who are not wilderness camping specialists.

## 1. Landmannalaugar Huts, 600 m — Fjallabak, southern Iceland

The starting point is the most important single overnight of the trek — not
because the huts are exceptional (they are functional, simple, and heavily
used) but because the setting is. The Landmannalaugar hot spring flows from
under a 1477 lava field at 600 m elevation; the rhyolite hills surrounding
the spring and the hut complex are striped with green, red, yellow, and brown
minerals. Soaking in the hot spring after arriving by 4WD bus from Reykjavík
and before walking the next morning is the classic Laugavegur beginning.
FÍ accommodation for approximately 200 people across multiple huts; self-
service cooking available; sleeping bag required.

## 2. Hrafntinnusker Huts, 1100 m — Laugavegur Day 1

The first day's walk (12 km, 5–7 hours) climbs from the geothermal lowlands
into the highest terrain of the route. The Hrafntinnusker area at 1100 m is
a world of obsidian glass, fumaroles, and — in early season — extensive
snowfields that require navigation ability. The FÍ hut complex has sleeping
bag capacity for approximately 80 people. Meals service in season. The fumaroles
are visible from the hut; steam venting from the hillside at -5°C and full
wind is one of the route's most memorable sensory combinations.

## 3. Álftavatn Huts, 540 m — Laugavegur Day 2

The descent from Hrafntinnusker to Álftavatn (540 m) drops from the volcanic
highlands to the glacial outwash lake — Swan Lake in Icelandic, named for the
whooper swans that rest here on migration. The FÍ hut at Álftavatn is one of
the most peacefully situated on the route. The glacier tongue of
Mýrdalsjökull is visible to the south. Day 2 also introduces the river crossing
challenge: the streams feeding Álftavatn from the north carry glacier melt and
require bare-foot crossings in summer (linking arms with companions is standard
practice in fast-flowing sections).

## 4. Hvangil/Hvanngil Hut, 570 m — Laugavegur variant

An alternative to the Álftavatn direct route, Hvanngil is a smaller FÍ camp
area at 570 m offering a quieter overnight option for parties who prefer
less company. Used by parties on the slower pace who want to break the Álftavatn-
to-Emstrur day.

## 5. Emstrur (Botnar) Huts, 490 m — Laugavegur Day 3

The camp at Emstrur (also named Botnar) at 490 m sits in the black sand and
canyon landscape of the Markarfljót outwash plain. The Markarfljót canyon
nearby — carved by glacial floods in repeated Jökulhlaup events — descends
200 metres in vertical walls of black basalt. The FÍ huts accommodate
approximately 70 people. This is the penultimate night for most groups; the
final day to Þórsmörk includes the route's most technical terrain.

## 6. Þórsmörk Huts (Langidalur), 200 m — Þórsmörk

The FÍ's Langidalur hut complex in Þórsmörk provides the primary endpoint
accommodation. The Þórsmörk valley is sheltered by three glaciers — Eyjafjallajökull,
Mýrdalsjökull, and Tindfjallajökull — and the microclimate is warmer and calmer
than the exposed highland sections above. Birch woodland covers the valley floor;
the contrast with the volcanic landscape of the previous four days is striking.

## 7. Volcano Huts, 200 m — Þórsmörk

A private alternative in the same Þórsmörk valley, the Volcano Huts offer
a slightly more comfortable (and expensive) overnight for those preferring
private rooms. Super-jeep accessible from Reykjavík.

## 8. Fimmvörðuskáli, 1060 m — Fimmvörðuháls ridge

For the extension of the Laugavegur onto the Fimmvörðuháls ridge toward Skógar
(an additional 23 km), the Fimmvörðuskáli hut at 1060 m between the two
glacier flanks is the overnight on the ridge. The 2010 Eyjafjallajökull eruption
created fresh lava fields just below the hut — still warm and steaming in the
first years after the eruption. FÍ hut; capacity around 80.

## 9. Skógar Hostel, 60 m — Skógar, south coast

If completing the Fimmvörðuháls extension to Skógar on the south coast of
Iceland, the hostel at Skógar near the famous 60-metre Skógafoss waterfall
is the final overnight before bus connections to Reykjavík.

## 10. Básar á Goðalandi, 200 m — Þórsmörk alternate

The Básar FÍ camp on the Goðaland side of Þórsmörk provides an alternative
to the main Langidalur huts — quieter, reached by a different super-jeep
track, with excellent trails into the Goðaland canyon area above.

## Booking and logistics

Laugavegur hut reservations are managed by the FÍ (Ferðafélag Íslands)
through utivist.is. The booking window opens in January for the following
summer season, and the most popular huts — Landmannalaugar and Þórsmörk —
fill within days. The full route in July and August can be booked to absolute
capacity; June (especially early June when snow may cover the highest sections)
and September (when weather is more unsettled but crowds are smaller) offer
better availability.

Access to the Laugavegur trailheads requires highland transport. Landmannalaugar
is 180 km from Reykjavík by F-road (highland road, F26 and F208); it is
accessible by 4WD super-jeep bus service from Reykjavík during the July–September
season. Standard 2WD vehicles cannot reach Landmannalaugar without multiple
unbridged river crossings. The Reykjavik Excursions bus service (re.is) runs
scheduled buses to Landmannalaugar in season, making it possible to do the
route without a vehicle.

Þórsmörk is similarly reached by F-road (F249) and requires a super-jeep
to cross the Krossá river. Bus connections back to Reykjavík from Þórsmörk
are available through Reykjavik Excursions and Trex.

## Weather and conditions

The Laugavegur route at its highest point (Hrafntinnusker, 1100 m) is
exposed to full Icelandic highland conditions: wind, rain, fog, and snow
are possible in any month of the walking season. Snowfields on the Day 1
approach to Hrafntinnusker can persist into July in heavy-snow years,
requiring basic snow navigation skills (using a compass or GPS in cloud).
The route is not technically difficult, but it is genuinely exposed and
parties who go unprepared for cold wet conditions have required rescue.

The Icelandic Met Office (vedur.is) provides 5-day forecasts for specific
highland locations including Landmannalaugar. The FÍ hut wardens also
provide route condition updates on the FÍ website during the season.
Waterproof clothing, gaiters, and sturdy footwear adequate for river
crossings are essential regardless of the forecast.

## The Fimmvörðuháls extension

The continuation from Þórsmörk to Skógar via the Fimmvörðuháls pass
(1067 m) is a spectacular addition that extends the trek to approximately
80 km and adds one extra day. The Fimmvörðuháls section crosses the ridge
between the Eyjafjallajökull and Mýrdalsjökull glaciers, passing the 2010
eruption craters — fresh lava fields still geologically newborn — and
descending via 26 waterfalls to the south coast at Skógar. The
Fimmvörðuskáli hut at 1060 m is the overnight on the ridge. The full
Laugavegur-Fimmvörðuháls combination is widely regarded as one of the
finest multi-day walks in the world.
