Not every alpine hut has a guardian cooking dinner. Across the Alps, a
substantial number of shelters operate in self-service mode — either
permanently, seasonally, or in designated self-catering wings of larger staffed
huts. Understanding the spectrum from fully staffed rifugio to locked metal
bivouac box determines what you carry, what you can safely plan, and whether
your route is viable without advance provisioning.

## The staffed hut in season

A fully staffed alpine hut runs from roughly mid-June to late September, with
some higher huts opening later (early July) and closing earlier (mid-September)
depending on snow conditions and access. During this window, a guardian and
often a small kitchen team are present, meals are served on a fixed half-board
schedule, and basic supplies are available for purchase. This is the standard
alpine hut experience.

Staffing is expensive and logistically complex. Most huts receive supplies by
helicopter one or two times per week. The guardian is responsible for the
safety of everyone on the premises and often serves as the first point of
contact for mountain rescue coordination. During the staffed season, the hut
is as close to a hotel as the Alps produce — relative to the alternatives.

## The winter room or Winterraum

When the main hut closes for the season, most SAC, ÖAV, and DAV huts maintain
a Winterraum or winter room: a separately accessible section of the building
with basic bunks, blankets, a wood or gas stove, and sometimes a small stock
of emergency food. The key to the Winterraum is usually collected from a named
location in the nearest valley village — a local alpine club office, a specific
hotel, or a key box with a code obtainable by phone or email.

Winterraum capacity is typically 8–20 people. There is no guardian, no meals,
and no reliable water unless the main spring is still running. Users are
expected to leave the space clean, replace any wood or supplies used, and
contribute to a log book. Heating a Winterraum in a snowstorm with a small
wood stove while cooking on a single gas ring is a fundamentally different
experience from arriving for a guardian-cooked dinner — bring this expectation
into your planning.

## Bivacchi and emergency shelters

The Italian bivacco fisso is the most basic fixed shelter in the Alps: a
metal or fibreglass box bolted to rock, typically accommodating 6–15 people
with sleeping platforms, a basic blanket supply, and sometimes a small stove.
No guardian, no meals, no toilet beyond a designated area outside. They exist
on high routes where building a full hut would be impractical and where
emergency shelter is genuinely life-saving.

Notable examples include the Bivacco Lissone on the ridge above the Dolomite
plateau, and multiple bivacchi on the descent routes from the Gran Paradiso
massif. These shelters are almost universally free to use and are intended as
emergency infrastructure — not as a budget accommodation choice on a route
where staffed huts are available.

Equivalent structures in the German-speaking Alps are called Biwakschachtel
(bivouac boxes) and serve the same function. In France, the term refuge
non-gardé or abri covers the spectrum from basic stone shelters to small
locked huts. In Norway, the DNT operates a network of unmanned self-service
hytter (hyttes) alongside its staffed options.

## Selbstversorgerhütten

Some huts operate as full Selbstversorgerhütten — self-catering huts — either
year-round or as a designated section of a larger staffed hut. These are more
common in Germany and Austria than in Switzerland or France. They provide
cooking facilities (gas rings, basic pots and utensils), sleeping bunks, and
sometimes a small pantry of communal supplies. Users bring all their own food.

The practical difference from a Winterraum is that Selbstversorgerhütten are
often available in the main season alongside the staffed option, and they
typically have running water and better sanitation. They serve ski tourers who
arrive outside the staffed window and hiking parties on a tight budget.

## Water sources off-season

Water is the critical planning variable for any self-catered or winter hut
stay. In season, the hut tap runs from a spring or snowmelt system managed by
the guardian. Off-season, the water may be turned off entirely to prevent
pipes freezing, the spring may be inaccessible under snow, or the cistern may
not have been filled since autumn.

For off-season hut use, assume no running water unless you have confirmed it
with the local alpine club section. Carry a water filter (Sawyer Squeeze,
BeFree, or equivalent) capable of treating snowmelt. Carry a gas stove and
fuel to melt snow — snow melting is slow and fuel-intensive, and you will use
substantially more gas than you expect.

## Gas culture and stove logistics

In staffed huts, gas cartridges are often sold at the hut shop — useful for
topping up if you have been cooking for several days. Standard 230g EN417
threaded cartridges (Primus, MSR, Campingaz C270) are widely available. The
Campingaz 470g push-in cartridges are available in France but not elsewhere,
and vice versa — check compatibility before buying in a different country.

For self-catered stays, carry one full 230g cartridge per person per two to
three days of cooking, adjusted for temperature. Cold temperatures — typical
above 2500 m — reduce cartridge output significantly, especially with
isobutane-heavy blends. A wind shield is essential; gas consumption doubles
without one.

## What each model suits

Staffed huts in season suit: guided parties, families with children, hikers
prioritizing comfort, and anyone on tight time constraints who cannot afford
a slow self-catered evening.

Winterraum and self-catered huts suit: experienced alpinists, ski tourers,
parties comfortable with self-sufficiency, and anyone extending the season
beyond the staffed window. They require more planning, more carried equipment,
and a higher tolerance for basic conditions.

Bivacchi suit: emergency shelter, lightweight alpinists on technical routes,
and parties whose itinerary requires a high overnight that no staffed option
covers. They are not an alternative to a planned hut booking on routes where
staffed huts exist.
