There is a quiet trick of geometry that makes Chimborazo extraordinary. The Earth is not a perfect sphere but bulges at the equator, and because this dormant volcano sits almost on the equator in central Ecuador, the point on its 6,263-metre summit stands farther from the centre of the planet than the top of Everest. You can climb the most distant place from Earth's core, and you do it from a pair of stone refuges high on the volcano's flank. The Carrel and Whymper huts, named for the great alpinists who first reached the summit, are the gateway to that strange superlative.

## The Mountain at the Edge of the World

Chimborazo rises in the Cordillera Occidental of the Ecuadorian Andes, dominating the provinces of Chimborazo and Bolivar. At 6,263 metres it is the highest mountain in Ecuador, a glaciated dormant volcano whose broad white dome is visible from Guayaquil on a clear day, far away on the Pacific coast. The first ascent came in 1880, when Edward Whymper, conqueror of the Matterhorn, reached the top with the Italian guides Jean-Antoine and Louis Carrel.

The summit's distinction as the farthest point from Earth's centre, roughly two kilometres farther out than Everest thanks to the equatorial bulge, has made Chimborazo a pilgrimage for climbers who relish a good superlative. But it is also a serious high-altitude objective, glaciated, crevassed and battered by wind, demanding mountaineering skill and proper acclimatisation. The refuges on its flank are where that effort begins.

## The Carrel and Whymper Refuges

Two huts sit high on Chimborazo's southwestern slope, within the Reserva de Produccion de Fauna Chimborazo. The lower, the Refugio Hermanos Carrel, stands at about 4,850 metres and is reached by road, a remarkable thing in itself: you can drive almost to the snow line. From the car park a steep half-hour walk climbs to the upper hut, the Refugio Whymper, at around 5,000 metres, the true base for summit attempts.

The Whymper refuge is a basic but welcome shelter: bunks, blankets, a kitchen serving hot soup and the famous canelazo, a warming cinnamon and aguardiente drink, and a common room where climbers doze fitfully before a midnight start. At 5,000 metres the air holds barely half the oxygen of sea level, and even walking to the toilet leaves you breathless. The huts are run in connection with the reserve and local operators, and most summit climbers spend a short, sleepless evening here before heading up.

## The Climb to the Roof of the Equator

The standard route to the summit, the Whymper or Normal route, leaves the refuge around midnight to climb while the snow is frozen and stable. It ascends past the Castillo rock buttress, up the El Corredor couloir, and onto the glaciated upper mountain to the Veintimilla summit at 6,228 metres and finally the slightly higher Whymper summit. The round trip takes most parties eight to twelve hard hours.

This is genuine mountaineering, not a walk. Climbers rope up, wear crampons, carry an ice axe and must judge rockfall in the lower couloir and crevasses higher up. Conditions on Chimborazo have grown more dangerous as the glacier thins and rockfall increases, and reputable operators watch the mountain closely. Success is far from guaranteed, but reaching the farthest point from Earth's centre, with the curve of the planet visible at dawn, is a reward few summits can match.

## Acclimatising on Ecuador's Volcanoes

Few climbers go straight to Chimborazo. Ecuador's Avenue of the Volcanoes, the string of great peaks flanking the central valley, offers a natural acclimatisation ladder. Many start on the Pichincha volcanoes above Quito, where the Refugio del Volcan Pichincha and the teleferico cable car give easy access to nearly 4,700 metres for a first taste of altitude.

From there, climbers move to bigger objectives. Cotopaxi, the near-perfect glaciated cone at 5,897 metres, has its own José Ribas refuge at 4,800 metres and is the classic warm-up before Chimborazo. The Refugio de Montaña Cotopaxi Cara Sur on the southern side offers another base. Other peaks such as Tungurahua, the Tungurahua Refugio, Cayambe and the Illinizas round out a high-altitude apprenticeship that prepares both body and nerve for the big volcano.

## The Vicunas and the Reserve

The drive up to the Carrel refuge passes through one of Ecuador's conservation success stories. The Reserva de Produccion de Fauna Chimborazo protects the high paramo and was the scene of a successful reintroduction of the vicuna, the wild relative of the llama and alpaca, prized for the finest wool in the world. Herds of these graceful animals now graze the tawny grasslands beneath the volcano, often seen from the access road.

The reserve also shelters the arenal, a strange high desert of volcanic sand on Chimborazo's flank, and is home to the Puruha indigenous communities and to the famous hieleros, the ice merchants who for generations cut blocks of glacier ice and carried them down by mule to market, a near-vanished trade now reduced to a single elderly practitioner. The mountain is layered with this human history as well as its geological superlative.

## Practicalities and Season

Ecuador's mountains can be climbed year-round, but the driest and most settled spells are usually December to January and June to September. Even so, equatorial weather is fickle, and Chimborazo makes its own clouds. Most climbers book through Quito or Riobamba operators, who provide guides, transport and the gear needed for a glaciated 6,000-metre peak.

Acclimatisation is the single most important factor. A typical programme spends a week or more climbing progressively higher volcanoes before Chimborazo, sleeping at refuges such as the José Ribas hut on Cotopaxi to let the body adapt. Riobamba, the handsome highland city at the volcano's foot, is the natural staging post, with its markets, colonial streets and grand views of the white dome that dominates everything around it.
