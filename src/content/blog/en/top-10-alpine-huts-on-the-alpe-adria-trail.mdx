The Alpe Adria Trail stretches 750 kilometres from the foot of the Grossglockner — Austria's highest peak at 3,798 metres — south through Carinthia, the Italian region of Friuli-Venezia Giulia, and Slovenia to Muggia on the Adriatic coast. Inaugurated in 2012 by a collaboration of regional tourism bodies across three countries, it is divided into 43 stages and descends from alpine terrain through karst plateaus, vineyards, and coastal villages. The trail is designed for walkers of moderate fitness and keeps technical difficulty low; almost no glacier crossing is required and the highest passes sit around 2,000 metres. Nevertheless, the northern sections near Grossglockner and the Nockberge hills in Carinthia pass genuine mountain terrain where hut nights are essential for multi-day traverses. The southern sections through the Friulian pre-Alps and Slovenian hills increasingly transition to farmhouse accommodation and village guesthouses. The following ten huts represent the trail's most characterful mountain shelter options.

## 1. Lucknerhaus, Grossglockner approach

Lucknerhaus at 1,918 metres in the Kals valley in East Tyrol is the gateway hut for the Grossglockner massif and the highest starting point for the standard normal route on Austria's highest peak. The building is a full mountain hotel — 60 beds, restaurant, and parking for approach by car — operated by a private family. For Alpe Adria walkers beginning at the Grossglockner, Lucknerhaus is both the first night and the dramatic alpine introduction to the trail. From here, the trail descends east through the Kals valley toward Carinthia, leaving the glacial peaks behind.

## 2. Hochschober Hütte, Schobergruppe

At 2,322 metres in the Schobergruppe range of East Tyrol, the Hochschober Hütte is among the more demanding overnight stops on the northern Alpe Adria section. Operated by ÖAV section Reichenberg (now Czech Liberec), it sleeps 60 and was built in 1927. The Schobergruppe is a less-visited crystalline range east of the Glockner massif, offering a genuine sense of high mountain solitude. Peaks above the hut exceed 3,200 metres. The Alpe Adria descends east from here toward the Möll valley and the Carinthian lowlands.

## 3. Valenzenerhütte, Nockberge

The Valenzenerhütte at around 1,700 metres in the Biosphärenpark Nockberge in central Carinthia is typical of the region's rounded, forested hills — quite different from the sharp peaks of the Glockner zone to the west. The Nockberge (literally "rounded hills") are a protected biosphere, and the hut provides overnight accommodation for walkers on the trail through this gentle but ecologically significant landscape. The building is privately operated with 30 beds and a kitchen serving Carinthian specialities such as Kasnudeln (cheese dumplings).

## 4. Moaralmhütte, Carinthian Alm country

The Moaralmhütte is an alpine dairy farm (Alm) at around 1,500 metres in the central Carinthian hill country, converted to serve hikers on the Alpe Adria. Accommodation is in farm-style rooms and a loft dormitory; meals feature farm produce — dairy, smoked meats, and apple juice from the lower valley orchards. These working-farm overnight stops are characteristic of the Alpe Adria's approach to mountain accommodation: rather than purpose-built alpine huts, the trail uses the existing agricultural infrastructure of alpine summer farming to house walkers. The surrounding meadows are carpeted in wildflowers from late May through July.

## 5. Planinarska kuća Lazica, Slovenia

Crossing the border from Austria into Slovenia, the trail enters the Karavanke range's southern foothills. Planinarska kuča Lazica is a Slovenian Alpine Club (PZS) hut at around 1,000 metres, operating in the transitional zone between the Carinthian lowlands and the Slovenian hills. It sleeps 30 and is a community-run facility staffed by PZS volunteers on weekends throughout the summer. The hut provides a cultural transition point: the menu shifts from Carinthian to Slovenian, the signage changes language, and the trail character becomes noticeably more forested and less open.

## 6. Planinski dom na Boču, Boč massif

Planinski dom na Boču sits at 978 metres on the elongated Boč massif above the Dravinja valley in northeastern Slovenia. The hut was established in 1934 and has served as a weekend destination for residents of Ptuj and Maribor for generations. It sleeps 40 and operates a full kitchen. The Boč massif is forested with oak and hornbeam at lower elevations and beech near the summit; views south over the Slovenian hills and north toward the Pohorje plateau are extensive. The Alpe Adria passes through here on its descent toward the Drava valley.

## 7. Koča pri slapu Mostnice, Bohinj valley

Koča pri slapu Mostnice — the hut near the Mostnica waterfall — sits at around 750 metres in the Mostnica gorge above the Bohinj valley, one of the most beautiful valley floors in Slovenia. The Mostnica stream has cut a narrow slot canyon through limestone, and the hut is positioned at the point where the gorge opens into the upper valley. It sleeps 30 and is open from May to October. Above the hut, trails ascend into the Triglav National Park zone toward the Voje valley meadows. This hut represents the Alpe Adria's traverse of the southern Triglav approaches.

## 8. Dom pod Reško planino, Lower Carniola plateau

Dom pod Reško planino at around 900 metres in the Lower Carniola region of central Slovenia is a small PZS hut on the edge of the karst plateau that dominates southern Slovenia. The hut provides accommodation for walkers crossing the Suha Krajina (Dry Carniola) landscape — a rolling karst plateau of rocky meadows, dolines, and sparse settlements. Accommodation is simple: 20 beds in bunk rooms. The plateau's dry limestone character makes it botanically interesting in spring, with orchids and rock cress flowering through May and June.

## 9. Štuhecov dom, second appearance

Štuhecov dom at 1,663 metres appears on both the Alpe Adria and the Via Alpina Blue Route, reflecting its strategic position at the main Golte massif saddle in the Kamnik-Savinja Alps. For Alpe Adria walkers approaching from the east, it is the highest overnight point on the trail's Slovenian section and offers dramatic views over the Savinja valley basin. The hut is discussed in the Via Alpina Blue Route guide above; here it functions as the last major mountain hut before the trail descends toward the Sava valley and the Slovenian lowlands.

## 10. Planinski dom pri Gospodični, near Trieste approach

Planinski dom pri Gospodični sits at around 400 metres in the Karst region (Kras) near the Slovenian-Italian border, in the final section of the Alpe Adria before it reaches the Adriatic coast at Muggia. The Karst plateau — the geological type locality that gives all karst terrain its name — is an open limestone plain with sparse vegetation, sinkholes, and old dry-stone walls. The dom (house) is a modest community hut used by both Slovenian and Italian day walkers on the plateau. From here the trail descends into Trieste's Karst hinterland and reaches the coast at Muggia, a small fishing town on the Adriatic just south of Trieste.
