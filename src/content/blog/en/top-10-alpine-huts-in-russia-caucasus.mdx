The Russian side of the Greater Caucasus encompasses the most dramatic concentration of high peaks in Europe — by the traditional definition that places the Eurasian boundary along the Caucasus watershed, Elbrus (5,642 m) is the highest point on the continent. The range extends roughly 1,200 kilometres from the Taman Peninsula near the Black Sea to the Caspian, with the highest peaks concentrated in the central massifs between Kabardino-Balkaria and North Ossetia. Soviet mountaineering culture developed an extensive infrastructure of alplageri (mountaineering base camps), high camps, and emergency shelters throughout the Caucasus from the 1930s onward. Many of these installations remain operational, and the distinctive orange or yellow fibreglass barrel shelters — бочки (bochki, "barrels") — became emblematic of Soviet-era high-altitude accommodation. Today the shelter network on the Russian Caucasus serves both technical climbers attempting the major summits and trail runners, ski mountaineers, and hikers on the lower approaches.

## 1. Бочки, Elbrus South

Бочки ("Barrels") at 3,750 m on the southern slopes of Elbrus are the most famous high-altitude shelter installation in the Russian Caucasus. The cluster of orange cylindrical fibreglass huts anchored to the slope just above the Garabashi cable car station serves as the standard acclimatisation camp before summit bids and emergency accommodation for parties caught by the frequent and violent weather changes on Elbrus. The barrels each sleep four to six people in tight but adequate conditions. They have been a fixture on the Elbrus south face since the Soviet era and have been upgraded repeatedly.

## 2. ЯкПриют, Elbrus South

ЯкПриют — "Yak Refuge" — at around 3,850 m is a newer, more comfortable hotel-style shelter near the Garabashi level on the south face of Elbrus. Built as a private enterprise in the 2000s to replace or supplement the barrel accommodation, it offers heated rooms, proper beds, and a restaurant service — significantly more comfortable than the barrels at effectively the same altitude. ЯкПриют is particularly popular with guided commercial expeditions that prioritise client comfort during the acclimatisation days before the summit push.

## 3. Сердце Эльбруса, Elbrus South

Сердце Эльбруса ("Heart of Elbrus") is a refuge at around 3,780 m, part of the modern ski and mountaineering infrastructure built on the southern slopes of Elbrus following the construction of the third cable car stage to the Mir station. The facility includes accommodation, a café, and guide booking services, and is used by both mountaineers beginning their Elbrus ascent and winter backcountry skiers exploring the extensive off-piste terrain above the lift system.

## 4. Diesel Hut, Elbrus South

Diesel Hut at approximately 3,900 m is positioned above the Barrels level on the south face, occupying the site of the original Priut 11 ("Refuge of Eleven") built in 1938 and destroyed by fire in 1998. The new structure was built using the surviving lower walls of the Soviet-era building and offers accommodation for around 48 climbers. At this altitude — above the barrels and below the Pastukhov Rocks — the Diesel Hut is the highest sleeping option on the standard south face route and the last comfortable shelter before the final summit push beginning at 4,700 m on the Pastukhov Rocks.

## 5. Верхний Корпус Альплагерь Цей, North Ossetia

Верхний корпус альплагерь Цей — the "Upper Building of the Tsei Mountaineering Camp" — is located in the Tsei gorge in North Ossetia's Alagir district. The Tsei alpinist camp was one of the most important Soviet mountaineering training bases, established in the 1930s and used through the twentieth century for courses in rock and ice climbing. The Tsei glacier descends to around 2,200 m — unusually low for the Caucasus — and the camp sits in a spectacular position below the surrounding peaks of the Digor group.

## 6. Приют Лакколит, Kabardino-Balkaria

Приют Лакколит is a shelter in the volcanic landscapes north of the Elbrus massif, positioned in the Lakkolitovaya area that gives the hut its name — a laccolith is a form of igneous intrusion, and the region's volcanic history is geologically visible in the rounded lava domes of the Baksan valley approaches. The shelter serves hikers and geologists visiting the unique geological formations and trail-runners on the northern Elbrus circuit.

## 7. Приют Монаха, Bezingi Area

Приют Монаха — "Monk's Refuge" — is located in the Bezingi area of Kabardino-Balkaria, the most technically demanding and least commercially developed sector of the Russian Caucasus. The Bezingi wall — a 12-kilometre section of unbroken peaks above 5,000 m including Shkhara (5,201 m), Jangi-tau, and Gestola — is the central objective here, and Monk's Refuge provides shelter for parties on the approaches to the wall and the col routes. The Bezingi base camp is one of the last operational Soviet-era alplageri in the Caucasus.

## 8. Спелеоприют Дорбункол, Speleological Shelter

Спелеоприют Дорбункол — "Speleological Shelter Dorbunkol" — is a specialist shelter associated with the cave systems of the western Caucasus, used by speleological expeditions exploring the deep cave systems that rival the Ukrainian Krubera-Voronya (the world's deepest known cave at over 2,200 m depth) in geological complexity. The shelter provides base camp facilities for multi-week underground expeditions and is not primarily a hiking hut in the conventional sense.

## 9. Пшадский Приют, Western Caucasus

Пшадский приют is a shelter in the western Caucasus near the Pshada river, in the forested lower ranges of Krasnodar Krai where the mountains drop toward the Black Sea coast near Gelendzhik. The western Caucasus is lower and more heavily forested than the central section, with trails passing through old-growth Colchic forest — a humid subtropical refugium with many relict Tertiary plant species. The shelter serves hikers on the Caucasus Nature Reserve trails.

## 10. Приют Мария, Kabardino-Balkaria

Приют Мария is a mountain shelter in Kabardino-Balkaria, named for the daughter of the hut founder. The refuge is in a valley tributary of the Baksan watershed and serves hikers exploring the lateral valleys branching south from the main Baksan road between Nalchik and the Elbrus tourism zone. The Baksan valley and its side gorges offer some of the most dramatic and accessible trekking in the Russian Caucasus away from the commercial Elbrus circuit.
