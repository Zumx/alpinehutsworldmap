Vorarlberg is the small, prosperous province in Austria's far west, wedged between Liechtenstein, Switzerland, Germany, and the Tyrol. Cut off from the rest of Austria by the Arlberg pass until the railway tunnel opened in 1884, it has always looked westward — toward the Rhine valley and Lake Constance — as much as toward Vienna, and its Alemannic dialect is closer to Swiss German than to Tyrolean. The mountains here are compact but serious: the limestone walls of the Rätikon along the Liechtenstein frontier, the granite peaks of the Silvretta on the Swiss border, and the gentler Bregenzerwald to the north. The huts below, run mostly by sections of the Austrian and German alpine clubs, give a route into one of the most varied small ranges in the eastern Alps.

## 1. Lindauer Hütte, Rätikon

The Lindauer Hütte at around 1,744 metres in the Gauertal above Tschagguns is one of the most popular huts in the Montafon valley and the classic base for the Rätikon limestone peaks. Above it rise the Drei Türme and the great wall of the Sulzfluh and Drusenfluh, some of the finest rock-climbing terrain in Vorarlberg. The hut is large, comfortable, and family-friendly, with an alpine garden of native flora beside it. It anchors the Rätikon Höhenweg, the high traverse that follows the Liechtenstein and Swiss frontier ridge through this dramatic limestone country.

## 2. Tilisuna Hütte, Rätikon

The Tilisuna Hütte at around 2,211 metres sits on a high karst plateau in the eastern Rätikon, close to the Swiss border and the Sulzfluh, a peak riddled with caves. It is a key stage on the Rätikon Höhenweg and a base for the straightforward ascent of the Sulzfluh at 2,817 metres, from whose summit you look down into both Switzerland and Liechtenstein. The plateau around the hut is classic limestone pavement, pitted and fissured, with hardy alpine flowers wedged into the cracks. The hut sleeps around 100 and is run by the German Alpine Club.

## 3. Heinrich-Hueter-Hütte, Rätikon

The Heinrich-Hueter-Hütte at around 1,766 metres in the Rells valley above Vandans serves the southern Rätikon and the Zimba, the elegant pyramid often called the Matterhorn of Vorarlberg. The Zimba at 2,643 metres is a serious scramble, and the hut is its natural base. The Rells valley is quieter than the busier Gauertal, and the approach through pasture and forest is gentle. The hut is a comfortable mid-sized facility with strong regional cooking and a terrace facing the Zimba's striking profile.

## 4. Bayreuther Hütte, Rofan

The Bayreuther Hütte at around 1,576 metres lies in the Rofan range, a compact limestone group with exceptional views over Lake Achensee. Although on the Tyrolean side of the regional boundary, it belongs to the same alpine-club network that serves Vorarlberg's walkers and is a popular destination for families and ridge walkers. The Rofan offers airy traverses on good rock, and the hut, run by the German Alpine Club's Bayreuth section, is a hospitable base with a long tradition of mountain hospitality.

## 5. Saarbrücker Hütte, Silvretta

The Saarbrücker Hütte at around 2,538 metres sits beneath the granite peaks of the western Silvretta near the Bielerhöhe and the Silvretta reservoir. This is a different world from the limestone Rätikon: dark granite, small glaciers, and the high alpine character that makes the Silvretta a favourite for ski touring in spring. The hut serves climbers heading for the Kleines and Grosses Seehorn and is a stage on the high Silvretta traverse. It sleeps around 70 and is reached by a steady climb from the Bielerhöhe road.

## 6. Wiesbadener Hütte, Silvretta

The Wiesbadener Hütte at around 2,443 metres is the principal base for the Piz Buin, at 3,312 metres the highest peak of the Silvretta and the most coveted summit in Vorarlberg. Set beside the Vermunt and Ochsental glaciers above the Silvretta reservoir, it is a large, busy hut that fills in summer with mountaineers and in spring with ski tourers. The glacier approach to the Piz Buin is the classic Silvretta route, and the Wiesbadener is its launch point. The hut is run by the German Alpine Club and sleeps over 200.

## 7. Dammkarhütte, Karwendel Approach

The Dammkarhütte at around 1,650 metres serves walkers in the limestone country toward the Karwendel, accessible from the Mittenwald area. While at the eastern margin of the broader region, it shares the character of Vorarlberg's limestone huts — solid, welcoming, and set beneath steep grey walls. The Dammkar itself is a famous high cirque, and the hut makes a useful base for both summer scrambling and the long off-piste descents that the area is known for in winter.

## 8. Rauhekopfhütte, Silvretta Glaciers

The Rauhekopfhütte at around 2,731 metres is a small, high shelter in the glaciated heart of the Silvretta, serving ski tourers and mountaineers on the long traverses between the Silvretta and Ötztal ranges. At this altitude the season is short and the terrain serious; the hut is a genuine high base rather than a comfortable lodge. It is the kind of place that anchors multi-day glacier journeys, and its position deep among the ice gives a sense of remoteness rare in the compact Vorarlberg ranges.

## 9. Eppzirler Alm, Northern Ranges

The Eppzirler Alm at around 1,460 metres is a working alpine pasture on the northern limestone ranges that fringe the region, serving walkers with simple food and overnight space in a classic alm setting. Huts like this — half dairy, half refuge — are the everyday face of alpine walking here, where herds still summer on the high pastures and the cheese is made on site. The surrounding terrain offers gentle family walks beneath steep limestone, a softer counterpart to the glaciated Silvretta.

## 10. Brunnsteinhütte, Limestone Frontier

The Brunnsteinhütte at around 1,560 metres serves the limestone frontier country in the north of the broader region, a small, friendly hut on a forested shoulder with views toward the higher ranges. It is the kind of unassuming hut that anchors local walking culture: a steady climb from the valley, a sunny terrace, hearty Alemannic cooking, and trails that connect into the wider network of frontier paths. It makes a quiet, accessible base for those exploring the gentler northern edge of Vorarlberg's mountains.
