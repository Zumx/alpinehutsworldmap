The morning you leave a glacier hut — the Konkordiahütte on the Aletsch, the Cabane des Vignettes on the Haute Route, the Rifugio Gnifetti below Monte Rosa — you rope up in the half-light and step onto ice that may be hiding holes deep enough to swallow a bus. Most crevasses on a well-travelled route are visible or bridged by firm snow, but every season people fall through hidden bridges, and the difference between a frightening story and a fatality is whether the party can pull the fallen climber back out. Crevasse rescue is not glamorous, it is rope drudgery, but it is the single most important skill a glacier-hut traveller can own, and it must be practised until it is automatic.

## Why You Drill It Before You Need It

A real crevasse fall is chaos. One moment your partner is walking; the next there is a jerk, a shout, and a taut rope cutting into the snow lip with a person hanging unseen below. You are alone on the surface, possibly arrested face-down in the snow, holding the full weight of your friend. There is no time to read instructions, no warden to call, and cold and shock are working against the victim every minute. Everything you do in the next ten minutes you must already know with your hands, not your head. That is why the drills below belong in a meadow or a climbing gym long before they belong on a glacier.

## Rope, Knots and Spacing

Crevasse rescue starts before the fall, with how you travel. On a glacier a party of two or three moves roped together with deliberate spacing — commonly around ten to fifteen metres between climbers on a small team, so that one person breaking through does not pull the others in. The slack is kept off the snow but not pulled tight. Many parties tie a couple of overhand knots in the rope between climbers; if someone falls, those knots jam in the snow at the lip and help arrest the fall, taking load off the belayer.

Each climber carries the rescue kit on their harness and knows where every piece is by feel: two prusik loops or mechanical rope clamps, two or three locking carabiners, a couple of slings, a snow anchor such as a buried ice axe or a snow picket, and ice screws if the surface is bare ice. None of it works if it is buried in the bottom of your pack.

## Step One: Arrest and Build an Anchor

The instant the rope comes tight, the surviving climbers drop into self-arrest, digging in to stop the slide and hold the load. This is the hardest physical moment, and it is why you keep the rope reasonably short and the team alert. Once the fall is held, the priority is to transfer that load from your body to a fixed anchor so you can move.

On snow the standard anchor is a buried ice axe or a snow picket set deep across the line of pull, backed up if time allows. You attach a prusik from the loaded rope to the anchor, ease the weight onto it, and only then come out of arrest. Now the victim hangs from the anchor, not from you, and you can finally walk to the lip and assess. Building a solid anchor under load, fast, is the skill people most often neglect and most need.

## Step Two: The Prusik and Talking to the Victim

A prusik is a thin loop of cord wrapped around the main rope in a friction hitch that grips when loaded and slides when relaxed. It is the humble heart of crevasse rescue: it lets you ascend a rope, capture progress so the load never slips back, and rig hauling systems. Every glacier traveller should be able to tie a prusik with gloves on, in the dark, without thinking.

Before hauling, pad the lip. The rope cuts a deep slot into the soft snow edge and will not move until you slide a pack, an axe or a ski under it to bridge the gap. Then talk to the victim. Often a conscious, uninjured climber can climb out themselves on two prusiks while you hold the rope, which is faster and safer than any haul. Only if they cannot — injured, exhausted, jammed under an overhang — do you build a hauling system.

## Step Three: The Z-Pulley Explained

The Z-pulley, or 3-to-1 system, is the workhorse haul that lets one or two people lift a body weight out of a hole. It takes its name from the Z shape the rope makes. You fix a prusik to the loaded rope down at the lip, run the rope back up through a carabiner or pulley at your anchor, and pull. A second prusik at the anchor acts as a ratchet, gripping the rope each time you reset so your hard-won progress is never lost.

The mechanical advantage means every metre you pull raises the victim by roughly a third of a metre, trading distance for force. It is slow and strenuous and you will reset the system many times, sliding the lower prusik back down the rope after each pull. For a heavier victim or a solo rescuer you can add a further loop to make a 5-to-1 or 6-to-1. Drilling the Z-pulley until you can build it from memory, with cold hands, is the core competence this whole article is about.

## Practising and Carrying the Right Kit

You cannot learn this from reading alone. Spend a day with a guide or an alpine club course — the SAC, DAV, ÖAV and CAI all run them — rigging real systems and hanging a willing partner in a sling to feel the loads. Then practise at home off a tree branch or a stairwell rail until the sequence is muscle memory: arrest, anchor, transfer, assess, pad the lip, haul or assist. Refresh it every season before your first glacier outing, because the skill decays fast.

Carry the kit every time you rope up, even on a casual hut-to-hut crossing of a flat-looking glacier, because the flat-looking glaciers are exactly where people grow careless. The Tour du Mont Blanc walker who never touches ice can skip all this; the moment your hut trip crosses a glacier, crevasse rescue stops being optional.
