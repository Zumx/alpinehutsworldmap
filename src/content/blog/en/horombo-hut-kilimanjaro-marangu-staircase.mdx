Spread across a high heather-covered shoulder at 3,720 metres on the eastern flank of Kilimanjaro sits a cluster of green A-frame huts that functions, for a few crowded weeks of each climbing season, as a small mountain village. Horombo Hut is the central acclimatisation camp on the Marangu route, the only one of Kilimanjaro's trails where climbers sleep in permanent huts rather than tents. From its perch above the clouds, trekkers look down on the rainforest they climbed through and up toward the snow-streaked summit cone of Kibo, still some 2,000 metres higher, where the rooftop of Africa waits at 5,895 metres.

## The Mountain and Its Only Hut Route

Kilimanjaro rises in northern Tanzania near the town of Moshi, a colossal freestanding volcano that is the highest mountain in Africa and the highest freestanding mountain in the world. It is protected within Kilimanjaro National Park, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and is climbed by tens of thousands of people each year along several established routes. Most of these, including the scenic Machame, Lemosho and Rongai routes, are camping routes where porters carry and pitch tents each night.

The Marangu route is the exception, and it is often called the Coca-Cola route, partly for its relative gentleness and partly because the huts historically sold soft drinks. It is the oldest and most established trail, ascending the southeastern side of the mountain on a well-graded path. Climbers sleep in dormitory huts at three main stages: Mandara Hut at 2,720 metres in the forest, Horombo Hut at 3,720 metres on the moorland, and Kibo Hut at 4,720 metres in the high alpine desert, from which the summit push begins.

## Life at Horombo

Horombo is the busiest and most atmospheric of the three. Because it serves both ascending and descending climbers, and because acclimatisation itineraries often include an extra rest day here, the camp can hold well over a hundred trekkers and their guides and porters at peak times. The A-frame huts sleep several people each in simple bunks, and a larger dining hut serves hot meals prepared by the porter teams. There is no luxury, but after a long day on the trail the warmth of a shared meal and a bunk out of the wind feels like genuine comfort.

The setting is the giant heather and moorland zone, where the vegetation thins from forest into open slopes dotted with the strange, ancient forms of giant groundsels and lobelias found only on East Africa's high mountains. White-necked ravens patrol the camp looking for scraps, and on clear evenings the summit of Kibo glows pink at sunset above the camp. Mawenzi, Kilimanjaro's jagged secondary peak, rises dramatically to the east.

## The Vital Acclimatisation Day

The single most important thing a climber can do at Horombo is spend an extra night. The Marangu route can be done in five days, but the success rate on that compressed schedule is poor because it gives the body too little time to adjust to altitude. The standard recommendation is a six-day itinerary that includes an acclimatisation day at Horombo, during which climbers take a day walk up toward the Mawenzi saddle or the Zebra Rocks at around 4,000 metres before returning to sleep at 3,720 metres.

This climb-high, sleep-low principle is the key to summiting Kilimanjaro safely. Altitude sickness is the main reason climbers fail or, in serious cases, must be evacuated. The mountain is not technically difficult, requiring no ropes or special skills on the standard routes, but the altitude is real and unforgiving. The extra day at Horombo can be the difference between standing on Uhuru Peak and turning back exhausted below the crater rim.

## The Summit Push from Kibo

From Horombo, the route climbs across the saddle between Kibo and Mawenzi to the bleak Kibo Hut at 4,720 metres, a stone building in a high desert of volcanic rubble. Here climbers rest briefly before the most demanding part of the entire trek. The summit attempt begins around midnight, climbing steep scree by headlamp toward Gillman's Point on the crater rim at 5,685 metres, reached around dawn. From there the trail traverses the crater rim to Uhuru Peak, the true summit at 5,895 metres.

The summit night is brutally cold and exhausting, with temperatures well below freezing and oxygen at roughly half the sea-level concentration. After reaching the top, climbers descend all the way back down to Horombo the same day, a punishing loss of altitude that nonetheless aids recovery. The contrast between the frozen summit at dawn and the relative warmth of the Horombo huts that evening is one of the defining memories of the climb.

## Practicalities, Porters and the Park

Climbing Kilimanjaro is tightly regulated. Every climber must go with a licensed operator and a registered guide, and the mountain's economy supports a large workforce of guides, cooks and porters. Ethical treatment of porters, including fair wages, proper gear and limited loads, is an important issue, and reputable operators adhere to established porter welfare standards. Park fees are substantial and are included in operator packages.

The best climbing seasons are the drier months of January to early March and June to October, avoiding the long rains. Even in season, the mountain creates its own weather, and climbers should be prepared for everything from tropical heat in the forest to driving snow near the summit. Warm layers, a serious down jacket, sturdy boots, sun protection and a headlamp are all essential.

## Where Horombo Fits in the Wider Picture

The hut-based experience of the Marangu route is unusual in African mountaineering, where camping is the norm. It draws a comparison with the staffed refuge traditions of the Alps, though Horombo is closer in spirit to a high-volume trekking lodge than to a small alpine club hut. The wider Kilimanjaro region around Moshi and Arusha is dotted with lodges and guesthouses that serve climbers before and after their ascents, and the great safari parks of northern Tanzania, including the Serengeti and Ngorongoro, lie within easy reach for those combining a climb with a safari.

For most trekkers, though, the memory that endures is Horombo itself: the green A-frames above the clouds, the giant lobelias, and the first clear sight of the summit they came to climb.
