The Haute Route from Chamonix to Zermatt is one of the great multi-day routes
in the Alps: roughly 180 km, 11,000 m of elevation gain, crossing the Pennine
Alps in 12–15 days via a series of high passes above 2500 m and glacier terrain
that demands rope and crampons on several sections. The Walker's Haute Route
(the summer walking version) stays largely off-glacier by using high ridges
and passes, while the ski mountaineering Haute Route — typically done in
April — crosses major glacier systems throughout. The huts below form the
core overnight chain for the summer route. All are plotted on the [map](/map).

## 1. Cabane du Mont Fort, 2457 m — Val de Bagnes, Valais

The first high SAC hut after Verbier on the Haute Route approach from
Champex-Lac or Verbier itself, the Mont Fort sits at 2457 m above the Lac du
Mauvoisin with views toward the Grand Combin (4314 m). Reached from Verbier
via Le Châble in about 3.5 hours. Capacity around 120. The hut is the logical
first alpine-level overnight for parties starting the Haute Route from Verbier,
allowing acclimatization before the higher nights to come.

## 2. Cabane de Prafleuri, 2624 m — La Barma, Valais

A distinctive hut — isolated on the Prafleuri plateau in a high, barren
landscape between the Lac des Dix and Verbier — that gives some Haute Route
parties an unexpected night of genuine wilderness. The route to Prafleuri
from Verbier via the Col de Prafleuri (2987 m) is a challenging stage of 7–8
hours. Capacity around 70, full SAC half-board in season.

## 3. Cabane des Dix, 2928 m — Lac des Dix, Hérens

Named for the Dixence valley and above the Grande Dixence dam — one of the
engineering wonders of Switzerland — the Cabane des Dix sits at 2928 m below
the Aiguilles Rouges d'Arolla. The approach from the dam road base takes 3–4
hours. Routes from here include the Mont Blanc de Cheilon (3870 m) and the
crossing to Arolla via the Pas de Chèvres — a ladder-equipped rocky pass
that is one of the most interesting sections of the summer Haute Route.
Capacity around 90.

## 4. Cabane des Vignettes, 3160 m — Arolla, Valais

One of the most famous overnight positions in the Alps — perched on a rock
outcrop at 3160 m surrounded by the Otemma and Pigne glaciers — the Vignettes
is the high point of the Haute Route in terms of elevation and visual drama.
Reached from Arolla in about 3.5 hours via the Glacier d'Arolla (crampons
required in most conditions). Capacity around 100. The sunrise view from the
terrace looking toward the Grand Combin and the Mont Blanc massif is one of
the definitive alpine experiences.

## 5. Cabane de Bertol, 3311 m — Bertol Col, Valais

The second consecutive high night above 3000 m on the Haute Route, the Bertol
requires ascending fixed chains and a short ladder on the final approach to
the col. At 3311 m, the views encompass the Zinalrothorn, Weisshorn, and the
Zermatt peaks ahead. The glacier crossing from the Vignettes to the Bertol
traverses the Haut Glacier d'Arolla; a rope, crampons, and glacier travel
competence are standard requirements. Capacity around 80.

## 6. Bertol to Schönbiel Transition — Tête Blanche Crossing

The crossing from the Bertol to the Schönbiel via the Tête Blanche (3724 m)
is the most technically demanding section of the summer Haute Route. The route
traverses the Stockji Glacier and the high snowfield below the Tête Blanche,
descending to the Zmutt valley above Zermatt. The departure from Bertol at
05:00–05:30 is standard, to complete the crossing before afternoon snow
softening. This is the section most likely to be affected by poor weather
causing route deviation — the alternative descent via the Col d'Hérens avoids
the highest terrain.

## 7. Schönbielhütte, 2694 m — Zmutt Valley, Wallis

Below the southwest face of the Matterhorn and above the Zmuttgletscher, the
Schönbielhütte is the reward for crossing the Tête Blanche: the most directly
dramatic view of the Matterhorn available from any staffed hut. At 2694 m,
reached from Zermatt in 3.5–4 hours via the Zmutt valley. SAC-managed, capacity
around 100. Parties arriving here on the Haute Route have typically been in
motion for 10 or more days; the kitchen treats this well. The Matterhorn's
southwest face looms overhead.

## 8. Europahütte, 2265 m — Europa Way, Wallis

Not on the classic Haute Route but on the Europa Way — the high-level
alternative route above Zermatt approaching the Täsch valley — the Europahütte
sits at 2265 m on a balcony above the Matter Valley with an unobstructed view
of the Matterhorn's north and east faces. Reached from Zermatt via the
Mettelhorn path or from Randa by cable car. An outstanding penultimate night
if the Haute Route is extended to Saas Fee rather than finishing in Zermatt.

## 9. Topali Hut (Täschlipta), 2214 m — Täsch, Wallis

Above Täsch in the Matter Valley, the Topali serves the continuation of the
Haute Route toward the Europaweg and the approach to Saas Fee via the passes
above the Täsch valley. The hut is quieter than the main Zermatt-side SAC huts
and serves parties extending the route east rather than finishing in Zermatt.

## 10. Täschhütte, 2701 m — Täschtal, Wallis

The highest SAC hut above Täsch, serving approaches to the Alphubel (4206 m)
and the crossing toward the Saas Fee glacier basin. The Täschhütte sits on the
eastern edge of the Haute Route's final glacier system — the Mischabeljoch
crossing that delivers the route into the Saas Fee valley, completing the
Zermatt-to-Saas extension.

## Glacier travel context

The summer Haute Route requires competence in glacier travel. Multiple sections
involve crevassed glaciers, crossing at dawn on frozen snow, and navigation
in poor visibility. A guide is strongly recommended for parties without
previous glacier experience, particularly for the Vignettes–Bertol–Tête
Blanche section. The SAC Guide Service in Verbier and Arolla can provide
this. Weather monitoring throughout is critical; the MeteoSwiss mountain
forecast (app and website) is the standard tool.

## The Chamonix to Zermatt context

The Haute Route connects the two most famous alpine towns in the French and
Swiss Alps: Chamonix, at the base of the Mont Blanc massif, and Zermatt,
at the base of the Matterhorn. Both are traffic-free mountain resorts with
a history of alpinism stretching back to the mid-19th century. Arriving in
Zermatt after 7 days on the glacier route, with the Matterhorn visible at
the end of the Matter valley, is one of the great finishes in alpine travel.

The intermediate towns and villages — Arolla, Les Haudères, Zinal, Gruben —
are the quieter side of Pennine Alps culture: small working valleys with
fewer tourists than the endpoint destinations. The Haute Route's character
comes partly from traversing through these valleys rather than simply
following the ridge.

## Saas Fee extension

Parties who want to extend the standard Zermatt finish can continue the
Haute Route to Saas Fee via the Europaweg or the Mischabeljoch crossing.
Saas Fee is smaller and quieter than Zermatt and provides a less-touristically
intense endpoint for the route. The extension adds 2–3 days; the terrain
east of Zermatt is somewhat less frequented than the main Haute Route.
