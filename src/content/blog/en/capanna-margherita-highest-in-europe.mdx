There is a refuge at 4554 metres above sea level. It stands on the summit of
Punta Gnifetti, one of the peaks of the Monte Rosa massif, in a position that
is not approached on a well-trodden valley path but climbed — fully roped, on
crampons, through glacier terrain and a final steep snow face — from the
glacier huts below. The Rifugio Capanna Regina Margherita is not a destination
for hut walkers. It is a mountaineering objective in its own right, and for
most visitors, the hardest night's sleep they will ever attempt.

## History and name

The hut was inaugurated in 1893, named after Queen Margherita of Italy
(Margherita di Savoia), who made the ascent to Punta Gnifetti the same year
and spent the night there — making her one of the first women to sleep above
4000 m in the Alps. The original stone building has been rebuilt and extended
several times; the current structure combines the historic shell with modern
insulated additions that maintain habitability in extreme conditions.

Operation by the Club Alpino Italiano (CAI) has been continuous since 1893,
making the Rifugio Capanna Regina Margherita one of the oldest continuously
operated mountain huts in the Alps. The CAI Valle d'Aosta section manages it
today.

## Getting there

The standard ascent begins at Punta Indren (3260 m), reached by cable car from
Alagna Valsesia on the Italian side. From there, the route traverses the
Indren Glacier, climbs to the Rifugio Quintino Sella al Felik (3585 m) or
the Rifugio Capanna Giovanni Gnifetti (3647 m) — both of which serve as
intermediate staging huts — and continues via the Lys Glacier and Colle del
Lys to the final steep approach to Punta Gnifetti.

The total climbing time from Punta Indren is 7–9 hours for a fit party;
most people break the ascent over two days with an overnight at the Rifugio
Gnifetti or the Rifugio Quintino Sella. This staged approach also provides
partial acclimatisation, though the altitude gain from Alagna to the Margherita
is still rapid enough to produce AMS symptoms in many visitors regardless.

From the Swiss side via Zermatt or Saas Fee, the approach is longer and
involves crossing the Monte Rosa massif from the west, typically via the
Neue Monte Rosa Hütte (2883 m) and the Colle del Lys. SAC members using this
approach can split the ascent across two huts before arriving at the summit.

## The overnight at 4554 m

Sleeping at 4554 m is physiologically challenging even for acclimatised
alpinists. The atmospheric oxygen at this altitude is approximately 58 percent
of sea-level concentration. Most guests experience disrupted sleep, frequent
waking, headache, and reduced appetite. Cheyne-Stokes respiration — the
cyclical deep-and-shallow breathing pattern common above 4000 m — makes
uninterrupted sleep rare.

The hut accommodates approximately 70 guests in dormitory sleeping. The
building is heated and meals are provided, but the kitchen operates under the
constraints of extreme altitude and helicopter-only supply. Dinner is typically
a simple hot meal; the emphasis is on caloric density rather than culinary
refinement.

The guardians of the Rifugio Capanna Regina Margherita are accustomed to
guests in various states of altitude-related distress. The hut maintains an
oxygen supply and a first-aid kit appropriate for high-altitude medicine.
HACE and HAPE both develop at this altitude, and the descent to lower
elevation takes several hours even under best conditions.

## The summit panorama

From Punta Gnifetti (4554 m), the panorama takes in the highest peaks of the
Alps: Dufourspitze (4634 m) immediately to the west, Dom (4545 m), Weisshorn
(4506 m), Matterhorn (4478 m), Monte Cervino, Grand Combin, Mont Blanc
(4808 m) on the far western horizon. In perfect conditions, the Bernese Oberland
and Bernina ranges are also visible.

The view from the Capanna Margherita is the broadest high-altitude panorama
available from a fixed overnight structure anywhere in Europe.

## Scientific use

The hut has housed high-altitude scientific research since its establishment.
The Margherita Scientific Research Station, co-managed by CAI and the National
Research Council of Italy (CNR), operates instruments for meteorology,
atmospheric chemistry, and astrophysics. Glaciologists have used the site to
study Monte Rosa glacier mass balance for decades.

## Practical preparation

The physiological challenge of sleeping at 4554 m requires serious preparation.
Parties attempting the Margherita without prior high-altitude exposure should
plan at least one or two nights at 3000–3500 m (the Rifugio Gnifetti or the
Monte Rosa Hut are appropriate acclimatisation stops) before the summit push.
The standard recommended acclimatisation schedule: arrive at Alagna, spend
night 1 at the valley (1191 m), night 2 at the Gnifetti (3647 m) or Quintino
Sella (3585 m), night 3 at the Margherita (4554 m). This three-night ascent
is faster than ideal but approximately what most guided parties achieve.

Diamox (acetazolamide) at 125–250 mg twice daily is commonly used by parties
ascending to this elevation; it accelerates acclimatisation but does not
replace the physiological process. Guests who develop HACE or HAPE symptoms
must descend immediately regardless of weather; the guardians maintain oxygen
and have emergency protocols, but the descent route to the Gnifetti takes
several hours even in good conditions.

Weather at 4554 m is extreme by any standard. Wind speeds exceeding 100 km/h
have been recorded at the Margherita; temperatures below -30°C occur in winter
and can approach -20°C in August storms. The summer window (July–August) is
the only practical time for a non-expedition visit; even within this window,
the average summit conditions are cold, windy, and frequently cloudy.

## The view

When conditions are clear — which happens on a meaningful number of summer days
— the view from Punta Gnifetti is the broadest in the Alps accessible from a
fixed overnight structure. The list of visible summits on a perfect day is
extraordinary: Monte Rosa itself in all its constituent peaks, Lyskamm,
Matterhorn (4478 m), Dent Blanche (4357 m), Grand Combin (4314 m), Weisshorn
(4506 m), Dom (4545 m), Bernese Oberland peaks, Mont Blanc (4808 m) far to
the west. In exceptional conditions, the Apennines and the Mediterranean
have been reported visible from the south.

## The research station

The Capanna Margherita houses the highest permanent meteorological and
atmospheric research station in Europe. The observatory, operated by the
Fondazione Sella and scientific institutions including the Italian National
Research Council (CNR), monitors long-term atmospheric conditions,
UV radiation, cosmic ray flux, and pollution tracers at 4554 m. Data
collected since 1893 provides one of the longest continuous high-altitude
atmospheric records in the world.

The research has documented the decline of the Grenzgletscher and
Gornergletscher below as a climate indicator, and monitors ozone column
variations above the Alps. Scientists rotating through the station typically
stay for 2–4 week periods; the guardians and researchers share the building
throughout the summer season.

For visitors, the research context adds a dimension to the overnight that
goes beyond the standard hut experience: this building exists not only for
mountain tourism but for science, and has done so continuously for over a
century. The instruments and monitoring equipment are visible in the building
exterior and available for viewing when the scientists are present.

## Booking and access

The Capanna Margherita is managed by CAI Varallo (the Club Alpino Italiano
section that has operated the hut since its construction). Reservations are
essential in July and August; capacity is 70 people in a combination of
dormitories. The guardian's schedule runs from approximately late June to
early September. Off-season access in the self-service winter room is possible
for experienced mountaineers with appropriate acclimatisation.
