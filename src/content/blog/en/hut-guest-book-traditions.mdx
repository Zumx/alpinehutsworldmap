Every staffed alpine hut in the European Alps contains, somewhere near the entrance or on the dining-room table, a large bound volume filled with handwriting. The Hüttenbuch — guest book in German, livre d'or in French, libro di vetta in Italian — records the names, routes, dates, and frequently the opinions of every party that has passed through. The oldest surviving examples date from the 1870s; the most recent entries were written yesterday. Between those dates lies a continuous social history of European mountaineering, written in pencil and ink by everyone from pioneering 19th-century alpinists to school groups on their first overnight trip.

## Origins: The Summit Register and the Hut Chronicle

The guest book tradition in alpine huts is an extension of an older practice: the summit register. A tin box or glass bottle sealed and wedged into a cairn on a summit ridge, containing a paper roll of names and dates, was deposited on significant Alpine peaks from the 1780s onward. Mont Blanc received its first summit register in 1817; the Grossglockner register dates from the 1820s. As huts replaced impromptu camp sites and provisional shelters from the 1860s onward, the register moved from summit to hut — a more convenient location that captured parties both before and after their climbs.

The first formal hut guest books in the Austrian and German Alps date from the period 1870-1885, coinciding with the founding of the major alpine clubs and the construction of the first purpose-built club huts. The ÖAV and DAV directed hut wardens to maintain a record of occupancy from the outset; the practical motive was insurance and liability, the cultural product was something far richer.

## What Famous Climbers Wrote

The guest books at key route huts inevitably attracted notable visitors, and many left substantive records. The Hörnli Hütte at 3,260 m, the standard base for the Matterhorn Nordost ridge, has guest books extending back to 1880 and includes entries by Edward Whymper — who was on the peak when four of his party died on the descent of the first ascent in 1865 and returned multiple times in later life — as well as by Guido Rey, whose 1904 book on the Matterhorn references the hut register extensively.

The Cabane des Cosmiques (3,613 m) at Chamonix, the primary base for Aiguilles du Midi traverses and the Frendo Spur, contains entries from many of the key figures of French alpinism in the postwar period. Gaston Rébuffat, who climbed all six classic north faces of the Alps and wrote extensively about them, left entries in several Chamonix hut books between 1945 and 1985 that have been cited in biographies.

The guest book of the Berliner Hütte in the Zillertal Alps, built in its current form in 1898, is a social document of exceptional depth. The hut served as a gathering point for the Austro-German alpine community through two world wars and the Depression; entries from the early 1930s reflect the political tensions of the period alongside the routes climbed. Scholars of alpine history have used it as a primary source for understanding who was climbing in the Zillertal in specific years.

## What Most Entries Actually Say

Famous climbers are rare; what fills guest books is the testimony of ordinary trips. A typical page from a July week at a mid-altitude Austrian hut contains: a family from Salzburg noting their children's first hut overnight and drawing a picture of the peak above; a trail runner noting a time for the ridge circuit; a Dutch couple recording fog and rain and their decision to stay an extra day; a solo walker from Slovenia noting the summit views were worth the effort; and a guide from Innsbruck logging his fifth traverse of the season.

The cumulative effect of a book with 50 years of such entries is powerful. Reading back through them reveals weather patterns — how many entries in a particular week mention storm or sunshine tells you the statistical weather reality of that week better than any forecast archive. It reveals route popularity shifts: sections that attracted dozens of parties per day in the 1990s and are now mentioned rarely. And it reveals the changing demographics of alpine visitors — the proportion of entries in English, Dutch, and east European languages has increased dramatically since 1990 in most Austrian hut books, while entries from France and Germany have declined proportionally.

## The Sketch Tradition

Many guest books contain drawings: hasty pencil sketches of the view from the hut, caricatures of the hut keeper, maps of routes taken, weather diagrams. Some huts have encouraged this tradition actively, providing coloured pencils alongside the book. The Glorer Hütte (2,642 m) in the Schober group of the Carnic Alps is known among visiting climbers for its maintained collection of illustrated guest books stretching back to 1920, with a particularly rich period of illustrated entries from the 1930s and 1940s.

The sketch tradition connects mountain guest books to an older travel journal culture. 18th and 19th-century Alpine travellers — Romantic painters, natural historians, and gentleman tourists — recorded their mountain experiences in illustrated journals that are now held in national libraries. The hut guest book is the democratic version of the same impulse: the desire to leave a mark that says I was here, it looked like this, I felt this.

## Digital Threats and Physical Preservation

Guest books are vulnerable. Fire, water, age, and the attrition of heavy use damage pages and spines. Several historically significant hut books have been lost in hut fires — the 1930 fire that destroyed the original Teplitzer Hütte in the Dolomites took an irreplaceable archive with it. The ÖAV and SAC have begun digitisation programmes for their most historically significant hut records, with the SAC Archive in Bern holding scanned versions of books from several key route huts.

The digital alternative — a tablet mounted at the hut entrance, or an online guest book system — exists and is used at a small number of modern huts as a supplementary record. It has not replaced the paper book at any staffed hut this author is aware of. The cultural resistance to digitising the guest book is strong and broadly shared: the physical act of opening a book and reading what was written by hand 40 years ago on a wet July evening is an experience that a database query cannot replicate.

## Contributing and Reading

When you arrive at a hut and are invited to sign the guest book, take three minutes rather than 30 seconds. Note the weather, the route, the condition of the path, something specific about the day. Future visitors will read it; your entry becomes part of the record. At old huts with multiple books, ask the warden if the historical books are accessible. Many keepers keep them behind the bar and are delighted when someone asks to look through the older volumes.
