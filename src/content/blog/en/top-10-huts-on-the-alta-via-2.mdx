The Alta Via 2 is the more demanding companion route to the Alta Via 1 —
a 150-kilometre traverse from Bressanone (Brixen) in the north to Feltre in
the south that crosses the most technically demanding terrain in the Dolomite
high routes. Where the Alta Via 1 is a long-distance trail accessible to fit
hikers, the Alta Via 2 includes via ferrata sections and exposed terrain
that require experience, a via ferrata harness, and comfort on steep rocky
ground. The rifugio sequence is one of the finest in the Dolomites.

## 1. Rifugio Schlüterhütte / Rifugio Genova, 2306 m — Puez-Odle, Dolomites

The Schlüterhütte/Rifugio Genova at 2306 m in the Puez-Odle Nature Park serves
the northern section of the Alta Via 2 approach from the Villnöss/Val di Funes
valley. This rifugio — combining German and Italian names reflecting the South
Tyrolean bilingual character — has capacity for approximately 80 guests and
provides the base for the extraordinary Odle massif (Geisler Spitzen) approach
and the walk toward the Puez plateau.

## 2. Utia de Puez (Rifugio Puez), 2475 m — Puez Plateau, Ladin Dolomites

On the vast Puez plateau at 2475 m — one of the most extraordinary geological
features in the Dolomites, a high limestone plateau above the Sella group —
the Rifugio Puez provides an isolated overnight in a landscape that feels
genuinely remote. Capacity around 60; the Ladin cultural character of this
area (Ladin is the third official language of South Tyrol) distinguishes it
from the more Germanised or Italianised Dolomite areas.

## 3. Rifugio Boè, 2871 m — Piz Boè, Sella Group

The Rifugio Plan Boè sits at the foot of Piz Boè (3152 m) — the highest point
of the Sella massif and accessible on a short walk from the hut. At 2871 m
above the Arabba ski resort area, the hut provides the high point of the
Alta Via 2's central section. Via ferrata ascent possible; the Sella plateau
above is wide, flat, and otherworldly.

## 4. Rifugio Pisciadù, 2585 m — Gardena Valley, Dolomites

Below the dramatic west face of the Pisciadù canyon, the Rifugio Pisciadù
at 2585 m sits beside the Lago Pisciadù — a mountain lake reflecting the
canyon walls above. Reached via the Pisciadù via ferrata from the Gardena
valley. Capacity around 60. One of the most photogenic rifugio positions
in the central Dolomites.

## 5. Rifugio Treviso, 1631 m — Pale di San Martino

In the northern approaches to the Pale di San Martino — the great southern
Dolomite massif — the Rifugio Treviso at 1631 m provides access to the pale
plateau above. Capacity around 50. The Alta Via 2's passage through the Pale
involves the Rosetta and Pedrotti rifugi on the plateau above.

## 6. Rifugio Pradidali, 2278 m — Pale di San Martino, Trentino

High in the Pale di San Martino at 2278 m, the Rifugio Pradidali serves the
most technically demanding section of the Alta Via 2 — the traverse of the
Pale plateau involves exposed terrain, via ferrata sections, and significant
route-finding. The plateau is a vast lunar landscape of pale limestone — the
most distinctive geological environment on the Alta Via 2.

## 7. Rifugio Pedrotti alla Rosetta, 2581 m — Rosetta, Pale di San Martino

On the Rosetta plateau reached by gondola from San Martino di Castrozza, the
Rifugio Pedrotti is the highest overnight on the Pale plateau section. The
Rosetta gondola provides access for day visitors as well as Alta Via walkers.
Capacity around 80; spectacular position above the Pala plateau.

## 8. Rifugio Padova, 1278 m — Zoldo approach, Belluno

On the lower southern section of the Alta Via 2 above the Zoldo valley, the
Rifugio Padova at 1278 m provides a lower-elevation overnight in the forested
zone transitioning from the high Dolomite terrain. The surrounding woods of
the Belluno foothills have a quieter character than the tourist-intensive
northern sections.

## 9. Rifugio Genova-Figari, 1874 m — Sexten area, alternate northern approach

An alternative northern approach hut for the Alta Via 2 near the Sexten
Dolomites, providing access from the Auronzo di Cadore direction. The Sexten
Dolomites — including the Drei Zinnen/Tre Cime di Lavaredo — are among the
most photographed in the range.

## 10. Rifugio Carestiato, 1834 m — Agordo, Belluno

In the Agordino area below the Civetta/Moiazza massifs, the Rifugio Bruto
Carestiato provides connection between the Alta Via 1 and Alta Via 2 corridor
routes in the southern Dolomites. The two routes run roughly parallel in this
section and can be combined or exchanged by motivated walkers.

## Via ferrata requirements

The Alta Via 2's via ferrata sections require a properly fitted harness,
a double-lanyard energy-absorbing via ferrata set, a climbing helmet, and
gloves for the iron rungs and cables. The key ferrata sections are on the
approach to Rifugio Pisciadù, on sections of the Pale di San Martino
plateau traverse, and in the approach corridors of the Puez plateau. None
of the standard via ferrata sections are rated above KS D on the Italian
scale (see the via ferrata progression article for grade definitions), but
the exposure is real and falls can be serious. Groups should confirm
their via ferrata experience before committing to the Alta Via 2.

The Via Alpina 2's demanding reputation means the huts are less crowded
than those on the Alta Via 1, particularly in September when schools are
back. This is the best time to walk the route: the summer holiday peak
has passed, the weather is generally stable, and the first autumn colours
appear in the lower valleys.

## The Pale di San Martino: the Dolomites' strangest landscape

The Pale di San Martino — the vast pale-limestone plateau that dominates
the southern half of the Alta Via 2 — is unlike anything else in the
Dolomites. Above 2500 m, the plateau is essentially flat: a grey-white
expanse of karst limestone stretching for kilometres in all directions,
with scattered rifugi visible from great distances. The effect is lunar,
disorienting, and spectacular in a way that the more clichéd Dolomite
scenery (the Tre Cime, the Sella towers) is not. Walking across the
Pale plateau — particularly in mist or in the dramatic light before a
thunderstorm — is one of the defining experiences of Dolomite high-route
walking.

The plateau is also botanically extraordinary. In summer, the shallow
depressions between the karst formations carry wildflowers found only
at high elevation on limestone: edelweiss (Leontopodium alpinum),
Alpine anemone, and several endemic species that occur only in the
eastern Dolomites.

## Practical access

The Alta Via 2 is reached from Bressanone (Brixen) in the north — served
by frequent rail connections from Innsbruck and Verona — and terminates
in Feltre in the south, connected by regional bus to Belluno and onward
by rail. The northern staging point of Bressanone has a full range of
accommodation; the southern endpoint Feltre is a smaller town but has
several guesthouses and a rail connection to Padova.

Rifugio reservations on the Alta Via 2 must be made well in advance for
July and August. Many of the Dolomite rifugi allow online booking through
their own websites or through the Club Alpino Italiano (CAI) national
booking platform. Arriving at a rifugio without a reservation in peak
season risks being turned away; the Pale di San Martino plateau has no
valley descent that is quick or easy.
