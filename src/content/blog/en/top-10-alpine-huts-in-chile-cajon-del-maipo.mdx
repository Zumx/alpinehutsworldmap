An hour east of Santiago the city stops and the Andes begin without apology. The Cajon del Maipo is the deep river canyon that carries the Maipo down from the Argentine frontier, and it is the climbing and trekking backyard for six million people. Within a couple of hours' drive from the capital you can be at the foot of glaciated 5,000-metre peaks, in cactus-studded foothills, or beside the headwaters that feed Santiago's taps. The shelter here ranges from club-built refugios on the city's nearest summits to remote huts on the volcanoes and border ranges. Chilean refugios are a patchwork — some maintained by mountaineering clubs, some by the national parks service CONAF, some little more than a roofed platform — but together they open up an extraordinary range of high country on the doorstep of a major capital.

## Refugio de Montaña Cerro Provincia

Cerro Provincia, at 2,750 metres, rises straight out of Santiago's eastern suburbs, and the refugio on its flank is the city's most accessible mountain shelter. The full ascent is a long, hot, 1,800-metre day from La Dehesa, and reaching the hut breaks it sensibly. From the summit the whole Maipo basin and, on a clear day, the great cone of Tupungato fill the eastern sky.

## Refugio Plantat

A historic stone shelter on the approach to Cerro El Plomo, the 5,424-metre peak that looms over Santiago, Refugio Plantat is one of the oldest mountain huts in the area. El Plomo is famous for the Inca mummy found near its summit, and the refugios on its flanks — Plantat among them — stage the long acclimatisation climb that the altitude demands.

## Refugio Federación / the El Plomo huts

Higher on El Plomo, a chain of basic shelters lets parties sleep progressively higher before the summit. This is non-technical but serious ground — above 5,000 metres the air is thin and the weather turns fast — and the refugios are spaced to allow a graded ascent over several days rather than a single brutal push.

## Refugio Glaciar Echaurren

The Echaurren glacier feeds the Maipo and, ultimately, much of Santiago's water, and it has been studied for over a century as one of the most closely monitored glaciers in the southern Andes. The Refugio Glaciar Echaurren sits in this high, austere basin, a base for glaciological work and for mountaineers exploring the upper valleys near the border.

## Refugio Volcan Tupungato

Tupungato, at 6,570 metres, is one of the highest volcanoes on Earth and straddles the Chile-Argentina border at the head of the Cajon. The high shelters on its approach — gathered loosely under the name Refugio Volcán Tupungato — serve the few expeditions that attempt this immense and remote peak, far up the Río Colorado drainage beyond the last roads.

## Refugio Arrieros

Arrieros — the muleteers who have moved livestock and gear through these valleys for generations — give their name to shelters along the old stock-driving routes. A Refugio Arrieros is typically a working hut on a high pasture, used by herders in summer and by trekkers crossing toward Argentina by one of the historic passes.

## Refugio Aleman / Rancho Aleman

German immigrants left a deep mark on Chilean mountaineering, and several shelters carry the name Alemán in their honour. The Refugio Alemán and the related Rancho Alemán are gathering points in the foothills, the kind of place where a climbing club keeps a key and a logbook and members stop on the way to the high routes.

## Refugio Toni Rohrer

Named for a figure in Chilean climbing history, Refugio Toni Rohrer is one of the club-maintained shelters that thread the ranges around Santiago. These named huts are reminders that the Cajon's infrastructure was built piece by piece by the country's mountaineering federations rather than by the state.

## Ranger Hut and the CONAF shelters

Where the Cajon enters protected land — the Monumento Natural El Morado is the best known, with its hanging glacier and the lagoon below San Francisco peak — CONAF maintains ranger huts and basic visitor shelters. The Ranger Hut style of building marks the boundary between casual day-hiking country and the serious high ground beyond.

## Refugio Charles and the lakeside outposts

Smaller outposts such as Refugio Charles round out the picture, scattered through the side valleys and along the reservoirs. The El Yeso reservoir, a startling turquoise lake at over 2,500 metres, anchors one of the most-photographed corners of the Cajon, and shelters near it serve both anglers and walkers heading deeper toward the frontier.

## The Chilean refugio system

Chile never built a state-funded hut network on the European model, and that fact shapes everything about shelter in the Cajon. The Club Andino de Chile, founded in 1933, and the Federación de Andinismo together built and maintain most of the named refugios, and access often depends on club membership or contacting the keeper for a key. CONAF, the national forestry corporation, manages the protected areas and their basic shelters. The result is a patchwork that rewards local knowledge: a hut that is open and staffed one season may be locked the next, and conditions vary from comfortable wardened lodges to roofless ruins. Joining a club or hiring a Santiago-based guide is the surest way to unlock the full network, and it brings the safety margin that the big, dry, fast-changing Andean weather demands.

## A weekend mountain for millions

What makes the Cajon extraordinary is its proximity. Six million people live within sight of these peaks, and on a summer weekend the lower trails — Cerro Provincia, the El Morado lagoon, the El Yeso road — fill with santiaguinos escaping the city smog. Buses run from metro stations to San José de Maipo and the canyon villages, and the contrast is jarring: from a congested capital you can be, within hours, alone on a glacier moraine. This accessibility is also the region's tension, as tourism, hydroelectric projects, and the water needs of a thirsty city press on a fragile high-mountain environment. Walking here means seeing both the wildness and the city that leans against it.

## When to go

The Chilean mountain season runs opposite to the northern hemisphere: roughly November to March is the window for the high peaks, when snow has cleared from the approaches and the days are long. Lower walks like Cerro Provincia are possible much of the year but brutally hot in midsummer — start at dawn. The Cajon is dry, dusty country; carry far more water than you think you need, and respect the afternoon wind that funnels up the canyon.
