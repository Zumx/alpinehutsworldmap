The Jungfrau region of the Bernese Oberland is dominated by a trinity of peaks that no photograph quite prepares you for: the Eiger, the Mönch and the Jungfrau, rising in a single wall above the green pastures of Grindelwald and Lauterbrunnen. Behind that wall lies the largest expanse of glacier ice in the Alps, the Great Aletsch, a frozen river twenty-three kilometres long. The huts of this region split between two worlds: the high glacier refuges of the Swiss Alpine Club reached only by serious mountaineers, and the balcony inns perched on the green ridges where day-walkers come for the view of the famous north faces.

## Two kinds of hut in one region

The Jungfrau region offers a rare contrast. On one side are the genuine alpine huts above the ice, where climbers rope up for the Mönch, the Jungfrau and the long traverses of the Aletsch. On the other are the Berggasthäuser, the mountain guesthouses on the grassy shelves above Grindelwald and Mürren, accessible by trail or cable car, serving rösti and the great north-face panorama. Knowing which kind you want shapes everything: equipment, fitness, season and how you sleep at night.

## Mönchsjochhütte

The Mönchsjochhütte at 3657 metres is the highest serviced hut in Switzerland, reached in about an hour across the glacier from the Jungfraujoch railway station, the highest in Europe. Its altitude makes it a unique place: walkers in trainers share the terrace with roped mountaineers preparing for the Mönch. The hut is the standard base for the Mönch (4107 m) by its south-east ridge and a remarkable acclimatisation point, since the train delivers you to nearly 3500 metres in minutes.

## Konkordiahütte

The Konkordiahütte at 2850 metres stands on a rock spur above the confluence of the great ice streams that feed the Aletsch glacier. It is one of the most storied huts in the Alps, and its long flight of metal steps up from the glacier grows each year as the ice surface sinks, a stark measure of glacial retreat. The hut serves the high Bernese traverses and the crossing of the Aletsch toward the Lötschental, classic multi-day glacier journeys.

## Hollandiahütte

The Hollandiahütte at 3240 metres sits on the Lötschenlücke, the great snow saddle at the head of the Aletsch firn, marking the divide between the Oberland and the Lötschental. It is a key staging post on the high glacier route and the base for the Äbeni Flue and Jungfrau approaches from the south. Reaching it means a full day on the glacier from Konkordia, a serious undertaking through crevassed terrain.

## Schreckhornhütte

The Schreckhornhütte at 2520 metres is reached by a demanding approach from Grindelwald up the wild Obers Ischmeer glacier valley. It serves the Schreckhorn (4078 m), the only four-thousander entirely within the canton of Bern and one of the hardest of the normal four-thousander routes. This is a serious climbers' hut, far from the tourist circuit, with a setting of raw glacier and granite that rewards the long walk in.

## Glecksteinhütte

The Glecksteinhütte at 2317 metres clings to a rock face above Grindelwald, reached by a famous exposed path with cables, looking directly across at the Wetterhorn and the hanging glaciers of the Schreckhorn group. It is the base for the Wetterhorn (3692 m), the peak whose ascent in 1854 is often taken as the start of the golden age of alpinism. The terrace, with ibex frequently visible on the surrounding cliffs, is one of the great hut viewpoints of the Oberland.

## Berggasthaus Faulhorn

The Berggasthaus Faulhorn at 2681 metres is one of the oldest mountain hotels in the Alps, in continuous operation since 1830. It crowns a grassy summit on the famous ridge walk between First and the Bachalpsee, with a 360-degree panorama that takes in the entire Eiger-Mönch-Jungfrau wall reflected in the lake below. This is the gentler face of the region, reached entirely on foot or with the help of the First cable car, no glacier skills required.

## Rotstockhütte and the Mürren side

Above Mürren and Lauterbrunnen, on the south side of the valley, the Rotstockhütte at 2039 metres sits in the meadows beneath the Sefinenfurgge pass on the route of the Via Alpina. It is a simpler, family-run refuge looking across at the Jungfrau's western flank, a favourite stop on the long-distance trail that crosses the Bernese Alps. The contrast with the harsh glacier huts opposite could hardly be sharper.

## Lobhornhütte

The Lobhornhütte at 1955 metres above Isenfluh is a small, traditional hut famous for its postcard view of the Jungfrau trinity and its sunset terrace. Privately run and reached on foot through forest and meadow, it embodies the cosy, low-altitude end of the regional hut spectrum, the kind of place where you sit out late watching the alpenglow fade on the great north faces across the valley.

## The Aletsch and the long glacier traverses

Behind the famous north faces lies the true prize of the region for experienced mountaineers: the great Aletsch glacier and the multi-day traverses that cross it. The classic high route runs from the Jungfraujoch across the Konkordiaplatz, the vast junction where four ice streams meet, to the Konkordiahütte, then on over the Grüneckhorn or the Lötschenlücke to the Hollandiahütte and down into the Lötschental. This is a serious glacier expedition lasting several days, demanding rope work, crevasse-rescue skills and steady navigation in a landscape that can become featureless in mist. The scale of the Aletsch is hard to grasp until you are on it: more than twenty kilometres of ice, hemmed by peaks such as the Aletschhorn (4194 m) and the Dreieckhorn, with the surface dropping year on year as the glacier retreats. The UNESCO World Heritage status of the Jungfrau-Aletsch region recognises both its beauty and its scientific value as one of the most studied glaciers on earth. For walkers, the Aletsch can also be admired from the safe vantage of the Eggishorn and Bettmerhorn on its southern flank, where lift-served viewpoints overlook the whole frozen river without any need to set foot on the ice.

## Practical notes for the Jungfrau huts

The high glacier huts staff roughly from late March for ski-touring through to mid-September; the balcony inns open from June through October. The contrast in requirements is total: the Konkordiahütte and Hollandiahütte demand full glacier competence, rope and crampons, while the Faulhorn and Lobhornhütte ask only good legs and decent boots. The Jungfraujoch railway is the region's secret weapon, lifting climbers to 3454 metres and making the Mönchsjochhütte the easiest high acclimatisation hut in the Alps. Reserve through the SAC system for the alpine huts; book the guesthouses directly.
