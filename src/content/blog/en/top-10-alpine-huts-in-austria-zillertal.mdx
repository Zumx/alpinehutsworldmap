The Zillertal Alps rise south of Innsbruck in a wall of granite and ice that marks the border between Tyrol and South Tyrol. This is one of the most heavily glaciated ranges in the Eastern Alps, and its valley heads cradle a chain of high refuges that together form the Berliner Hohenweg, a multi-day circuit that contours from hut to hut at the foot of three-thousand-metre peaks. Walking it, you cross moraine, snow patches in July, and meltwater streams the colour of slate, sleeping each night at altitude with the cold breath of the glaciers at your door.

## The Berliner Hohenweg and how the huts connect

The Berliner Hohenweg, sometimes called the Zillertal Rucksack Route, links roughly nine refuges in a horseshoe around the Zemmgrund and Stillupgrund valleys. Most walkers begin from Mayrhofen, the valley town that serves as the gateway to the whole range, and take six to eight days to complete the loop. The route never drops far into the valley; instead it holds a line between 2000 and 2300 metres, crossing several passes above 2500 metres. Each stage is built to end at a staffed hut, so a single rucksack carries you the whole way without resupply runs to the valley.

## Berliner Hütte

The Berliner Hütte, opened in 1879, is the grande dame of the range and the only Austrian alpine hut listed as a protected historical monument. Set at 2042 metres at the head of the Zemmgrund, it has the proportions of a mountain hotel: a grand staircase, panelled dining rooms, and chandeliers that feel entirely out of place above the treeline. It sleeps over 170 and sits beneath the Hornkees and Waxeggkees glaciers, making it a natural base for ascents of the Großer Mörchner and the Schwarzenstein.

## Greizer Hütte

Reached from the Floitengrund, the Greizer Hütte stands at 2227 metres beneath the Floitenkees glacier. It is smaller and more austere than the Berliner Hütte, with a working-refuge feel that many walkers prefer. The stage from the Berliner Hütte over the Mörchenscharte (2872 m) is one of the most demanding of the whole Hohenweg, with fixed cables and a long snow traverse that can hold well into August.

## Kasseler Hütte

The Kasseler Hütte at 2178 metres in the Stillupgrund is a long, low building looking out over a deep glacial trough. It is the hub between the Greizer Hütte and the Edelhütte, and the crossing of the Lapenscharte (2701 m) on the way in is a serious mountain day. The hut is owned by the German Alpine Club section of Kassel, one of the many DAV sections that built and still maintain huts on Austrian soil.

## Edelhütte

Perched on a rock shoulder at 2238 metres directly above Mayrhofen, the Edelhütte is the oldest hut in the Zillertal, dating to 1888, and the usual final stage of the Hohenweg before the descent to the valley. It is the base for the Ahornspitze (2973 m), an accessible summit that gives a panorama across the entire range you have just traversed. A cable car from Mayrhofen shortens the approach for those climbing the peak as a day trip.

## Furtschaglhaus

On the western arm of the circuit, the Furtschaglhaus sits at 2295 metres above the Schlegeis reservoir, the great turquoise dam that has become the most photographed spot in the valley. It is the springboard for the Großer Möseler (3480 m), one of the easier of the Zillertal three-thousanders, and for the demanding Schönbichler Horn crossing (3134 m), the highest point of the entire Hohenweg.

## Olpererhütte

The Olpererhütte at 2389 metres is famous far beyond hiking circles for the simple suspension footbridge below it, which frames the Schlegeis reservoir and the peaks behind in a single sweep. Rebuilt in modern timber-and-glass form in 2007, it is one of the most architecturally striking huts in the Alps and a popular short hike from the Schlegeis dam road, which makes it busier than the remote eastern huts.

## Friesenberghaus

A short traverse from the Olpererhütte brings you to the Friesenberghaus at 2498 metres, built in the 1920s by a Jewish-led section of the Alpine Club and today maintained as a memorial against intolerance, with an inscription at the door that gives the place real moral weight. It overlooks the small Friesenbergsee and the Hoher Riffler (3231 m), and its history makes it one of the more reflective stops on the route.

## Gamshütte and the lower stations

The Gamshütte at 1916 metres marks the western end of the Hohenweg, lower and greener than the glacial huts, set among dwarf pine with a view across to the Tuxer Alps. Below it the valley fans into pasture, and rustic mountain inns such as the Bayreuther Hütte and other Alpenvereinshäuser of the Rofan and Brandberg areas offer simpler overnight stops for walkers stitching together their own variants away from the main circuit.

## Crossing the high passes

What links these refuges into a single journey is the chain of passes between them, and they are the crux of the Zillertal experience. The Mörchenscharte at 2872 metres, the Lapenscharte at 2701 metres and the Schönbichler Horn at 3134 metres are not gentle saddles but rocky, often snow-filled notches reached by zigzagging paths secured with steel cables and iron pegs. On the Schönbichler crossing, the highest point of the whole circuit, the ground falls away on both sides and a cold wind almost always blows; the reward is a sudden view across the Schlegeis basin to the Hochfeiler, at 3510 metres the highest peak of the Zillertal Alps, rising just over the border in South Tyrol. Walkers planning the full Hohenweg should treat each pass day as a serious mountain undertaking, with an early start to be over the top before the afternoon clouds build. In a heavy snow year the Mörchenscharte in particular can hold a steep firn slope where an ice axe makes the difference between confidence and fear, and hut wardens will give an honest assessment of conditions each evening for the stage ahead.

## Practical notes for the Zillertal huts

The Zillertal huts staff from late June to late September, and many require crossing snowfields or short glaciated sections even in high summer, so an ice axe and basic crampons are sensible for the higher passes. Reservation is essential in July and August; the Alpine Club booking system covers most of the chain. Membership of the DAV, ÖAV or SAC brings substantial discounts and priority in emergencies. Weather in the Zillertal turns fast, and the cables on the high passes become genuinely dangerous in thunderstorm, so an early start is the rule, not the exception.
