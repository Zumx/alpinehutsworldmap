The Fagaras Mountains (Munții Făgăraș) form the highest and most extensive ridge in the Carpathian arc, stretching nearly 70 kilometres along the main watershed of the Southern Carpathians in Romania. The range contains fifteen peaks above 2,500 metres, including Moldoveanu (2,544 m) — the highest point in Romania — and Negoiu (2,535 m). The Translagaric or Transfogarasan high route is the ridge traverse connecting these peaks, typically walked from east to west or west to east over eight to twelve days depending on pace and side excursions. The route is serious: exposed ridge sections, unstable scree, and rapidly changing weather — including summer snowstorms — make it a genuine mountain undertaking rather than a waymarked tourist path. Infrastructure exists but is basic; the cabane (cabins) along the ridge are staffed in summer and offer dormitory accommodation and simple meals. The season runs from early July to late September; earlier or later arrivals face snow on the highest passes. The name Translagaric derives from the Transfăgărășan road (DN7C), one of Romania's most famous mountain roads, which crosses the range by road tunnel.

## 1. Cabana Bârcaciu, western approach

Cabana Bârcaciu at 1,550 metres near the town of Avrig provides the western starting point for the Fagaras ridge traverse. The building is a traditional Romanian mountain cabin (cabana) with 40 beds in dormitory rooms, a dining room serving hot food, and a covered terrace. The approach from Avrig takes around three hours through spruce forest. From the cabin, the trail climbs steeply to the main ridge above 2,000 metres. The Bârcaciu valley below is one of the deepest glacial incisions on the southern flank of the range, with walls rising nearly 1,000 metres from valley floor to ridge.

## 2. Cabana Negoiu, near the second summit

Cabana Negoiu at 1,546 metres is positioned below the approach to Negoiu (2,535 m), Romania's second highest peak. The cabin sleeps 50 and is one of the most used bases on the southern flank of the Fagaras — Negoiu's north face drops in dramatic headwalls above the cabin, providing a compelling backdrop. The ascent to the summit and back takes four to five hours on a well-marked trail. In the dormitory room walls, decades of climbers have left inscriptions and dates — an unofficial but vivid record of the range's mountaineering history.

## 3. Cabana Podragu, Podragu lake basin

Cabana Podragu at 2,136 metres is the highest permanently staffed cabin in the Fagaras and one of the highest staffed mountain shelters in the entire Carpathian range. It sits in the glacial basin below Lacul Podragu — a tarn at 2,054 metres — with the main ridge rising sharply behind. The cabin sleeps 50 in tight dormitory conditions and the kitchen serves simple but hot food. Wind channelled down from the ridge can make the basin extremely cold even in July, and the staff maintain the stove through the season. The ridgeline above provides access to the central Fagaras peaks including Moldoveanu.

## 4. Cabana Capra, Transfagarasan road

Cabana Capra at 1,587 metres sits beside the Transfăgărășan road at the point where the road crosses the range's main watershed through the Bâlea tunnel. The cabin benefits from road access, making it one of the more comfortable options on the traverse — supplies arrive by vehicle rather than on foot or helicopter. It sleeps 60 and has en-suite room options alongside dormitories. The road access also brings large numbers of day-trippers, particularly in August, and the immediate surroundings can be crowded despite the genuine mountain setting. The Bâlea Lake above is one of Romania's most photographed mountain scenes.

## 5. Cabana Suru, Suru cirque

Cabana Suru at approximately 1,400 metres in the Suru valley on the southern flank of the western Fagaras is a smaller, quieter base for explorations of the less-frequented western third of the range. It sleeps 30 and is known among Romanian mountaineers for the quality of its isolation — the Suru valley sees a fraction of the traffic that fills Bârcaciu and Negoiu. The cirque above the cabin contains small tarns and a colony of chamois (capre negre) that can be observed from the ridge. Access from the valley town of Avrig via a different path than Bârcaciu takes four hours.

## 6. Cabana Lacul Avrig, high lake basin

Cabana Lacul Avrig at around 2,060 metres occupies a glacial basin in the eastern sector of the range, above one of the numerous high lakes (lacuri glaciare) that dot the Fagaras ridge. The cabin is basic — 25 beds, a small kitchen, no private rooms — and is used primarily by ridge walkers who have descended from the exposed crest in deteriorating weather. The lake beside the cabin is clean enough to swim in through July and August, and the morning light on the granite walls above turns vivid orange for a brief ten minutes at sunrise.

## 7. Cabana Laita, eastern Fagaras

Cabana Laita is situated at around 1,470 metres on the southeastern approach to the Fagaras, in the Laita valley above the town of Curtea de Argeș. It is a private cabin with 35 beds and is used primarily by walkers ascending toward the eastern peaks of the Fagaras ridge — Vânătarea lui Buteanu (2,507 m) and the Moldoveanu approach from the south. The Curtea de Argeș approach is less known internationally than the northern Transfăgărășan routes, making this corner of the range reliably uncrowded even in the peak August weeks.

## 8. Cabana Turnuri, rocky towers approach

Cabana Turnuri at around 1,520 metres is named for the "towers" (turnuri) — eroded granite formations visible on the ridge above. The cabin sleeps 30 and is operated by a mountaineering association based in Sibiu. It is used by climbers tackling the technical rock routes on the Turnuri formations as well as by ridge traversers. Simple food and basic dormitory conditions make it authentic rather than comfortable, but the setting in a narrow glacial valley with steep walls on three sides is striking.

## 9. Cabana Dejani, Dejan valley

Cabana Dejani at approximately 1,450 metres in the Dejan valley on the northern flank of the Fagaras provides an overnight option for walkers approaching the ridge from the Fagaras town side. It sleeps 25 in a mixed-use building that serves both mountaineers and holiday families from the Fagaras basin below. The northern flank of the range is generally steeper and less frequently visited than the southern side served by the Transfăgărășan road, and the approaches from the northern valley towns involve serious forest and scrub ascents before reaching the ridge.

## 10. Cabana Postăvaru, Brasov region

Cabana Postăvaru at 1,604 metres above Poiana Brasov is part of the broader Bucegi-Fagaras highland system. Technically at the eastern end of the Southern Carpathians rather than strictly on the Fagaras ridge, it serves walkers completing the full Carpathian traverse by linking the Fagaras system with the Bucegi Massif above Sinaia. The cabin sleeps 50 and benefits from close proximity to Brasov, Romania's second largest city, making it one of the most visited mountain shelters in the entire country. Gondola access from Poiana Brasov ski resort means summit hiking is within reach of day visitors from Bucharest and beyond.
