Water is the resource that shapes almost every practical decision in the operation of a high-altitude hut. A staffed rifugio at 2,800 metres needs something between 300 and 600 litres of clean water per day for drinking, cooking, and hygiene when operating at capacity. There is no mains supply. The water must be found, collected, and in many cases treated before it is safe to use, and the source — a spring, a snowmelt stream, a cistern fed by a roof catchment area — will behave differently in June than in September, in a wet year than in a drought, after an afternoon thunderstorm than on a clear morning. Understanding the water systems at alpine huts helps trekkers make sensible decisions about when they can trust a tap and when they should not.

## Springs and Groundwater

The most reliable water source for a high-altitude hut is a spring — a point where groundwater fed by winter precipitation and snowmelt emerges from the rock at a temperature of roughly four to eight degrees Celsius, year-round or for most of the season. Springs at altitude typically drain through calcium-rich limestone or dolomite rock, which naturally filters and partially mineralises the water. The taste is often noticeably clean and slightly mineral.

Most established huts with a spring nearby have built a basic collection system: a covered concrete cistern fed by a pipe from the spring, with a float valve to maintain constant level and an overflow that diverts excess water away from the building. The cistern is inspected and cleaned at the start of each season. Water from a properly maintained spring-fed cistern at altitude is almost always safe to drink without further treatment, and the tap water in the majority of DAV, ÖAV, and SAC huts comes directly from this source.

The limiting factor is that spring flow often diminishes in late summer, particularly in years with low winter snowpack. By September, some springs that ran generously in July are reduced to a trickle, and the hut may need to supplement its spring supply with roof-collected rainwater or snowmelt.

## Snowmelt Streams and Their Risks

Fast-running snowmelt streams are visually appealing water sources — clear, cold, and seemingly pristine. In practice, they present water-safety risks that are easy to underestimate. Above the treeline, the main contamination risks are not industrial but biological: marmot, chamois, ibex, and sheep all defecate near and occasionally in water courses, introducing Giardia lamblia, Cryptosporidium, and campylobacter bacteria at low but real concentrations.

These pathogens are not destroyed by cold water temperature and can survive for weeks in snowmelt streams. Giardia infection — colloquially called beaver fever, though beavers are rarely present at altitude — causes gastrointestinal illness with a 1-3 week incubation period, meaning the symptoms typically appear after you have returned home and may be attributed incorrectly to something eaten in a restaurant.

The practical guidance is: do not drink directly from snowmelt streams unless you have either treated the water or are confident the catchment above the drinking point is clean and free of grazing animals or livestock. At Alpine huts the warden will tell you whether the outdoor tap or trough is for drinking water or for washing boots; when in doubt, ask.

## UV Treatment, Filters, and Chemical Treatment

Huts that cannot guarantee the safety of their primary water source use one or more treatment systems. UV treatment — exposing water to ultraviolet light in a flow-through chamber — kills bacteria, viruses, and protozoa effectively and leaves no taste or residue. It is now the standard treatment system in Swiss and Austrian huts that have identified any biological contamination risk in their source water. A properly sized UV unit can treat the full daily water demand of a 50-person hut with minimal energy consumption and minimal maintenance.

Mechanical filtration through ceramic or hollow-fibre membranes removes bacteria and protozoa but not viruses. For the European Alpine environment, where enteric viruses are rare in mountain water (unlike parts of Asia and North America), membrane filtration alone is generally adequate. Activated carbon post-filtration removes taste and colour from collected rainwater or turbid snowmelt.

Chemical treatment with chlorine or iodine is a legacy approach still used at some smaller or more remote huts as a backup system. Chlorinated water from a hut tap has a faint but noticeable taste. Iodine treatment, more commonly encountered in packaged form carried by individual walkers rather than used in hut systems, produces a stronger taste and should not be used as a long-term drinking-water solution for anyone with thyroid conditions.

## Rainwater Collection

Huts with large roof areas and reliable summer precipitation — which includes most huts in the northern Alps, where fronts arriving from the Atlantic bring regular summer rain — collect rainwater as a supplement to spring and snowmelt supplies. The roof directs water into a large underground cistern, typically 10,000-50,000 litres, which provides a buffer against dry periods.

Collected rainwater at altitude is generally cleaner than at lower elevations because it falls through a less polluted atmosphere and because the roof surfaces are washed regularly by rain. The main contamination risks are bird droppings on the roof catchment area and contamination from the cistern itself if it is not maintained and cleaned annually. Most huts treat their rainwater cistern water with UV or chlorination before distribution to taps.

## What Trekkers Should Know and Carry

The hut tap water in a well-maintained DAV, SAC, CAI, or ÖAV hut is safe to drink. Outdoor water troughs and boot-washing taps may not be. Springs marked with a drinking symbol on trail maps are generally safe, though the trail map information is often years old and may not reflect changes in land use above the spring. Streams fed directly by glacier melt without a significant soil or rock passage are cleaner than streams that pass through pasture, but not reliably clean.

For trekkers who want a personal safety margin without carrying a heavy filter system, a 1-litre SteriPen with UV function handles the water-safety question at all points on the route. A hollow-fibre squeeze filter (Sawyer Squeeze or equivalent) weighs under 90 grams and provides safe drinking water from any surface source. For casual day walkers who stay in staffed huts, neither is necessary — the hut supply is reliable.

## The Outdoor Fountain: When It Is Safe

A very common Alpine feature is the outdoor Brunnen — a stone or concrete trough fed by a pipe from a spring, typically located at the entrance to a village or at a trail junction at some intermediate elevation. These are almost always safe to drink from in the Austrian and Swiss Alps, where the water comes directly from a tested spring and is not treated or processed. The tradition of marking a genuinely good drinking spring with a small sign is taken seriously, and a Brunnen that is not safe to drink from is usually signed accordingly or not signed at all.

At huts specifically, the outdoor Brunnen next to the entrance is usually safe. If you are unsure, fill your bottle at the indoor tap from the warden's kitchen water supply instead.

## Water and the Full Map

The relationship between water availability and the location of Alpine huts — most were built near reliable springs, which is one reason the network looks the way it does — is one of the invisible structures behind the map. [Explore the hut network at /map](/map) and you will notice that hut density follows the contours of well-watered massifs: the Bernese Oberland, the Zillertal, the Dolomites. Ranges with less reliable water, like the drier parts of the southern Limestone Alps, have fewer huts and larger gaps. The availability of safe drinking water shaped the entire system you are walking through.
