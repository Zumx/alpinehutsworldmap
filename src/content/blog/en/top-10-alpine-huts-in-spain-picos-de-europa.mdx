The Picos de Europa are one of the most dramatic mountain ranges in western Europe, rising from near sea level on the Cantabrian coast to summits above 2,600 metres in a horizontal distance of barely 25 kilometres. The range is divided into three massifs — the Western (El Cornión), Central (Los Urrieles), and Eastern (Ándara) — each with a distinct character. The Central Massif is the most challenging terrain, dominated by the Torre Cerredo (2,650 metres), the highest point in the range, and the labyrinthine Cares Gorge running below. The refugios network here is smaller and simpler than in the main Pyrenean chain, but several huts occupy spectacular positions that more than compensate for the basic facilities. The season runs broadly from June through September, with some huts staffed only at weekends outside peak summer.

## 1. Refugio de Montaña AMA Torrecerredo, Central Massif

The Refugio Torrecerredo at approximately 2,200 metres in the heart of the Central Massif is the main base for ascents of Torre Cerredo and several neighbouring summits. The approach from the Vega de Urriellu cable car station or from the valley below is serious and requires route-finding ability on karst terrain. The hut is managed by the AMA (Asociación de Montañeros Asturianos) and sleeps around 50 guests. From July through September it is staffed, and the limited beds make advance reservation strongly advisable.

## 2. Refugio La Chanata, Western Massif

The Refugio La Chanata at around 1,600 metres in the Western Massif above Covadonga serves the extensive plateau terrain of El Cornión — a broad karst upland full of caves, dolinas, and quiet summits that receives far fewer visitors than the Central Massif. The hut provides access to the Lagos de Covadonga, two small mountain lakes at 1,100 metres that are among the most visited natural sites in Asturias, as well as to the higher routes on the Cornión peaks. It is a simple facility with around 30 beds.

## 3. Refugio El Salvador, Eastern Massif

In the Eastern Massif (Ándara) above Sotres, the Refugio El Salvador at approximately 1,900 metres provides the only staffed overnight option in this least-visited section of the Picos de Europa. The Ándara massif contains some of the range's most interesting karst geology and the old mining infrastructure of the Mazarrasa zinc mines, active in the early twentieth century, still visible on the plateau. The refugio sleeps around 35 and is staffed in summer.

## 4. Refugio de Valdesirgas, Cares Gorge area

The Refugio de Valdesirgas sits at around 1,700 metres above the Cares Gorge on the Asturian side of the Central Massif, serving hikers who have completed the famous Cares route — a path cut into the walls of the gorge for 11 kilometres between Poncebos and Caín — and who continue up into the high terrain rather than returning to the valley. It is a small, simple facility suited to parties tackling the Central Massif from the north.

## 5. Refugio del Txolope, Central Massif

The Refugio del Txolope at around 1,900 metres in the Central Massif above Sotres provides access to the eastern approaches of Torre Cerredo and the peaks around the Jou de los Cabrones. This section of the Picos is characterised by deeply pitted, fractured limestone terrain where routes require careful navigation. The hut is basic but staffed in peak season and is used by serious mountaineers approaching the harder routes on the central towers.

## 6. Refugio la canaleja alta, Central Massif

Situated at approximately 2,050 metres in the high interior of the Central Massif, the Refugio la canaleja alta is one of the simpler shelters in the Picos network, designed primarily to provide emergency shelter on the mountain plateau rather than full overnight hospitality. Its position in the canaleja — a distinctive long gully — provides a reference point for parties crossing the massif in poor visibility, when the featureless limestone plateau can become disorienting.

## 7. Refugio Collado del Buey, Central-Eastern boundary

At around 1,780 metres on the pass between the Central and Eastern massifs, the Refugio Collado del Buey (Pass of the Ox) sits on one of the traditional crossing points between Asturias and Cantabria. The route via the Collado del Buey has been used by shepherds for centuries as a transhumance corridor, and the surrounding terrain in July and August still carries cattle and horses grazing on the high pastures. The refugio is a simple staffed shelter with about 25 beds.

## 8. Refugio del Pico de la Calar, Ándara

The Refugio del Pico de la Calar at approximately 1,950 metres in the Ándara massif provides access to the highest points of the Eastern Massif and connects to trails descending toward Bejes and Tresviso in the deep gorges on the southern side. The approach from Sotres takes around three hours and crosses the distinctive red-brown terrain of the Ándara, coloured by iron-rich minerals in the limestone.

## 9. Cabaña Verónica, Torre de Cerredo

The Cabaña Verónica at 2,325 metres is the most famous bivouac shelter in the Picos de Europa — a converted cable car cabin bolted to a rock face in the heart of the Central Massif. It sleeps around 14 people in basic conditions and is maintained by the Spanish Army mountain troops who installed it in the 1960s. The Verónica has no staffing, no food service, and limited amenities, but its position at altitude and its extraordinary accessibility by the Fuente Dé cable car (which rises to 1,850 metres) make it one of the most frequently visited high shelters in Spain.

## 10. Refugio Jou de los Cabrones, Central Massif

The Refugio Jou de los Cabrones at around 2,100 metres in the Jou de los Cabrones (the ibex hollow) is positioned in a broad depression in the Central Massif above Sotres, surrounded by some of the most technically demanding peaks in the Picos — the Naranjo de Bulnes (Picu Urriellu, 2,519 metres) rises to the west, one of the most famous rock climbing objectives in Spain. The hut provides basic shelter and is used primarily by climbing parties approaching Naranjo routes from the east.

## Exploring the Picos

The Picos de Europa are compact enough that a fit walker can cross from north to south in a day, yet complex enough in their karst terrain that parties regularly become lost on the plateau. The refugios network is thinner than in the Pyrenees, and planning your nights carefully is essential.
