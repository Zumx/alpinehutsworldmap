For hut-based alpine travel, you may never need a stove at all — the hut
provides all food and you carry only trail snacks. But on traverse routes
with mixed hut-and-camp approaches, in countries with thin hut networks, or
on shoulder-season trips when some huts are unstaffed, cooking capability
matters. Understanding the fuel options — and their specific performance
at altitude — is practical knowledge.

## Canister stoves (isobutane/propane)

The dominant stove type for European alpine travel is the integrated canister
system: a self-contained unit where a burner head screws directly onto a
pressurised fuel canister. The Jetboil Flash, MSR Reactor, Primus Lite+, and
similar systems are the reference products.

These systems are fast (600 ml of water boils in 2–3 minutes), light (complete
system weight 200–400 g), and simple to use: no priming, no pumping, no
spillable liquid fuel. The sealed canister eliminates the fire and contamination
risk of liquid fuel.

The limitation is altitude performance. Isobutane canister pressure drops
significantly below freezing and at high altitude. A standard isobutane/propane
blend (typically 80/20 or 70/30) performs adequately down to around -5°C but
becomes sluggish below that. At 4000 m, where both temperature and atmospheric
pressure are lower, boil times increase noticeably.

The workaround: warm the canister in a sleeping bag or jacket before use in
cold conditions; use inverted-canister systems (where available) that deliver
liquid fuel to the burner for improved cold-temperature performance.

Fuel availability: Canister fuel (standard Lindal valve EN417) is widely
available across Europe — in outdoor shops, petrol stations, and supermarkets
in mountain towns. Taking an isobutane canister on an aircraft is prohibited
in all jurisdictions; plan to purchase locally or use the hut meal service
for flight-based trips. In the US, canisters are found at REI, outdoor co-ops,
and most trailheads serving popular wilderness areas.

Weight management: a 100 g canister holds enough fuel for approximately 4–6
days of 2-person use (boiling water for drinks and rehydrated meals). A 230 g
canister extends this to 8–12 days. Estimating consumption accurately and
starting with the right canister size reduces carried fuel weight significantly.

## Alcohol stoves

The alcohol stove — typically a simple titanium or aluminium cup burner, using
denatured ethanol — is the lightest stove option. Systems like the Trail
Designs Caldera Cone run well under 100 g including pot. They are simple,
silent, and require no pressurised canisters.

The trade-offs are significant: boil times are long (8–12 minutes for 600 ml
in alpine conditions), performance in wind is poor, and alcohol burns with an
invisible flame that can cause burns if the stove is picked up while lit.
At altitude and in cold conditions, alcohol evaporation and combustion
efficiency drop substantially.

Fuel availability for alcohol stoves in Europe is good — methylated spirits
(Brennspiritus in German, alcool à brûler in French) is sold in hardware stores
and chemists throughout the Alps. In the US, denatured alcohol is similarly
available. The main problem is that you cannot reliably find fuel in small
mountain villages; plan supply carefully.

Alcohol stoves are appropriate for solo ultralight travellers on warm-weather
low-altitude routes or for supplemental use in unstaffed hut winterrooms where
waiting for water to boil is not a problem.

## Liquid-fuel stoves

White gas (petrol camping fuel) stoves — MSR WhisperLite, MSR XGK, Primus
OmniLite — are the tool for serious high-altitude and expedition use. They
operate reliably in extreme cold (down to -30°C), at high altitude, and with
multiple fuel types. The MSR XGK can run on petrol, kerosene, unleaded
petrol, and white gas, making it appropriate for travel in countries where
canister fuel is not available (Nepal, Pakistan, remote South America).

The costs: liquid-fuel stoves require priming (a fire-starting procedure),
are heavier (200–400 g for stove alone), involve a separate fuel bottle
(100–600 ml titanium or aluminium bottles), and produce a roaring sound that
is conspicuous in hut areas. Spilled liquid fuel is a fire hazard.

For the Karakoram or Himalayan routes — K2 base camp approaches, Nepal
expeditions — where logistics make canister resupply impractical, a liquid-fuel
stove is the correct choice. For a two-week Alpine hut traverse, it is
unnecessary complexity.

## Altitude cooking adjustments

Water boils at a lower temperature at altitude: 100°C at sea level becomes
approximately 92°C at 2500 m and 87°C at 4000 m. This means that
rehydration of meals takes longer, and anything requiring cooking (pasta, rice)
needs extended time. Bringing food designed for cold-water rehydration
(some expedition meals, coffee, tea) avoids this problem entirely.

Pressure cooking — using a lightweight titanium pressure cooker — restores
boiling point to nearly sea-level temperatures and dramatically reduces cooking
times at altitude. The weight penalty (typically 200–300 g over a standard
pot) is worthwhile for longer high-altitude trips.

## What to cook

For hut-supported travel with only occasional self-cooked meals, the kit
simplifies to: a 500 ml titanium mug (doubles as cooking pot for rehydrated
meals and drinks), a spork, and a small zip-bag of instant coffee or tea.
No plates, no washing up beyond a quick rinse, minimum weight.

For longer self-sufficient trips, rehydrated meals (Tactical Foodpack,
Expedition Foods, Mountain House) provide 600–800 kcal per bag with no
cooking beyond boiling water. Combined with trail snacks (nuts, cheese,
salami, energy bars), they cover the caloric requirement of active alpine days.

## Fuel availability across regions

Isobutane/propane canisters (the standard for Alpine hut-based travel)
are available in outdoor gear shops throughout the European Alps, but this
availability should not be assumed globally. In Nepal, white gas (Coleman
fuel) is available in Namche Bazaar and Kathmandu, but isobutane canisters
are not reliably available above Namche. In Iceland, the tourist shops at
Landmannalaugar sell isobutane canisters at significant premium; carrying
enough from Reykjavik is the standard approach.

In Kyrgyzstan and Central Asia, fuel canister availability outside Bishkek
is minimal. Liquid fuel (white gas or naphtha) is the practical choice for
any extended trip into areas without outdoor gear retail. The multi-fuel
capability of stoves like the MSR WhisperLite International or the Primus
OmniLite is specifically valuable for this reason.

Alcohol (methylated spirits) for alcohol stoves is available almost everywhere
— hardware stores, pharmacies, petrol stations. For trips where weight is
critical and cooking is minimal (hut-to-hut with only morning coffee
preparation), an alcohol stove and 100–150 ml of fuel per day is a legitimate
ultra-light approach.

## Water purification integration

A stove capable of boiling water performs double duty as a water purification
system. At high altitude (above 4000 m), where pathogens are rare but water
is often from snowmelt or glacier streams, boiling is sufficient. At lower
elevations where water sources may be contaminated, a stove with adequate
fuel supply provides a reliable sterilisation method.

Filter-based purification (Sawyer Squeeze, MSR Guardian) and chemical
purification (Aquatabs, iodine tablets) are lighter alternatives to boiling
for drinking water; they do not require fuel and are more practical for
continuous use on a multi-day trek. The stove's water-purification value
is primarily relevant where fire is the only practical option.
