High on the pale towers of the Dolomites, where climbers today clip into a steel cable and haul up sun-warmed rungs, the iron was first bolted into the rock for reasons that had nothing to do with recreation. The via ferrata — the iron road — was born of war, hammered into the limestone by soldiers fighting one of the strangest campaigns of the First World War, a conflict waged at altitude among the rifugi and the peaks. What began as a desperate means of moving troops and supplies across vertical ground has become the signature alpine adventure linking hut to summit to hut.

## A War Fought in the Vertical

When Italy entered the First World War in 1915, the front with Austria-Hungary ran directly through the Dolomites, a wall of vertical rock turned overnight into a battlefield. Both armies found themselves fighting not just each other but the mountains, hauling artillery to summits, tunnelling through peaks, and stringing supply lines up faces that peacetime climbers had only just begun to attempt. Positions changed hands among towers like the Tofane, the Lagazuoi and the Marmolada, the highest peak of the range.

To move men and matériel across this terrain, engineers fixed ladders, pegs, cables and walkways into the rock, creating protected routes up otherwise impassable ground. These were the first true vie ferrate, built for survival rather than sport. The mountains around Cortina d'Ampezzo still carry their scars: tunnels bored through the Lagazuoi, gun emplacements on cols, and the rusting remains of wartime ironwork that later generations would rediscover and rebuild.

## From Battlefield to Playground

After the war the front fell silent, but the iron remained, and a new generation of climbers and the Italian Alpine Club saw possibility in it. Through the 1930s and especially the post-war decades, old military routes were repaired, extended and supplemented with purpose-built lines, turning relics of conflict into a network of protected climbs. The CAI and local guides led much of this work, and the Dolomites became the heartland of the discipline they had inadvertently invented.

The transformation was cultural as much as physical. A via ferrata let walkers with a head for heights, but without rock-climbing technique, reach summits and traverse exposed ground that would otherwise demand a rope and a guide. It opened the vertical Dolomites to a far wider public, and it did so in a way that naturally linked the region's dense network of huts, with many famous routes running directly from one rifugio to another.

## How a Via Ferrata Works

The modern via ferrata is defined by a continuous steel cable bolted to the rock at intervals, which the climber follows for both route-finding and protection. Where the rock is too steep or smooth to climb freely, iron rungs, pegs, ladders and the occasional suspension bridge bridge the gaps. The climber wears a harness and a specialised via ferrata lanyard with two arms and an energy-absorbing element, clipping both arms to the cable and moving them past each anchor in turn so that one is always attached.

This system is what makes the iron road accessible yet serious. The cable provides security, but a fall on a via ferrata is still violent, and the energy-absorbing lanyard exists precisely because a slip onto a fixed cable would otherwise generate dangerous forces. Helmets are essential against rockfall, and routes are graded for difficulty and seriousness, from gentle protected paths to overhanging athletic test-pieces that demand real strength.

## The Great Routes and Their Huts

The Dolomites remain the spiritual home, and many of the classic lines are inseparable from their huts. The routes on the Tofane and around the Cinque Torri trace the old front above Cortina; the Lagazuoi galleries lead through wartime tunnels with a hut at the top reached by cable car or on foot. Further afield, the Brenta Dolomites hold the famous Bocchette routes, a high traverse of ledges and ladders strung between rifugi that ranks among the finest hut-to-hut ferrata journeys anywhere.

Beyond Italy the idea spread. Austria built an extensive network in the Tyrol and the Dachstein, Germany added routes in the Allgäu and Berchtesgaden, Switzerland and France developed their own, and the French gave the genre its alternative name, the via corda or simply ferrata. Many of these newer routes are recreational from the start rather than military relics, but all trace their lineage to the iron the soldiers left in the Dolomite walls.

## The Modern Boom and Its Tensions

Via ferrata has exploded in popularity, and with good reason: it offers the exposure and summit reward of climbing with a far shorter apprenticeship, and it pairs perfectly with the hut system, letting parties string together multi-day journeys with a warden's dinner at each end. Equipment has improved enormously, gradings have been standardised, and guidebooks and apps map thousands of routes across the Alps.

The boom brings tensions. Popular routes can queue like a ferris ride on a fine summer day, and the very accessibility that makes them wonderful also draws people who underestimate the seriousness, the rockfall, and the speed at which afternoon storms turn a steel cable into a lightning hazard. Clubs and guides stress that a via ferrata is mountaineering, not a fairground ride, and that the iron road still demands the judgement its inventors learned the hard way.

## Respecting the Iron Road Today

To climb a via ferrata well is to honour its history and its risks at once. Check the grade and your own strength honestly, since the harder routes are genuinely athletic and committing. Carry the proper lanyard, harness and helmet, never the makeshift slings of earlier decades, and learn the clipping discipline that keeps one arm always attached. Start early to beat the storms, watch the sky, and retreat if it darkens.

Above all, treat the older routes with the reverence their origins deserve. To haul up a tunnel bored through the Lagazuoi, or to traverse the Bocchette between Brenta huts, is to move through a landscape where men once fought and died on the same iron. The via ferrata is the Alps' most democratic vertical adventure, but it remains a serious mountain undertaking, and the hut at the end of the cable is both reward and reminder of that long, hard history.
