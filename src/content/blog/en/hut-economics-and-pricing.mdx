Guests often express surprise at alpine hut prices — either thinking they are
expensive for what appears to be basic accommodation, or not understanding
why a plate of pasta costs EUR 18 at 3000 m. The economics of running a mountain
hut are unusual, and understanding them changes the relationship between guest
and hut in a useful way.

## The helicopter resupply cost

Everything in an Alpine hut that did not walk up under its own power arrived
by helicopter. Food, drink, fuel, cleaning products, tools, spare parts,
mattress replacements — all of it is transported by cargo helicopter at a per-
kilogram cost that has no equivalent in valley logistics.

In Switzerland and Austria, a standard hut resupply flight costs between
CHF 2,500 and CHF 5,000 depending on hut elevation, remoteness, and load weight.
The typical load is 600–800 kg. This means that the per-kilogram delivered cost
is approximately CHF 4–8, before any storage, preparation, or service is
included. A 500 g pasta portion that cost CHF 2.50 at the valley cash-and-carry
cost CHF 4–6 to deliver to the hut. Multiply by staffing costs, energy costs,
and the short operating season, and the EUR 18 plate of pasta becomes easier
to understand.

Italian rifugi in the Dolomites — including the Rifugio Lagazuoi and the
Rifugio Tissi in the Civetta area — face the same resupply economics but often
operate with the additional complexity of road access to a lower-elevation
parking area, with the final resupply leg by helicopter.

## Bed pricing

The bed price in a staffed Alpine hut covers the mattress, blankets, the
communal sleeping space, and typically access to toilet and washing facilities.
It does not include dinner or breakfast, which are typically priced separately
or as a half-board (demi-pension/Halbpension) package.

In Switzerland, the SAC non-member bed rate for a Matratzenlager place is
approximately CHF 45–55. The SAC member rate is approximately CHF 25–32 —
a roughly 50 percent reduction. At the Konkordia Hütte above the Aletsch
Glacier or the Cabane des Vignettes CAS, these rates apply and are non-
negotiable.

Austrian ÖAV huts operate similarly: full member rates are typically EUR 12–18
for dormitory, compared to EUR 22–32 for non-members. Italian CAI huts are
slightly cheaper in absolute terms, typically EUR 18–26 for members, EUR 30–38
for non-members, though Italian huts more often include meals in the quoted price.

French CAF refuge rates in the Mont Blanc massif run EUR 45–60 demi-pension for
members, EUR 60–80 for non-members — often higher in absolute terms than other
countries, reflecting the Mont Blanc area's high operating costs and demand.

## Membership payback calculation

SAC adult membership costs CHF 105–170 per year depending on category. One
week of hut travel at SAC member rates (6 nights) saves approximately
CHF 120–150 compared to non-member rates. A single week's hut travel pays
back the annual membership fee.

For regular mountain travellers — even one or two trips per year — membership
in the relevant national club is economically rational and provides reciprocal
benefits at affiliated huts across the Alps and beyond (UIAA member clubs).
The practical value of a single ÖAV, SAC, or CAI membership card carrying
reciprocal rights across all Alpine countries is considerable.

## Food markup

The food markup in an Alpine hut is not profit-seeking but cost-reflecting.
A rough rule: expect hut food prices to be 2.5–3.5 times the cost of
equivalent food in a valley restaurant, and valley restaurant prices to be
already above urban rates in mountain towns. At the Rifugio Averau in the
Dolomites, a dinner might cost EUR 35–40 per person for a full three-course
meal — comparable to a mid-range restaurant in a small Italian city.

Given the delivery and preparation costs, this is reasonable. The only
meaningful comparison is not to city restaurants but to the cost of carrying
your own food to the same location: the weight of food, fuel, and cooking
equipment for a week at altitude is around 8 kg per person. The hut service
costs money but buys back considerable pack weight.

## Seasonal economics

Most staffed Alpine huts operate for 3–4 months in summer (mid-June to
early October) and, for winter-room-equipped huts, a shorter ski-touring
season in spring (February–May). The annual revenue is concentrated in this
window; fixed costs — maintenance, insurance, capital repayment — are spread
across twelve months.

Hut keepers who hold long-term leases from the Alpine clubs typically earn
their full annual income in three to four months of intensive operation. A
full house for sixty nights is the operational target that makes the economics
work; a wet summer with below-average bookings can put a season into deficit.

## The helicopter supply chain

The economics of helicopter resupply are the dominant factor in hut pricing.
Every hut above the road network that is not accessible by cable car or
goods ropeway depends on helicopter deliveries for food, equipment, maintenance
supplies, and — in emergencies — personnel. The cost of helicopter flying
time in the Alps is approximately CHF 3,000–4,500 per flight hour; a single
resupply flight to a high hut carries perhaps 500–800 kg of provisions.

This cost per kilogram of delivered food is substantially higher than any
valley delivery logistics. A crate of beer that costs CHF 40 in the valley
costs CHF 40 plus perhaps CHF 30 of helicopter delivery cost per equivalent
weight fraction. The hut is not marking up for profit; it is recovering actual
delivery cost.

Some huts have reduced their helicopter dependency through goods ropeways
(mechanical cable systems that carry supplies up the mountain on a continuous
loop). The Rifugio Torino above Courmayeur, the Konkordia Hütte, and several
Swiss SAC huts have ropeways; these reduce the operating cost per unit of
food but require significant capital investment in the ropeway infrastructure.

## The DAV and ÖAV club membership system in detail

The German Alpine Club (Deutscher Alpenverein, DAV) is the largest Alpine
club in the world with approximately 1.5 million members. Annual membership
costs EUR 66 for adults (2025 rates) and provides member rates at all DAV
and ÖAV huts, third-party liability insurance for mountain activities, and
mountain rescue insurance for Europe.

The Austrian Alpine Club (Österreichischer Alpenverein, ÖAV) has approximately
650,000 members and operates approximately 240 huts directly, with another
150 huts owned by member sections. Member rates at ÖAV huts are approximately
50% of the non-member rate.

International members — non-German and non-Austrian citizens — can join either
club and receive full reciprocal benefits at SAC (Switzerland), CAI (Italy),
CAF (France), and most other European Alpine club huts. For a single two-week
alpine hut tour, the club membership fee is typically recovered within 3–4
nights through the member-rate savings.
