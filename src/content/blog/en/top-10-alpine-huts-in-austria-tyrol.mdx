Austrian Tyrol packs an extraordinary density of alpine terrain into a relatively compact region. The province stretches from the Allgäu Alps in the west through the glaciated Ötztal and Stubai massifs to the dramatic limestone walls of the Wilder Kaiser in the east. Overnight huts here span everything from simple stone shelters above 2,500 metres to large mountain inns with full menus and comfortable dormitories. The following ten represent some of the finest stopping points for anyone exploring Tyrol's high terrain — chosen for their position, character, and the quality of routes they unlock.

## 1. Purtschellerhaus, Wilder Kaiser

Perched at 1,692 metres above Ellmau on the southern flanks of the Wilder Kaiser, the Purtschellerhaus sits at the foot of some of the most imposing limestone rock in the northern Alps. Built by the Austrian Alpine Club section in Salzburg, the hut has served climbers since the late nineteenth century as a base for routes on the Ellmauer Halt, the highest summit in the Wilder Kaiser at 2,344 metres. The approach from the Wochenbrunner Alm parking area takes roughly 90 minutes. The hut operates from late May through October and accommodates around 80 guests in dormitories and smaller rooms. Route variety here is exceptional: technical rock routes, the popular Klettersteig Ellmauer Halt, and the demanding Kaiserklettersteig are all accessible within an hour of leaving the front door.

## 2. Bayreuther Hütte, Rofan

The Bayreuther Hütte stands at 1,575 metres on the flanks of the Rofan range above Maurach on the Achensee, offering direct access to the Gschöllkopf ridge and a cluster of marked hiking routes that cross the Rofan. Opened in 1899 and maintained by the Bavarian section in Bayreuth, the hut is a compact, welcoming structure that rarely feels overcrowded. The cable car from Maurach brings you to 1,838 metres at the Erfurter Hütte, but the Bayreuther makes a more atmospheric overnight base for traverses heading south across the range toward the Inn Valley. The season runs from mid-May to late October.

## 3. Brunnsteinhütte, Karwendel

At 1,558 metres on the southern Karwendel above Mittenwald, the Brunnsteinhütte rewards a moderately steep two-hour ascent with sweeping views over the Inn Valley and direct trails onto the Brunnstein summit at 1,778 metres. The hut was built in 1931 and is privately operated, making it feel more like a mountain inn than a club facility. Its terrace catches afternoon sun and overlooks both the German and Austrian sides of the frontier ridge. The surrounding trail network links into the broader Karwendel Höhenweg, one of the finest multi-day traverses in the northern Alps.

## 4. Dammkarhütte, Karwendel

The Dammkarhütte occupies a broad glacial cirque at 1,680 metres beneath the Dammkar couloir above Mittenwald, a north-facing gully that holds snow well into early summer and is used by ski tourers into April. The hut is compact — around 40 beds — and provides a useful midpoint on routes across the central Karwendel toward Scharnitz. In winter, the Dammkar is accessed via the Mittenwald ski resort, and the long couloir descent back to valley is a local classic. Summer visitors can continue along high paths to the Karwendelhaus.

## 5. Rauhekopfhütte, Mangfall Mountains

Situated at 1,689 metres in the Mangfall range east of Munich, the Rauhekopfhütte may lie on the northern periphery of the Alps, but it serves as an important hub on the long-distance Alpenwelt Karwendel trail. The hut is managed by the DAV section Miesbach, sleeps around 50 people, and is open from late spring through autumn. Its position above the Aurach valley provides access to a series of modest summits in the 1,700–1,800 metre range that are popular with families and trail runners from the Munich area.

## 6. Berggasthaus Herzogstand, Bavarian-Tyrolean borderland

The Berggasthaus Herzogstand stands at 1,578 metres on the summit plateau of the Herzogstand above Walchensee, reached either by cable car from the Walchensee shore or by a two-hour ascent on foot from Kochel am See. Though administratively Bavarian, this mountain inn sits directly on the cultural border between Bavaria and Tyrol and offers an excellent base for the Herzogstand–Heimgarten traverse, a classic ridge walk above the lake. The inn operates year-round, a rarity among higher-elevation huts, and its panorama across Walchensee and toward the Zugspitze is one of the most photographed in the region.

## 7. Tegelberghaus, Allgäu Alps

Perched at 1,707 metres above Schwangau on the Tegelberg massif, the Tegelberghaus is reachable by cable car from the valley or by trail from Füssen in roughly two and a half hours. It sits immediately below the Tegelberg summit at 1,881 metres and overlooks the Forggensee reservoir and the fairy-tale silhouette of Neuschwanstein Castle far below. The Allgäu Alps here are less glaciated than the central Tyrol ranges, making the Tegelberghaus a particularly accessible introduction to hut culture for visitors from Germany who may be new to overnight mountain stays. The surrounding terrain is well suited to both day hikes and multi-day tours connecting to Füssen and the surrounding foothills.

## 8. Brunnkopfhütte, Ammergau Alps

The Brunnkopfhütte stands at 1,602 metres in the Ammergau Alps above Oberammergau, a privately operated mountain inn with comfortable accommodation and a terrace that faces south across the pre-Alpine foothills. The hut is a popular destination from the village of Oberammergau, reachable in about 90 minutes via a marked forest trail. It serves as a base for routes onto the Brunnenkopf and the connecting ridge toward the Ettaler Mandl. The nearby chairlift operates in summer, though many hikers prefer the full ascent on foot as a warm-up for longer routes in the area.

## 9. Kaiserkogelhütte, Wilder Kaiser

The Kaiserkogelhütte sits at the head of the Kaisertal valley near Kufstein at approximately 1,295 metres, making it one of the lower huts on this list but one of exceptional scenic value: the Kaisertal is a narrow, car-free valley enclosed entirely by the vertical walls of the Wilder Kaiser, and the Hütte provides direct access to a range of via ferrata routes and technical climbs on the north face. The valley itself is a nature reserve, and the walk from Kufstein takes about two hours. The hut runs from April through October and is well suited to climbers tackling multi-pitch routes on the Kaiser's north wall.

## 10. Eppzirler Alm, Karwendel

The Eppzirler Alm at around 1,460 metres sits in the Eppzirler Tal north of Zirl, a quiet tributary valley that cuts into the southern Karwendel. The alm is a working farm as well as a mountain inn, and guests in summer can observe cattle grazing on the surrounding meadows — a contrast to the higher, more austere terrain above. It serves as a starting point for the ascent of the Hohe Munde (2,662 metres) and the Erlspitze, both worthwhile summits with routes graded between T3 and T4 on the Swiss scale. The alm opens from late spring through early October.
