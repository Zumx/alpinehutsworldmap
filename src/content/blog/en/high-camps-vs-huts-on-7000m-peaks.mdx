There is a quiet line on the world's mountains, somewhere above five thousand metres, where the masonry stops. Below it you sleep behind walls, on a bunk, with a warden cooking somewhere downstairs. Above it you sleep in nylon you carried on your back, pitched on ice you levelled with a shovel, and everything you eat you melted from snow over a roaring stove. Understanding where that line falls on the great ranges, and why, is the difference between planning an Alpine outing and planning an expedition.

## The ceiling of the European hut system

The Alps run out of mountain before they run out of huts, and that single fact shapes how Europeans think about altitude. The highest staffed refuges in the Alps sit just above 3,800 metres. The Refuge des Cosmiques at 3,613 metres and the Rifugio Capanna Margherita on the Signalkuppe at 4,554 metres — the highest building in Europe — show how far solid shelter reaches even on the highest massif. Mont Blanc itself, at 4,808 metres, is climbed from huts: the Goûter refuge at 3,835 metres and the tiny Vallot bivouac at 4,362 metres mean a climber never needs a tent. The Alps are, in effect, a fully serviced range from valley to summit.

This breeds a particular expectation. A European mountaineer can spend a lifetime on serious 4,000-metre peaks and never once melt snow for water or sleep in a tent on a glacier. The hut does it all: shelter, warmth, food, weather information, even rescue coordination. That comfort has no equivalent on the ranges where the truly high peaks stand.

## Where the huts simply stop

Step onto a 7,000-metre peak and the masonry vanishes. There is no warden, no bunk, no soup. The Himalaya, Karakoram, Pamir, Tien Shan and the high Andes have almost no fixed high-altitude huts of the Alpine kind; what shelter exists is a scatter of base lodges, teahouses and the occasional rudimentary refuge, and they end far below the climbing. On Aconcagua, at 6,961 metres the highest peak outside Asia, you walk in to a base camp like Plaza de Mulas at around 4,300 metres, and above it everything is tents — Nido de Cóndores, Cólera, the high camps where you sleep before the summit push.

The reason is brutally practical. A staffed hut needs resupply, waste removal, fuel, and a structure that survives winter winds of two hundred kilometres an hour and snow loads that would crush an Alpine roof. Above six thousand metres there is no economic or logistical case for it, and on most peaks no legal framework either. So the climbing world solved the problem differently: it brings the shelter with it.

## The architecture of a high camp

An expedition replaces the hut with a chain of camps, each a day apart, stocked by repeated carries. On an 8,000-metre giant the pattern is familiar — Base Camp, then Camp 1 through Camp 4, each higher, smaller and harsher than the last. On Everest, Base Camp sits at 5,364 metres on the Khumbu Glacier; the South Col camp at 7,900 metres is a wind-scoured terrace of torn tents. On K2, Camp 4 on the Shoulder sits near 7,800 metres before the Bottleneck.

A high camp is not a place to live but a place to survive briefly. Tents are pitched on platforms hacked from ice or stone, anchored against winds that flatten anything loose. There is no running water; you melt snow for hours to fill bottles, and a single litre can cost half a canister of gas. Cooking is done in the vestibule or, dangerously, inside, with the constant risk of carbon monoxide. Sleep comes in snatches. The whole arrangement exists to push the climber a little higher each day so the body can adapt before the summit attempt.

## Acclimatisation: the hidden reason for the difference

The deepest reason huts cannot serve 7,000-metre peaks is the human body. On Mont Blanc a fit climber can go from valley to summit in a couple of days because the altitude, while serious, is survivable on quick exposure. On a 7,000-metre peak the body must build extra red blood cells over weeks, and this only happens through the slow rhythm of climbing high and sleeping low. The famous mantra of "climb high, sleep low" requires multiple camps so a team can ascend to tag a higher point, then descend to sleep and recover. A single fixed hut, however comfortable, cannot provide that staircase of altitudes. The tent camp is not just shelter; it is a tool of acclimatisation.

## The grey zone in between

Between the fully serviced Alps and the tent-only giants lies an interesting middle ground. Some ranges have pushed huts higher than the Alpine ceiling for specific peaks. On Kilimanjaro, at 5,895 metres, the Marangu route uses a chain of huts — Mandara, Horombo and Kibo — letting trekkers reach Africa's roof without a tent, a rare case of fixed shelter near six thousand metres. In the Caucasus, the Mount Elbrus routes use fixed huts and the famous Barrels at around 3,800 metres, with a few shelters higher still, blurring the line on a 5,642-metre peak. These exceptions exist because the peaks are popular, the logistics manageable, and the terrain forgiving enough to build on.

## What this means for the climber

The practical lesson is that the skill set changes as you cross that line. Alpine hut climbing rewards speed, fitness and route-reading; everything else is provided. Expedition climbing on a 7,000-metre peak demands self-sufficiency at a level the hut walker never touches: tent craft in storm, stove discipline, snow-melting logistics, load carrying, and above all the patience to spend weeks acclimatising. Moving from one world to the other is less a matter of being stronger than of learning to be your own warden, your own kitchen and your own roof, in the thin air where the buildings end.
