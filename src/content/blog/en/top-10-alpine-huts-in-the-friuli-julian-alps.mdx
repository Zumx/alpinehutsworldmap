The Julian Alps and Carnic Alps together form the northeastern corner of the Alpine chain, where Italy, Slovenia, and Austria meet in a mountain landscape defined by grey Triassic limestone, clear-water streams, and the cultural overlay of Venetian, Habsburg, and Yugoslav historical influences. The Italian region of Friuli-Venezia Giulia contains the Italian Julian Alps east of Udine, while the Slovenian Triglav National Park protects the main Julian massif around Mount Triglav (2,864 m). The Carnic Alps form the border chain between Friuli and Austrian Carinthia to the west of the Julians. The First World War was fought with extraordinary intensity in these mountains — the Isonzo (Soča) front saw twelve major battles between 1915 and 1917, and the ridgelines above Caporetto and Plezzo still bear traces of trenches, barbed wire, and fortified positions. Mountain huts in this region blend Italian rifugio culture (CAI-affiliated, full hot meals, keeper-run) with Slovenian PZS koče (club-owned, community volunteers, simpler facilities) and occasional private operations. The following ten shelters cover the finest positions across both ranges.

## 1. Rifugio Gilberti, Val Saisera

Rifugio Gilberti at 1,850 metres in the Val Saisera above Malborghetto in Friuli is a classic CAI rifugio with a history stretching back to 1909. It sleeps 80 in a large stone building on the approach to the Jôf di Montasio (2,753 m), the highest peak entirely within Italy's Julian Alps. The rifugio takes its name from Enrico Gilberti, an early Italian alpinist, and is operated seasonally from late June to early October by a CAI Trieste section keeper. The valley below is one of the most evocative in the Friulian Alps, with ruined fortifications from the First World War visible on the ridgelines above.

## 2. Rifugio Pellarini, Val Rio Bianco

Rifugio Pellarini at 1,499 metres in the Val Rio Bianco above Resiutta is a quieter CAI hut serving the less-visited western sector of the Friulian Julian Alps. Built in 1959 and named after a local guide, it sleeps 30 and provides full meals. The surrounding terrain — forests of silver fir, beech, and mountain pine giving way to limestone scree — is characteristic of the pre-alpine zone between the Po plain and the main Julian ridge. The hut is used as a base for ascents of the Jôf Fuart (2,666 m) approach on the Italian side.

## 3. Rifugio Marinelli, Jôf di Montasio north face

Rifugio Marinelli at 1,969 metres on the north face approaches of the Jôf di Montasio group is one of the most important technical climbing huts in the Friulian Alps. The CAI Udine section has operated it since 1903. It sleeps 50 and caters primarily to climbers tackling the demanding north face routes on the Montasio group, which rise nearly 800 metres from the rifugio in compact limestone walls. Approach from the Sella Nevea ski resort takes two hours.

## 4. Rifugio Brentari, Jôf Fuart

Rifugio Brentari at 1,838 metres stands below the Jôf Fuart (2,666 m), the second highest summit in the Friulian Julian Alps, at the head of a dramatic glacial cirque that retains a small permanent snowfield into late summer. The hut sleeps 40 and is jointly operated by CAI sections from Gorizia and Cividale. Access involves a steep three-hour approach from the Valbruna valley via the Sella di Sompdogna. The rifugio provides a link point on the Alta Via Carnica, a multi-day route traversing the Carnic and Julian ranges.

## 5. Rifugio Calvi, Carnic Alps

Rifugio Calvi at 1,857 metres in the Carnic Alps above Forni Avoltri is a large, well-equipped hut sleeping 100 guests, operated by the CAI Friuli section. It lies near the main watershed between Italy and Austria and provides access to the Coglians group (2,780 m), the highest point in the Carnic Alps. The hut is a key stop on the Carnic Alta Via (High Route 7), one of the ten official Alta Vie of the Eastern Alps. Autumn weekends see significant traffic from walkers completing the Alta Via in September.

## 6. Rifugio Lambertenghi-Romanin, Paularo area

Rifugio Lambertenghi-Romanin at 1,073 metres in the hills above Paularo is a lower-elevation hut serving the gentler walking territory of the Friulian Prealps. Named after two Friulian alpinists from the early twentieth century, it provides a comfortable base for day walks on the surrounding peaks and is used by local hiking groups from Udine throughout the summer. The surrounding landscape is forested with beech and hornbeam — a pastoral, accessible mountain environment that contrasts with the severe limestone above.

## 7. Dom pod Špicem, Slovenian Karavanke

Dom pod Špicem (House below the Špic) at approximately 1,080 metres on the Slovenian side of the Karavanke range, above Jesenice, is a PZS-affiliated hut with 40 beds. The Karavanke (Karawanken) form the main ridge between Slovenia and Austria in this sector, reaching over 2,000 metres at Stol. The dom provides access to the Karavanke ridge trail and the cross-border walking connections toward Carinthian Ferlach (Borovlje). Slovenian mountain huts in the Karavanke zone are often staffed by teams of local volunteers on a weekend rotation system.

## 8. Rifugio Nordio, Trieste karst hinterland

Rifugio Nordio at around 500 metres in the Karst plateau north of Trieste is the nearest mountain hut to a major city in the entire Alpine chain. Trieste's urban boundary touches the edge of the karst, and the rifugio provides a genuine — if low-altitude — mountain shelter experience accessible by public transport from the city centre in under an hour. The karst landscape of rocky meadows, dolines, and scrub oak has its own austere beauty. The hut is named after Mario Nordio, a Triestine alpinist and journalist.

## 9. Rifugio Tita Piaz, Pale di San Martino, Dolomites border zone

While strictly in the Dolomites rather than the Julian or Carnic Alps, Rifugio Tita Piaz at 2,260 metres above Falcade is included here for its proximity to the eastern Alpine chain and its exceptional rock-climbing heritage. Named after Giovanni Battista "Tita" Piaz (1879-1948), who was nicknamed the "Devil of the Dolomites" for his bold first ascents of Dolomite walls at a time when the rock grades he climbed were considered impossible, the rifugio sleeps 40 and provides access to the technical routes on the Torre Giuliana and surrounding buttresses.

## 10. Rifugio Lavaredo, Tre Cime di Lavaredo

Rifugio Lavaredo at 2,344 metres at the foot of the Tre Cime di Lavaredo (Three Peaks of Lavaredo) is one of the most visited mountain huts in the entire Alps. The Tre Cime — three isolated dolomite towers rising 300 metres above the rifugio — are the most photographed mountains in Italy and among the most recognisable in the world. The hut sleeps 60 and operates a full restaurant service; in July and August the surrounding plateau attracts thousands of day walkers who approach by car or bus from the Misurina and Dobbiaco road systems. The north face of Cima Grande (2,999 m) was first climbed in 1933 by Emilio Comici and the Dimai brothers — one of the most significant alpine ascents of the twentieth century.
