The Cordillera Huayhuash is small on the map and enormous in reputation. A compact knot of ice peaks barely 30 kilometres long, it crams six summits above 6,000 metres into a wilderness with no roads through its heart. This is the range Joe Simpson made famous in Touching the Void, his account of crawling off Siula Grande with a shattered leg. The classic Huayhuash circuit loops the entire massif over roughly eight to twelve days, crossing high passes near 5,000 metres and sleeping not in alpine club huts but in a chain of base camps, shepherd shelters and a handful of simple lodges. To walk it is to enter one of the most spectacular and committing trekking landscapes on Earth.

## A Range Without a Road

The Huayhuash lies in the Andes of central Peru, in the regions of Ancash, Lima and Huanuco, south of the better-known Cordillera Blanca. Most trekkers start from the town of Huaraz, the climbing capital of Peru at 3,050 metres, and drive several hours to trailheads such as Llamac, Cuartelhuain or Pocpa. From there the circuit begins, and for the next week or more there is no resupply, no road and no rescue closer than a long mule ride out.

The peaks are the heart of the matter. Yerupaja, at 6,635 metres, is the second-highest mountain in Peru and the highest in the Amazon watershed. Siula Grande, Jirishanca, Rasac and Sarapo ring the range in a wall of fluted ice. Between them lie turquoise lakes, the lagunas Carhuacocha, Jahuacocha, Mitucocha and Viconga, each a base camp in its own right and each mirroring the summits in still morning water.

## Base Camps, Not Club Huts

It is important to be honest about what shelter means here. Unlike the Alps, the Huayhuash has almost no built mountain huts in the European sense. The circuit is a camping trek: trekkers carry tents or, far more often, travel with an arriero, a muleteer, whose animals carry the camp. Each night is spent at a named base camp beside a lake or stream, where the cook tent goes up and the high peaks turn pink at dusk.

That said, a few fixed shelters and lodges have appeared on the fringes. The Refugio Llaca and similar refuges sit in the neighbouring valleys near Huaraz, used for acclimatisation and for ascents in the Cordillera Blanca. Lodges such as Lucma Lodge near Carhuaz offer a soft bed before and after the wild days. Within the circuit itself, the shelter is the tent, the cook tent and the rough stone corrals the herders use, and that austerity is precisely the point.

## The High Passes

The Huayhuash circuit is defined by its passes. The route crosses a succession of cols, the Cacananpunta, the Punta Cuyoc near 5,000 metres, the Paso San Antonio and the Siula pass, each a hard climb followed by a knee-jarring descent. The highest, the optional Paso Trapecio and the side trip to the San Antonio viewpoint, push close to 5,000 metres and reward the effort with face-on views of Siula Grande and the Sarapo wall.

Altitude is the constant companion. Camps sit between roughly 4,000 and 4,750 metres, and trekkers sleep higher than the summit of any peak in the Alps for the entire circuit. Proper acclimatisation in Huaraz beforehand is not optional; people who skip it routinely turn back with altitude sickness. The reward for the suffering is a sustained immersion in high country that few treks on the planet can match.

## Carhuacocha and the Three Lakes

If the Huayhuash has a single iconic camp, it is Laguna Carhuacocha, where the still water reflects Yerupaja, Siula and Jirishanca in a single sweep. From here the classic variant climbs to the Tres Lagunas viewpoint, a string of three turquoise lakes stepping up beneath the ice walls, widely held to be the finest single day of the entire circuit.

Other camps have their own character. Laguna Jahuacocha, often the last camp before the exit to Llamac, sits in a green basin beneath Rasac and Jirishanca with grazing horses and a sense of arrival. Viconga, on the far side of the range, offers natural hot springs where trekkers soak tired legs after the high passes, an unlikely luxury at over 4,400 metres.

## Communities, Conservation and Fees

The Huayhuash is not empty land. It is owned and managed by the campesino communities of the surrounding villages, and the circuit passes through a series of community-controlled zones, each charging an entry fee that funds local conservation and provides income to families who have herded these valleys for generations. Carrying enough cash for these fees, typically collected at each community boundary, is an essential part of planning.

These fees and the use of local arrieros are central to keeping the range protected and the local economy alive. The Huayhuash was for a time threatened by mining interests, and community-based tourism has become a key argument for preserving it. Trekkers who hire local muleteers and guides, buy supplies in Huaraz and pay the fees willingly are taking part in that conservation story.

## Planning the Circuit

The trekking season runs from about May to September, the Andean dry season, with June to August the most reliable. Even then nights drop well below freezing, and snow on the passes is possible at any time. Most parties use an organised trek out of Huaraz with mules, cook and guide; independent trekking is possible but demands experience, navigation skill and full self-sufficiency in a place where help is days away.

The full circuit is long and hard, but shorter variants exist, the so-called mini Huayhuash trims it to four or five days while still touching the great lakes. Whichever version you choose, the essentials are the same: acclimatise thoroughly, carry warm gear for genuine cold, respect the altitude, and treat the communities and their land with care. The Huayhuash gives nothing easily and rewards nothing more richly.
