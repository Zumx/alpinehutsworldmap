The Rifugio Locatelli stands at 2,405 metres on a broad saddle in the Sesto Dolomites, and from its terrace rises the single most reproduced image in all of alpine photography: the three sheer north walls of the Tre Cime di Lavaredo, the Cima Grande flanked by the Cima Ovest and the Cima Piccola, lifting more than five hundred metres of vertical pale dolomite straight from the scree. Known in German as the Dreizinnenhütte and in Italian as the Rifugio Antonio Locatelli, the hut sits in the South Tyrol borderland where Italian and Austrian alpine culture meet, and it has drawn walkers, climbers, and photographers for well over a century.

## The View That Defines the Dolomites

Almost everyone who comes to the Locatelli comes for the same reason. The three towers face north, which means the great walls fall into shadow for much of the day and then flush gold at sunset and again at first light, when photographers gather on the slopes behind the hut to catch the alpenglow on the pale rock. The Cima Grande (2,999 m) is the central and highest of the three; its 550-metre north face, first climbed in 1933 by Emilio Comici by a direct line that remains a benchmark of big-wall history, is one of the most famous rock faces in the world. From the terrace the whole composition is laid out as if staged, with the two small chapel-like watchtowers of the old fortifications adding to the foreground.

## A Frontier Hut with a Wartime Past

The Locatelli's setting carries history beyond climbing. This was the front line during the First World War, when the border between the Kingdom of Italy and Austria-Hungary ran across these ridges, and the mountains around the hut are scarred with tunnels, trenches, and gun positions from the brutal high-altitude fighting. The hut itself was originally built by the German and Austrian Alpine Club in 1883 and later passed to the Italian Alpine Club, the CAI, after the border shifted; it carries the name of Antonio Locatelli, an aviator and partisan. Walkers exploring the area find open-air war remains everywhere, and the nearby Monte Paterno is threaded with a famous via ferrata that follows wartime galleries through the rock.

## How to Reach It

The Locatelli is one of the more accessible major Dolomite refuges, which is both its blessing and its burden. The standard approach starts from the toll road at the Rifugio Auronzo (2,320 m), reached by car or bus, from where a broad and nearly level path traverses beneath the Tre Cime past the little Rifugio Lavaredo and over the Forcella Lavaredo to arrive at the Locatelli in roughly an hour and a half to two hours. Longer and quieter approaches climb from the Val Fiscalina and the Fischleintal to the north, or from the Lago di Landro on the Dobbiaco road. The easy main route means the area can be crowded in high summer, but those who stay overnight reclaim the silence after the day-trippers descend.

## The Hut and Its Climbing

With around 130 to 150 beds, the Locatelli is a large, busy CAI refuge, wardened through the summer season and offering the standard half-board of hearty mountain cooking, dormitory sleep, and an early breakfast. It is the base for some of the most celebrated rock climbing in the Dolomites: the north faces of the Tre Cime draw hard climbers from across the world, while the Monte Paterno via ferrata and the network of war-path trails give less technical visitors plenty to do. The famous circuit of the Tre Cime, walking right around the three towers, passes the hut and ranks among the classic day-walks of the entire range. Sunrise and sunset shifts on the terrace are the hut's signature ritual.

## The Wider Dolomite and Alpine Context

The Locatelli is one of the great names in a vast Italian rifugio system that stretches from the Dolomites across the Alps to the Aosta valley. In the surrounding South Tyrol and Trentino, walkers find places like the Plosehütte - Rifugio Plose above Bressanone, the Baita dell'Alpino, the Rifugio Sauch, the Rifugio Serot, and the Martellerhütte - Rifugio Martello deep in the Ortles group. Far to the west, on the Italian side of the great peaks, sit the Rifugio Monte Bianco and the Rifugio Pavillon beneath Mont Blanc, the Rifugio Gabriele Boccalatte e Mario Piolti on its rocky perch, and the high refuges of the Aosta valley such as the Rifugio Barbustel and the Rifugio del Misérin. Together they show the astonishing range of the Italian hut tradition, from the towering walls of Lavaredo to the glaciers of the western frontier.

## Planning a Stay

The Locatelli is open through the summer season, roughly from late June to early October depending on snow, and booking is strongly advised given its popularity. The best experiences come from staying overnight: arriving in the afternoon as the crowds thin, watching the sunset light the north walls, sleeping at altitude, and waking for the dawn before the first day-walkers appear. Sturdy footwear and warm layers are essential even in midsummer, since the saddle is exposed and the weather can turn quickly. For photographers, the slopes of the Sasso di Sesto behind the hut give the classic elevated viewpoint of the three peaks aligned. Combining the hut with the Monte Paterno ferrata or the full Tre Cime circuit makes for an unforgettable Dolomite day.

## Two Names, Two Cultures

Part of what makes the Locatelli so fascinating is that it sits at a genuine cultural seam. The whole of South Tyrol was Austrian until the end of the First World War, and the region remains predominantly German-speaking to this day, with Italian and the local Ladin language alongside it. The hut's two names — Dreizinnenhütte and Rifugio Locatelli — are not mere translations but markers of two overlapping traditions of mountaineering, hospitality, and history. Walkers arriving from the Austrian-flavoured villages of the Pustertal find German spoken and Tyrolean dumplings on the menu; the official Italian designation honours an Italian national hero. This duality runs through the whole experience of the Sesto Dolomites, where place names appear in both languages on every signpost and the food, architecture, and customs blend the two worlds. For the visitor, it adds a rich layer of meaning to the spectacular scenery: the Tre Cime are not only a geological marvel but a monument to a contested and shared past, and the Locatelli, sitting directly beneath them, embodies that meeting of cultures as clearly as any building in the Alps.
