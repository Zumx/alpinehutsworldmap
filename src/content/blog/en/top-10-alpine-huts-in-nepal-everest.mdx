The Everest Base Camp trek in Nepal's Khumbu region draws more high-altitude
trekkers than any route on Earth. The route from Lukla airport to Everest
Base Camp (5364 m) at the foot of the Khumbu Icefall is 65 kilometres each
way and passes through terrain that rises from 2860 m at Lukla to the glacier
terminus below the world's highest mountain. The "huts" on this route are
teahouses — locally owned lodges offering dormitory or private rooms and full
meals. They range from basic to, at the lower elevations, surprisingly well-
appointed. The acclimatisation profile built into the standard 12–14 day
itinerary is what makes the route feasible for trekkers without climbing
experience.

## 1. Namche Bazaar, 3440 m — Solukhumbu District

The hub of the Khumbu region, Namche Bazaar is the largest settlement on the
trek (approximately 1,500 permanent residents) and the standard first
acclimatisation stop after the Lukla flight. The town sits in a natural
horseshoe amphitheatre above the Bhote Koshi and Dudh Koshi river confluence.
Teahouses here range from basic to full hotel standard, with Wi-Fi, espresso
bars, and hot showers available. The acclimatisation day here (a rest day or
a walk to the Everest View Hotel at 3880 m) is mandatory for proper altitude
adjustment before proceeding higher.

## 2. Tengboche, 3860 m — Solukhumbu

The monastery of Tengboche is one of the most photographed locations in Nepal:
the main gompa building, rebuilt after a 1989 fire, sits on a promontory above
the valley with Ama Dablam (6812 m), Thamserku, and Everest itself visible
on clear mornings. The teahouses at Tengboche — including the Sagarmatha Lodge
and several others — provide overnight accommodation at 3860 m. This elevation
requires one or two nights to acclimatise before proceeding. The Mani Rimdu
festival in November draws substantial crowds.

## 3. Dingboche, 4360 m — Solukhumbu, Imja valley

The village of Dingboche in the Imja valley is the standard second
acclimatisation stop. The teahouses here are the last point where the landscape
has any vegetation; above Dingboche the terrain becomes rocky moraine and
glacier ice. The Pillichon Hut (2760 m in another context) marks the lower
valley; the Dingboche teahouses at 4360 m include several well-established
lodges. The acclimatisation day walk to Nangkartshang Peak (5083 m) is one
of the finest on the route.

## 4. Lobuche, 4940 m — Khumbu Glacier moraine

Lobuche teahouses (several competing establishments at 4940 m) are the last
fully serviced overnight stop before Gorak Shep. At this elevation, AMS
symptoms are common and appetite drops significantly. The teahouses provide
limited menu options — dal bhat (rice and lentils), noodle soup, eggs — powered
by yak-dung stoves. The nights are cold (below -15°C in October–November) and
sleeping quality is generally poor due to altitude. The Korchon hut and
Lobuche Peak base camp are nearby.

## 5. Gorak Shep, 5164 m — Khumbu Glacier

The last teahouse settlement before Base Camp, Gorak Shep (5164 m) is the
most basic and most dramatically situated cluster of lodges on the route.
Two or three teahouses with limited facilities, kerosene-heated dining rooms,
and dormitory sleeping at an elevation where almost every guest has AMS symptoms.
From Gorak Shep, the day walk to Everest Base Camp (5364 m) takes 3 hours
return; the walk to Kala Patthar viewpoint (5644 m) provides the most celebrated
panorama on the entire route. Overnight at Gorak Shep is recommended over
descending immediately from Base Camp.

## 6. Pheriche, 4371 m — Khumbu valley

An alternative acclimatisation stop to Dingboche, at a similar elevation
(4371 m) but in the main Khumbu valley rather than the Imja side valley.
Pheriche has a Himalayan Rescue Association aid post that provides medical
consultations for altitude illness — the most significant medical resource
on the trekking route. Teahouses include the Arun Lodge and others.

## 7. Pangboche, 3985 m — Solukhumbu

The village of Pangboche at 3985 m is the highest permanently inhabited
village in the Khumbu region (Dingboche is seasonally occupied). The gompa
here is claimed to be the oldest in the Khumbu; the surrounding terrain
includes the approach to Ama Dablam Base Camp. Teahouses include the Pumori
Lodge and smaller establishments.

## 8. Khumjung, 3790 m — above Namche

Above Namche on the acclimatisation day walk, Khumjung village (3790 m)
hosts the Hillary School (built with Sir Edmund Hillary's support from 1961)
and the Khumjung monastery — where a supposed yeti scalp is kept. The teahouses
here are quiet and less visited than Namche.

## 9. Phortse, 3810 m — Solukhumbu

A quiet alternative to the main trekking highway, Phortse at 3810 m is a
traditional Sherpa village above the confluence of the Imja and Dudh Koshi
valleys with good views of Ama Dablam. Used by parties taking the Mong La
variant or the route toward Gokyo.

## 10. Chhukung, 4730 m — Imja valley

In the upper Imja valley above Dingboche at 4730 m, Chhukung is the base
for the Island Peak (6189 m) climbing route and the Chhukung Ri viewpoint
(5550 m). The teahouses here are basic but provide the launching point for
one of the most popular 6000 m trekking peaks in Nepal.

## TIMS card and ACAP/Sagarmatha permit

All trekkers in the Khumbu region require a TIMS (Trekkers' Information
Management System) card and a Sagarmatha National Park entry permit.
The TIMS card (approximately NPR 2,000 for independent trekkers, NPR 1,000
for guided groups) is issued by the Trekking Agencies Association of Nepal
(TAAN) and Nepal Tourism Board offices in Kathmandu and Pokhara.

The Sagarmatha National Park permit (approximately NPR 3,000 for non-SAARC
nationals) is obtained at the park entrance at Monjo (2835 m). Permits can
also be arranged through trekking agencies in Kathmandu. The national park
fee funds conservation activities in the park, including the Sagarmatha
Pollution Control Committee (SPCC) which coordinates waste management across
the trekking network.

## Teahouse standards in the Khumbu

The Khumbu teahouse network has transformed since the 1970s when lodging
was genuinely basic. Today, the major teahouses in Namche, Tengboche,
Dingboche, and Lobuche offer heated dining rooms, private rooms (at higher
cost), hot showers (expensive — USD 5–10 per shower above 4000 m), wifi
(satellite-connected; slow and expensive), and meals that go well beyond
the traditional dal bhat. The quality decreases with altitude — the
teahouses at Gorak Shep (5170 m) are functional but not comfortable in
the way that Namche's lodges are comfortable.

The price gradient with altitude is predictable: a bed that costs NPR 500–700
in Namche costs NPR 1500–2500 at Lobuche and NPR 2500–4000 at Gorak Shep.
The teahouses recover the additional cost of helicopter-delivered supplies
and generator fuel through their room and meal prices.

The Gokyo Lakes route — an alternative to the direct Namche-Tengboche-EBC
line — passes through the Gokyo valley to the Gokyo Ri viewpoint (5360 m)
before crossing the Cho La pass (5420 m) to join the EBC route at Lobuche.
The Gokyo lodges are smaller and less crowded than the EBC corridor; the
views from Gokyo Ri toward Everest, Lhotse, and the Ngozumpa Glacier (the
largest glacier in Nepal) are comparable to any viewpoint on the classic route.
