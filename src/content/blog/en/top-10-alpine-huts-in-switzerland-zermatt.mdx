Zermatt sits at the head of the Mattertal in the Swiss canton of Valais, hemmed in by the greatest gathering of four-thousand-metre peaks anywhere in the Alps. No cars reach the village; you arrive by the cog railway from Täsch, and the silence that follows is the first sign that this is a place built around mountains rather than traffic. Above the rooftops the Matterhorn stands alone, and behind it spreads the Monte Rosa massif with its half-dozen named summits. The huts that ring these peaks are run by the Swiss Alpine Club, and they form the working backbone of one of the most serious mountaineering arenas on earth.

## Zermatt as a hut-climbing base

Most of Zermatt's huts are not casual day-walk destinations but launch points for glaciated ascents. The cableways to Schwarzsee, Rothorn and the Matterhorn Glacier Paradise lift climbers and walkers high above the valley, shortening approaches that would otherwise take a full day. From these stations the hut paths branch out across moraine and rock toward the snouts of the Gorner, Findel and Theodul glaciers. Knowing which lift serves which hut is half the planning; the other half is judging whether you have the glacier skills the route demands.

## Hörnlihütte

The Hörnlihütte at 3260 metres is the most famous climbing hut in the country, the springboard for the Matterhorn by the Hörnli ridge, the line of the first ascent in 1865. Rebuilt for the 150th anniversary of that climb, it is a sleek modern building clinging to the foot of the ridge at the Schwarzsee end of the mountain. On a clear summer morning a procession of headlamps leaves it before dawn; the hut exists almost entirely to serve that single, demanding route.

## Monte Rosa Hut

The Monte Rosa Hut, the silver geodesic structure visible from the Gornergrat railway, is one of the most photographed buildings in the Alps. Standing at 2883 metres on a rock island amid the Gorner and Grenz glaciers, it was rebuilt in 2009 as a near-self-sufficient showcase of mountain engineering, drawing most of its energy from the sun. It is the base for the Dufourspitze (4634 m), the highest point in Switzerland, reached across a long and crevassed glacier approach.

## Gandegghütte

The Gandegghütte at 3029 metres sits just below the Trockener Steg cable station, with a terrace that looks straight onto the Breithorn and the Theodul glacier. Privately run rather than SAC-owned, it is more relaxed than the climbing huts and popular with skiers in winter and walkers in summer who want a high refuge without a serious approach. From here the Breithorn (4164 m), often called the easiest four-thousander in the Alps, is a short glacier outing.

## Rothornhütte

Above the Rothorn lift, the Rothornhütte at 3198 metres serves the Zinalrothorn (4221 m) and Trifthorn, classic Valais rock-and-ice routes. The old hut was decommissioned as the ground beneath it destabilised with the retreat of the permafrost, and a new building has taken its place a little higher up the slope, a vivid example of how a warming climate is reshaping the hut network across the range.

## Trift and Berggasthaus Trift

Lower down, the Berggasthaus Trift at 2337 metres sits in a green hanging valley between the Mettelhorn and the glaciers above. It is a much-loved rest stop on the walk from Zermatt toward the Rothornhütte and the Mettelhorn, a four-thousand-metre walking peak. The Trift hut is famous for its homemade cakes and its position in the angle between the Triftbach and the high ice, a softer counterpoint to the austere climbing huts.

## Schönbielhütte

The Schönbielhütte at 2694 metres looks across the Zmutt glacier at the savage west face of the Matterhorn, a view many consider finer than the classic Hörnli profile seen from the village. The approach from Zermatt up the Zmuttal is one of the great hut walks of the region, climbing past the moraines of the retreating glacier. The hut serves the Dent Blanche traverse and the high Haute Route variants that cross from Arolla.

## Britanniahütte and the Saas side

Just over the ridge from the Mattertal lies the Saastal, where the Britanniahütte at 3030 metres serves the Allalin, Rimpfischhorn and the famous ski-touring terrain of the Saas valley. Reached easily from the Felskinn cable car, it is one of the busiest SAC huts in the Alps, central to the Spaghetti Tour, the multi-day chain of four-thousanders that links the Monte Rosa huts on both the Swiss and Italian flanks of the massif.

## The Italian flank: Rifugio Guide d'Ayas and Rifugio Mezzalama

The Monte Rosa massif straddles the frontier, and the Italian huts of the Aosta valley belong to the same high world. The Rifugio Ottorino Mezzalama at 3036 metres and the Rifugio Guide d'Ayas at 3394 metres serve the Castor and Pollux peaks and the Italian half of the Spaghetti Tour above Champoluc and Ayas. From their terraces the southern walls of Monte Rosa rise in a single sweep, and the contrast in style between SAC and CAI huts becomes part of the journey.

## A massif shaped by retreating ice

No range in the Alps shows the effects of a warming climate more starkly than the peaks around Zermatt, and the huts are on the front line of that change. The Gorner glacier, second largest in the Alps, has thinned and shrunk so dramatically that the approach to the Monte Rosa Hut now involves a steep, ladder-equipped descent onto bare ice that did not exist a generation ago. The old Rothornhütte had to be abandoned when the permafrost binding its foundations thawed, and the rock around the Hörnli ridge has become more prone to rockfall in hot summers, prompting guides to start ever earlier. Hut wardens here keep a close eye on conditions and will turn parties back when the ice is too broken or the stonefall too active. For visitors this means that the classic seasons are shifting: early summer increasingly offers safer snow on the high routes, while late August can bring bare, brittle ground. Understanding this changing landscape is now part of planning any serious outing from a Zermatt hut, and the village guides are an invaluable source of current information.

## Practical notes for the Zermatt huts

The Zermatt huts open mainly from mid-June to mid-September, with the higher climbing huts dependent on glacier conditions that vary year to year. Almost every route from these huts is glaciated, requiring rope, crampons, ice axe and the competence to use them, or a qualified mountain guide, of which Zermatt has many. Reservation through the SAC system is essential in the short summer season. Acclimatisation matters here as nowhere else in the Alps; sleeping a night at a 3000-metre hut before a four-thousander is the standard, sensible approach.
