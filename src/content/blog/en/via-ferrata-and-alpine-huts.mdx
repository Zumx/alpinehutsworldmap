Via ferrata — the Italian term for "iron road" — is a style of mountain route
equipped with fixed iron rungs, cables, stemples, and ladders that allow
walkers and hikers to access terrain that would otherwise require rock climbing
technique. The Dolomites contain the largest and most varied concentration
of via ferrata routes in the world, a legacy partly of First World War military
infrastructure. Alpine huts are inseparable from via ferrata travel: they sit
at the base of routes, on ridge junctions between routes, or at the summit
of major climbs. The [map](/map) shows hut positions relative to ferrata routes
across the main areas.

## Required gear

A via ferrata set consists of three components: a harness, a helmet, and a
via ferrata lanyard with energy absorber. The lanyard typically has a Y-shape
with two short arms, each with a carabiner, connected to a central energy
absorber. The Y-shape allows continuous attachment while passing intermediate
anchors — you clip the second arm before unclipping the first, so you are
always secured.

The energy absorber is not optional. A simple sling or climbing lanyard
without a shock absorber can generate forces of 6–12 kN on the cable anchors
during a fall — sufficient to cause anchor failure or severe injury. The
absorber limits peak force to around 6 kN at most, and modern systems with
proper energy-absorbing function stay at 3–4 kN.

Harness: a standard climbing harness or a dedicated via ferrata harness.
The via ferrata-specific harness is lighter and fits under a pack more
comfortably; a standard climbing harness works if you own one.

Helmet: a climbing or cycling helmet. A walking helmet without impact-
absorption capability on the crown is inadequate.

Gloves: leather or synthetic work gloves extend the life of your hands on
iron cables and warm metal rungs.

## Grading systems

Two grading systems dominate European via ferrata description:

The Schall system (used in Austria, Germany, and Switzerland): A (easy), B
(moderate), C (difficult), D (very difficult), E (extremely difficult), with
combined grades like BC or CD common. An A or AB via ferrata is accessible to
walkers with no technical background; a D or higher requires good fitness,
no fear of exposure, and some climbing technique.

The Hüsler system (common in Italy and French guidebooks): 1 (easy) to 6
(extreme), with the same intermediate scale. Roughly: 1–2 corresponds to
Schall A–B; 3–4 to B–C/D; 5–6 to D–E.

Route descriptions in Italian use the abbreviations F (facile — easy),
PD (poco difficile), AD (assez difficile), D (difficile), TD (très difficile),
and ED (extrêmement difficile), borrowed from Alpine climbing grading.

## Rifugio Lagazuoi — Dolomites Ferrata Hub

The Rifugio Lagazuoi (2752 m) on the Piccolo Lagazuoi above Passo Falzarego
is the finest ferrata hub in the Dolomites. From the hut, multiple routes
descend through the wartime tunnel system (Gallerie di Lagazuoi) — a Grade B
ferrata on the tunnel walls themselves — and the classic Via Ferrata Ivano
Dibona traverses the Cristallo ridge toward Cortina. The cable car access makes
Lagazuoi usable as a base for a full day of ferrata activity without a long
approach. Set aside a full day for the tunnel descent and Nuvolau traverse.

## Brenta Dolomites — Rifugio Tuckett and the Bocchette Routes

The Brenta Dolomites west of the main chain host the Bocchette Alta and
Bocchette Centrale routes — two of the classic via ferratas in the Alps.
The Bocchette Alta (Grade C–D, Hüsler 3–4) traverses the high ridge of the
Brenta group at 2700–2900 m, passing through notches and crossing exposed
ledges for several hours. The Bocchette Centrale connects the same ridge at
a slightly lower and less committing grade.

Rifugio Tuckett (2272 m) and the adjacent Rifugio Brentei (2182 m) serve as
base huts for both routes, reachable from Madonna di Campiglio. A full
Bocchette Alta day requires an early start (06:30–07:00 from the hut),
good physical fitness, and a full via ferrata set. Afternoon thunderstorms
on the exposed Brenta ridge are the main objective hazard.

## Marmolada — Punta Rocca and the Western Ridge

The Marmolada (3343 m) — the "Queen of the Dolomites" — has several via
ferrata routes on its southern face reaching the summit plateau. The Via
Ferrata Eterna Brigata Cadore (Grade C) is the most popular approach.
Rifugio Castiglioni (2050 m) in the Fedaia area serves as the approach base.
The Marmolada glacier on the north face has retreated dramatically in recent
decades; the summit plateau terrain changes annually.

## Pisciadù and the Sella Group — Rifugio Pisciadù

The Via Ferrata Pisciadù (Grade B) from the Passo Gardena to the Rifugio
Pisciadù (2585 m) on the Sella plateau is one of the best-value ferrata
experiences in the Dolomites — a full mountain day with significant exposure
without reaching very high difficulty grades. The rifugio sits on the plateau
with direct views of the Marmolada and the Fanes. From Pisciadù, the Via
Ferrata Tridentina continues to Rifugio Boè and Piz Boè (3152 m).

## Tofana di Rozes — Cortina d'Ampezzo

Above Cortina, the Via Ferrata Lipella (Grade C–D) ascends the south pillar
of the Tofana di Rozes (3225 m) — one of the classic Dolomite ferrata
objectives. Start from Rifugio Dibona (2083 m) reached from the Ra Valles
cable car. The Dibona serves parties on multiple Tofana routes. Summit by
noon to avoid afternoon electric weather on the high ridges.

## Timing and the thunderstorm rule

Via ferrata routes in the Dolomites are most dangerous in the early afternoon
when convective thunderstorms develop. Lightning on an iron cable route is
extremely serious — the cable conducts current across its entire length.
The standard practice is: start early (by 07:00 on multi-section routes),
plan to be off the exposed ridge by 13:00, and watch cloud development above
the surrounding peaks throughout the morning. A cloud building rapidly above
a summit 30 km south is a two-hour warning, not a five-hour warning.

If a storm approaches while on a cable: disconnect the lanyard from the cable,
move to the lowest available point away from the fixed ironware, crouch on
insulating material (your pack) with feet together, and wait for the
lightning risk to pass before moving.

## Booking huts around ferrata itineraries

Via ferrata huts at popular routes — Lagazuoi, Tuckett, Pisciadù — fill
quickly in July and August. Contact the specific CAI section managing each
hut directly for reservations, or use the hut's own website. Book the night
before and after your ferrata day — approaching huts after a full route can
take longer than expected, particularly if conditions were challenging.
