The Adlerweg (Eagle Walk) is Tyrol's flagship long-distance mountain trail —
a 413-kilometre route from St. Johann in Tirol in the east to St. Anton am
Arlberg in the west, crossing the northern Tiroler Alps from the Kaisergebirge
to the Arlberg. The route takes 24 stages and is divided into two sections:
the northern main route crossing the Karwendel and Wetterstein ranges, and
the southern variation through the more glaciated main Alpine chain. The huts
below are the defining overnight positions on the northern route.

## 1. Falkenhütte, 1848 m — Karwendel, Tirol

In the heart of the Karwendel — one of the largest nature reserves in the
Alps, covering 730 square kilometres of wilderness limestone above the Inn
valley — the Falkenhütte at 1848 m is one of the most dramatically positioned
ÖAV huts in Austria. The approach from the Eng valley (accessible by bus in
summer) takes 2.5 hours. The Falkenhütte sits directly below the Lalidererspitzen
— a sheer 600-metre limestone wall that is one of Austria's great rock climbing
destinations. Capacity around 120; full half-board.

## 2. Karwendelhaus, 1765 m — Karwendel, Tirol

The central hut of the Karwendel traverse, the Karwendelhaus at 1765 m in
the Hinterautal sits beside the Isar river source and provides the main staging
base for the high Karwendel peaks including the Birkkarspitze (2749 m).
ÖAV Karwendel section; capacity around 140. An ÖAV-owned Alpine bus serves
the Hinterautal valley from Scharnitz in summer, making the Karwendelhaus
more accessible than many Karwendel huts.

## 3. Memminger Hütte, 2242 m — Lechtal Alps, Tirol

In the Lechtal Alps west of the Zugspitze massif, the Memminger Hütte at
2242 m serves the highest section of the northern Adlerweg. Owned by the
DAV Memmingen section; capacity around 80. The route between the Memminger
and the following stages involves some of the most demanding terrain on the
Adlerweg — exposed ridgelines and significant elevation change. The Lechtal
Alps are less visited than the Wetterstein or Karwendel and offer genuine
wilderness character.

## 4. Erfurter Hütte, 1834 m — Rofan, Tirol

On the Rofan massif above Lake Achensee — one of the largest lakes in Tyrol
— the Erfurter Hütte at 1834 m is the entry hut for the Adlerweg from the
eastern direction. Reached by cable car from Maurach on the lake shore (summer
operation) or on foot from the valley in 2 hours. The Rofan is characterised
by dramatic limestone formations — the Seekarlspitze (2261 m) is the standard
summit objective. DAV Erfurt section; capacity around 100.

## 5. Lamsenjochhütte, 1953 m — Karwendel, Tirol

On the Lamsen saddle at 1953 m above the Maurach area, the Lamsenjochhütte
connects the Rofan stage with the main Karwendel approach. ÖAV Mittenwald
section; capacity around 90. The hut is also the base for the Lamsenjoch
summit and the high traverse toward the Karwendelhaus.

## 6. Nördlingerhütte, 2239 m — Mieminger Plateau, Tirol

On the western section of the Adlerweg in the Mieminger and Zugspitze area,
the Nördlingerhütte at 2239 m provides access to the Zugspitz massif approaches
from the Austrian side. DAV Nördlingen section; capacity around 50.

## 7. Reintalangerhütte, 1370 m — Partnachklamm area, Bayern/Tirol border

On the border of Bavaria and Tyrol in the Partnach gorge system below the
Zugspitze, the Reintalangerhütte at 1370 m is a large DAV hut (DAV Munich
section) serving the approach to the Zugspitze from the south via the Reintal.
Capacity around 120. The Zugspitze (2962 m) is the highest peak in Germany
and a dominant objective from this hut.

## 8. Coburger Hütte, 1917 m — Zugspitz area, Tirol/Bayern

On the Austrian side of the Zugspitze massif at 1917 m, the Coburger Hütte
serves the Blindsee approach to the Zugspitze and the southern Wetterstein
traverses. DAV Coburg section; capacity around 60.

## 9. Augsburger Hütte, 2232 m — Lechtaler Alps

Above the Zugspitze area on the Adlerweg extension, the Augsburger Hütte at
2232 m provides the transition hut from the central Adlerweg toward the Arlberg
end section. DAV Augsburg section.

## 10. Darmstädter Hütte, 2384 m — Verwall, Tirol

Near the Arlberg end of the Adlerweg, the Darmstädter Hütte at 2384 m in the
Verwall group above St. Anton provides the final high-mountain overnight before
the trail descends to the Arlberg railway junction. DAV Darmstadt section.

## The Karwendel character

The Karwendel is the largest nature reserve in the Alps and deserves a
special note. Unlike the well-manicured trail networks of the Stubai or
the commercial circuits of the Dolomites, the Karwendel retains a genuinely
wild character. The peaks are not high by Alpine standards (Birkkarspitze,
2749 m) but the rockscapes are dramatic — sheer grey and white limestone towers,
long scree runs, and the deep-cut gorges of the Isar and its tributaries.
The wildlife is exceptional: Bearded vultures (Bartgeier) have been successfully
reintroduced and are now regularly seen over the Karwendel; chamois herds are
common; ibex have been established on the limestone ridges.

Walking in the Karwendel requires navigation competence and preparation for
rapidly changing weather: the range intercepts both the northern Alpine weather
systems from the Bavarian foothills and the foehn winds from the south. The
Falkenhütte and Karwendelhaus are the fixed reference points in this navigation
challenge; parties planning the full Karwendel traverse independently should
carry 1:25,000 maps and have downloaded offline GPS tracks.

## The Zugspitze section

The central Adlerweg passes through the Zugspitze area — across the German-
Austrian border at the Zugspitzmassiv. The Zugspitze (2962 m) is the highest
German peak and carries significant tourist infrastructure: a cable car from
Garmisch-Partenkirchen, a rack railway to the summit, and an Austrian cable
car from the Tiroler side. The Adlerweg route does not ascend to the summit
(which is dominated by the cable car infrastructure) but traverses the lower
terrain of the Wetterstein, using the summit views as background rather
than objective.

## Planning the Adlerweg

The full 413-kilometre Adlerweg takes 24 stages and is typically completed
over 3–4 weeks, though most walkers select sections — the Karwendel traverse
(stages 1–8) and the Lechtal section (stages 12–18) are the most popular
independent itineraries. Tyrol's official Adlerweg website provides GPX
tracks and a hut booking service; the ÖAV online reservation system covers
all Austrian alpine club huts along the route.

Stages in the eastern Karwendel section are rated T3–T4 in difficulty; the
Lechtal section includes some T4 terrain with fixed-rope assistance on the
more exposed ridges. Walking poles are valuable on the long descent sections.
A good forecast window of 3–4 days is needed for the high sections; the
Karwendel's position north of the Alpine main chain means it catches both
northern weather systems and the periodic foehn from the south, with rapid
transition between conditions.

Hut reservations on the Adlerweg are essential from July onwards. The
Falkenhütte and Karwendelhaus both fill weeks in advance on fine-weather
summer weekends. The shoulder season — late May to mid-June and September —
offers better availability, fewer crowds on the trail, and the autumn
larch-colour turning that makes the Karwendel particularly photogenic in
the first weeks of October.

## The Arlberg end section

The western end of the Adlerweg at St. Anton am Arlberg provides a
remarkable contrast to the wild Karwendel start. St. Anton is one of
Austria's great ski resorts in winter and a lively summer hiking centre
in the walking season, with the full infrastructure of an international
mountain resort. The Darmstädter Hütte (2384 m) in the Verwall above
St. Anton — the last high hut before the trail descends to the valley —
sits in quieter terrain than the resort itself, and the final day's walk
down from the Verwall through the Rosanna valley to the Arlberg railway
junction provides a fitting end to the traverse.

The ÖAV section huts along this final stretch are less crowded than the
famous Karwendel huts and offer a more genuinely local experience: day-
tripping Austrians rather than international Adlerweg walkers, and the
chance to hear the specific Vorarlberg dialect that distinguishes this
western end of Tyrol from the rest of the province.
