## The Via Alpina as a System

The Via Alpina is not a single trail but a network of five long-distance routes designed to traverse the entire Alpine arc from its eastern end in Slovenia to its western and southern ends in Monaco, Nice, and Trieste. The project was formalised in 2002 and involves the cooperation of eight countries — Slovenia, Austria, Liechtenstein, Germany, Switzerland, France, Italy, and Monaco — whose national alpine organisations waymarked and maintain their respective segments.

The five routes — designated by colour as Red, Blue, Yellow, Green, and Purple — intersect at multiple points and share stretches of trail, which makes it possible to combine segments, switch between routes at trail junctions, and design custom itineraries that use the Via Alpina as a framework rather than a rigid prescription. Understanding each route individually is the starting point.

## The Red Route: The Grand Traverse

The Red Route is the flagship — 2500 kilometres from Triglav, the highest peak in Slovenia, to Monaco on the Mediterranean coast. It is the longest of the five, crossing all eight nations and requiring roughly 90 walking days for a continuous thru-hike. The route passes through Triglav National Park, the Carnic Alps, the Stubai and Ötztal ranges of Tyrol, the Allgäu Alps, the Bernese Oberland, the Valais, the Mont Blanc massif, the Mercantour, and the Ligurian Alps.

The Red Route does not aim for the highest terrain on every section — it seeks continuous connectivity between the major alpine regions, which sometimes routes it through valley towns and lower passes to maintain a practical daily stage structure. Accommodation ranges from staffed ÖAV and SAC huts at high elevations to village hotels and pensions in the valleys.

## The Blue Route: Austria and the Eastern Alps

The Blue Route runs approximately 660 kilometres through Austria, from Trieste in Italy to Salzburg. It traverses the Julian Alps, the Carnic Alps, the Karawanken, and the Dachstein massif before finishing near Salzburg. The Blue Route is the most Austria-centric of the five, spending the majority of its length in the ÖAV hut network and offering a dense concentration of staffed huts spaced at convenient day-stage intervals.

The Blue Route is a strong choice for walkers whose primary interest is in the eastern Alps and who want to complete a substantial traverse without committing to a multi-month undertaking. Many walkers combine the Blue and Red routes to create a custom circuit.

## The Yellow Route: Bavaria and the German Alps

The Yellow Route covers approximately 490 kilometres from Lindau on Lake Constance through the Bavarian Alps to Berchtesgaden, traversing the Allgäu, the Zugspitze region, the Bavarian highlands, and the Berchtesgadener Land. It is the most Germany-focused route and passes through or near popular areas including Garmisch-Partenkirchen, the Karwendel, and the Königssee.

The Yellow Route is accessible to a wide range of fitness levels — it uses the dense network of DAV huts in Bavaria, which are particularly well-maintained and well-booked in summer, and crosses terrain that is challenging but rarely technical in the alpine sense. The section over the Zugspitze massif is the technical high point.

## The Green Route: Switzerland End-to-End

The Green Route runs roughly 650 kilometres from Vaduz in Liechtenstein to Ventimiglia on the French-Italian border, traversing Switzerland almost entirely. It passes through the Graubünden, the Ticino, and the Valais, crossing high passes including the Albula, the Maloja, and several Valais cols above 2800 metres. The Green Route uses SAC huts more extensively than any other route in the network.

Switzerland's consistent trail infrastructure, reliable signage, and predictable hut quality make the Green Route one of the most comfortable long-distance traverses in the Alps. It is also the most expensive — SAC hut prices and Swiss costs generally mean the Green Route is a significant budget commitment. The compensation is the quality and reliability of every night's accommodation.

## The Purple Route: Southwestern Alps and France

The Purple Route covers approximately 460 kilometres from Menton (near Monaco) on the Mediterranean coast through the Alpes-Maritimes and the Écrins to Chamonix. It traverses the Mercantour National Park, the Écrins massif (including approaches to the Barre des Écrins at 4102 metres), the Belledonne range, and the Mont Blanc massif.

The Purple Route is the most diverse in character — it begins at near-sea-level, climbs through Mediterranean scrub and limestone terrain in the Mercantour, enters serious alpine terrain in the Écrins, and finishes in the glaciated high Alps around Chamonix. It is the strongest choice for walkers who want the full altitudinal range of the Alps in a single route.

## Connecting and Combining the Routes

The intersection points between routes — marked on official Via Alpina maps as junction nodes — allow multi-route itineraries. The most natural combination links the Red and Green routes at their shared Valais section, creating a loop through Switzerland and France. The Red and Blue routes share their start point at Triglav and diverge toward different Austrian valleys, allowing an east-to-west Walker to choose between the two.

The Via Alpina website maintains an official stage planner broken into day stages with accommodation recommendations for each. The stages are designed for walkers averaging 20 to 25 kilometres per day; strong hikers typically cover two stages in one day, while acclimatising or weather-delayed walkers may split a stage across two days.

## Practical Booking and Seasonal Considerations

The Red Route is the only one that runs through all eight nations, which means it requires the most complex booking strategy — five or more national club platforms or direct hut phone calls, depending on the segment. July and August are the peak months on all routes; the Swiss sections of the Green Route and the Mont Blanc sections of the Purple Route are the most heavily booked and require reservations four to six weeks in advance.

Most Via Alpina routes are viable from late June to late September, with the Slovenian and lower Austrian sections accessible from mid-May. Some high passes on the Red and Green routes may require ice axe and crampons in early June if snowpack has been heavy.
