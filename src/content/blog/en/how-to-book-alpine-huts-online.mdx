Online booking for Alpine huts has improved significantly since the mid-2010s but remains fragmented across four main platforms that share no common interface, have different cancellation policies, and handle membership discounts in different ways. Understanding how each system works before you sit down to book a multi-day tour will save you from the frustration of discovering on a Thursday evening that the DAV system requires a member number you do not have, or that the SAC platform only shows rooms in Swiss francs with a non-refundable deposit. This guide walks through the four main club booking systems in practical detail.

## The DAV Hüttenbuchung System

The Deutscher Alpenverein runs its reservation platform at huetten.dav.de. The platform covers the 322 DAV-owned huts and a growing number of huts owned by affiliated clubs in South Tyrol. To book at member rates — typically 50 percent less than the non-member (Nichtmitglieder) price — you need your DAV membership number, which is printed on your membership card and in your online DAV profile at alpenverein.de.

The booking flow works as follows. Enter your travel dates and select your destination hut, either by searching by name or by browsing a map view. The calendar view shows available nights highlighted in green, partially booked nights in yellow, and full nights in red. Select your arrival date, then choose bed type — Matratzenlager, shared room (Mehrbettzimmer), or private room (Einzelzimmer or Doppelzimmer) — and enter the number of guests. On the next screen you will see the price breakdown with and without the member discount.

Log in with your DAV member credentials or create a guest account. The platform will ask for dietary restrictions (relevant for dinner bookings), approximate arrival time, and whether you want to book the half-board dinner. Half-board at DAV huts is not automatic — many huts offer it but it must be selected explicitly. Pay by credit card or PayPal. You will receive a confirmation email with a booking reference number; this number is your only proof of reservation and should be saved in an offline backup, because mobile signal is unreliable on most approach routes.

## The SAC HutBooking Platform

The Swiss Alpine Club uses a separate system at sac-cas.ch/en/huts. It covers 152 SAC huts, the majority of them in Switzerland but including a few in the French and Italian border zones. SAC membership from any nationality receives the member rate; holders of DAV, ÖAV, or CAI membership pay a slightly higher affiliated-member rate that is still considerably cheaper than the full non-member price.

Booking on the SAC platform requires creating an account with an email address. Non-SAC members can book but must enter their home club's membership number to receive the reciprocity discount. The platform operates in German, French, Italian, and English; language selection at the top of the page persists throughout the session. Payment is in Swiss francs regardless of your home country; the platform accepts Mastercard, Visa, and Twint.

One specific feature of the SAC system that catches new users: the dinner booking is separate from the bed booking and handled in a second confirmation step. After your bed reservation is confirmed, the system prompts you to select dinner preferences and confirm the dinner booking before finalising. If you close the browser before completing this second step, the dinner is not booked, even though the bed is. Some huts will accommodate a dinner request made on arrival if space allows; others are strict about advance booking.

## The ÖAV Online Reservation

The Österreichischer Alpenverein's booking system lives at alpenverein.at/huetten. It covers the 231 ÖAV huts and uses an interface that closely resembles the DAV platform — both were developed in collaboration and share backend infrastructure. ÖAV members book at the Mitglieder rate; DAV and SAC members at the Partnerklub rate.

The ÖAV system has one notable difference from the DAV version: many huts list their own contact email address on the hut detail page, and for huts with fewer than 20 beds, the platform recommends contacting the hut directly by email rather than booking online, since the small huts update their calendars manually and the online availability may lag behind the actual situation by a day or two. This is less a flaw than an honest acknowledgment of the limits of centralised systems in a network of very small, family-run operations.

Cancellation policy on the ÖAV platform is standardised at no charge up to seven days before arrival, 50 percent of the bed fee between seven days and 48 hours, and the full bed fee for cancellations within 48 hours or no-shows. This policy applies uniformly across all huts in the ÖAV system.

## Booking CAI Huts Through Section Websites

The Club Alpino Italiano does not operate a single centralised booking platform. Instead, responsibility is distributed across more than 500 local sections, each of which manages its own huts. The practical result is that booking a CAI rifugio requires first identifying which section owns it, then finding that section's website, then either using their local online form or sending an email directly to the custode (hut warden).

Some sections — particularly in the South Tyrol, the Veneto, and Trentino — have built functional online booking portals that accept credit card payment and send automated confirmations. Many others still operate on a system of phone reservation followed by payment on arrival. The main CAI portal at cai.it provides a searchable hut directory with each hut's section affiliation and a link to the relevant booking contact, which is the most reliable starting point.

For the most popular CAI rifugi — Auronzo, Locatelli, Vajolet, and similar high-traffic huts — booking opens in January for the following summer and fills quickly. Weekend beds in August at Rifugio Auronzo are typically gone by February. The workaround is to book weekday nights or to arrive on foot for a day visit and use the rifugio for lunch rather than an overnight, freeing the beds for those who have made the logistical investment of a full trek.

## Booking Huts Not Covered by Club Systems

A number of Alpine huts — particularly those in France, Spain, and the Balkans — are operated privately or by national parks rather than by the major clubs and do not appear on any of the four platforms above. These are typically bookable through Refuges.info (covering France and parts of Spain), through the relevant national park's own website, or directly by phone or email. The French system at refuges.info is the most polished alternative platform, with real-time availability for more than 3,000 shelters and a clean mobile interface.

For huts across the full Alpine arc and beyond, [the interactive map at /map](/map) provides contact details and booking links for each hut in the database. When you identify a hut you want to stay at, the map entry shows which club or operator manages it and provides the direct link to their booking system, so you spend less time searching and more time planning.

## Practical Tips for Avoiding Booking Problems

Book as far in advance as the relevant platform allows — typically six months for most DAV and ÖAV huts, and the full calendar year for SAC huts. If you are planning a fixed-date tour in July or August on a well-known route like the Haute Route, the Tour du Mont Blanc, or the Zillertaler Runde, treat the booking as urgent from the moment your travel dates are confirmed. Flexibility on arrival day by 24 hours nearly always unlocks options that a fixed date exhausts. And regardless of platform, always carry a printed or screenshots copy of your confirmation: at a hut with no mobile signal, a PDF in your phone's offline storage is the only evidence you have.
