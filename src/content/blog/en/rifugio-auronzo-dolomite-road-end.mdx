You can reach almost no famous alpine hut by car, and that is precisely what makes Rifugio Auronzo unusual. A toll road climbs in tight hairpins from Lago d'Antorno to a broad gravel terrace at 2,320 metres, and there, with a car park spilling out beneath it, sits the long stone-and-render building that most visitors to the Tre Cime di Lavaredo see first. Behind it rise the three monumental towers themselves, their south faces catching the morning light in shades of grey and amber. The rifugio is the trailhead, the snack bar and the safety net for one of the most photographed mountain landscapes on earth, and on a July weekend it can feel like the busiest balcony in the Dolomites.

## Where the road ends and the loop begins

Rifugio Auronzo stands at the southern foot of the Tre Cime, on the saddle that separates the Auronzo valley from the high basins of the Sesto (Sexten) Dolomites. The toll road from Misurina is the only motorised approach, and it deposits hikers within a few minutes' walk of the start of the great loop. From the terrace the path contours east, almost level, toward the small chapel and then Rifugio Lavaredo, before climbing gently to the Forcella Lavaredo, the notch where the famous north faces suddenly reveal themselves. Most people walk the circuit anticlockwise, returning past the Langalm meadows and the Rifugio Auronzo car park three to four hours later.

What the rifugio offers is access without the long approach march that most Dolomite huts demand. That is a double-edged gift: it democratises a spectacular landscape, but it also concentrates crowds. Arriving before eight in the morning, or staying overnight, transforms the experience entirely.

## The three towers above

The Tre Cime di Lavaredo, known in German as the Drei Zinnen, are the Cima Grande (2,999 metres), Cima Ovest and Cima Piccola. Their sheer north walls are among the most storied in the history of climbing; the 1933 ascent of the Cima Grande north face by Emilio Comici remains a landmark of Dolomite alpinism. From Rifugio Auronzo you see the less dramatic south side, but the loop delivers you, within an hour, to the foot of those vertical faces and to the wartime trenches and tunnels that scar the surrounding ridges, relics of the front line that ran through here during the First World War.

## A rifugio built for volume

Rifugio Auronzo belongs to the CAI section of Auronzo di Cadore and is one of the larger huts in the eastern Dolomites, with bunkrooms and private rooms, a big self-service hall and a sunny terrace. Because it is reachable by road it functions partly as a mountain restaurant, serving canederli, polenta and strudel to day visitors who never lace up a boot. But it also shelters genuine trekkers: those walking the Alta Via 4, climbers heading for the via ferrata routes on the towers, and anyone continuing deeper toward the Rifugio Pian di Cengia and the Sesto high country. Booking ahead for an overnight stay is essential in summer.

## A hub for longer journeys

For walkers who want more than the standard circuit, Auronzo is a gateway rather than a destination. The path system fans out toward Rifugio Locatelli (Dreizinnenhütte), whose terrace gives the textbook postcard view of the north faces, and onward into the wild Fiscalina valley. Hut-to-hut trekkers crossing the Dolomites string together refuges such as the Rifugio Carota above the Comelico, the Rifugio Dolomieu al Dolada on the Alpago ridge, and the simpler Posto Tappa lodgings that punctuate the Alta Via routes. Auronzo is the busy, accessible anchor at one end of that quieter network.

## Crowds, cars and the future

The very accessibility that built Auronzo's popularity has become a problem. On peak days thousands of vehicles grind up the toll road, and the authorities of Auronzo and Misurina have repeatedly debated shuttle buses, vehicle caps and timed entry to protect the fragile plateau. Visitors who care about the place can do their part by arriving early, using public transport from Cortina d'Ampezzo or Dobbiaco where possible, and treating the meadows and the war remains with respect. The Dolomites became a UNESCO World Heritage site in 2009 partly because of landscapes exactly like this one.

## When to go and what to expect

The toll road opens once the snow has cleared, usually from late spring to October, and the hut operates through the warm months. July and August bring the most reliable weather and the worst crowds; late September offers crisp air, golden larches and far fewer people, though afternoon storms can arrive without much warning at this altitude. Even in midsummer the temperature on the terrace can drop sharply when cloud rolls in off the Sesto peaks, so a warm layer and waterproofs belong in every pack. The full Tre Cime loop is around ten kilometres with modest ascent, well within reach of fit families, but the thin air at 2,300 to 2,400 metres still asks for an easy pace.

## Beyond the standard circuit

If the classic loop leaves you wanting more, the area rewards exploration. The via ferrata routes on the towers and the surrounding ridges range from straightforward to serious and demand proper equipment and experience. The wider Sesto Dolomites hold quieter trails toward the Croda dei Toni and the Comelico, and the Cadore valleys below preserve a string of modest, family-run refuges and Posto Tappa shelters where the rhythm of a true hut-to-hut journey reasserts itself. Rifugio Auronzo may be the easiest hut in the Dolomites to reach, but it is also a door into some of the range's wildest corners.
