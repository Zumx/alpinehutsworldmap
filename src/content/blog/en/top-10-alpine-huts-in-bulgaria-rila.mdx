Bulgaria's mountains are among the least-known in Europe for foreign visitors
and among the most rewarding for those who seek them out. The Rila Mountains
contain the highest peak in the Balkans — Musala (2925 m) — and a remarkable
concentration of glacial lakes above 2000 m that form the centrepiece of the
national park. The Pirin Mountains to the southwest add marble peaks and a
more rugged character. The Bulgarian term for mountain hut is hizha (хижа),
and the network of huts across Rila and Pirin provides infrastructure for
multi-day walking that is far less crowded than the Alps.

## 1. Hizha Rilski Ezera (Seven Lakes Hut), 2200 m — Rila National Park

The Seven Rila Lakes (Sedemte Rilski Ezera) is the single most famous natural
attraction in Bulgaria, a group of glacial lakes at 2100–2500 m forming a
cirque of extraordinary beauty. The hizha at 2200 m serves the crowds of
summer visitors who ascend by chairlift from Panichishte and by foot from
the Rilski Monastery. The overnight stay, rather than the day visit, provides
the lakes in their early-morning and late-evening solitude. Capacity around
80; open June to October.

## 2. Hizha Maljovica, 1870 m — Maljovica area, Rila

Below the Maljovica peak (2729 m) in the northern Rila, the hizha provides
the base for one of the finest summit routes in Bulgaria — the Maljovica
ridge to the summit involves dramatic rocky terrain and views across the entire
Rila massif. Capacity around 70; the approach from Maljovica village takes
approximately 2.5 hours. The hizha has a good reputation for food among the
Bulgarian mountain community.

## 3. Hizha Musala, 2389 m — Musala massif, Rila

The highest situated staffed hizha in Bulgaria, at 2389 m on the slopes below
Musala (2925 m — the highest peak in Bulgaria and the Balkans outside the
Caucasus). The standard summit route passes near the hizha on the ascent from
the Yastrebets cable car above Borovets ski resort. Overnight capacity around
50. The summit views from Musala on a clear day take in most of the Balkan
Peninsula's major ranges.

## 4. Hizha Skakavica, 1960 m — Rila National Park, northwestern sector

In the western Rila sector, the Skakavica hizha sits near the impressive
Skakavitsa waterfall and serves as a staging point for routes into the high
terrain of the Stob Pyramids and the Beli Iskar valley. Less visited than the
Seven Lakes or Musala areas, offering a quieter Rila experience. Capacity
around 40.

## 5. Hizha Vada (хижа Вада), 1900 m — Rila, Iskar valley headwaters

Near the headwaters of the Iskar river, the Vada hizha provides access to
some of the most remote terrain in the Rila Mountains — the high plateau
country west of the main Musala ridge. Multi-day routes connecting Vada with
the Seven Lakes area and the Ribni Ezera (Fish Lakes) make it a key hut for
serious traverse walkers.

## 6. Hizha Bezbog, 2236 m — Pirin Mountains

In the northern Pirin above Dobrinishte, the Bezbog hizha is reached by the
Bezbog cable car (July–August operation) and sits in the glacial cirque below
Bezbog peak (2882 m). The surrounding Pirin terrain — marble formations, glacial
lakes, and the distinctive silvery rock of the Pirin peaks — is unlike the
Rila's granite. The approach to Vihren (2914 m — the highest Pirin peak) begins
from here. Capacity around 80.

## 7. Hizha Vihren, 1950 m — Pirin National Park, Bansko area

The primary base for ascents of Vihren (2914 m) and the UNESCO World Heritage
Pirin massif, the Vihren hizha sits at 1950 m above the Bansko ski resort area.
The hut is accessible by the Bansko gondola cable car and then a 2-hour walk,
making it one of the most easily reached high huts in Bulgaria. Capacity around
70; popular in both summer and winter (Bansko is Bulgaria's main ski resort).

## 8. Hizha Yavorov, 1828 m — Pirin Mountains, southern sector

Named after the Bulgarian symbolist poet Peyo Yavorov (who spent time in the
Pirin mountains), the Yavorov hizha serves the southern Pirin traverses and
the approach to the Sinanitsa lakes. Quieter than the Vihren-area huts, with
a characterful stone building in a high meadow setting.

## 9. Hizha Ambaritsa (х.Амбарица), 1560 m — Central Balkan Range

In the Central Balkan National Park — not the Rila or Pirin but the ancient
folded mountains of the Balkan range running east-west across Bulgaria — the
Ambaritsa hizha is one of the finest starting points for traverses along the
Balkan main ridge. The Central Balkan range is lower than the Rila (reaching
2376 m at Botev Peak) but more remote and wilder in character.

## 10. Hizha Kartala, 1895 m — Vitosha Mountain, Sofia

The Vitosha massif rises immediately to the south of Sofia — one of the few
European capitals with a national park within its city limits. The Kartala
hizha at 1895 m, below the Cherni Vrah summit (2290 m), is the most visited
mountain hut in Bulgaria by sheer visitor numbers — accessible by bus from
Sofia central and on foot in 3–4 hours. An evening walk from the city to
Kartala and back is a Sofia ritual.

## The Bulgarian hizha system

Bulgaria has approximately 340 mountain hizhi (хижи) distributed across the
country's four principal ranges: the Rila, Pirin, Rhodopes, and Balkans.
They are operated by a combination of the Bulgarian Tourist Union (BTS),
regional tourist clubs, and private operators. The standard is simpler than
Austrian or Swiss huts — bunk dormitories, basic hot meals, communal
facilities — but the prices are significantly lower. A night in a Bulgarian
hizha typically costs BGN 25–40 (approximately EUR 12–20) per person
including dinner and breakfast.

Bulgarian mountain culture has roots in the late 19th-century national
revival movement; the founding of the first tourist clubs in the Rila in
the 1890s was connected to the broader movement for national cultural
identity and independence. The mountains were not just recreational
destinations but symbols of Bulgarian landscape heritage — a significance
that still colours the culture of the hizhi today.

## Access and practical notes

The Rila and Pirin ranges are served by road connections from Sofia (the
Rila Monastery valley is 110 km from the capital) and the regional centres
of Blagoevgrad and Bansko. Most major hizhi are reachable by trail from
road-accessible starting points in 2–4 hours. The Borovets and Bansko ski
resorts provide winter-season access to the lower sections of the trail
network; in summer, the entire network is accessible without technical
equipment on most routes.

The Seven Rila Lakes area is served by a cable car from the monastery
guesthouse complex — the most visited section and quite crowded in July and
August. The less-visited western Rila around Maljovitsa is considerably
quieter and has equally rewarding terrain. For those seeking solitude, the
Pirin granite — the more dramatic and less visited range — offers some of
the finest ridge walking in Bulgaria with fewer crowds and a higher sense
of remoteness.
