South Tyrol is the cultural borderland where Italian and Austrian mountain
traditions meet — German-speaking, Tyrolean in character, Italian in
administration since 1919. The Ortler massif (3905 m, the highest peak in the
Eastern Alps outside Austria) rises from this ambiguous geography, and the
multi-day ski-touring circuit around it — the Ortler Skirunde — is one of the
most varied and dramatic ski-touring routes in the Alps.

## The route

The Ortler Skirunde is not a fixed, signposted route but a tradition: a
circumnavigation of the Ortler massif over three to five days, using a
combination of staffed huts and glacier camps, linking the Italian ski resorts
of Sulden (Solda) and Bormio via the high passes of the Livrio, Cedeh, and
Monte Pasquale glaciers.

The classic starting point is Sulden (Solda) in the Vinschgau, at 1900 m.
From here, the first day ascends via the Suldenfernergletscher to the huts
in the upper basin — typically the Schaubach Hütte (2581 m) or the higher
glacier camps — and sets up the first high crossing.

The crossing into the Italian Val Furva involves descending to the Rifugio
Pizzini-Frattola (2700 m), a classic mountain hut at the foot of the Vioz
glacier. The Rifugio Pizzini is one of the finest working huts in the Alps:
a stone and timber building that has been continuously operated since the
early 20th century, serving skiers and mountaineers in a valley that retains
its traditional character. Beds for approximately 80 guests; full meal service.

From the Rifugio Pizzini, the route continues via the Colle della Forcola and
the Plaghera glacier — challenging terrain with significant crevasse hazard
in late spring — and crosses back toward the Ortler north side via the
Zufallhütte (2265 m) in the Martello Valley. The Zufallhütte is a large
staffed hut (ÖAV-affiliated, capacity around 100) that provides a warm and
well-provisioned rest point midway through the circuit.

## Difficulty and conditions

The Ortler Skirunde is rated red (demanding) in ski-touring terms: sustained
glacier travel with crevasse management, some sections at 35–40 degrees, and
the logistical complexity of a multi-day route with limited bail-out options
on the high sections. The technical crux is the Cedeh glacier crossing, which
can be heavily crevassed by late April and requires careful route-finding.

Optimal conditions: late February to mid-April, when the snowpack is consolidated
but not yet wet. By May, the lower valleys lose snow cover and the glacier
surfaces begin to soften, making the approach and descent sections significantly
more demanding.

Weather windows are crucial. The Ortler sits where Mediterranean and Atlantic
weather systems interact, and storms can arrive with less warning than in the
more exposed western Alps. A portable weather radio and daily consultation with
local alpine meteorological services is standard practice.

## The Ortler itself

The Ortler (3905 m) stands above all of this as the dominant peak of the
massif. The normal route from the Schaubach Hütte via the Hintergrat is rated
AD — a full day's undertaking on glacier and mixed terrain with some exposed
rock sections near the summit. Most Ortler Skirunde parties do not summit;
the circuit itself is the objective.

The Ortler holds historical significance as the highest point of the Habsburg
Empire (it sits now entirely in Italy but was Austrian territory through 1918)
and was climbed first in 1804 by Josef Pichler, a Vinschgau hunter who led
an Archduke's party to the top.

## Access and logistics

Sulden is reached by road from Merano or Schlanders in the Vinschgau. Car
access to the valley base is straightforward; parking at the lower Sulden cable
car station is available for multi-day trips.

Val Furva and the Rifugio Pizzini are accessible from Bormio, reached by road
from Tirano or via the Stelvio Pass (closed in winter, open from approximately
late May).

Hut reservations on the Ortler Skirunde are essential in the popular late-
February and March window. The Rifugio Pizzini and Zufallhütte both book out
weeks in advance on clear-weather weekends.

## Gear notes for the Ortler Skirunde

Standard ski-touring equipment applies: AT or pin bindings, climbing skins,
beacon-shovel-probe in all conditions. The Skirunde has a higher than average
crevasse exposure for a circuit route — rope and crevasse rescue equipment are
strongly recommended for the glacier crossings. A 30 m glacier rope (single
or half rope) with prussik loops per person is the minimum; a full 60 m rope
gives more flexibility for larger parties or more demanding conditions.

The Ortler Skirunde involves more vertical height than a typical weekend
day tour: cumulative daily elevation gains of 1200–1500 m are normal on the
crossing days, with corresponding descents. Fitness for sustained multi-day
ski touring is a prerequisite; the circuit is not an appropriate first multi-
day route for newcomers to hut-based ski touring.

A qualified UIAA/IFMGA mountain guide with Ortler area experience is strongly
recommended for parties without prior experience of the glacier systems
involved. Guides based in Sulden and Bormio are familiar with current condition
reports and can advise on route modifications based on the season's snowpack.

## The Stelvio connection

The circuit can be extended or varied via connections to the Stelvio National
Park terrain to the east and the Umbrail Pass area to the west. The Stelvio
Pass (Passo dello Stelvio, 2758 m) is the second-highest paved road pass
in the Alps and opens the Ortler approaches to the Livrio glacier area. In
summer, the Stelvio road is a cycling pilgrimage; in winter and spring, the
high terrain around it is prime ski-touring ground for those willing to
approach on foot from the valley.

## The Ortler's cultural position

The Ortler (3905 m) was the highest peak in the Austrian Empire when it was
first climbed in 1804 by Josef Pichler (Josele) under the direction of the
Austrian Archduke Johann — the same patron who financed early Alpine exploration
across the eastern Alps. Pichler, a local chamois hunter, was one of the first
generation of guides in the Tyrolean tradition. The Schaubach Hütte is named
for Adolf Schaubach, whose 1834 mountain description of the Eastern Alps was
one of the founding documents of recreational alpinism in Austria.

The Ortler area changed from Austrian to Italian sovereignty after World War I
(1919); the German and Italian bilingualism of the huts and the Ladin-culture
villages reflects this history. The Sulden/Solda and Stilfs/Stelvio valley
communities have maintained their South Tyrolean Alpine identity within the
Italian state — a cultural continuity that is part of what makes the Ortler
circuit feel distinctive from the purely Italian or purely Austrian Alps.

Hut bookings for the Ortler Skirunde should be made at least 2 months
in advance for the prime March–April ski touring season. The Rifugio
Pizzini-Frattola (CAI-managed) books through the rifugio directly by
phone or email (Italian); the Zufallhütte books through its own website.
Both fill for weekends well in advance of the optimal March window. The
weekday options within the same week as weekend bookings are typically
still available with shorter notice.
