Winter hut touring in the Alps is a different activity in almost every respect
from summer hut travel. The terrain is transformed by snow; the guardians
are gone; the routes require navigation by map and compass or GPS rather than
paint blazes; and the primary hazard is no longer afternoon thunderstorms but
avalanche. The huts exist in winter as Winterraum — basic, unstaffed sleeping
spaces that are the overnight infrastructure of ski mountaineering and ski
touring culture. This is a serious mountain discipline, not an accessible
extension of summer hiking.

## The Winterraum

When a staffed alpine hut closes for the winter season — typically between
late October and late May, depending on elevation and snowpack — it locks the
main building but leaves a smaller wing accessible as a Winterraum. This is
a basic dormitory with 6–20 bunks, heavy wool blankets, a wood or gas stove,
a sink (sometimes frozen), and sometimes a small stock of emergency provisions
left by the last guardian or by previous winter parties.

Access to the Winterraum requires collecting a key from the nearest valley
before departure. This is typically arranged through the local alpine club
section — ÖAV, DAV, or SAC — by phone or email. Key collection locations vary:
a hotel in the village, a club section office, or a key box with a combination
provided on booking. Some huts use a universal member key that opens all
Winterraum of the managing club; confirm which system applies before setting out.

Do not arrive at a hut in winter expecting a Winterraum to be unlocked. Doing
so in poor conditions is the most reliable path to a serious survival situation.

## Avalanche transceiver standard

Every member of a winter touring party wears an avalanche transceiver
(Avalanche Rescue Beacon, LVS-Gerät) switched to transmit before leaving the
valley. Not in the pack — worn on the body, under the first layer, so it remains
accessible and cannot be separated from the carrier in a burial. The standard
frequency is 457 kHz; all modern transceivers are compatible.

Each party member also carries: a snow probe (240 cm minimum), a collapsible
avalanche shovel with a metal blade (not a lightweight plastic camping shovel),
and has been trained in organized companion rescue. The 15-minute survival
window — after which probability of survival in a burial drops steeply — makes
organized companion rescue the only technique that matters. External rescue,
regardless of helicopter response times, typically arrives after the window.

## The 3×3 Method

The 3×3 method is the standard decision-making framework for avalanche risk
assessment in ski touring, developed by Werner Munter and now taught in all
major avalanche safety courses (ÖAV, DAV Lawinen-Sicherheits-Training, SNL).

The three scales are: the regional scale (current avalanche danger rating,
1–5 on the European scale), the local scale (specific slope angle, aspect,
and elevation zone), and the zonal scale (the individual decision point
whether to enter a specific slope).

The European Avalanche Danger Scale: 1 (low), 2 (limited), 3 (considerable),
4 (high), 5 (very high). Scale 3 is where most avalanche accidents occur —
the danger is serious but the weather is often good, encouraging parties to
proceed. The rule applied by professional mountain guides: at scale 3,
significant terrain steeper than 35 degrees is off-limits unless the
snowpack stability has been locally verified by digging and testing.
At scale 4 or 5: touring is not justifiable.

## Classic winter routes

The Haute Route on skis (Chamonix to Zermatt, typically 7 days in April) is
the most famous ski touring route in the Alps and uses the same hut chain as
the summer Walker's Haute Route — Cabane du Mont Fort, Cabane de Prafleuri,
Cabane des Dix, Cabane des Vignettes, Cabane de Bertol, Schönbielhütte — but
on skis across terrain that is extensively glaciated and consistently exposed
above 3000 m. This route is done entirely in Winterraum or staffed spring
conditions (some huts open earlier for the ski touring season). A guide is
strongly recommended for the first traverse.

The Ortler Skirunde in South Tyrol (the ski touring circuit of the Ortler
group, typically 6–7 days) uses huts in both winter room and spring-staffed
configuration. The Ortler (3905 m) is the highest peak in South Tyrol and
the circuit's terrain is serious — sustained glacier approaches and exposed
ridges are standard throughout.

Shorter circuits in the Bernese Oberland (Lauteraarhorn circuit, Wildhorn
crossing), the Silvretta (Silvretta Runde), and the Adamello range provide
two-to-four-day winter touring options with established Winterraum chains.

## Food provisioning in winter

Most Winterraum are self-service: no guardian, no kitchen, no meals. This
means carrying all food for the duration of the tour. The provisioning
calculation: approximately 700–900 calories per hour of ski touring activity,
for parties moving 5–8 hours per day. Three to four days of food at that
caloric density — using freeze-dried meals, nuts, hard cheese, salami, energy
bars, and chocolate — typically weighs 1.5–2.5 kg per person.

Some huts leave an honesty box and a small supply of emergency provisions
(instant soup, oatmeal, canned goods, tea). These are emergency reserves,
not a reliable food source. Carry your full ration regardless.

Stove and gas: a lightweight canister stove (MSR, Primus, Jetboil) plus two
230g canisters per person for three days is the standard load. Cold
temperatures reduce canister output — keep the canister warm in a sleeping bag
or jacket pocket before use.

## Water

Below freezing, spring water is unavailable. Above freezing, running water
at a Winterraum may or may not function depending on the specific plumbing.
The reliable winter water source is snow. Snow-to-water yield is approximately
10:1 by volume — melting a full 1-litre pot of packed snow produces around
100 ml of water. Plan accordingly and start melting early.

## Navigation in winter conditions

Trail blazes are buried under snow. Landscape features — ridgelines, rock
outcrops, valley floors — are transformed by a winter snowpack of 1–3 m.
Navigation in winter requires: a 1:25,000 or 1:50,000 paper map with annotated
waypoints, a compass, a GPS device with the route loaded before departure, and
the skill to correlate terrain with map in flat light and whiteout conditions.

The SAC Swisstopo app with offline layers downloaded, or a dedicated GPS device
with loaded routes, is the practical tool. Relying on phone GPS without offline
maps is not adequate in winter conditions where battery life is halved by cold.

## The ski touring guide — when to hire one

Winter glacier touring requires a level of avalanche terrain assessment and
navigation competence that is genuinely different from summer hiking. For
parties without prior off-piste skiing experience in avalanche terrain, a
qualified IFMGA mountain guide is not optional — it is the correct safety
provision. The Haute Route on skis is consistently recommended as a guided
first experience precisely because the guide provides the avalanche decision-
making, the navigation across featureless glacier terrain in cloud, and the
technical security on the steep sections before the Cabane des Vignettes and
the Schönbiel.

IFMGA guides for winter ski touring are available through the national guide
associations: the Bergführerverband (Austria, Germany), Bureau des Guides
(France, Chamonix), and the Swiss Mountain Guide Association. For the Haute
Route ski traverse, a 7-day engagement with a guide for a party of 2–3 typically
costs CHF 3,000–4,500 for the guide's fee, with hut costs additional.

Self-guided winter touring at the appropriate level — two-day circuits in the
Silvretta, three-day Bernese Oberland crossings for experienced ski tourers —
is a reasonable objective for parties who have completed an avalanche training
course (ÖAV or DAV LST level 1) and have experience on off-piste and touring
terrain below 35 degrees.

## Ski touring gear and the Winterraum overnight

The gear requirements for a winter Winterraum stay are substantially greater
than for a summer staffed hut night. A 0-degree sleeping bag (compressed to
a manageable volume in a -5 degrees Celsius dormitory) is the minimum; a
-10 degree bag provides meaningful comfort margin. The heavy wool blankets
provided in most Winterraum supplement but do not replace a good bag.

Heating a Winterraum typically requires finding, chopping (if a wood supply
is stacked outside), and managing a wood stove over several hours. The
hut is rarely warm on arrival — plan on an hour of stove management before
the interior temperature becomes comfortable. Some huts have gas heaters
with cartridge supply left by the guardian; confirm this before departure.
