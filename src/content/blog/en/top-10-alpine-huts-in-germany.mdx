The German Alpine Club (Deutscher Alpenverein, DAV) operates the largest hut
network in the Alps by membership and one of the most maintained in terms of
trail signage, SAR coordination, and infrastructure investment. The Bavarian
Alps are lower and more accessible than the central Swiss or French ranges, but
weather in the Karwendel and Wetterstein can be as dangerous as anywhere in
the Alps — afternoon thunderstorms between June and September are extremely
reliable and extremely fast. All ten huts below are accessible on the
[map](/map).

## 1. Watzmannhaus, 1930 m — Berchtesgaden Alps

The main staging hut for the Watzmann (2713 m), the third-highest peak in
Germany and one of the most famous ridge traverses in Bavarian climbing.
The Watzmannhaus sits at 1930 m above Berchtesgaden town, reached in about
2.5 hours from the Wimbachbrücke car park. Capacity around 450 — one of the
larger DAV huts — with a full restaurant, guardian staff year-round, and
both dormitory and private room options. The Watzmann Traverse requires a
guide or serious experience with exposed ridges.

## 2. Höllentalangerhütte, 1387 m — Wetterstein, Zugspitze

Halfway up the Höllental gorge on the direct approach to the Zugspitze
(2962 m) from Garmisch. The gorge section involves fixed iron rungs and a
short via-ferrata ladder — appropriate for fit hikers, not for young children
or anyone with vertigo. Capacity around 60, DAV-managed, with full meals.
The Zugspitze summit via the Höllentalferner glacier requires crampons, rope,
and glacier travel competence; the via ferrata Jubiläumsgrat route requires
full via-ferrata equipment. This hut is the gateway to both.

## 3. Knorrhütte, 2051 m — Wetterstein, Zugspitze

On the south side of the Zugspitze above the Reintal valley, the Knorrhütte
serves as the high camp for the Zugspitze normal route via the Zugspitzplatt
glacier. Reached from Garmisch via the Reintal — a long, gradual valley walk
of 5–6 hours. Capacity around 120. The Zugspitzplatt route from here is
glacier-covered until late summer; crampons and awareness of crevasse risk are
required. DAV-managed with full guardian service.

## 4. Reintalangerhütte, 1369 m — Wetterstein

Lower on the Reintal than the Knorrhütte, this hut serves as the first overnight
stop on the Garmisch–Zugspitze traverse. Capacity around 100, comfortable,
with a strong menu and a position at the valley floor ideal for acclimatization.
The Reintal is one of the longer valley approaches in Bavaria — 14 km from the
Garmisch trailhead — and vehicle access is not permitted, which keeps the
atmosphere quiet even in high season.

## 5. Karwendelhaus, 1765 m — Karwendel

The hub hut of the Karwendel range in Tyrol (the German Alpine Club routes
extend across the Austrian border). Reached from Scharnitz or from Pertisau
on the Achensee, the Karwendelhaus sits at the head of the Hinterautal valley.
It is the central overnight stop on the Karwendel Traverse (Herzogstand to
Achensee), one of the most popular multi-day routes in the northern Alps.
Capacity around 120, DAV Sektion München-managed.

## 6. Falkenhütte, 1848 m — Karwendel

On the eastern Karwendel above the Enger Ground valley, the Falkenhütte is the
base for the Laliderer Wände — a famous rock face attracting sport climbers
and alpinists. Less busy than the Karwendelhaus, with capacity around 120 and
a reputation for quieter evenings. Reached from Hinterriss in Austria in about
2.5 hours. The Karwendel Traverse passes through here on the northern route.

## 7. Berliner Hütte, 2042 m — Zillertal Alps

Technically in Austria but operated by the DAV Sektion Berlin and consistently
listed among the finest hut buildings in the Alps. The Berliner Hütte is a
late-19th-century structure with a formal dining room, mosaic floors, and the
atmosphere of a Viennese mountain palace. At 2042 m in the Zemmgrund valley,
reached from Ginzling in about 3 hours. It serves as a key stage on the
Berliner Höhenweg, a demanding multi-day circuit of the Zillertal.

## 8. Memminger Hütte, 2242 m — Lechtal Alps

On the Lechtal Traverse (Reutte to Landeck), one of the finest long-distance
routes in the northern Alps, the Memminger Hütte sits at 2242 m above the
Parseiertal. It is often cited as the best-positioned overnight stop on the
traverse, with panoramic views of the Zugspitze and the Allgäu ranges. Capacity
around 90. The DAV Sektion Memmingen-managed hut is guardian-staffed from June
to October.

## 9. Coburger Hütte, 1917 m — Zugspitz Arena, Heiterwand

Between the Zugspitze and the Zugspitz Arena near Ehrwald, the Coburger Hütte
is a well-placed overnight on approaches to the Maldongrat ridge and the
southern Wetterstein. Capacity around 90, with full meals and an exceptionally
well-stocked bar by alpine standards. Good base for day hikes on the
Austrian-German border ridge.

## 10. Hochalmhütte, 1350 m — Ammergauer Alps

One of the more accessible DAV huts in the Ammergauer Alps above
Oberammergau, the Hochalmhütte serves as an entry-level overnight introduction
to alpine hut culture for hikers based in Munich or Garmisch. At 1350 m it is
below the treeline and suitable for families. Capacity around 80. A useful
first hut experience before attempting higher or more demanding routes in the
Wetterstein or Karwendel.

## Notes on the Bavarian hut network

The DAV runs a highly standardized system: reservations through Alpsonline or
directly with each hut, member discounts of around €5–10 per night, and strong
safety culture on the trail network. Afternoon thunderstorms in the Bavarian
Alps are as consistent in summer as anywhere in the range — aim to be in the
hut or below the ridge by 14:00 on any day with building convection.

## The Berliner Hütte — architecture and character

The Berliner Hütte at 2042 m in the Zillertal Alps (Austria, DAV Sektion
Berlin) deserves a special note as a monument to the golden age of alpine hut
building. Constructed in 1879 and substantially expanded in 1900–1905, it is
among the most formally ambitious hut buildings in the Alps: a full three-
storey structure with a formal dining room, mosaic tile floors, hunting
trophies on the walls, and curtained windows that create an almost Viennese
interior. At 2042 m with a capacity of around 100, it operates as a full
half-board hut in season.

The Berliner Hütte is the centrepiece of the Berliner Höhenweg — a 7-day
circuit of the Zillertal Alps that is among the finest multi-day hut routes
in the Eastern Alps. The route is demanding (sustained T4–T5 terrain on the
high sections) but uses a chain of well-maintained DAV and ÖAV huts. Parties
with solid alpine experience who find the major Swiss and French circuits too
crowded consistently rate the Berliner Höhenweg as a better experience for
that reason.

## Access from Munich

The Bavarian Alps are uniquely positioned relative to a major European city:
the fastest approach from Munich to a DAV hut goes through Garmisch-
Partenkirchen (1 hour by train from Hauptbahnhof) and from there to the
Reintalangerhütte trailhead in another hour by road. No other major European
city has 4000 m of ascent to a serious DAV hut available within 3 hours of
the urban centre.

This accessibility defines the Bavarian hut culture. These huts are used
intensively by Munich weekenders, which means they are crowded on fine-
weather Saturdays and quiet on weekdays. Midweek visits to the Watzmannhaus
or Karwendelhaus in summer feel genuinely different from the weekend rush
and are the better choice for anyone with the schedule flexibility to use them.
