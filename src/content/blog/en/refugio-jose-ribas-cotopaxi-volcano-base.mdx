Few mountain huts in the world sit on the rim of an active volcano the way the Refugio José Ribas does. Perched at roughly 4,800 metres on the northern flank of Cotopaxi, just below where the rock gives way to glacier ice, the refuge is the staging post for one of the great non-technical high-altitude climbs on the planet. From its windows you look out across the Ecuadorian Avenue of the Volcanoes, and above you the near-perfect snow cone rises another 1,100 metres to a steaming crater at 5,897 metres. This is a working volcano, and the refuge has more than once stood empty when the mountain reawakened.

## A Volcano in the Avenue of the Volcanoes

Cotopaxi lies about 50 kilometres south of Quito within the Parque Nacional Cotopaxi, one of Ecuador's most visited protected areas. The German explorer Alexander von Humboldt attempted the mountain in 1802 and famously failed; it was the Bavarian-Ecuadorian climber Wilhelm Reiss, with the Colombian Ángel Escobar, who made the first ascent in 1872. The volcano has erupted many times in recorded history, with major events in the nineteenth century and a significant reawakening in 2015 that closed the summit to climbers for months. Even today, access depends on the alert level set by Ecuador's geophysical institute.

The wider park is a high paramo plateau of tussock grass, wild horses and the occasional Andean condor, ringed by other great peaks. From the refuge you can pick out Rumiñahui, the Ilinizas, and on clear mornings the distant cone of Chimborazo. It is a landscape that rewards a few days of acclimatisation before any summit attempt.

## The Refuge Itself

The Refugio José Ribas was first built in the early 1970s by the Nuevos Horizontes mountaineering club, and the refuge has been expanded and renovated several times since. It offers bunk-style dormitory sleeping for several dozen climbers, a communal dining area, a kitchen serving hot meals and the indispensable cups of coca or chamomile tea, and basic toilet facilities. There is no luxury here: water is limited, the air is thin, and the wind off the glacier finds every gap in the walls.

Reaching the refuge is deceptively easy by mountain standards. A rough road climbs to a car park at around 4,500 metres, and from there a steep, sandy trail of perhaps 45 minutes to an hour gains the last 300 metres of elevation to the hut. That short walk is the first real taste of the altitude, and many day visitors who only intend to see the refuge find themselves gasping well before they reach the door.

## Climbing Cotopaxi

The standard ascent of Cotopaxi is a glacier climb that requires crampons, ice axe, a rope and a harness, along with the skills to use them or a qualified guide who has them. It is not technically hard — there is no steep ice or rock — but it is a genuine alpine undertaking at altitude, on a glacier that has retreated and changed substantially in recent decades, opening crevasses and reshaping the route from season to season.

Summit day begins brutally early. Climbers typically rise around midnight, eat a small breakfast in the refuge, and set off by headlamp around 1 AM to climb the roughly 1,100 metres to the crater rim. The pre-dawn start is deliberate: the snow is firmest and safest while it is still frozen, and the goal is to be back below the glacier before the daytime sun softens the bridges over the crevasses. A round trip from the refuge commonly takes eight to ten hours.

## Acclimatisation Before You Go High

Cotopaxi should never be a climber's first encounter with altitude. The standard preparation in Ecuador is to spend several days ascending lesser peaks first. Many climbers warm up on the Rucu Pichincha above Quito, reached by the TelefériQo cable car, or on the Pasochoa and Rumiñahui. The twin Ilinizas, with their own refuge below the saddle, are a favourite acclimatisation objective, with the easier Iliniza Norte at 5,126 metres serving as an excellent test before committing to a glacier.

The body needs time to manufacture extra red blood cells, and there is no shortcut. A sensible itinerary might allow a week in Ecuador before the Cotopaxi attempt, sleeping progressively higher and watching carefully for the headaches and nausea that signal acute mountain sickness. Quito itself, at 2,850 metres, already starts the process the moment you land.

## Ecuador's Wider Refuge Network

Cotopaxi's hut sits within a broader Ecuadorian system of mountain refuges, several of which carry names that echo the country's climbing history. On Chimborazo, the Refugio Carrel and the higher Refugio Whymper honour the Italian guide and the British alpinist who first reached that summit. The Refugio del Volcán Pichincha serves the peaks above Quito, while the Tungurahua Refugio and the Refugio de Montaña Cotopaxi Cara Sur extend access to the southern and central volcanoes. Further into the Amazon foothills, places like the Cabañas Oyacachi and the eco-lodges around Sumaco show the range of mountain accommodation the country offers.

Together these refuges make Ecuador one of the most accessible high-altitude climbing destinations anywhere, with multiple 5,000-metre and near-6,000-metre summits reachable within a single trip from a base in Quito.

## Practical Notes and Seasons

Ecuador straddles the equator, so it has no true summer or winter; instead the climbing seasons are defined by the drier months. The most reliable windows on Cotopaxi are generally December to early February and June to August, though equatorial weather is fickle and a clear dawn can turn to whiteout by mid-morning. A guide is mandatory on the glacier within the national park, and entry, climbing fees and current volcanic alert status should all be checked before travelling.

Dress for genuine cold despite the equatorial latitude: a layered system, a windproof shell, warm gloves, goggles and category-4 glacier glasses are all essential above the refuge.
