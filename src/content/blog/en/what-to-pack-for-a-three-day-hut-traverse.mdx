The single greatest advantage of a hut-to-hut traverse over a self-supported wild camp is what you get to leave at home. No tent, no sleeping bag, no stove, no cooking pots, no fuel, no three days of food. A staffed Alpine hut provides a roof, a mattress, blankets, an evening meal, and breakfast, which strips kilograms from a pack and transforms a punishing carry into a comfortable day's walking. Yet hut traverses tempt walkers into the opposite error — over-packing for an experience that rewards restraint. A well-judged three-day hut pack should weigh somewhere between six and eight kilograms including water, and assembling it is an exercise in knowing exactly what the huts do and do not supply.

## What the Hut Provides, and What It Doesn't

Before packing a single item, understand the baseline a staffed hut gives you. It provides a sleeping place — a mattress in a shared dormitory, the Lager or Matratzenlager — with pillows and blankets or a duvet. It provides cooked meals: dinner, usually a hearty multi-course affair, and breakfast. It provides drinking water in most cases, though sometimes for a charge, and hot drinks for sale. It provides shelter, warmth in the common room, and basic toilets and washing facilities.

What it does not provide is a sheet. Almost every Alpine club requires guests to use a personal sleeping bag liner, a hüttenschlafsack, for hygiene over the shared blankets. This single item is non-negotiable: a silk or lightweight synthetic liner weighs little and is mandatory at DAV, SAC, and most CAI huts. The hut also will not lend you a towel, soap, a torch, or trekking poles, and it will not necessarily have a card machine. These gaps define the core of what you must carry.

## The Sleep System: A Liner, Not a Bag

This is where the weight savings begin. A full sleeping bag, the heaviest single item in most backpacking kits, is entirely redundant on a staffed hut traverse. In its place you carry only a sleeping bag liner. A silk liner weighs around 100-150 grams and packs to the size of an orange; a slightly warmer synthetic liner is a little heavier. Either satisfies the hygiene requirement and adds a touch of warmth on a cold night under the hut blankets.

The exception is a traverse that includes an unstaffed hut or winter room, where no blankets are provided. If a single night of your route falls in such a place, you may need a light sleeping bag for that night only — a reason to check the staffing status of every hut on the itinerary before deciding. For a fully staffed three-day route, the liner alone suffices.

## Clothing: The Layering Principle

Mountain weather changes fast, and the clothing system must handle sun, rain, wind, and near-freezing nights without bulk. The proven approach is three layers worn or carried: a base layer of merino or synthetic against the skin, an insulating mid-layer such as a light fleece or down jacket, and a waterproof, breathable shell jacket. Add waterproof overtrousers for serious rain, a warm hat and gloves even in summer at altitude, and a sun hat and sunglasses for the glare above the treeline.

For a three-day walk, carry one set of walking clothes worn each day and one set of dry clothes kept clean for the hut in the evening — a base layer and light trousers are enough. Resist packing a fresh outfit per day; the weight is real and the huts have no laundry. Spare socks matter more than spare shirts: two or three pairs, rotated and dried overnight on the hut's drying rack, prevent blisters and are worth their weight.

## Footwear, Poles, and Walking Hardware

Boots are the one item where weight should not be the deciding factor. A traverse on rocky, exposed alpine paths needs supportive footwear with a stiff sole and ankle support; broken-in boots prevent the blisters that can end a trip. Trekking poles, which the hut will not supply, save the knees on long descents and aid balance on scree and snow patches, and most walkers now consider them essential rather than optional.

Beyond the boots, a few small items earn their place: a headtorch for the early-morning alpine start and the dim hut corridors, a basic first-aid kit with blister plasters foremost, a map and compass or GPS, and a small repair kit with tape and cord. A pair of lightweight hut shoes or sandals is a genuine comfort, though many huts provide communal slippers (hüttenschuhe), so this is one item you can sometimes skip.

## Food, Water, and the Half-Board Logic

Because the huts cook dinner and breakfast, you carry food only for the trail — lunch and snacks for three days of walking. The sensible load is high-energy, low-bulk fare: nuts, dried fruit, energy bars, hard cheese, cured sausage, and perhaps bread bought at the first hut. Many huts sell packed lunches (a Lunchpaket) to take onward, which can replace some carried food and lighten the start.

For water, two strategies coexist: carry one to one-and-a-half litres and refill at huts and reliable springs, or carry more if the route is long and dry between sources. A water bottle or hydration bladder of around a litre and a half is usually enough on a well-hutted route. Carry some cash, in the right currency, for meals, drinks, and showers, since not every hut takes cards, and remember that drinks and snacks at altitude cost more than in the valley because everything was flown or carried up.

## The Small Essentials and What to Leave Behind

A handful of small things round out the kit: sunscreen and lip balm for fierce alpine UV, a personal toiletries bag kept minimal, earplugs for the snoring symphony of a shared dormitory, a phone with offline maps and a small power bank, and any personal medication. A lightweight dry bag keeps the sleep liner and dry clothes safe from rain inside the pack.

The discipline of a good hut pack lies as much in what stays home. Leave the tent, the sleeping bag, the stove and pot, the fuel, the heavy camera you will not use, the third book, and the just-in-case items that never come out of the pack. The huts exist precisely to make this lightness possible; packing as though they do not defeats the purpose and burdens every step of the traverse.
