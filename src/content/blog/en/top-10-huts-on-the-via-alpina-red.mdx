The Via Alpina is a network of five long-distance trails crossing the Alpine
arc, of which the Red Trail is the longest and most ambitious: a 161-stage
traverse of over 2600 kilometres from Trieste on the Adriatic coast of
Slovenia/Italy to Monaco on the Mediterranean. The full route crosses eight
countries — Slovenia, Italy, Austria, Germany, Liechtenstein, Switzerland,
France, and Monaco — and can be walked complete over a full season (typically
May to October) or in sections across multiple years. The hut network is the
backbone of the route.

## 1. Dom Planica, 897 m — Julian Alps, Slovenia

The Via Alpina Red enters Slovenia from Trieste and crosses the Julian Alps —
one of the least-visited but most beautiful sections of the entire traverse.
The Dom Planica (mountain lodge) in the Planica valley near the Austrian border
is one of the key overnight stops in the Slovene section, at 897 m below the
Triglav National Park peaks. The surrounding terrain of the Julian Alps rivals
the Dolomites in quality but receives a fraction of the visitors.

## 2. Koča pri Triglavskih jezerih, 1685 m — Triglav National Park, Slovenia

The mountain hut at the Triglav Lakes (Triglavska jezera) at 1685 m is the
most celebrated overnight in Slovene mountain tourism. Seven glacial lakes
above the treeline in the heart of Triglav National Park; capacity around 100.
The approach from the Bohinj valley side takes 3–4 hours. The Via Alpina
Red route here connects the Slovenian Julian Alps to the Carnic Alps and the
transition into Austria.

## 3. Obstansersee Hütte, 2304 m — Carnic Alps, Carinthia

In the Carnic Alps of southern Austria and northern Italy, the Obstansersee
Hütte at 2304 m sits beside a high mountain lake on the Austro-Italian border
ridge. This section of the Via Alpina traverses the historic Carnic Main Ridge
(Karnischer Höhenweg) — a World War I front line with preserved fortifications.
ÖAV-managed; capacity around 40.

## 4. Lienzer Hütte, 2145 m — Lienz Dolomites, Tirol

In the Lienz Dolomites — a southern Tyrolean limestone range less visited
than the main Dolomites — the Lienzer Hütte provides a key overnight on the
Via Alpina approach to the main Tiroler Alps. The Hochstadel (2681 m) above
is the highest peak in this range.

## 5. Barmer Hütte, 1620 m — Defereggen Alps, Tirol

In the Defereggen valley of East Tyrol, the Barmer Hütte serves the transition
section of the Via Alpina between the Carnic Alps and the main Ötztal approach.
The Defereggen Alps are a quiet range with good walking terrain and fewer
visitors than the more famous Tiroler ranges to the north.

## 6. Nürnberger Hütte, 2297 m — Stubai Alps, Tirol

The Nürnberger Hütte (see also the Stubai Höhenweg article) serves the Via
Alpina Red where it passes through the Stubai Alps section. The route here
overlaps with the Stubai Höhenweg circuit and provides a connection between
the eastern and western sections of the Tiroler Alps traverse.

## 7. Jamtalhütte, 2165 m — Silvretta, Vorarlberg

In the Silvretta range on the Vorarlberg-Graubünden border, the Jamtalhütte
(see also the Silvretta Hochalpenroute article) at 2165 m is a major Via
Alpina waypoint. The route crosses from Austria into Switzerland near here.

## 8. Chamanna da Tschierv, 1621 m — Engadine, Graubünden

In the Lower Engadine above the Münstertal valley, this SAC hut provides a
base for the traversal through the Swiss National Park — the oldest national
park in the Alps (established 1914) and a region where the landscape has been
allowed to develop without human intervention for over a century.

## 9. Refuge du Lac de Barberine, 1985 m — Bas-Valais, France-Switzerland border

On the traverse through the Franco-Swiss borderland above Martigny, this refuge
above the Lac de Barberine reservoir provides the overnight for the Via Alpina
Red approach toward the Mont Blanc massif's outer ridges.

## 10. Refuge du Grand Col Ferret, 2537 m — Mont Blanc massif, France-Italy border

Near the Grand Col Ferret (2537 m) — the border crossing between the French
and Italian sides of the Mont Blanc massif — this refuge serves both the TMB
(Tour du Mont Blanc) and the Via Alpina Red route. The final push toward
Monaco from here crosses the Alpes Maritimes over 10 further days.

## Planning the Via Alpina Red

The Via Alpina was established in 2002 as an international cooperative
project between the eight Alpine countries. The Red Trail's 161 stages are
documented at via-alpina.org, where stage descriptions, elevation profiles,
and accommodation lists are maintained. The full route requires approximately
four months to complete uninterrupted, making it one of the longest continuous
hiking routes in Europe. Most walkers complete it in sections over multiple
seasons.

Each country section of the Via Alpina Red has its own character and
infrastructure level. The Slovenian Julian Alps section is arguably the
most dramatic, combining excellent Slovenian mountain hut infrastructure
with genuinely wild terrain. The Austrian Tirolean section — crossing the
Carnic Alps, Lienz Dolomites, and Stubai Alps — provides the densest hut
network. The Swiss section through Graubünden and the Valais combines
high alpine passes with the SAC hut system. The French section through
the Préalpes and Alpes Maritimes is less dramatic but historically rich,
ending at Monaco with a descent from mountain terrain to the Mediterranean.

The Liechtenstein section of the Via Alpina Red is the shortest — crossing
the country in a single day — but provides the route's most curious
interlude: a crossing of Europe's smallest alpine state, a principality of
25,000 people with a genuine alpine hinterland above the Rhine valley.

## The Julian Alps as a route highlight

The Slovenian section of the Via Alpina Red — the Julian Alps from Kranjska
Gora to the Italian border — is consistently rated by long-distance walkers
as the most surprising highlight of the entire traverse. The Julian Alps
are the easternmost limestone range of the Alpine arc, geologically related
to the Dolomites and sharing their dramatic rock architecture but receiving
far fewer visitors. Triglav National Park (covering much of the Slovenian
Julian Alps) was established in 1924 and is the country's only national
park. The hut network in the Julian Alps is dense, well-maintained, and
operated by the Planinska zveza Slovenije (Slovenian Mountain Association),
which charges reasonable overnight rates.

## Alpine cultural diversity

Walking the full Via Alpina Red is an immersion in Alpine cultural diversity
that no other route matches. In a single traverse, walkers pass through
Slovenian mountain culture (mountain cabins called koče or domi), Italian
Alto Adige culture (German-speaking South Tyrol with its rifugi that also
operate as Schutzhütten), Austrian Tyrolean culture (the ÖAV Schutzhütte
tradition at its most developed), Swiss culture (SAC huts with their
distinctive efficiency and mixed-language character), and French Alpine
culture (refuges CAF with their evening table d'hôte). The Alpes Maritimes
at the southern end introduce the Provençal character — olive trees and
lavender at lower elevations, limestone peaks above.
