New Zealand's Department of Conservation (DOC) operates one of the most
comprehensive mountain hut networks in the Southern Hemisphere: over 950 huts
spread from the top of the North Island to the southern tip of Fiordland.
The Southern Alps in particular — anchored by Aoraki/Mount Cook at 3724 m —
hold a concentrated collection of serious alpine huts serving everything from
moderate tramping tracks to glacier approaches requiring full mountaineering
equipment. All ten below are plotted on the [map](/map).

## 1. Mueller Hut, 1800 m — Aoraki/Mount Cook National Park

Reached by a steep 1000 m climb from the Hermitage area via the Mueller Ridge,
Mueller Hut is the most dramatic overnight in the Cook region accessible
without glacier equipment. Built in 2003, it holds 28 people in bunk
accommodation and offers an unobstructed view of Aoraki/Mount Cook (3724 m),
the Hooker Glacier, and the Sealy Tarns below. The approach takes 3–4 hours
and involves a metal ladder section near the ridge crest. Book through the DOC
Backcountry Hut system. A full serviced hut ticket covers Great Walk standard;
standard backcountry hut tickets apply here.

## 2. Plateau Hut, 2200 m — Grand Plateau, Aoraki

The alpine hut for serious climbing on Aoraki's east face and the Grand Plateau
approach. Accessed by ski plane or helicopter from Mount Cook Village, or by
a full-day glacier approach via the Hooker and Plateau glaciers requiring
rope, crampons, and crevasse rescue competence. Capacity 24. This is a working
alpinist's hut — not a tramping destination. The landing strip conditions
(affected by temperature, snow, and weather) determine flight availability.
Book the flight and hut well in advance for January–March prime season.

## 3. Routeburn Falls Hut, 1005 m — Routeburn Track, Fiordland/Mount Aspiring

The higher Great Walk hut on the Routeburn Track, positioned above the bush
line with views down the Routeburn gorge. The Routeburn Track is a 32 km
two-to-three-day Great Walk through the Fiordland and Mount Aspiring national
park boundary. Routeburn Falls Hut holds 48 people in Great Walk standard
accommodation — warden on site, gas rings for cooking, flush toilets. Great
Walk bookings open in June for the following season and sell out within hours
for peak January–February dates.

## 4. Mintaro Hut, 975 m — Milford Track, Fiordland

The second night hut on the Milford Track (54 km), widely cited as the finest
walk in the world. Mintaro sits below the MacKinnon Pass, the highest point of
the route at 1154 m. Great Walk standard, capacity 40, warden-staffed in
season. The Milford Track is strictly regulated: independent walkers must
book through DOC, numbers are capped at 40 independents per day, and the
direction of travel (Clinton Valley to Milford Sound) is fixed. Flooding is
a genuine hazard — the Fiordland annual rainfall exceeds 6000 mm. The warden
provides daily weather briefings.

## 5. Welcome Flat Hut, 440 m — Copland Track, Westland

At the end of the Copland Track from the Westland Tai Poutini National Park
boundary, Welcome Flat offers the unique combination of natural hot pools at
the base of the Southern Alps west face. The hut itself is standard backcountry
category, sleeping 28, with no warden in residence year-round. The hot pools
are managed by DOC. The Copland Track does not cross the main divide to Mount
Cook Village — this is an out-and-back from the West Coast. The upper Copland
Valley route to Douglas Pass requires technical mountaineering equipment.

## 6. French Ridge Hut, 1470 m — Matukituki Valley, Mount Aspiring NP

In the West Matukituki Valley above Wanaka, French Ridge Hut is the standard
base for Mount Aspiring (3033 m) — New Zealand's "Matterhorn" — via the
French Ridge and Bonar Glacier approach. The hut holds 20 people in basic
bunk accommodation. The approach from the Raspberry Creek car park takes
4–5 hours. The Bonar Glacier above the hut requires rope and crampons;
guided trips from Wanaka-based operators are available and recommended for
parties without glacier experience.

## 7. Pinnacles Hut, 960 m — Kauaeranga Valley, Coromandel Peninsula

On the Pinnacles Walk in the Coromandel Forest Park north of Thames, Pinnacles
Hut is one of the most popular overnight destinations in the North Island.
The Pinnacles summit (759 m) sits above the hut and is a short scramble. The
hut is large by DOC standards — capacity 80 — with bunk accommodation and
gas cooking. The Kauaeranga Valley approach follows the Thames Valley road to
the visitor centre. Book well in advance for holiday weekends.

## 8. Centre Pass Hut, 1720 m — Inland Kaikoura Range, Marlborough

One of the more remote huts in the South Island, accessed via the Clarence
River catchment above Hanmer Springs. The Inland Kaikoura Range reaches
2885 m at Mount Tapuae-o-Uenuku — the highest peak outside the central
Southern Alps main divide. Centre Pass Hut is a key overnight on routes
crossing to the Awatere Valley. Basic bunk hut, 12 people. Navigation skills
required — marked route cairns may be snow-covered in winter and spring.

## 9. Sefton Bivouac, 1510 m — Mount Sefton, Aoraki NP

A small emergency bivouac and climbing shelter on the approach to Mount Sefton
(3151 m) from the Hermitage area. Capacity 6–8 in basic conditions. This is
an alpinist's shelter — no cooking equipment, no warden, and access requires
glacier travel on the lower Mueller and Hooker glaciers. The retreat of these
glaciers over the past two decades has significantly changed the approach
logistics; check the NZ Alpine Club conditions reports before planning.

## 10. Tasman Saddle Hut, 2300 m — Tasman Glacier, Aoraki NP

Positioned on the Tasman Saddle above the Tasman Glacier — the longest glacier
in New Zealand at 23 km — this is the high camp for routes across to
Malte Brun and the central Southern Alps. Reached by ski plane or by a full
glacier approach from the Ball Shelter. Capacity 16. The Tasman Glacier surface
is heavily crevassed above the snow line and smooth ski terrain in winter;
summer glacier travel requires considerable crevasse-avoidance skill.

## DOC booking and hut passes

Great Walk huts (Routeburn, Milford) require specific Great Walk booking through
the DOC system, typically opening in June for the following season. Standard
backcountry huts take an annual hut pass (valid at most huts) or nightly ticket
purchases. Alpine huts accessed by ski plane (Plateau, Tasman Saddle) require
separate flight arrangements through Mount Cook-based operators.

## Fiordland weather and planning margins

Fiordland National Park receives the highest average annual rainfall of any
national park in New Zealand — and one of the highest in the world. The
Milford Sound area averages over 7000 mm per year. The Milford Track's fame
for fine-weather beauty goes hand in hand with the reality that it is also
frequently experienced in rain, mist, and flood. The warden's daily weather
briefing at Mintaro Hut is not optional information — it determines whether
the MacKinnon Pass crossing on day three is a highland experience or a
survival exercise in cold rain.

The Great Walk booking system builds a degree of weather resilience into the
itinerary: you can make one free date change to your booking if weather causes
closures. Plan for the Milford by packing full waterproofs at every level —
jacket, trousers, and pack cover — and bring spare dry socks in a zip-lock.
The rewards on a fine day are extraordinary; the experience on a wet day is
still remarkable, just very wet.

## Aoraki and the New Zealand Alpine Club

The Mount Cook area huts — Mueller, Plateau, Tasman Saddle, and the Sefton
Bivouac — are used primarily by parties affiliated with or guided through the
New Zealand Alpine Club (NZAC) and the commercial guiding operations based in
Mount Cook Village. The NZAC provides route information, conditions reports, and
membership-based access to the hut system for serious alpinists.

Aoraki/Mount Cook is not a hiker's peak — the normal route via the Linda Glacier
is a sustained glacier climb at around New Zealand grade 4 (equivalent to Alpine
AD) — but the surrounding terrain has outstanding walking routes, particularly
the Hooker Valley Track (easy, 3 hours return, views to the Hooker Glacier face)
and the Sealy Tarns track to Mueller Hut. These are accessible to any fit hiker
without technical equipment and provide some of the best mountain scenery in
the Southern Hemisphere.
