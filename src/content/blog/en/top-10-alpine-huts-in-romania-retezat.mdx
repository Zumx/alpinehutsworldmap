The Retezat Mountains rise in the western reaches of the Southern Carpathians, a granite massif holding more than eighty glacial lakes scattered across high cirques between 1,800 and 2,300 metres. This is the heart of Romania's oldest protected area: the Retezat National Park, established in 1935 and later designated a UNESCO biosphere reserve, which safeguards one of the last expanses of true mountain wilderness in Europe, home to chamois, brown bears, and the Carpathian wolf. The highest summit, Peleaga, reaches 2,509 metres, and beneath it lies Bucura, the largest glacial lake in Romania. Shelter here comes in the form of the cabana — the Romanian mountain hut — supplemented by free emergency refuges and well-established lakeside camps. These ten bases anchor a trek through one of the most beautiful corners of the Carpathians.

## Romania's oldest national park

The Retezat became Romania's first national park in 1935, thanks largely to the botanist Alexandru Borza, who recognised the exceptional richness of its alpine flora. At its core lies a strictly protected scientific reserve, the Gemenele zone, closed to general access to preserve its untouched habitats. The wider park is open to walkers, who come for the lake-strewn high country and the sense of remoteness rare in modern Europe. The name Retezat — meaning roughly cut off or flattened — refers to the truncated profile of the main peak as seen from the lowlands.

## Cabana Pietrele

Cabana Pietrele, at around 1,480 metres in the Pietrele valley above the village of Nucșoara, is the principal northern gateway to the Retezat and one of the busiest huts in the range. It sits at the foot of the great cirque that holds the lakes beneath Retezat Peak (2,482 metres) and Bucura, and it serves as a base for ascents and as the first night for many trekkers entering from the north. From here the trail climbs past Lacul Pietrele and Lacul Stânișoara into the high lake basins.

## Lake Bucura and the central camp

Lake Bucura, at around 2,040 metres, is the largest glacial lake in Romania at roughly ten hectares, set in a broad cirque ringed by the highest peaks of the range. There is no hut on its shore but a long-established and heavily used camping area, which functions as the central hub of any Retezat traverse and the base for climbing Peleaga (2,509 metres) and Retezat Peak. The shoreline camp at Bucura is the natural meeting point of routes from every direction and the emotional heart of the massif.

## Cabana Buta

Cabana Buta, at around 1,580 metres in the southern Retezat, is the main southern access point, reached from the Jiu de Vest valley and the village of Câmpu lui Neag. It opens the quieter, more forested southern approaches and the route over the Plaiul Mic ridge toward Bucura. Far less crowded than the northern Pietrele entry, the Buta side rewards walkers with solitude and a different character of terrain, with limestone outcrops in the neighbouring Piule-Iorgovanu mountains.

## Cabana Gențiana

Cabana Gențiana sits at around 1,670 metres in the Pietrele valley, a little above Cabana Pietrele, and serves as a higher staging point for parties heading into the lake country. Smaller and simpler than the main cabana below, it offers basic bunk accommodation and a head start on the long climb to the high cirques. Its position among the spruce at the upper edge of the forest makes it a peaceful alternative to the busier hut downstream.

## The lake circuit: Zănoaga, Florica, Viorica, Ana

Above Bucura lies a string of named tarns that give the Retezat its character: Lacul Zănoaga, the deepest glacial lake in Romania at around 29 metres; and the chain of small lakes — Florica, Viorica, Ana, Lia — that descend the Bucura valley like steps. A day spent looping among these lakes, with the peaks reflected in still water, is for many the defining Retezat experience. The terrain is open alpine tundra, easy to walk but fully exposed to weather.

## Peleaga and the high traverse

Peleaga (2,509 metres) is the highest summit of the Retezat and the natural high point of any visit, usually climbed from the Bucura camp in a few hours. From the summit, the panorama takes in dozens of lakes, the long ridge to Păpușa (2,508 metres), and the distant Parâng and Godeanu massifs. The main ridge traverse, linking Peleaga, Bucura Peak, and the surrounding tops, is a serious full day of exposed walking on boulder and scree, rewarding in clear weather and dangerous in storm.

## Emergency refuges and free shelters

Beyond the staffed cabanas, the Retezat is dotted with unstaffed refugii — small free emergency shelters such as the one near the Bucura saddle and the Stânișoara refuge — that provide a roof in bad weather but no services. These are not intended as planned accommodation but as life-saving shelter when storms sweep the high country, which they do suddenly and violently in summer. Knowing their locations is part of safe travel in the range, where the nearest staffed hut can be hours away.

## The cabana as an institution

The Romanian cabana is an institution with deep roots, and understanding it is the key to travelling in the Retezat. Many of the mountains' huts were built in the interwar years and the socialist era, when mountain tourism was encouraged and a network of cabanas was raised across the Carpathians. Some fell into disrepair after 1989 while others were taken over by private hosts, so standards vary widely: some offer cooked meals, heating, and bunk beds, others little more than a roof and a sleeping platform. In the Retezat, the cabanas at Pietrele and Buta serve as the reliable anchor points, while the high country's lakes are reached mainly by camping. The hosts are often a source of invaluable local knowledge about weather, trail conditions, and where bears have recently been seen. Combining a night at a staffed cabana with a night in a tent by a lake gives the fullest picture of the massif, and it is how most Romanian walkers explore their oldest national park.

## Wildlife, weather, and park rules

The Retezat is genuine large-carnivore country: brown bears and wolves are present, and chamois are commonly seen on the high crags. Park regulations restrict camping to designated areas such as Bucura and Pietrele, prohibit fires, and close the Gemenele scientific reserve entirely. The hiking season runs from June to October, with July and August the most reliable, though afternoon thunderstorms are frequent and the high lakes can freeze at the season's edges. Walkers should carry all food securely and follow the park's bear-aware guidance.
