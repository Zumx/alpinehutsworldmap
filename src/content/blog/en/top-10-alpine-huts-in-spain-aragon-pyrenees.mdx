The Aragonese Pyrenees hold the roof of the entire range, the great granite massif of the Maladeta crowned by Aneto (3404 m), the highest peak in the Pyrenees, and the limestone cathedral of Monte Perdido (3355 m) at the heart of the Ordesa national park. This is high, wild country of glaciers, deep canyons and pale walls, far drier and sunnier than the French side, and it is served by a network of refugios run mainly by the Federación Aragonesa de Montañismo and the alpine clubs. From the granite world of Benasque to the limestone canyons of Ordesa, the Aragonese huts open the way to the most serious mountaineering in Spain.

## The two great massifs

The Aragonese high mountains divide into two contrasting worlds. To the east stands the Maladeta massif, a block of granite carrying the last true glaciers of the Pyrenees and the summit of Aneto, reached from the Benasque valley. To the west rises the limestone realm of Monte Perdido, the third-highest peak of the range and the highest limestone mountain in Europe, set within the Ordesa y Monte Perdido national park, one of Spain's oldest. Between them lie the Posets massif and a chain of valleys, ibones (glacial lakes) and high passes that string the refugios into classic traverses.

## Refugio de la Renclusa

The Refugio de la Renclusa at 2140 metres is the historic base for Aneto, set beneath the Maladeta at the foot of the normal route to the highest summit in the Pyrenees. From here climbers cross the Aneto glacier, the largest in the range, and traverse the famous Paso de Mahoma, a short exposed ridge, to reach the summit cross. Owned by the Centro Excursionista de Cataluña and run as an alpine club hut, the Renclusa has welcomed Pyrenean mountaineers for well over a century and remains the heart of climbing in the Maladeta.

## Refugio de Góriz

The Refugio de Góriz at 2200 metres is the essential base for Monte Perdido, set on a high shelf within the Ordesa national park beneath the great limestone walls. It is the only hut high in the park and is famously busy, the launch point for the normal route up Monte Perdido and a key stage on the long-distance routes through Ordesa. From Góriz walkers reach the Brèche de Roland on the French frontier and explore the great canyons of Ordesa and Añisclo, making it one of the most important refugios in all the Pyrenees.

## Refugio de Estós

The Refugio de Estós at 1890 metres lies in the beautiful Valle de Estós above Benasque, in the heart of the Posets-Maladeta natural park beneath the Posets massif (3375 m), the second-highest peak in the Pyrenees. A large, comfortable hut, it is a base for ascents of Posets and a key stage on the Posets-Maladeta circuit and the trans-Pyrenean routes. The Estós valley, with its meadows, forests and grazing herds, is one of the loveliest approaches in the Aragonese mountains.

## Refugio Ángel Orús

The Refugio Ángel Orús at 2148 metres above Eriste is the southern base for the Posets massif, set in a steep granite cirque beneath the Llardana, the local name for Posets. A modern hut run by the Aragonese federation, it serves the southern routes on the second-highest peak in the Pyrenees and the surrounding three-thousanders. The approach from the Eriste valley is steep and rewarding, leading into a granite world of slabs, lakes and high cirques.

## Refugio de Respomuso

The Refugio de Respomuso at 2200 metres sits beside the great reservoir of Respomuso in the upper Tena valley, in the heart of the granite peaks above Sallent de Gállego. A large modern hut, it is the base for the Balaitous (3144 m), one of the most serious mountaineering peaks in the western Pyrenees, and for the cross-border routes toward the French refuges. The setting, among glacial lakes and granite spires near the French frontier, is classic high Pyrenean country, and the hut is a key stage on the GR11 traverse of Spain.

## Refugio de Bachimaña

The Refugio de los Ibones de Bachimaña at 2200 metres sits among a constellation of glacial lakes, the ibones de Bachimaña, in the high country above the spa town of Panticosa. Set in a granite landscape of lakes and three-thousanders near the French border, it is a base for peaks such as the Vignemale region and a stage on the high routes linking the Tena and Ara valleys. The setting among the high ibones is among the most beautiful in the Aragonese Pyrenees, and the hut is a favourite for both walkers and climbers.

## Refugio de Bujaruelo and the Ordesa canyons

The valley of Bujaruelo, above Torla at the western gate of the Ordesa national park, holds historic refuges beside the old pilgrim bridge of San Nicolás de Bujaruelo, a crossing point to France for centuries. From here walkers enter the great canyon of Ordesa, with its waterfalls and beech forests beneath the Monte Perdido walls, and climb toward the Brèche de Roland. The refugios of Bujaruelo serve as a gentle base for exploring one of the most spectacular limestone landscapes in Europe, where the Cola de Caballo waterfall marks the head of the valley.

## Refugio de Respomuso and the GR11

The great long-distance route of the Spanish Pyrenees, the GR11 or Senda Pirenaica, runs the length of the range from the Atlantic to the Mediterranean, and the Aragonese refugios form its high mountain heart. Linking Respomuso, Bachimaña, Góriz and the Estós huts, the GR11 crosses the highest and wildest country in Spain, with long stages over passes above 2500 metres. These refugios, spaced a day apart, allow walkers to traverse the Aragonese Pyrenees from the granite west to the limestone east, one of the great mountain journeys of Europe.

## Wildlife, glaciers and the high country

The Aragonese Pyrenees shelter some of Spain's richest mountain wildlife. Ibex roam the limestone of Ordesa, where the Pyrenean ibex sadly became extinct in 2000, and chamois (here called sarrio) are common on the high slopes. The bearded vulture, the lammergeier, has one of its European strongholds here, gliding over the canyons, alongside griffon vultures and golden eagles. Marmots, reintroduced in the twentieth century, now whistle from the high meadows. The glaciers of the Maladeta and Monte Perdido, the southernmost in Europe, are retreating fast, and the huts are stark witnesses to their decline. The national park of Ordesa, created in 1918, was one of Spain's first, and its strict protection has preserved a landscape of canyons, forests and high pastures of extraordinary richness.

## Practical notes for the Aragonese huts

The Aragonese refugios are staffed mainly from June to September, with weekends in spring and autumn. Gateways include Benasque for the Maladeta and Posets, Torla for Ordesa, Sallent de Gállego for Respomuso, and Panticosa for Bachimaña. The walking approaches range from two to five hours, and the summit routes on Aneto, Posets and Monte Perdido require glacier or scrambling skills, with the Aneto glacier crossing and the Paso de Mahoma demanding rope, crampons and a head for heights. Reservation through the huts is essential in summer, when Góriz in particular is heavily booked, and the GR11 links the refugios into a full traverse.
