Italy has more high-mountain refuges than almost anywhere on the map, and the
Italian *rifugio* is a category of its own: part shelter, part restaurant, part
institution. The best of them combine a serious mountain position with kitchens
that take their pasta as seriously as their weather forecasts. Here are ten that
consistently stand out.

## 1. Rifugio Lagazuoi, Dolomites

Perched above 2,700 m on a ridge reached by cable car or a long walk, Lagazuoi
delivers the single most photographed sunset in the Dolomites. First World War
tunnels run straight through the rock below the terrace.

## 2. Rifugio Locatelli (Dreizinnenhütte)

The classic view of the Tre Cime di Lavaredo is from this hut's doorstep. It is
busy for a reason — the three towers fill the entire northern sky.

## 3. Rifugio Vittorio Emanuele II, Gran Paradiso

The staging post for Italy's only entirely-Italian 4,000 m peak. Climbers leave
before dawn; day hikers come for the glacier views and the alpine quiet.

## 4. Rifugio Bonatti, Mont Blanc massif

A modern, beautifully run hut on the Tour du Mont Blanc with a balcony view
straight across to the Grandes Jorasses. Reservations go fast in July.

## 5. Rifugio Auronzo

A road-accessible base on the southern side of the Tre Cime, making it the
gentlest gateway to one of the Alps' great loop walks.

## 6. Rifugio Sella, Gran Paradiso

Stone-built and historic, this is the long-standing home of ibex watching in the
national park. Early risers are almost guaranteed sightings on the slopes above.

## 7. Rifugio Brentei, Brenta Dolomites

The heart of the Brenta. From here the via ferrata network fans out across some
of the most vertical limestone in the Alps.

## 8. Rifugio Quintino Sella al Monviso

A handsome refuge under the pyramid of Monviso, the peak that gives the Po its
source. A favourite first 3,800 m objective for fit hikers.

## 9. Rifugio Marmolada (Pian dei Fiacconi area)

On the flanks of the Dolomites' highest mountain, this is the spot to watch a
shrinking glacier and to understand why these huts double as climate witnesses.

## 10. Rifugio Città di Mantova, Monte Rosa

High on the Monte Rosa massif, this is altitude training in hut form — a
practical, friendly base for the Margherita ascent.

## How the Italian hut system works

Italian rifugi are run by the Club Alpino Italiano (CAI), local sections, or
private families. CAI members and members of partner alpine clubs get a
discounted overnight rate, but anyone can stay. Half board — bed, dinner, and
breakfast — is the standard arrangement, and dinner is usually a fixed menu
served at a set time. Cash is still king at remote huts; card readers exist but
should never be relied on.

## When to go

The main season runs from mid-June to mid-September. Open huts outside that
window are rare and usually staffed only at weekends. July and August book out
weeks ahead for the famous Dolomite huts, so a September trip — shorter days but
emptier terraces and stable weather — is the connoisseur's choice.

## Booking and etiquette

Reserve by phone or email and confirm the day before; a no-show in the mountains
is a genuine problem for a hut that has cooked for you. Bring a sleeping bag
liner (sacco lenzuolo), indoor shoes are provided, and lights-out is early
because everyone else is starting before sunrise.

## Plan your own route

Every hut above appears on the [interactive map](/map). Filter by country, zoom
into the Dolomites or the western Alps, and click any marker for elevation and
links. Stringing three or four huts into a hut-to-hut traverse is the best way
to experience the Italian Alps — you walk light, eat well, and wake up already
in the mountains.
